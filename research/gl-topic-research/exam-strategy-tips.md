## 1. Understanding the GL Assessment Format

The GL Assessment 11+ is used by grammar schools across England, including the Bournemouth and Poole Grammar Schools Consortium (Bournemouth School, Bournemouth School for Girls, Parkstone Grammar School, and Poole Grammar School). It consists of three multiple-choice papers:

| Paper | Questions | Time | Approx. per Question |
|-------|-----------|------|---------------------|
| English | ~50 | ~50 mins | 60 seconds |
| Maths | ~50 | ~50 mins | 60 seconds |
| Verbal Reasoning | ~80 | ~50 mins | 37 seconds |

**Key facts every child should know:**
- All questions are multiple choice (typically 5 options, A-E)
- There is **no negative marking** -- wrong answers do not lose marks
- Every question is worth the same number of marks
- Questions are drawn from a large question bank, so no two papers are identical
- The test is paper-based and non-adaptive (all children get the same questions)

## 2. How GL Assessment Scores Work

Understanding how scoring works should shape your child's exam strategy.

### Raw Scores
The raw score is simply the number of questions answered correctly. If a section has 50 questions and your child answers 38 correctly, their raw score is 38. There is no partial credit and no deductions for wrong answers.

### Standardised Age Scores (SAS)
Raw scores are converted into Standardised Age Scores (SAS) that account for the child's age in years and months. This ensures a child born in September is not unfairly advantaged over one born in August.

- The **average SAS is 100**
- The standard deviation is 15 points
- Approximately two-thirds of children score between 85 and 115
- The SAS range is typically 69 to 141
- Younger children receive a slightly higher age adjustment

**Example:** A September-born child and an August-born child both answer 100 questions correctly. The older child might receive an SAS of 118, while the younger child gets 121.

### Pass Marks
There is no fixed, published pass mark. The qualifying standard varies each year based on:
- The number of applicants
- The overall performance of the cohort
- The number of available places

Achieving the qualifying standard does not guarantee a place, as all four consortium schools are regularly oversubscribed. Different schools may also weight subjects differently.

### Why This Matters for Strategy
Since there is **no negative marking**, the strategic implication is clear:

> **Never leave a question blank. Ever. A guess gives you a 20% chance of a free mark. A blank gives you 0%.**

This single principle can be worth several raw marks across a full paper.

## 3. Exam Day Strategy

### The Night Before

1. **Pack everything the evening before.** Pencils (at least 3 sharp ones), eraser, sharpener, ruler, a clear water bottle, an analogue watch. Check what the school's guidance says is permitted -- some ban coloured pencils or pencil cases.

2. **No last-minute cramming.** Research consistently shows that cramming the night before adds anxiety without adding knowledge. If your child has prepared well, one more evening will not make a difference. If they haven't, one evening of panic won't help either.

3. **Eat a normal dinner.** Include protein and complex carbohydrates. Salmon or oily fish is excellent for brain function (omega-3 fatty acids). Avoid anything unusually heavy or unfamiliar that might cause stomach upset.

4. **Aim for 9-10 hours of sleep.** This is when memory consolidation happens. A well-rested child will outperform a tired one who revised an extra hour. If your child struggles to sleep, do a calm activity like reading fiction (not exam content) before bed.

5. **Set out clothes.** Comfortable layers are best -- exam rooms can be unpredictably warm or cold. Avoid clothes with distracting logos or text.

6. **Talk about the plan, not the stakes.** "Tomorrow you'll do your best and we'll be proud of you whatever happens" is far more helpful than "This really matters." Remind them of the practical plan: where to go, who's dropping them off, what time.

### The Morning Of

7. **Stick to the normal routine.** Anything unusual increases anxiety. If your child normally has cereal, don't suddenly present a full cooked breakfast they're not used to.

8. **Eat a good breakfast.** The best exam-day breakfasts combine slow-release carbohydrates with protein:
   - Porridge or oatmeal with fruit
   - Wholegrain toast with eggs
   - Low-fat yoghurt with fruit and granola
   - Avoid sugary cereals or pastries (they cause an energy crash)

9. **Hydrate.** The brain is approximately 73% water. Even mild dehydration impairs concentration. Have water with breakfast and bring a clear bottle to the exam.

10. **Arrive early but not too early.** 15-20 minutes before doors open is ideal. Arriving too early means standing around absorbing other children's (and parents') anxiety.

11. **Final reminders should be brief and positive:**
    - "Read each question twice"
    - "If you're stuck, move on and come back"
    - "Answer every single question, even if you guess"
    - "You've got this"

12. **Do not discuss specific topics or try quick-fire questions** in the car park. This creates last-minute panic about things they can't remember.

### In the Exam Room

13. **Listen carefully to the invigilator's instructions.** They may provide crucial information about how to fill in the answer sheet, whether to write in the question booklet, and how time warnings will be given.

14. **Write your name and details carefully.** It sounds obvious, but rushing this step can cause problems.

15. **Wear an analogue watch.** Many exam rooms don't have a visible clock, and invigilators can forget to announce time. Children must take responsibility for tracking their own time. Digital watches and smart watches are typically banned.

16. **Expect some children to be distressed.** Prepare your child for the fact that some children may cry or become upset during the exam. They must stay focused on their own paper and not be drawn into others' emotions.

17. **Keep your head down.** Looking at other candidates' papers can result in the invigilator moving you, issuing a warning, or in extreme cases disqualifying your paper.

18. **If you finish early, check your work.** Go back to any questions you flagged, then do a final scan of the answer sheet to make sure every question has been answered.

## 4. Time Management Techniques

Time management is the single biggest differentiator between children who perform to their ability and those who underperform. A child who knows the material but runs out of time will score lower than a child who manages the clock.

### The Maths of Time

| Paper | Total Time | Questions | Time per Q | Checkpoint |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| English | 50 mins | ~50 | 60 sec | Q25 at 25 mins |
| Maths | 50 mins | ~50 | 60 sec | Q25 at 25 mins |
| VR | 50 mins | ~80 | 37 sec | Q40 at 25 mins |

### The Clock-Check Habit

Teach your child to glance at their watch at regular intervals:
- **After every 10 questions** for English and Maths
- **After every 15-20 questions** for Verbal Reasoning
- **At the halfway point** (25 minutes), check: "Am I roughly halfway through?"

If they're behind, they need to speed up -- which means being more willing to guess and move on. If they're ahead, they can afford to spend a little longer on tricky questions.

### The 50-30-10-10 Rule

A practical framework for splitting time across difficulty levels:

1. **First 50% of time (25 mins):** Answer all the questions you can do quickly and confidently. These are your "banked" marks. Do not get bogged down.
2. **Next 30% of time (15 mins):** Work through the questions that need more thought. These are the ones that require a method or some working out.
3. **Next 10% of time (5 mins):** Attempt the hardest questions. Use estimation and elimination.
4. **Final 10% of time (5 mins):** Review, check flagged questions, ensure every question is answered.

### Time Traps to Avoid

- **The "nearly there" trap:** Spending 3 minutes on a question because "I almost have it." If it's taking more than 90 seconds, flag it and move on.
- **The perfectionist trap:** Checking every answer immediately after answering it. Save checking for the end.
- **The reading trap:** Reading the passage three times before looking at any questions (for English comprehension).
- **The calculation trap:** Working out an exact answer when estimation and elimination could identify the correct option in seconds.

## 5. The Two-Pass Strategy

This is the single most important exam technique for the 11+. It should be practised until it becomes automatic.

### How It Works

**Pass 1 -- Quick Sweep (first 25-30 minutes):**
- Work through every question in order
- Answer immediately if you know it or can work it out in under 45 seconds
- If a question looks difficult or time-consuming, make your best guess, put a small pencil mark next to the question number, and move on
- The goal is to reach the end of the paper, banking every easy and medium mark

**Pass 2 -- Return Sweep (remaining time):**
- Go back to every marked question
- With remaining time and a calmer head, attempt the harder questions properly
- If you still cannot answer, keep your original guess (research shows first instincts are often right)
- In the final 2 minutes, scan the answer sheet to ensure nothing is blank

### Why It Works

- **It prevents time theft:** One hard question at the start of the paper cannot steal time from five easy questions at the end
- **It builds confidence:** Answering 30+ questions in the first pass creates momentum and reduces anxiety
- **It uses the brain's background processing:** While working on other questions, your subconscious mind often continues working on the tricky ones
- **It maximises marks:** Every easy mark is secured before any time is spent on hard questions

### Teaching It

The two-pass strategy must be practised during mock papers. Children who learn it for the first time on exam day will not use it effectively. During practice:
- Use a timer
- Insist on moving on after 60 seconds per question
- Review how many marks they gained versus lost by skipping
- Celebrate the discipline of moving on (this feels unnatural to children who want to "finish" each question)

### The Bubble Sheet Warning

When skipping questions on the answer sheet, children must be extremely careful not to get out of sync. If question 12 is skipped but the answer for question 13 is put in row 12, every subsequent answer will be in the wrong row. Two options:
1. **Put in your best guess** for skipped questions (recommended -- keeps the sheet in sync and gives you a chance at the mark)
2. **Leave the row blank** but be very careful when you return to fill it in

Option 1 is strongly recommended for GL Assessment since there is no negative marking.

## 6. Multiple Choice Elimination Techniques

With 5 options per question (A-E), a random guess has a 20% chance of being correct. But elimination can dramatically improve those odds.

### The Elimination Method

1. **Read the question carefully** -- make sure you know what's being asked
2. **Read all five options** before choosing (don't jump at the first plausible answer)
3. **Cross out obviously wrong answers** mentally or with a light pencil mark
4. **If you can eliminate 2 options**, you now have a 1-in-3 chance (33%)
5. **If you can eliminate 3 options**, you now have a 1-in-2 chance (50%)

### Elimination Techniques by Question Type

**Maths:**
- **Estimation:** Before calculating precisely, estimate the answer. If the question asks "What is 48 x 27?", estimate 50 x 30 = 1,500. Any options far from 1,500 can be eliminated.
- **Units check:** If the answer should be in cm, eliminate any options in m or cm squared.
- **Odd/even check:** If two odd numbers are multiplied, the answer must be odd. Eliminate even options.
- **Last digit check:** For multiplication, the last digit of the answer is determined by the last digits of the numbers being multiplied. 48 x 27: 8 x 7 = 56, so the answer ends in 6.

**English:**
- **Context clues:** For vocabulary questions, reread the sentence with each option substituted in. Which one sounds right in context?
- **Part of speech:** If the answer needs to be a verb, eliminate any options that are nouns or adjectives.
- **Extreme answers:** Options using words like "always", "never", or "everyone" in comprehension are often wrong.

**Verbal Reasoning:**
- **Test each option systematically:** Don't just pick the first option that looks right. Test each one against the rule or pattern.
- **Check for common traps:** In analogy questions, check whether the relationship is the same type (synonym, antonym, part-whole, etc.).

### The Research on Changing Answers

Many children (and adults) believe you should "stick with your first answer." **Research consistently shows this is wrong.**

Studies found that when students change answers:
- 65% of changes go from wrong to right
- Only about 10% go from right to wrong
- 25% go from wrong to wrong (no net loss)

**The rule:** Change your answer if you have a clear, specific reason to do so (you misread the question, you spotted a calculation error, you realised a better approach). Do not change your answer based on vague anxiety or second-guessing.

## 7. Common Mistakes Children Make

Based on professional tutor experience and mock exam analysis, these are the most common mark-losing errors.

### Mistake 1: Not Reading the Question Properly

**The problem:** Children skim-read questions and miss crucial words like "not", "except", "which of these is false", or "give your answer in centimetres."

**The fix:**
- Underline key words in the question before answering
- Read the question twice -- once to understand it, once to check you haven't missed anything
- Pay special attention to words in CAPITAL LETTERS or bold
- Watch for questions that ask for the "odd one out" or "which is NOT correct"

### Mistake 2: Rushing Through Easy Questions

**The problem:** Approximately 40% of children get the easiest questions wrong in mock tests. They assume easy questions don't need attention and make careless errors.

**The fix:**
- Easy marks are still marks. Treat every question with respect.
- Read all five options even if option A looks correct
- Do a quick sanity check: "Does this answer make sense?"

### Mistake 3: Spending Too Long on Hard Questions

**The problem:** Children get stuck on a difficult question and refuse to move on, burning 3-4 minutes on one question while 5 easy questions at the end go unanswered.

**The fix:**
- Practise the two-pass strategy (Section 5)
- Set a hard limit: if you're not making progress after 60 seconds, guess and move on
- Remind them: one hard question is worth exactly the same as one easy question

### Mistake 4: Not Checking Units

**The problem:** The question asks for the answer in metres but the child gives it in centimetres. Or the question involves hours and minutes and the child treats everything as minutes.

**The fix:**
- Circle the units mentioned in the question
- Before selecting an answer, check: "What unit should my answer be in?"
- For conversions, practise the common ones until automatic: mm-cm-m-km, g-kg, ml-l

### Mistake 5: Misaligning the Answer Sheet

**The problem:** The child skips a question but doesn't skip the corresponding row on the answer sheet. Every subsequent answer goes in the wrong row.

**The fix:**
- Always put a guess in for skipped questions (keeps the sheet aligned)
- Every 10 questions, do a quick check: "Is question 10's answer in row 10?"
- If you discover a misalignment, stop and fix it immediately -- don't continue hoping to sort it out later

### Mistake 6: Changing Correct Answers to Wrong Ones

**The problem:** During review time, children second-guess themselves and change correct answers based on anxiety rather than reason.

**The fix:**
- Only change an answer if you have a specific, concrete reason
- "I feel unsure" is not a good enough reason
- "I misread the question" or "I made a calculation error" are valid reasons
- Mark changed answers so you can review the pattern in practice papers

### Mistake 7: Running Out of Time

**The problem:** The child reaches the end of the paper with 15 questions unanswered and no time to attempt them.

**The fix:**
- In the final 2 minutes, if there are unanswered questions, fill in guesses for all of them immediately
- Even random guessing across 15 questions should net 3 marks on average (with 5 options)
- Those 3 marks could be the difference between passing and failing

### Mistake 8: Ignoring the Explanation in Practice

**The problem:** During practice, children check whether they got the right answer but don't read the explanation for wrong answers. They make the same mistake repeatedly.

**The fix:**
- Keep a mistake journal: write down every wrong answer and the correct method
- After each practice paper, spend 10-15 minutes reviewing mistakes
- Give your child the same question again a week later to check if the lesson stuck

### Mistake 9: Over-Reliance on Memorisation

**The problem:** The 11+ tests reasoning, problem-solving, and application of knowledge -- not memorised facts. Children who have drilled thousands of questions but don't understand the underlying concepts will struggle with unfamiliar question formats.

**The fix:**
- Ask your child to explain their method, not just give the answer
- Vary question types so they can't rely on pattern-matching
- If they get a question right, occasionally ask: "How did you work that out?"

### Mistake 10: Leaving Questions Blank

**The problem:** The child doesn't know the answer, so they leave it blank. With no negative marking, this is the single worst thing they can do.

**The fix:**
- Drill this into them: "Never leave a blank. Put something. Anything."
- Even random selection gives a 20% chance
- Elimination plus guessing can give 33-50% chance
- Make it a non-negotiable rule during every practice paper

## 8. Question Approach by Subject

### 8A. Maths: Word Problems

Use the RUCSAC method -- a systematic approach that prevents children from diving in without understanding what's being asked:

1. **R**ead the question carefully (twice)
2. **U**nderline the key information and the question being asked
3. **C**hoose the operation(s) needed (add, subtract, multiply, divide)
4. **S**olve the problem step by step
5. **A**nswer the actual question (not an intermediate step)
6. **C**heck -- does the answer make sense? Is it in the right units?

**Common word problem traps:**
- Multi-step problems where children answer the first step, not the final answer
- "How many more..." questions where children give the total instead of the difference
- Problems involving money where children forget to convert between pounds and pence
- Time problems that cross hour boundaries (e.g., 45 minutes after 10:40)

**The estimation check:** Before answering, quickly estimate what a reasonable answer would be. If the question asks about the cost of 8 items at approximately $3 each, the answer should be around $24. If your calculated answer is $240 or $2.40, something has gone wrong.

### 8B. English: Reading Comprehension

**The "Read First" strategy is recommended for most children:**

1. Read the entire passage once, steadily and carefully (2-3 minutes)
2. As you read, notice: Who? Where? When? What's happening? What's the mood?
3. Then read the questions
4. For each question, go back to the relevant part of the text to find or confirm your answer
5. Answer using evidence from the text, not your own assumptions

**Why read the passage first?**
- Complex inference questions require understanding of the whole text
- Character feelings and themes can only be grasped by reading in full
- The structure and tone of the passage provide clues scattered throughout
- Trying to scan for answers without context leads to misinterpretation

**Question types and techniques:**

| Question Type | What It Tests | Technique |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|
| Information retrieval | Finding facts stated directly | Scan for key words from the question |
| Inference | Reading between the lines | Look for clues about feelings, motives, implications |
| Vocabulary in context | Understanding word meaning from context | Re-read the sentence, try substituting each option |
| Figurative language | Understanding metaphor, simile, personification | Identify the device, explain what it suggests |
| Author's purpose | Why the writer made specific choices | Consider effect on the reader, not just meaning |

**Tricky comprehension traps:**
- "According to the text" means the answer is IN the text -- don't use outside knowledge
- Vocabulary questions ask what the word means IN THIS CONTEXT, not its usual meaning
- Inference questions often have "suggest", "imply", or "what do you think" in them
- If two options both seem correct, the one supported by textual evidence is the answer

### 8C. Verbal Reasoning: Systematic Approaches

VR requires the most disciplined approach because of the time pressure (~37 seconds per question for 80 questions). Each question type has a specific technique.

**General VR principles:**
- Read the instructions for every question -- they tell you exactly what to do
- Work methodically, not by instinct
- For vocabulary questions, if unsure, say each option aloud in your head -- the right answer often "sounds" right
- For code questions, check every letter/number, don't assume the pattern continues

**Key VR question types and techniques:**

**Synonyms and Antonyms:**
- If you don't know the word, think about where you've seen or heard it before
- Look for word roots (e.g., "un-" means not, "re-" means again, "pre-" means before)
- Try each option in a sentence to see which fits the same meaning

**Hidden Words:**
- Work systematically: look at the end of each word joining the start of the next word
- Move through the sentence left to right, checking every word boundary
- The hidden word is almost always 4 letters, spanning two words

**Compound Words:**
- Think of common compound words: football, bedroom, sunshine, etc.
- Test each option as both the first half and second half of a compound
- Say them aloud in your head -- compound words should sound natural

**Letter Codes:**
- Work out the code by comparing the first example (word with its code)
- Check: is each letter shifted by the same number of places in the alphabet?
- Be careful -- the shift might be different for each letter position
- Always verify your rule against ALL given examples before applying it

**Analogies (A is to B as C is to ?):**
- First, identify the relationship between A and B
- Then apply the same relationship to C to find the answer
- Common relationships: synonyms, antonyms, part-whole, category-member, cause-effect, size, degree

**Odd One Out:**
- Find what 4 of the 5 words have in common
- The answer is the one that doesn't share that property
- Watch for trap answers where a word shares ONE property but not the defining one

### 8D. Error Spotting

For questions that ask children to find spelling, grammar, or punctuation errors:

**The segment-by-segment technique:**
1. Read the sentence in chunks: subject | verb | object | rest
2. Check each chunk separately for:
   - Spelling (does every word look right?)
   - Grammar (does the verb agree with the subject? Is the tense consistent?)
   - Punctuation (are apostrophes, commas, and full stops in the right places?)
3. Pay special attention to commonly confused words: there/their/they're, its/it's, where/were/we're, affect/effect, practice/practise
4. Check that the sentence makes sense as a whole

## 9. Mental Maths Speed Techniques

In a timed exam, being able to calculate quickly in your head saves precious seconds on every question. These shortcuts should be practised until automatic.

### Multiplication Shortcuts

**Multiplying by 10, 100, 1000:** Move the decimal point right by 1, 2, or 3 places.
- 34 x 10 = 340
- 34 x 100 = 3,400

**Multiplying by 5:** Multiply by 10, then halve.
- 34 x 5 = 34 x 10 / 2 = 340 / 2 = 170

**Multiplying by 11:** For two-digit numbers, add the digits and put the result between them.
- 43 x 11: 4+3=7, answer is 473
- 67 x 11: 6+7=13, carry the 1: answer is 737

**Multiplying by 15:** Multiply by 10, then add half of that.
- 24 x 15: 24 x 10 = 240, half of 240 = 120, 240 + 120 = 360

**Multiplying by 25:** Multiply by 100, then divide by 4.
- 36 x 25 = 36 x 100 / 4 = 3,600 / 4 = 900

**Multiplying by 9:** Multiply by 10, then subtract the original number.
- 34 x 9 = 34 x 10 - 34 = 340 - 34 = 306

**The 9s finger trick:** Hold up all 10 fingers. For 9 x 3, fold down finger 3 (counting from left). Fingers to the left = 2, fingers to the right = 7. Answer: 27.

### Division Shortcuts

**Dividing by 5:** Double the number, then divide by 10.
- 145 / 5: 145 x 2 = 290, 290 / 10 = 29

**Dividing by 4:** Halve the number, then halve again.
- 124 / 4: 124 / 2 = 62, 62 / 2 = 31

### Percentage Shortcuts

**The 10% building block:** Find 10% by dividing by 10, then build up:
- 10% of 350 = 35
- 5% = half of 10% = 17.5
- 20% = double 10% = 70
- 15% = 10% + 5% = 35 + 17.5 = 52.5
- 40% = 4 x 10% = 140
- 25% = divide by 4 (or halve, then halve again)

**The flip trick:** X% of Y = Y% of X.
- 8% of 50 is hard. But 50% of 8 = 4. Same answer.
- 4% of 25 is hard. But 25% of 4 = 1. Same answer.

**Common percentage-fraction equivalents to memorise:**
| Percentage | Fraction | Decimal |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| 50% | 1/2 | 0.5 |
| 25% | 1/4 | 0.25 |
| 75% | 3/4 | 0.75 |
| 10% | 1/10 | 0.1 |
| 20% | 1/5 | 0.2 |
| 33.3% | 1/3 | 0.333... |
| 12.5% | 1/8 | 0.125 |

### Fraction Shortcuts

**Comparing fractions quickly:** Cross-multiply to compare.
- Is 3/7 or 2/5 bigger? 3x5=15 vs 2x7=14. 15 > 14, so 3/7 is bigger.

**Adding fractions with the butterfly method:**
- 2/3 + 1/4: Cross-multiply tops with opposite bottoms: (2x4)+(1x3) = 8+3 = 11. Multiply bottoms: 3x4 = 12. Answer: 11/12.

### Divisibility Rules

These help children quickly check whether division will work cleanly:

| Divisible by | Rule | Example |
|-------------|------|---------|
| 2 | Last digit is even | 374 (yes, ends in 4) |
| 3 | Sum of digits divisible by 3 | 372: 3+7+2=12 (yes) |
| 4 | Last two digits divisible by 4 | 316: 16/4=4 (yes) |
| 5 | Ends in 0 or 5 | 435 (yes) |
| 6 | Divisible by both 2 AND 3 | 372 (yes) |
| 8 | Last three digits divisible by 8 | 1,024: 024/8=3 (yes) |
| 9 | Sum of digits divisible by 9 | 738: 7+3+8=18 (yes) |
| 10 | Ends in 0 | 450 (yes) |

### Estimation as a Checking Tool

After calculating an answer, do a quick estimate to check if it's reasonable:
- Round numbers to the nearest 10 or 100
- Do the simplified calculation in your head
- If your precise answer is wildly different from your estimate, recalculate

**Example:** 48 x 27 = ? Your calculation gives 1,296. Quick estimate: 50 x 30 = 1,500. That's in the right ballpark, so 1,296 is likely correct. If your calculation had given 12,960, the estimate would flag the error.

### Daily Practice Routine

5-10 minutes of daily mental maths practice produces noticeable improvement within 3-4 weeks. Full automaticity typically takes 8-12 weeks. Activities:
- Times tables speed drills (aim for instant recall of all tables to 12x12)
- "What's 10% of..." quick-fire rounds
- "Double it, halve it" chains
- Estimation games with real-world numbers

## 10. Practice Paper Strategy

Practice papers are the backbone of 11+ preparation, but they must be used strategically. Mindlessly grinding through papers without reflection produces diminishing returns.

### How to Use Practice Papers Effectively

**Phase 1 -- Untimed (Months 6-4 before exam):**
- Complete papers without time pressure
- Focus on accuracy and understanding
- Read every explanation for every wrong answer
- Build confidence and familiarity with question types

**Phase 2 -- Loosely Timed (Months 4-2 before exam):**
- Give slightly more time than the real exam (e.g., 60 minutes for a 50-minute paper)
- Begin developing time awareness
- Practise the two-pass strategy
- Review mistakes and identify patterns

**Phase 3 -- Strictly Timed (Final 2 months):**
- Exact exam conditions: correct time, no breaks, no help
- Use an analogue watch (not a phone timer)
- Practise filling in answer sheets
- Build exam stamina

**Phase 4 -- Exam Conditions (Final 2-3 weeks):**
- Sit papers at the same time of day as the real exam
- Complete all papers back-to-back as they would in the real exam
- Use proper answer sheets
- No talking, no bathroom breaks except between papers

### The Mistake Review Process

This is where the real learning happens. After every practice paper:

1. **Mark the paper** and record the score
2. **Categorise every wrong answer:**
   - Silly mistake (knew the method but made an error)
   - Time pressure (ran out of time, guessed)
   - Knowledge gap (didn't know the method or concept)
   - Misread question (understood the topic but answered the wrong thing)
3. **For knowledge gaps:** Re-teach the concept, then do 3-5 similar questions
4. **For silly mistakes:** Identify the pattern. Same type of error? Same topic?
5. **For time pressure:** Practise speed on that question type specifically
6. **Keep a mistake journal:** Write the question, your wrong answer, and the correct method. Review weekly.

### How Many Practice Papers?

- **Enough to be familiar with every question type** -- typically 8-15 full papers
- **Not so many that your child is exhausted and resentful** -- watch for burnout
- **Quality over quantity:** One paper with thorough review is worth more than three papers with no review
- **Variety matters:** Use papers from different publishers (CGP, Bond, Letts, Collins, GL Assessment official papers) to avoid over-familiarity with one style

### Diminishing Returns

Research on practice and skill acquisition shows that returns diminish over time. The first 5 practice papers produce enormous improvement. Papers 6-10 produce moderate improvement. Beyond 15-20 papers, the returns are minimal unless you're doing targeted work on specific weaknesses.

**Signs of diminishing returns:**
- Scores plateau despite continued practice
- Your child is making the same mistakes repeatedly
- Motivation is dropping
- Anxiety is increasing rather than decreasing

**When this happens:** Stop doing full papers. Switch to targeted practice on specific weak topics, or take a break and return refreshed.

## 11. The Final 4 Weeks

The last month before the exam requires a careful shift in approach. The bulk of learning should already be done. Now it's about sharpening, not building.

### Week 4 (4 weeks before): Consolidation

- Complete 1-2 full timed papers per week
- Identify any remaining weak areas from these papers
- Do targeted work on those specific topics (15-20 minutes per day)
- Continue daily mental maths (5-10 minutes)
- Continue daily reading (15 minutes)

### Week 3 (3 weeks before): Exam Technique Focus

- Complete 1 full timed paper
- Focus entirely on technique: time management, two-pass strategy, answer sheet discipline
- Review the mistake journal -- look for recurring patterns
- Practise estimation and elimination techniques
- Begin winding down the intensity slightly

### Week 2 (2 weeks before): Tapering

- Complete 1 final full timed paper (ideally at the same time of day as the real exam)
- Short, focused practice sessions (20-30 minutes maximum)
- Focus on confidence-building: do questions in your child's strongest topics
- Begin shifting from "preparation" to "readiness"
- Ensure good sleep habits are established

### Week 1 (Final week): Rest and Readiness

**Do:**
- Light revision only: 10-15 minute sessions on familiar topics
- Keep mental maths ticking over with quick games
- Maintain the daily reading habit
- Focus on wellbeing: exercise, fresh air, time with friends
- Prepare all equipment and logistics
- Remind your child of their exam technique plan
- Eat well and sleep well

**Do NOT:**
- Introduce any new topics or question types
- Do full timed papers (this increases anxiety)
- Cram vocabulary lists or formulas
- Talk constantly about the exam
- Show your own anxiety (children absorb parental stress)
- Change the daily routine dramatically
- Promise rewards contingent on results (this adds pressure)

### The Night Before and Morning Of

See Section 3 for detailed guidance. The key message: normality, calm, confidence.

### What to Say (and Not Say)

**Say:**
- "Whatever happens, we're proud of you"
- "Just do your best"
- "Remember your plan: read twice, skip and come back, answer everything"
- "You've prepared really well for this"

**Don't say:**
- "This is really important"
- "You need to get X marks"
- "Your friend is also sitting it -- I wonder how they'll do?"
- "Don't worry" (this paradoxically increases worry)
- "I'm sure you'll pass" (creates fear of letting you down)

## 12. What Professional Tutors Say

These insights are compiled from professional 11+ tutor blogs, tuition centre advice, and experienced tutor forums.

### What Separates Children Who Pass from Those Who Don't

According to experienced tutors, the differentiating factors are consistently:

1. **Exam technique, not just knowledge.** "The 11+ is as much about time management and technique as it is about knowledge." Children who know the material but lack technique consistently underperform children who have drilled exam strategies.

2. **Consistent, spaced practice over time.** "A little-and-often approach is far more beneficial than a 6-hour block." Tutors repeatedly emphasise that 20-30 minutes daily is vastly more effective than weekend marathon sessions.

3. **Familiarity with the specific exam format.** "Familiarisation with the type of questions results in a significant increase in children's scores." GL Assessment questions have a specific style. Children who have practised with GL-format papers perform markedly better than those who have only done generic maths or English.

4. **The ability to move on from difficult questions.** Tutors identify this as perhaps the hardest skill to teach. Many bright children want to solve every question and cannot bring themselves to skip one.

5. **Active learning from mistakes.** "Most children don't improve with more practice -- they improve with better reflection on their practice." The children who pass tend to have a process for reviewing what went wrong and why.

6. **Reading fluency and breadth.** Every tutor emphasises this. Children who read widely and regularly have a natural advantage in vocabulary, comprehension speed, inference skills, and general knowledge that no amount of cramming can replicate.

7. **Emotional regulation under pressure.** "Many children do well in practice exercises at home but struggle under the pressure of a ticking clock." Tutors stress that mock exams under real conditions are essential, not for the content but for the emotional practice.

### Tutor Red Flags

Professional tutors warn about these patterns:

- **Starting too late:** Beginning preparation 3 months before the exam is possible but stressful. 12-18 months is ideal for building skills gradually.
- **Over-reliance on tutoring alone:** A tutor provides structure and expert guidance, but improvement happens between sessions through practice.
- **Practising without reviewing:** "Stop wasting practice papers." If your child isn't learning from mistakes, additional papers are pointless.
- **Neglecting reading:** "No shortcut replaces reading." Vocabulary, comprehension speed, and inference skills develop through reading, not through vocabulary lists alone.
- **Parental anxiety transfer:** Tutors consistently report that the most anxious children tend to have the most anxious parents. Your calm is their calm.

### The Three-Stage Tutor Approach

Many professional tutors follow a three-stage model:

1. **Foundation (Year 4 / Early Year 5):** Build core skills -- reading accuracy and speed, mental maths fluency, vocabulary development, reasoning familiarity
2. **Application (Mid Year 5):** Apply skills to 11+ question types, develop exam technique, identify and address weaknesses
3. **Peak Performance (Summer of Year 5 / Early Year 6):** Timed papers, mock exams, fine-tuning technique, building speed and confidence

## 13. What Parents Say Worked

Compiled from Mumsnet, ElevenPlusExams forum, and parent discussion threads. These are real experiences from parents whose children went through the process.

### Preparation Strategies That Worked

- **"A word a day" post-it note** on the front door. The child finds the meaning and uses it in a sentence by the end of the day. Simple, consistent, effective.
- **Little and often:** "From January of Year 5, 15-30 minutes most weekday mornings before school. Keeping sessions short as part of the morning routine meant there was no drama."
- **Variety of publishers:** "Don't just use Bond. CGP books are excellent. Also Letts, Collins, Schofield & Sims, First Past the Post." Each publisher has slightly different question styles, which builds adaptability.
- **Mock exams:** "Book a mock or two if you can afford it over Easter or Summer. Don't worry if they do terribly -- it's just to get them used to time pressure."
- **Making it normal:** Several parents stressed making 11+ practice part of the routine, not a special event. "It was just something we did for 20 minutes after school, like piano practice."

### Exam Day Experiences

- **Arrive with time but not too much:** "We arrived 15 minutes early. Some families had been there 45 minutes and the children were in tears from anxiety."
- **Have a treat planned:** "We had cinema tickets for the afternoon. She came out knowing something fun was happening, which took the edge off."
- **Don't ask how it went:** "The worst thing you can do is interrogate them about specific questions. Just say 'well done for getting through it' and move on to something fun."
- **Prepare for other children's reactions:** "My daughter was thrown because the girl next to her started crying. We'd talked about this in advance, which helped."

### What Didn't Work

- **Cramming:** "We tried doing 3 papers a day in the last week. It was a disaster. She was exhausted and scored worse than she had three weeks earlier."
- **Rewards tied to results:** "We promised a new phone if he passed. The pressure was awful. He sat there thinking about the phone instead of the questions."
- **Comparing with friends:** "She found out her best friend had a tutor and was doing 2 hours a day. The comparison stressed her out massively."
- **Over-practising VR:** "We did so much VR that he was burned out on it. He actually did better on the maths (which we'd practised less) because he was still fresh."

## 14. Quick Reference Checklist

Print this page and pin it somewhere visible in the weeks before the exam.

### The Night Before
- [ ] All equipment packed (3+ sharp pencils, eraser, sharpener, ruler, water bottle, analogue watch)
- [ ] Clothes laid out (comfortable layers)
- [ ] Know the route, parking, drop-off point, and timing
- [ ] Normal dinner, no junk food
- [ ] No revision -- light reading or a calm activity
- [ ] Early to bed, aim for 9-10 hours of sleep
- [ ] Positive but low-key conversation about tomorrow

### The Morning Of
- [ ] Normal breakfast (porridge, eggs on toast, yoghurt with fruit)
- [ ] Water with breakfast
- [ ] No last-minute quizzing
- [ ] Leave in good time
- [ ] Brief, calm reminders: read twice, skip and come back, answer everything

### In the Exam: The Golden Rules
- [ ] Read every question twice
- [ ] Underline key words
- [ ] Use the two-pass strategy (answer easy ones first, come back to hard ones)
- [ ] Check the clock every 10 questions
- [ ] Never spend more than 90 seconds on one question
- [ ] Eliminate wrong answers before guessing
- [ ] NEVER leave a question blank
- [ ] If in doubt, go with your first instinct (unless you spot a clear error)
- [ ] In the last 2 minutes, check every question has an answer

### After the Exam
- [ ] Celebrate the effort, not the outcome
- [ ] Don't ask about specific questions
- [ ] Do something fun and completely unrelated
- [ ] If there are more papers, stay calm and repeat the routine
- [ ] Remind them: "What's done is done, and you did your best"
