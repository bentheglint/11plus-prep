# GL Assessment Research: Reading Comprehension

## Format

### Paper Structure (Dorset Consortium — Bournemouth/Parkstone Grammar)
- **Total English paper:** 45 minutes, ~49 questions, entirely multiple choice (A-E, 5 options)
- **Comprehension section:** ~13-18 questions on a single passage of approximately 500 words (~2 pages)
- **Remaining questions:** Spelling (~4-8), Punctuation (~4-8), Grammar/Cloze (~4-8)
- **Answer method:** Separate OMR bubble sheet
- **No creative writing** — the entire English paper is multiple choice
- **Key insight:** Comprehension accounts for approximately 50% of the English paper marks. Technical English (SPaG + cloze) accounts for the other 50%.

### Passage Characteristics
- Single continuous passage, approximately 500 words (roughly 2 pages of text)
- Reading level is deliberately above chronological age — pitched at a Year 7-8 reading level for Year 6 children
- Passages are drawn from published works, not written specifically for the exam
- Text may be fiction, non-fiction, or poetry — traditional or contemporary
- Children read the passage first, then answer all comprehension questions relating to it

### Question Presentation
- Questions are NOT separated by type — retrieval, inference, vocabulary, and literary device questions are mixed together in a "scattershot" format
- This requires children to constantly shift between different thinking modes
- Questions roughly follow the order of the passage (early questions reference the opening, later questions reference the end or the whole text)
- Final questions often require understanding of the passage as a whole (summary, overall purpose, tone)

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

| Skill | GL Weight | KS2 Content Domain | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Information Retrieval | ~35-45% | 2b | Locate and extract facts explicitly stated in the passage |
| Inference & Deduction | ~20-30% | 2d | Read between the lines; deduce meaning from context clues |
| Vocabulary in Context | ~15-20% | 2a | Determine word meanings from surrounding text; identify synonyms |
| Literary Devices & Language | ~5-10% | 2g | Identify techniques (simile, metaphor, etc.) and explain their effect |
| Word Class Identification | ~5-10% | — | Classify words by grammatical function (noun, verb, adjective, etc.) |
| Author's Purpose & Tone | ~3-5% | 2g/2f | Understand why the author made specific choices |
| Summary & Main Idea | ~2-5% | 2c | Identify the main point of a section or the whole passage |
| Prediction | Rare | 2e | Anticipate outcomes based on stated and implied information |
| Comparison | Rare | 2h | Compare elements within the text |

### Skill Breakdown Notes

**Retrieval** is the most heavily tested skill because it forms the foundation — children who cannot locate information cannot perform higher-order tasks. However, GL makes retrieval questions less straightforward than they first appear by burying key details within dense paragraphs and offering distractors that contain words from the passage but answer a different question.

**Inference** is the most discriminating skill — this is where the strongest candidates separate from the rest. GL inference questions require combining multiple clues from different parts of the text to reach a conclusion that is never explicitly stated.

**Vocabulary in Context** bridges comprehension and the standalone vocabulary/cloze sections. GL specifically chooses words with multiple meanings and tests whether the child can identify the correct meaning in context, not just know the word in isolation.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**Retrieval-focused.** The answer is explicitly stated in a clearly identifiable part of the passage.

- Questions reference information in a single, identifiable sentence or phrase
- The relevant section of text is easy to locate (often near the beginning of the passage)
- Answer options use similar or identical wording to the passage
- Only one option is remotely plausible; the other four are clearly wrong
- Vocabulary tested is common, high-frequency (Year 3-4 level)
- Literary devices are obvious and well-known (e.g., identifying a clear simile with "like" or "as")
- Word class questions involve prototypical examples: action verbs (ran, jumped), colour/size adjectives (red, enormous), proper nouns (London)

**Example question stems:**
- "According to the passage, what colour was the...?"
- "Where did the character go after...?"
- "What happened first in the story?"

**Distractor design at D1:**
- Wrong answers are from completely different parts of the passage or are obviously unrelated
- No partially correct options — there is one clear right answer
- Distractors may be true statements from the passage but answer a different question

---

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**Inference and vocabulary-focused.** The answer requires combining information or understanding nuance.

- Questions require linking information from two or more sentences
- Some inference needed — the answer is implied but not directly stated
- Vocabulary questions use Year 5-6 level words with context-dependent meanings
- Requires understanding of character motivation, mood, or attitude from indirect clues
- Literary device questions require naming a specific technique (e.g., distinguishing metaphor from simile)
- Word class questions involve less obvious cases: abstract nouns (joy, freedom), state verbs (seemed, appeared), adverbs without -ly (often, soon)
- Two or three options may seem plausible at first glance

**Example question stems:**
- "What does the word 'X' suggest about the character's feelings?"
- "Which word is closest in meaning to 'reluctant' as used in line 4?"
- "What type of word is 'appeared' in this sentence?"
- "Why do you think the character decided to...?"

**Distractor design at D2:**
- One distractor is a "near-miss" — partially correct or correct for a different part of the passage
- Distractors are semantically related to the correct answer (same domain but wrong)
- Options may include words that appear in the passage but in a different context
- Vocabulary distractors exploit common misconceptions (e.g., "reluctant" distractor: "forced")

---

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**Complex inference, author's craft, and sophisticated vocabulary.** The answer requires synthesis across the whole passage.

- Questions require understanding the passage as a whole, not just one section
- Deep inference — synthesising multiple clues to reach a conclusion not stated anywhere
- Vocabulary involves low-frequency, sophisticated words (ominous, benevolent, dilapidated)
- Questions about author's purpose, tone, effect on the reader, or structural choices
- Word class questions involve dual-function words ("light" as adjective, "run" as noun, -ly words that are adjectives like "friendly")
- Requires distinguishing between very similar options where all seem partially correct
- May require understanding figurative language, irony, or subtle tone shifts

**Example question stems:**
- "What impression does the author create in the final paragraph?"
- "Which statement best summarises the overall message of this passage?"
- "Why does the author describe the setting in such detail at this point?"
- "What effect does the phrase 'X' have on the reader?"
- "Which of the following is a fact, not an opinion?"

**Distractor design at D3:**
- All five options are plausible — child must identify the BEST answer, not the only possible one
- Distractors reflect partial understanding (correct about one aspect but wrong overall)
- Options may be true inferences but not the one the question asks about
- Subtle vocabulary distinctions between near-synonyms (understate vs undermine)
- Morphologically similar words used as traps (e.g., words sharing the same prefix)

## Common Question Types

### 1. Retrieval Questions (~35-45%)

**What they test:** Can the child locate specific information that is directly stated in the passage?

**Typical question stems:**
- "According to the passage, what did [character] do when...?"
- "Where did the events take place?"
- "What happened after...?"
- "Which of the following is true according to the text?"
- "How many [things] were mentioned in the passage?"

**What makes GL retrieval tricky:**
- Key details are often buried in the middle of a long paragraph rather than at the start
- The passage may mention the same topic in multiple places — the child must find the specific reference the question asks about
- Answer options use synonyms and paraphrases rather than quoting the passage directly
- Some distractors contain exact words from the passage but answer a different question

---

### 2. Inference Questions (~20-30%)

**What they test:** Can the child read between the lines and deduce meaning from context clues?

**Typical question stems:**
- "What does this suggest about [character's] feelings?"
- "How do you think [character] felt when...?"
- "What can you infer from the fact that...?"
- "The passage suggests that the character was..."
- "Which of the following is most likely true based on the passage?"
- "What impression does the reader get of [character/place]?"

**Key inference sub-types:**
1. **Character inference** — deducing feelings, motivations, attitudes from actions and dialogue
2. **Setting inference** — understanding mood/atmosphere from descriptive language
3. **Event inference** — working out what happened from indirect references
4. **Relationship inference** — understanding how characters relate to each other
5. **Causation inference** — understanding why something happened without it being stated

**What makes GL inference hard:**
- The answer is never explicitly written in the text
- Children must combine clues from multiple sentences or paragraphs
- The correct answer slightly extends the passage's meaning — it does not require large leaps
- Distractors are often surface-level readings that miss the deeper implication
- Children who focus on literal text often pick "obvious" answers that miss the inference

---

### 3. Vocabulary in Context Questions (~15-20%)

**What they test:** Can the child determine the meaning of a word as it is used in a specific passage context?

**Typical question stems:**
- "Which word is closest in meaning to 'X' as used in line Y?"
- "What does the word 'X' mean in this passage?"
- "In this context, the word 'X' most nearly means..."
- "Which of the following could replace 'X' in the sentence without changing the meaning?"

**GL's approach to vocabulary:**
- Words chosen often have multiple meanings — the child must identify the correct meaning for this context
- The correct answer is typically a synonym, not the exact word from the passage
- GL uses formal/literary vocabulary that children may not encounter in everyday speech (countenance, hastened, commenced)
- Context is essential — the same word might have different meanings elsewhere

**What makes these tricky:**
- Distractors include valid definitions of the word that do not apply in this context
- One distractor is often a word associated with the target word but not a synonym (semantic associate trap: monarch -> "crown" instead of "ruler")
- Morphological lookalikes used as traps (understate/undermine/understand)
- The correct option may use a common, simple synonym for a complex word (e.g., "commenced" = "began")

---

### 4. Literary Device & Language Questions (~5-10%)

**What they test:** Can the child identify and understand the effect of specific literary techniques?

**Typical question stems:**
- "What technique is used in the phrase '...'?"
- "The phrase '...' is an example of..."
- "'The wind howled through the trees' is an example of which literary device?"
- "Why does the author use the word 'X' instead of 'Y'?"
- "What effect does the author's description of [thing] create?"

**Literary devices tested (in order of frequency):**
1. **Simile** — comparison using "like" or "as" (most common at D1/D2)
2. **Metaphor** — direct comparison without "like/as" (D2/D3)
3. **Personification** — giving human qualities to non-human things (D2)
4. **Onomatopoeia** — words that sound like their meaning: crash, buzz, whisper (D1/D2)
5. **Alliteration** — repetition of initial consonant sounds (D1)
6. **Hyperbole** — deliberate exaggeration for effect (D2/D3)
7. **Oxymoron** — contradictory terms placed together: "deafening silence" (D3)
8. **Colloquialism** — informal/regional speech patterns (D3)
9. **Imagery** — vivid descriptive language appealing to the senses (D2/D3)
10. **Emotive language** — words chosen to provoke an emotional response (D3)

---

### 5. Word Class Identification Questions (~5-10%)

**What they test:** Can the child correctly classify a word by its grammatical function in context?

**Typical question stems:**
- "What type of word is 'X' in this sentence?"
- "Which word in the line is an adverb?"
- "What type of words are 'joy' and 'anger'?"

**Word classes tested (in order of frequency):**
1. Nouns (especially sub-types: common, proper, collective, abstract, concrete) — ~25-30%
2. Verbs (especially state verbs: was, seemed, appeared) — ~20-25%
3. Adjectives — ~15-20%
4. Adverbs (especially non-ly forms: soon, often, very) — ~15-20%
5. Prepositions — ~10-15%
6. Pronouns, conjunctions, determiners — ~5-10% combined

---

### 6. Author's Purpose & Tone Questions (~3-5%)

**What they test:** Can the child understand why the author wrote in a particular way and what effect this has?

**Typical question stems:**
- "Why does the author use the word 'X'?"
- "What is the author's purpose in the final paragraph?"
- "The overall tone of this passage is..."
- "The author wrote this passage to..."
- "Which word best describes the mood created in paragraph 2?"

---

### 7. Summary Questions (~2-5%)

**What they test:** Can the child identify the main idea or key points of a section or the whole passage?

**Typical question stems:**
- "Which sentence best summarises the passage?"
- "What is the main idea of paragraph 3?"
- "Which of the following would make the best title for this passage?"

## Text Types & Features

### Fiction Passages (~50-60% of GL papers)

**Sources:**
- Classic children's literature: C.S. Lewis, Roald Dahl, Kenneth Grahame, E. Nesbit, Frances Hodgson Burnett, Lewis Carroll, Arthur Conan Doyle, Thomas Hardy, Jerome K. Jerome
- Contemporary fiction: Philip Pullman, J.K. Rowling, David Almond, Michael Morpurgo
- Historical fiction, adventure stories, school stories, humorous tales

**Features GL selects for:**
- Rich, descriptive language that supports vocabulary-in-context questions
- Characters whose feelings and motivations can be inferred from actions/dialogue
- A narrative arc within the extract (setup, development, tension/resolution)
- Formal or semi-formal register — language above everyday Year 6 conversation
- Dialogue that reveals character traits
- Settings described with literary techniques (personification, imagery, simile)

**Why fiction dominates:**
- Supports the widest range of question types (retrieval, inference, vocabulary, literary device, character analysis)
- Allows inference about feelings, motivations, and relationships — the most discriminating skill
- Classic literature introduces children to formal/archaic vocabulary naturally

### Non-Fiction Passages (~25-35% of GL papers)

**Sources:**
- Biography and autobiography
- Travel writing
- Popular science
- Historical accounts
- Nature and geography

**Features GL selects for:**
- Factual content that supports retrieval questions
- Subject-specific vocabulary that supports vocabulary-in-context questions
- Logical argument structure (cause/effect, comparison, chronological order)
- Mix of facts and opinions — supports "fact vs opinion" discrimination questions

### Poetry Passages (~10-15% of GL papers)

**Sources:**
- Anthology pieces, narrative poems
- Classic and modern poetry accessible to Year 5-6 children

**Features GL selects for:**
- Concentrated figurative language (metaphor, simile, personification, imagery)
- Rhythm and sound devices (alliteration, onomatopoeia, rhyme)
- Compressed meaning — every word carries weight
- Emotional content that supports inference about mood and feeling

**Why poetry is hardest:**
- Children must understand non-literal language throughout
- Meaning is often implied rather than stated
- Vocabulary tends to be more literary/unusual
- Structure is different from prose — children used to paragraphs may struggle

## Distractor Design

GL Assessment uses sophisticated distractor design to differentiate between children with genuine comprehension and those with surface-level understanding. Every wrong answer serves a diagnostic purpose.

### Core Distractor Strategies

| Strategy | How It Works | Which Children It Catches |
|----------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Passage echo** | Uses exact words/phrases from the passage that relate to a different question | Children who scan for familiar words instead of reading carefully |
| **Surface reading** | Offers the literal/obvious interpretation when inference is required | Children who cannot read between the lines |
| **Partial truth** | Contains some correct information but is incomplete or answers a slightly different question | Children with partial understanding |
| **Semantic associate** | Offers a word associated with the correct answer but not a synonym (crown instead of ruler) | Children who think of related words rather than meanings |
| **Context mismatch** | Gives a valid definition of a word that does not apply in this particular context | Children who know word meanings in isolation but cannot adapt to context |
| **Morphological lookalike** | Uses words that look similar to the correct answer (understate/undermine/understand) | Children who rely on visual pattern matching |
| **Over-inference** | Goes beyond what the text supports — a plausible but unsupported conclusion | Children who add too much of their own interpretation |
| **Chronological confusion** | References events from the passage but in the wrong order or from the wrong section | Children who do not track sequence carefully |
| **Register trap** | Uses a formal synonym when informal is correct, or vice versa | Children unfamiliar with register differences |

### The "Best Answer" Principle

GL frequently designs questions where two or even three options could be argued as partially correct. The child must select the BEST answer — the one most fully supported by the text. This is a deliberate design choice that tests precision of thought, not just general understanding.

Children who are used to questions with one obviously correct answer struggle here. The skill is not just "understanding the text" but "evaluating which answer is most justified by the evidence."

## Common GL Traps

### 1. The "It Says It in the Text" Trap
The child sees familiar words from the passage in an answer option and selects it immediately. But the option answers a different question or applies to a different part of the text. **Fix:** Always check the option answers the specific question asked, not just that it matches words from the passage.

### 2. The Literal Reading Trap
For inference questions, children select the surface-level answer that states what literally happened rather than what it implies. For example, a character "trembling" — the surface answer is "cold" but the inferential answer might be "nervous" based on wider context. **Fix:** Ask "What does this tell us about how the character feels/thinks?"

### 3. The "I Already Know This" Trap
Children bring outside knowledge that contradicts or extends beyond the passage. GL requires answers based solely on the text provided. If a child knows about a topic from school, they may select an option that is true in general but is not supported by this specific passage. **Fix:** Every answer must be provable from the passage.

### 4. The First Plausible Answer Trap
Under time pressure, children select the first option that seems correct without checking the remaining options. In GL papers, a more precise answer often appears later in the options. **Fix:** Read all five options before selecting.

### 5. The Wrong Question Trap
Children answer the question they think is being asked rather than what is actually asked. Common with inference questions where children explain what happened instead of how someone felt. **Fix:** Underline the key words in the question — especially "why", "how", "what does this suggest about".

### 6. The Vocabulary Assumption Trap
For vocabulary-in-context questions, children select a known meaning of the word without checking whether that meaning fits this specific context. Words like "set," "fair," "light," "bark," "bank" have multiple meanings. **Fix:** Mentally substitute the answer back into the passage sentence — does it still make sense?

### 7. The "Too Obvious" Trap at D3
At the hardest level, the most obvious answer is usually wrong. GL places it first or second in the options to catch children working quickly. The correct answer requires deeper thought and is often phrased more carefully. **Fix:** If an answer seems too easy for the question, check the other options.

### 8. The Negation Trap
Questions phrased as "Which of these is NOT true?" or "Which does NOT describe...?" catch children who miss the negative word. **Fix:** Circle "NOT" in the question.

## Key Reading Skills Reference

### Skills Hierarchy (from lowest to highest order)

| Level | Skill | Description | GL Question Type |
|-------|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Decoding** | Reading words accurately | Not directly tested (assumed) |
| 2 | **Retrieval** | Finding explicitly stated information | "According to the passage..." |
| 3 | **Vocabulary** | Understanding word meanings in context | "Closest in meaning to..." |
| 4 | **Simple Inference** | Drawing conclusions from single clues | "This suggests that..." |
| 5 | **Complex Inference** | Synthesising multiple clues across the text | "What impression does..." |
| 6 | **Language Analysis** | Understanding the effect of word/technique choices | "What effect does..." |
| 7 | **Evaluation** | Judging author's purpose, distinguishing fact/opinion | "The author wrote this to..." |
| 8 | **Summary** | Distilling main ideas from detail | "Best summarises..." |

### Reading Strategies Children Need

1. **Skimming** — quickly reading the passage to get the overall idea before looking at questions
2. **Scanning** — searching for specific words, names, or phrases when answering retrieval questions
3. **Close reading** — re-reading a specific section carefully when answering inference or vocabulary questions
4. **Cross-referencing** — checking multiple parts of the passage to confirm an inference
5. **Substitution** — replacing a vocabulary word with the answer option to check it fits
6. **Elimination** — ruling out definitely wrong options to narrow the choice

## Key Terminology

Children should know and understand these terms for the comprehension section:

### Text Types
- **Fiction** — made-up stories (novels, short stories)
- **Non-fiction** — factual writing (biography, reports, articles, instructions)
- **Poetry** — writing using rhythm, imagery, and condensed language
- **Narrative** — writing that tells a story (can be fiction or non-fiction)
- **Prose** — ordinary written language (not poetry)
- **Extract** — a section taken from a longer text

### Literary Techniques
- **Simile** — comparison using "like" or "as" ("as brave as a lion")
- **Metaphor** — direct comparison without "like/as" ("he was a lion in battle")
- **Personification** — giving human qualities to non-human things ("the wind whispered")
- **Onomatopoeia** — words that sound like their meaning (crash, buzz, sizzle)
- **Alliteration** — repetition of initial consonant sounds ("Peter Piper picked")
- **Hyperbole** — deliberate exaggeration ("I've told you a million times")
- **Oxymoron** — contradictory terms together ("deafening silence")
- **Imagery** — vivid description appealing to the senses
- **Emotive language** — words chosen to provoke an emotional response
- **Pathetic fallacy** — weather/environment reflecting mood ("dark, stormy sky = danger")
- **Foreshadowing** — hints about what will happen later
- **Colloquialism** — informal, everyday speech ("gonna", "mate")
- **Dialect** — regional speech patterns

### Comprehension Terms
- **Retrieve** — find and extract information directly from the text
- **Infer** — work out something that is not directly stated, using clues from the text
- **Deduce** — reach a logical conclusion from evidence in the text
- **Synonym** — a word with the same or very similar meaning
- **Antonym** — a word with the opposite meaning
- **Context** — the surrounding words/sentences that help determine meaning
- **Tone** — the overall feeling or attitude of a piece of writing (formal, humorous, sad)
- **Mood/Atmosphere** — the feeling created for the reader by the writing
- **Author's purpose** — why the author wrote the text (to inform, persuade, entertain, argue)
- **Evidence** — specific words, phrases, or details from the text that support an answer

### Word Classes (tested within comprehension)
- **Noun** — person, place, thing, or idea (sub-types: common, proper, collective, abstract, concrete)
- **Verb** — action or state (including state verbs: was, seemed, appeared)
- **Adjective** — describes a noun
- **Adverb** — modifies a verb, adjective, or other adverb (not always -ly: soon, often, very)
- **Preposition** — shows position or relationship (on, under, before, between)
- **Pronoun** — replaces a noun (he, she, it, they, which, who)
- **Conjunction** — joins words, phrases, or clauses (and, but, because, although)
- **Determiner** — precedes a noun (the, a, this, those, every)

### Year 5-6 National Curriculum Reading Requirements

Pupils should be taught to:

**Maintain positive attitudes to reading and understanding of what they read by:**
- Reading and discussing an increasingly wide range of fiction, poetry, plays, non-fiction, and reference books
- Reading books structured in different ways and for a range of purposes
- Identifying and discussing themes and conventions in and across a wide range of writing
- Making comparisons within and across books
- Learning a wider range of poetry by heart
- Preparing poems and plays to read aloud with appropriate intonation, volume, and tone

**Understand what they read by:**
- Checking that the book makes sense, discussing understanding and exploring the meaning of words in context
- Asking questions to improve understanding
- Drawing inferences such as inferring characters' feelings, thoughts, and motives from their actions, and justifying inferences with evidence
- Predicting what might happen from details stated and implied
- Summarising the main ideas drawn from more than one paragraph, identifying key details that support the main ideas
- Identifying how language, structure, and presentation contribute to meaning

**Discuss and evaluate how authors use language by:**
- Considering the impact on the reader of figurative language and vocabulary choices
- Distinguishing between statements of fact and opinion
- Retrieving, recording, and presenting information from non-fiction
- Participating in discussions about books, building on their own and others' ideas and challenging views courteously
- Providing reasoned justifications for their views

### How GL Goes Beyond the National Curriculum

The GL 11+ comprehension tests Year 5-6 curriculum skills but at a higher standard:
- **Texts are more complex** — reading level above chronological age (Year 7-8 level prose)
- **Vocabulary is more demanding** — formal, literary, and archaic words children would not typically encounter
- **Inference is deeper** — requires synthesis across the whole passage, not just a paragraph
- **Time pressure is significant** — approximately 1 minute per question, including reading time
- **Multiple choice adds difficulty** — distractors are designed by psychometricians to exploit common errors
- **No partial credit** — unlike SATs where short-answer questions can earn partial marks, GL multiple choice is binary (right or wrong)

## Teaching Notes

### Priority Skills by Impact

1. **Inference** (highest impact) — this is where most marks are lost and where the strongest differentiation occurs. Children who can retrieve but cannot infer will plateau at D1-D2.
2. **Vocabulary breadth** — a wide vocabulary makes everything easier: retrieval is faster, inference is more accurate, literary device recognition is more natural.
3. **Reading stamina** — the passage is ~500 words of dense, above-level text. Children who tire or lose concentration halfway through will struggle with questions about the second half.
4. **Question interpretation** — understanding exactly what is being asked is a skill in itself. Many marks are lost to answering the wrong question.
5. **Elimination strategy** — when unsure, systematically ruling out wrong answers improves odds from 20% (random guess) to 33-50%.

### Common Weaknesses to Address

| Weakness | Symptom | Intervention |
|----------|---------|--------------|
| Surface-level reading | Always picks literal answers; misses inference questions | Practice with "What does this tell us about how X feels?" questions |
| Limited vocabulary | Cannot determine word meanings from context; guesses randomly | Wide reading + explicit vocabulary instruction + context clue practice |
| Poor question reading | Answers a different question to the one asked | Highlight/underline key words in questions; practice identifying what each question actually asks |
| Time management | Runs out of time; leaves last questions blank | Timed practice; teach skimming/scanning; do not get stuck on any single question |
| Over-reliance on background knowledge | Brings outside knowledge not supported by the passage | Practice with "prove it from the text" — every answer must be supported by specific passage evidence |
| Inability to evaluate options | Picks the first plausible answer without checking alternatives | Practice reading ALL five options before selecting; practice elimination |
| Unfamiliarity with classic texts | Confused by formal/archaic language and sentence structures | Regular reading of age-appropriate classics (Roald Dahl, C.S. Lewis, E. Nesbit, Frances Hodgson Burnett) |

### Effective Practice Approach

1. **Read widely and often** — fiction, non-fiction, poetry. Variety of register and period. This is the single most important preparation.
2. **Discuss what is read** — ask open-ended questions: "Why do you think the character did that?", "How do you think she felt?", "What does this word tell us?"
3. **Build vocabulary explicitly** — learn 5-10 new words per week in context, not just definitions. Practice substitution ("What other word could you use here?").
4. **Practice with GL-style multiple choice** — get used to the scattershot format, the distractor design, and the "best answer" principle. CGP, Bond, and Atom Learning provide GL-format materials.
5. **Develop elimination technique** — teach children to cross out definitely-wrong answers first, then choose between remaining options.
6. **Time practice realistically** — build up to full timed conditions (45 minutes for the whole paper, roughly 1 minute per comprehension question plus reading time).

### The Four-Step Exam Technique

1. **Read the passage once through** — get the overall gist, noting key characters, events, and the general subject
2. **Read each question carefully** — underline key words, especially "why", "how", "suggest", "NOT"
3. **Find the evidence in the passage** — go back to the specific part of the text the question references
4. **Read ALL five options** — eliminate wrong answers, then select the best remaining answer

### What Not to Do

- Do not start questions without reading the whole passage first — later questions often require full-passage understanding
- Do not spend more than 90 seconds on any single question — mark it, move on, come back if time allows
- Do not leave any question blank — there is no negative marking, so a guess is always better than nothing
- Do not rely solely on memory — always go back to the passage to verify
- Do not pick an answer just because it contains words from the passage — this is the most common trap
