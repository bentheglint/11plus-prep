## Format

### Where Grammar Appears in the GL English Paper

Grammar is tested in **three distinct locations** within the GL English paper:

| Section | Questions | Format | Grammar Focus |
|---------|-----------|--------|---------------|
| **Word Class & Grammar (Q23-Q25)** | 3 questions | 5 options (A-E), passage-based | Word class identification, sentence types, terminology |
| **Punctuation Error-Spotting (Q34-Q41)** | 8 questions | 4 segments (A-D) + N (no mistake) | Punctuation rules applied grammatically |
| **Cloze / Grammar Gap-Fill (Q42-Q49)** | 8 questions | 5 options (A-E), inline boxes in a passage | Applied grammar: verb forms, tenses, conjunctions, prepositions, agreement |

**Total grammar-related questions: ~19 out of 49 (38.8% of the paper)**

This means grammar is the **single largest skill area** when you combine all three sections. Comprehension accounts for 18 questions (36.7%), but comprehension also tests inference, retrieval, and literary knowledge — grammar is a more focused, targetable skill.

### Paper-Level Context (Dorset Consortium)

- **Paper duration:** 45-50 minutes (varies by consortium reporting; our practice paper analysis shows 50 minutes)
- **Total questions:** 49
- **Format:** All multiple-choice, answers on a separate OMR bubble sheet
- **Always 5 options** (A-E) except error-spotting which uses A-D + N

### How Each Grammar Section Works

#### 1. Word Class & Grammar (Q23-Q25) — Passage-Based

These sit within the comprehension section and always reference the main reading passage.

**Three question sub-types:**
- **"What type of words are these?"** (~50-60%) — four words from the passage displayed; identify their shared word class
- **"Which word is a [class]?"** (~25-35%) — a quoted sentence from the passage; pick the word belonging to the named class
- **Terminology identification** (~10-15%) — identify a sentence type or punctuation feature (e.g. "This is a:" → rhetorical question)

#### 2. Punctuation Error-Spotting (Q34-Q41) — Standalone Passage

A **separate short passage** (~100-120 words, narrative fiction) is provided — different from the comprehension passage. Each line is divided into 4 labelled segments (A-D). The child identifies which segment contains a punctuation error, or selects N if there is no mistake.

**Key feature:** 1-2 questions per paper have NO mistake (answer = N). This prevents children from assuming every line has an error.

#### 3. Cloze / Grammar Gap-Fill (Q42-Q49) — Standalone Passage

Another **separate short passage** (~80-100 words, narrative fiction). Blanks appear inline, each with 5 word options displayed in horizontal boxes. The child reads the passage as a story and selects the grammatically correct word to complete each gap.

**Key feature:** The passage must be read as continuous text. Context from earlier sentences can determine the correct tense or pronoun for later gaps. Children who treat each blank in isolation will make avoidable errors.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy — ~30% of grammar questions)

**Cloze/Gap-Fill:**
- Common homophones where context makes the answer obvious: "They went ___ the park" (to/too/two/tow/tan) — "to" is clearly correct
- Simple past tense of regular verbs: "Yesterday, she ___ to school" (walked/walks/walking/walk/walker)
- Basic conjunctions: "I was hungry ___ I ate a sandwich" (so/but/and/or/yet) — "so" is the only logical connector
- Distractors include obviously wrong options (made-up words, clearly wrong tense)
- No ambiguity — one option is unambiguously correct

**Punctuation Error-Spotting:**
- Missing capital at the start of a sentence after a full stop
- Missing apostrophe in a common contraction: "Im going home" → "I'm going home"
- Missing full stop at the end of a sentence
- The error is conspicuous; other segments have simple, correct punctuation

**Word Class:**
- Obvious action verbs (ran, jumped, kicked)
- Clearly descriptive adjectives (beautiful, red, enormous)
- Proper nouns that are obviously names (London, Elizabeth)
- -ly adverbs (quickly, carefully, slowly)

**Characteristics of D1 grammar questions:**
- One answer is clearly, unambiguously correct
- Wrong options are from different grammatical categories (not close competitors)
- Tests knowledge a child who reads regularly would have absorbed naturally
- No trick elements or dual-function words

### D2 (Medium — ~40% of grammar questions)

**Cloze/Gap-Fill:**
- Trickier homophones: "The dog wagged ___ tail" (its/it's/its'/is/i'ts) — requires knowing its vs it's
- Irregular past tenses: "She ___ the ball across the field" (threw/throw/thrown/throwed/through)
- Conjunctions requiring logical reasoning: "She studied hard, ___ she still failed the test" (although/but/because/so/therefore) — both "although" and "but" seem plausible, but only one fits the sentence structure
- "Should have" vs "should of" — a common speech-to-writing error
- Preposition choices: "different ___ what I expected" (from/to/than/of/with)

**Punctuation Error-Spotting:**
- Missing possessive apostrophe: "the girls coat was red" → "the girl's coat"
- Comma before a conjunction in a compound sentence: "She was tired but she kept going" → comma before "but"
- Missing comma after a fronted adverbial: "After dinner we played games" → comma after "After dinner"
- Requires understanding of grammatical structure, not just pattern recognition

**Word Class:**
- Abstract nouns that children don't recognise as nouns (joy, freedom, courage)
- State verbs that don't feel like "doing" words (was, seemed, appeared, became)
- Adverbs without -ly (soon, often, very, quite, already)
- Noun sub-type discrimination (common vs proper vs collective vs abstract)
- Prepositions, pronouns, determiners (less common word classes)

**Characteristics of D2 grammar questions:**
- Two options seem plausible at first glance
- Requires understanding the rule, not just "what sounds right"
- Tests knowledge from Year 5-6 curriculum (not just absorbed from reading)
- One strong distractor designed to catch common misconceptions

### D3 (Hard — ~30% of grammar questions)

**Cloze/Gap-Fill:**
- Subtle tense shifts within a passage: requires maintaining tense consistency across multiple sentences
- Past participle vs past simple: "She had ___ the letter before he arrived" (wrote/written/writed/writing/write) — many children confuse "wrote" and "written"
- Subjunctive mood: "If I ___ you, I would leave now" (was/were/am/is/be) — the subjunctive "were" feels wrong to children who default to "was"
- Passive voice construction: "The cake ___ by the time we arrived" (had been eaten/had ate/was eating/had been ate/has eaten)
- Pronoun agreement in complex sentences: "Each of the boys brought ___ own lunch" (his/their/there/they're/them)
- Near-homophone clusters: rots/rotes/routes/roots/rites — multiple real words, only one fits context

**Punctuation Error-Spotting:**
- Semicolon vs comma: "It was raining, we stayed inside" → should be semicolon or "...raining; we stayed inside"
- Colon before a list vs semicolon between clauses
- Apostrophe in plural possessive: "the children's toys" vs "the childrens' toys" vs "the childrens toys"
- No-mistake lines where everything looks suspicious but is actually correct
- Speech punctuation with interruption: '"Wait," she said, "I'm coming too."'

**Word Class:**
- Dual-function words in their less common class: "run" as a noun, "light" as an adjective, "round" as a preposition
- -ly words that are adjectives, not adverbs: friendly, lonely, lovely, lively, costly
- Present participles functioning as adjectives: "the running water" (adjective, not verb)
- Words that can be preposition, conjunction, or adverb depending on context: before, after, since

**Characteristics of D3 grammar questions:**
- Multiple plausible-looking options that are real words
- Exploits common errors children (and adults) make in speech
- Requires explicit knowledge of grammar rules, not just "ear" for correctness
- May test Year 6 curriculum concepts (passive voice, subjunctive, formal register)
- Distractor options are common real-world errors (things children hear adults say incorrectly)

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Cloze / Grammar Gap-Fill (8 questions per paper)

**How it works:** A short narrative passage (~80-100 words) with 8 blanks. Each blank has 5 options displayed in inline boxes. The passage is read as a continuous story.

**Example question patterns:**

1. **Homophone selection:**
   "They packed ___[A: there / B: their / C: they're / D: thier / E: ther] bags and headed to the station."

2. **Verb tense selection:**
   "By the time we arrived, the concert had already ___[A: began / B: begun / C: beginned / D: begin / E: beginning]."

3. **Modal verb correctness:**
   "She ___[A: should of / B: should have / C: should off / D: should has / E: should had] waited for the bus."

4. **Conjunction selection:**
   "The children were tired, ___[A: so / B: but / C: because / D: although / E: however] they had been playing all day."

5. **Preposition selection:**
   "The painting was quite different ___[A: from / B: to / C: than / D: of / E: with] what she had expected."

6. **Comparative/superlative:**
   "Of the three mountains, Everest is the ___[A: more / B: most / C: much / D: many / E: greater] famous."

### Type 2: Punctuation Error-Spotting (8 questions per paper)

**How it works:** A short narrative passage split line-by-line. Each line divided into 4 segments (A-D) with grey dividers. Child identifies the segment with a punctuation error, or selects N for no mistake.

**Example error patterns:**

1. **Missing capital after full stop:**
   A: "The sun set early." | B: "she walked" | C: "home through" | D: "the park." → Error in B

2. **Missing apostrophe (contraction):**
   A: "Emma said she" | B: "didnt want" | C: "to go to" | D: "the party." → Error in B

3. **Missing apostrophe (possession):**
   A: "She picked up" | B: "the schools" | C: "trophy and" | D: "smiled proudly." → Error in B

4. **Wrong end punctuation:**
   A: "Have you ever" | B: "been to" | C: "the Natural" | D: "History Museum." → Error in D (should be ?)

5. **Missing comma:**
   A: "After a long" | B: "tiring walk" | C: "they finally" | D: "reached camp." → Error in B (comma after "walk")

### Type 3: Word Class Identification (3 questions per paper)

**How it works:** Always passage-based. Three sub-formats:

1. **Shared class:** "story, men, swamps, profit — What type of words are these?" → Nouns
2. **In sentence:** "'Catalon lived to the ripe old age of 94.' Which word is a verb?" → lived
3. **Terminology:** "'Did he know where the treasure was?' This is a:" → Rhetorical question

## Distractor Design

GL designs grammar distractors to exploit specific misconceptions and common errors. Understanding the distractor strategy is essential for both creating realistic questions and teaching children to avoid traps.

### Cloze / Grammar Gap-Fill Distractors

| Distractor Strategy | How It Works | Example |
|---------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Made-up verb forms** | Include non-existent but plausible-sounding past tenses | "choosed" alongside chose/chosen; "bringed" alongside brought; "knewed" alongside knew |
| **Speech-to-writing errors** | Include common spoken English errors as options | "should of" instead of "should have"; "could of" instead of "could have" |
| **Near-homophones** | Multiple real words that sound similar | rots / rotes / routes / roots / rites — only one fits the context |
| **Wrong tense, real word** | All options are real English words but in wrong tense | wrote / written / writing / write / writes — all real, only one grammatically correct in context |
| **Plausible conjunction** | Multiple conjunctions that could connect sentences but only one preserves the logical relationship | but / because / although / so / however — all connectors, different logical relationships |
| **Common misspelling of correct answer** | The correct answer is misspelled as one option | "thier" alongside "their" — catches children who know the right word but can't spell it |

### Punctuation Error-Spotting Distractors

| Distractor Strategy | How It Works | Example |
|---------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Doubt-inducing correct segments** | Correct segments contain punctuation that looks suspicious | A correctly used semicolon makes children second-guess and pick that segment as the error |
| **No-mistake lines** | All segments are correct but contain tricky punctuation | Every segment has apostrophes, commas, or colons — all correct, but children feel compelled to find an error |
| **Near-miss possession** | Possessive apostrophe present but in a segment that isn't the error | "the dog's bowl" (correct) alongside a missing capital letter elsewhere |
| **Speech punctuation complexity** | Correctly punctuated speech looks wrong | "Wait," she said, "I'm coming too." — children think the comma inside speech marks is an error |

### Word Class Distractors

| Distractor Strategy | How It Works | Example |
|---------------------|-------------|---------|
| **Surface feature traps** | -ly ending → child picks "adverb" but word is actually adjective | "friendly" = adjective, not adverb |
| **Action bias for verbs** | State verbs don't feel like verbs, so "adjective" is offered | "seemed" → child picks "adjective" instead of "verb" |
| **Adjective vs Adverb confusion** | Both always included when testing either | Testing "fast" (adverb) but "adjective" is a plausible distractor |
| **"Plural" as red herring** | "Plural" isn't a word class but children who've learned about plurals may pick it | Options: noun / verb / plural / adjective / adverb |

## Common GL Traps

### Trap 1: "Sounds Right" vs "Is Right"
Children rely on their ear for what "sounds right" in spoken English. GL exploits this by including options that are commonly heard in speech but grammatically incorrect.
- "Me and Tom went to the shop" sounds normal → correct: "Tom and I went to the shop"
- "She done her homework" sounds normal in some dialects → correct: "She did her homework"
- "There was less people" sounds normal → correct: "There were fewer people"
- "I could of gone" sounds exactly like "I could've gone" → correct: "I could have gone"

### Trap 2: Tense Inconsistency Across the Passage
The cloze passage is continuous narrative. GL sets up a past-tense story but includes present-tense distractors for later blanks. Children who don't read the whole passage pick present tense for a past-tense story.

### Trap 3: Irregular Past Tense and Past Participle Confusion
GL loves testing irregular verbs where the past simple and past participle differ:
- bring → brought (not "bringed" or "brung")
- choose → chose (past simple) / chosen (past participle)
- write → wrote (past simple) / written (past participle)
- do → did (past simple) / done (past participle) — "I done it" is a classic trap
- see → saw (past simple) / seen (past participle) — "I seen it" is a classic trap
- go → went (past simple) / gone (past participle)

### Trap 4: its vs it's
This is the single most common grammar trap across all GL papers. "Its" (possessive, no apostrophe) vs "it's" (contraction of "it is" or "it has"). Children who have learned that apostrophes show possession incorrectly add one to "its".

### Trap 5: Apostrophe Overuse
After learning possessive apostrophes, children add them to plurals: "the dog's are playing" instead of "the dogs are playing". GL includes these as distractors.

### Trap 6: who's vs whose
Same pattern as its/it's. "Who's" = "who is" or "who has". "Whose" = belonging to whom. "Whose bag is this?" not "Who's bag is this?"

### Trap 7: Formal vs Informal Register
GL occasionally tests whether children can identify or produce formal English:
- "The council requests that everyone ___ present" → "be" (subjunctive) not "is"
- Choosing "discover" over "find out", "request" over "ask for" in a formal context

### Trap 8: Double Negative
"I don't have nothing" = actually means "I have something". The correct form is "I don't have anything" or "I have nothing". Children from some dialect backgrounds may not spot this as an error.

### Trap 9: Pronoun Case After Prepositions
"Between you and I" sounds educated but is incorrect → "Between you and me" is correct. GL tests this because children who have been told "don't say 'me and Tom'" over-correct to "I" everywhere.

### Trap 10: Comparative vs Superlative
Two items = comparative (-er / more). Three or more items = superlative (-est / most).
- "She is the tallest of the two sisters" → incorrect, should be "taller"
- "Of all the children, she ran more quickly" → incorrect, should be "most quickly"

## Key Grammar Concepts Reference

### Verb Tenses (All Tested in GL)

| Tense | Formation | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Simple present** | base verb / base + s | I walk / She walks |
| **Simple past** | base + ed / irregular | I walked / She ran |
| **Simple future** | will + base | I will walk |
| **Present progressive** | am/is/are + -ing | I am walking |
| **Past progressive** | was/were + -ing | I was walking |
| **Present perfect** | has/have + past participle | I have walked |
| **Past perfect** | had + past participle | I had walked |

**GL focus:** Past simple vs past participle confusion is the most tested verb tense concept. The present perfect ("I have written") and past perfect ("I had written") use the past participle, NOT the past simple.

### Irregular Verbs Most Tested by GL

| Base | Past Simple | Past Participle | Common Error |
|------|-------------|-----------------|--------------|
| begin | began | begun | "beginned" / "begun" as past simple |
| bring | brought | brought | "bringed" / "brung" |
| choose | chose | chosen | "choosed" |
| do | did | done | "I done it" (using participle as past simple) |
| drink | drank | drunk | "drinked" / "drunk" as past simple |
| eat | ate | eaten | "eated" |
| go | went | gone | "goed" / "I gone" |
| know | knew | known | "knowed" / "knewed" |
| see | saw | seen | "I seen it" (using participle as past simple) |
| speak | spoke | spoken | "speaked" |
| swim | swam | swum | "swimmed" / "swum" as past simple |
| take | took | taken | "tooked" / "taked" |
| write | wrote | written | "writed" / "wrote" as participle |

### Subject-Verb Agreement

The subject and verb must agree in number (singular/plural):
- **Singular:** The dog **runs**. / She **is** happy. / Each student **has** a book.
- **Plural:** The dogs **run**. / They **are** happy. / The students **have** books.

**GL traps:**
- Interrupting phrases: "The box of chocolates **is** on the table" (not "are" — the subject is "box", not "chocolates")
- Collective nouns: "The team **is** playing well" (team = singular in British English)
- "Each" and "every" take singular verbs: "Every one of the children **was** excited" (not "were")
- "Neither...nor" takes the verb that agrees with the nearest subject: "Neither the teacher nor the students **were** ready"

### Active and Passive Voice

- **Active:** The dog (subject) bit (verb) the man (object).
- **Passive:** The man (subject) was bitten (verb) by the dog (agent).

**How GL tests this:**
- Cloze: selecting the correct passive construction: "The cake had been ___" (eaten / ate / eating / eat / eated)
- Word class section: identifying a sentence as active or passive
- Understanding that passive uses a form of "be" + past participle

### Clauses

- **Main clause:** Can stand alone as a sentence. "I went home."
- **Subordinate clause:** Cannot stand alone. "...because it was late."
- **Relative clause:** A subordinate clause introduced by a relative pronoun (who, which, that, where, when, whose). "The boy **who scored the goal** celebrated."

**GL tests clause knowledge through:**
- Conjunction selection in cloze (choosing the right subordinating conjunction)
- Comma placement in punctuation error-spotting (commas before/after subordinate clauses)
- Relative pronoun selection: "The woman ___[who/which/whose/whom/what] lives next door is a teacher"

### Conjunctions

| Type | Conjunctions | Function |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Coordinating** | and, but, or, nor, yet, so, for | Join equal clauses |
| **Subordinating** | because, although, if, when, while, until, after, before, since, unless, whereas, as | Introduce subordinate clauses |
| **Correlative** | either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also, both...and | Paired conjunctions |

**GL focus:** Choosing between conjunctions that create different logical relationships:
- "She was tired **because** she had run a race" (cause)
- "She was tired **but** she kept going" (contrast)
- "She was tired **so** she went to bed" (result)
- "She was tired **although** she had slept well" (concession)

### Modal Verbs

can, could, may, might, shall, should, will, would, must, ought to

**GL tests modals through:**
- "Should have" vs "should of" (the most common test)
- Degree of certainty: might (less certain) vs will (certain)
- Permission: may (formal) vs can (informal)
- The construction "modal + have + past participle": "She must have forgotten" (not "must of forgotten")

### Pronouns

| Type | Examples | GL Trap |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Personal** (subject) | I, you, he, she, it, we, they | "Me and Tom" → "Tom and I" |
| **Personal** (object) | me, you, him, her, it, us, them | "Between you and I" → "between you and me" |
| **Possessive** | my/mine, your/yours, his, her/hers, its, our/ours, their/theirs | its vs it's |
| **Relative** | who, whom, which, that, whose | who vs which (people vs things) |
| **Reflexive** | myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves | "theirselves" (non-standard) |
| **Demonstrative** | this, that, these, those | |

### The Subjunctive (Year 6 — D3 Questions Only)

Used in very formal writing and speech:
- "If I **were** you" (not "if I was you")
- "I suggest that she **leave** immediately" (not "leaves")
- "The headteacher requested that every pupil **be** present" (not "is")

GL rarely tests this directly but may include it as a D3 cloze option. Children who recognise the subjunctive have a significant advantage.

### Formal vs Informal Language (Year 6)

| Informal | Formal |
|----------|--------|
| find out | discover |
| ask for | request |
| go in | enter |
| get | obtain / receive |
| loads of | a significant number of |
| kids | children |
| start | commence |
| end | conclude |

GL may test this through cloze questions set in a formal context (letter, newspaper report) where the formal option is correct.

## National Curriculum Alignment

### English Appendix 2: Year 5-6 Grammar Requirements

**Word-Level:**
- Converting nouns or adjectives into verbs using suffixes (-ate, -ise, -ify): "class" → "classify", "advert" → "advertise"
- Verb prefixes: dis-, de-, mis-, over-, re-: "disagree", "decompose", "mislead"
- Vocabulary typical of formal speech vs informal speech: "find out" → "discover"

**Sentence-Level:**
- Relative clauses beginning with who, which, where, when, whose, that, or implied relative pronoun
- Use of commas to clarify meaning or avoid ambiguity
- Modal verbs to indicate degrees of possibility (might, should, will, must)
- Adverbs to indicate degrees of possibility (perhaps, surely, certainly)
- Linking ideas across paragraphs using adverbials of time (later), place (nearby), and number (secondly)
- Use of the passive voice to affect the presentation of information: "I broke the window" → "The window was broken"
- Subjunctive forms in formal writing: "If I were", "I suggest that she leave"
- The difference between structures typical of informal/formal speech and writing

**Punctuation (Grammar-Adjacent):**
- Brackets, dashes, or commas to indicate parenthesis
- Use of commas to clarify meaning or avoid ambiguity
- Colons to introduce a list; semicolons within lists
- Punctuation of bullet points consistently
- How hyphens avoid ambiguity: "man eating shark" vs "man-eating shark"
- Semicolons, colons, and dashes to mark the boundary between independent clauses
- Colons to introduce a clause that expands or explains the first

### KS2 SATs GPS Paper 1 (45 minutes) — Comparable Assessment

The KS2 Grammar, Punctuation and Spelling (GPS) test covers the same content and is the closest formal assessment to the GL grammar questions. The GL 11+ is broadly at this level but applied through multiple-choice format rather than short-answer.

## Teaching Notes

### Priority Order for Grammar Revision

Based on GL weightings and frequency of testing, prioritise in this order:

1. **Homophones in context** — highest frequency, most marks available
2. **Verb tenses (especially irregular past tenses and past participles)** — second highest frequency
3. **Apostrophes (contraction AND possession)** — tested in both punctuation and cloze sections
4. **Subject-verb agreement** — fundamental skill tested across all sections
5. **Conjunctions and logical connectors** — requires reading comprehension as well as grammar
6. **Modal verbs (especially "should have" not "should of")** — high-value quick win
7. **Pronouns (case, agreement, its/it's)** — frequent trap questions
8. **Commas (lists, fronted adverbials, clauses)** — most tested punctuation mark
9. **Active/passive voice** — Year 6 curriculum, tested at D2-D3
10. **Formal/informal register and subjunctive** — D3 only, but high-value differentiator

### Common Misconceptions to Address

| Misconception | Reality | Teaching Approach |
|---------------|---------|-------------------|
| "Verbs are doing words" | State verbs (is, was, seemed) are also verbs | Show verbs that describe states, not actions |
| "Adverbs end in -ly" | Many don't (soon, often, very, here, there, fast) | Test with non-ly adverbs frequently |
| "-ly words are always adverbs" | friendly, lovely, lonely, lively are adjectives | Practice identifying -ly adjectives |
| "Apostrophes show plurals" | Apostrophes show possession or contraction, never plurals | Drill the rule: "no apostrophe for plurals, ever" |
| "'Could of' is correct" | Always "could have" (contracted to "could've") | Explain the phonetic confusion explicitly |
| "'Me and Tom' is wrong everywhere" | "Me and Tom" is wrong as subject but "Tom and me" is correct as object | Teach the removal test: "Give it to me" → "Give it to Tom and me" |
| "Was/were are interchangeable" | "Were" with plural subjects and in the subjunctive | Teach subject-verb agreement + subjunctive as separate concepts |

### Exam Strategy for Grammar Questions

**For Cloze/Gap-Fill:**
1. Read the WHOLE passage first before answering any blanks
2. Identify the tense of the passage (usually past tense)
3. For each blank, read the sentence aloud in your head with each option
4. Eliminate made-up words first (e.g. "choosed", "bringed")
5. Check: does the verb agree with the subject? Is the tense consistent?
6. For homophones, substitute the full form: "they are" → if that works, choose "they're"

**For Punctuation Error-Spotting:**
1. Read the whole line as a sentence first
2. Check capital letters (start of sentence, proper nouns, "I")
3. Check apostrophes (is one missing? is one wrongly added?)
4. Check end punctuation (full stop for statements, question mark for questions)
5. Check commas (after fronted adverbials, before conjunctions in compound sentences)
6. Don't forget N — not every line has an error (expect 1-2 per paper)

**For Word Class:**
1. Read the word in context, not in isolation
2. Ask: "What job is this word doing in THIS sentence?"
3. Watch for dual-function words: check the function, not the word itself
4. Don't be tricked by -ly endings — check if it describes a noun (adjective) or a verb (adverb)

### Connections to Other English Paper Sections

Grammar knowledge supports performance across the entire paper:
- **Comprehension:** Understanding complex sentence structures helps decode difficult passages
- **Vocabulary in context:** Recognising word classes helps narrow down synonym options
- **Spelling:** Grammar knowledge helps with homophones (their/there/they're — knowing the grammar determines the spelling)
