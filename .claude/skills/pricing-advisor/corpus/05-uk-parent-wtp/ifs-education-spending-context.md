## Source

The Institute for Fiscal Studies (IFS) is the UK's leading independent economic research institute. It publishes annual reports on education spending in England. The IFS does not directly study private tutoring as a household expenditure line, but its reports provide the public education spending context within which private tutoring demand is understood — specifically documenting real-terms school funding cuts that created the conditions for tutoring growth.

Relevant publications:
- *Annual Report on Education Spending in England: 2023* (IFS, 2023)
- *Annual Report on Education Spending in England: 2024–25* (IFS, January 2025)
- *School Spending in England: A Guide to the Debate During the 2024 General Election* (IFS, 2024)

## Key findings (the headline numbers)

- **Total public education spending** in the UK in 2023–24: approximately **£116 billion** (including net cost of student loans, in 2024–25 prices). (IFS, Annual Report 2024–25)
- **School spending per pupil** fell by **9% in real terms** between 2009–10 and 2019–20 — described by the IFS as "the largest and most sustained cut in school spending per pupil in England in at least 40 years." (IFS, *School Spending in England: A Guide to the Debate*, 2024)
- By 2024–25, school spending per pupil had partially recovered but remained **below 2009–10 peak** levels in real terms in many non-salary areas.
- The IFS has not published a direct figure for household spending on private tutoring, but the Sutton Trust cross-references IFS data when contextualising the £2 billion private tutoring market as a parallel education economy growing in response to public school under-funding.
- **Pre-primary spending** (early years): public spending per child in early years education remains lower in England than comparable OECD nations.
- The IFS notes that the **National Tutoring Programme** (2020–2024) represented approximately £1 billion in government catch-up spending over four years, averaging roughly £250 million per year — a fraction of the estimated £2 billion annual private tutoring market.

## Sample / methodology (if known)

IFS spending reports draw on HM Treasury Public Expenditure Statistical Analyses (PESA), DfE school funding data, and Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) projections. These are public administrative datasets, not surveys. The reports are published annually and are regarded as the authoritative independent source on UK education spending trends.

## Direct quotes

1. "The largest and most sustained cut in school spending per pupil in England in at least 40 years." — IFS, *School Spending in England: A Guide to the Debate During the 2024 General Election*, 2024 (describing the 2009–2019 real-terms decline).

2. "Total public spending on education in the UK stood at £116 billion [in 2023–24, in 2024–25 prices]." — IFS, *Annual Report on Education Spending in England: 2024–25*, January 2025.

3. Note: The IFS does not publish direct commentary on private tutoring or household willingness-to-pay for educational tools. The contextual relevance is structural: the sustained under-investment in public school capacity is the policy backdrop that explains why the private tutoring market grew from an estimated £1 billion to £2 billion between 2010 and 2026.

## Applicability to UK 11+ app pricing

The IFS data is contextual rather than direct. The decade-long real-terms cut in school funding — followed by partial recovery but not full restoration — has left a structural gap in individualised pupil support. Parents filling that gap with private tutoring spending represent a large and motivated market. An 11+ app competes not with free school provision (which the IFS confirms has been under-resourced) but with the £40–£80/hour private tutoring market the Sutton Trust documents. The IFS data reinforces why parents willingness-to-pay for private educational support is high: public schools cannot fill the gap.
