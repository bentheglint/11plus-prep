## Who / what

Dan Ariely is a professor of behavioural economics and psychology at Duke University, a founding member of the Centre for Advanced Hindsight, and the author of *Predictably Irrational: The Hidden Forces That Shape Our Decisions* (2008), one of the best-selling books in behavioural economics. His research demonstrates, through controlled experiments with MIT students and real-world price data, that consumer purchasing decisions are systematically irrational in predictable ways — and that these irrationalities can be mapped, measured, and designed around. His pricing-relevant experiments (the Economist subscription decoy test, the aspirin price-quality link, the zero-price effect study) have been replicated widely and are now standard reference points in pricing design. His work builds on earlier anchoring research by Kahneman and Tversky but applies it directly to commercial pricing decisions.

## Core ideas (their key beliefs / frameworks)

- **Consumers do not evaluate prices in absolute terms — they evaluate them in context**: the same price feels cheap or expensive depending on what it is presented next to, what order prices are shown, and what number was encountered first. "Most people don't know what they want unless they see it in context."
- **Anchoring**: the first price a consumer encounters for a category becomes a reference point that distorts all subsequent price evaluations. "Once we buy a new product at a particular price, we become anchored to that price." Ariely demonstrated that MIT students' WTP for a variety of products correlated significantly with the last two digits of their own social security number — a genuinely irrelevant number — proving that anchors need not be meaningful to be powerful.
- **The Decoy Effect (Asymmetric Dominance)**: introducing a third option that is dominated by one of two existing options makes the dominating option significantly more attractive. Ariely's most cited example is The Economist's subscription pricing test:
  - Original offer: Online only ($59) vs. Print + Online ($125). Most chose online-only.
  - Modified offer: Added Print-only ($125) — the same price as Print + Online but obviously inferior.
  - Result: the decoy shifted the majority from online-only to print + online, increasing average revenue by approximately 43%.
  - The decoy option itself was never chosen; its sole function was to reframe the comparison.
- **The Zero Price Effect**: "free" carries a disproportionate psychological value that exceeds its economic value by a wide margin. Consumers exhibit irrational excitement around anything free and will trade up — pay more, wait longer, take less — to obtain a free element. Amazon's free shipping rollout drove measurable sales increases; a small shipping charge, even £0.01, suppressed orders significantly. Zero is not simply the lowest price on a continuum — it crosses a psychological threshold.
- **Price-quality association**: higher prices enhance perceived quality and actual experience, not just perceived value. In a controlled study, subjects reported more pain relief from a £1 aspirin than a 10p aspirin — despite both being identical placebos. "A 50-cent aspirin can do what a penny aspirin can't." This is directly relevant to pricing strategy: artificially low prices can actively harm the product experience.
- **Loss aversion and the endowment effect**: once consumers own or perceive ownership of something, they overvalue it relative to market price due to loss aversion. Free trials exploit this: users who experience a product as "theirs" during a trial will pay more to keep it than they would have paid to acquire it cold.
- **Relative comparison replaces absolute evaluation**: when multiple price options are present, consumers abandon attempts to assess absolute value and switch to relative comparison. This makes tier and bundle design a lever for steering choices rather than a neutral menu.

## Method / how to apply

**The Decoy — how to implement**
1. Identify your target tier (the option you most want buyers to choose).
2. Create a "dominated" third option: price it identically to or slightly below the target tier, but make it visually and functionally inferior (fewer features, less access, same price as target).
3. Present all three options simultaneously with the decoy visually adjacent to the target.
4. The decoy shifts the comparison frame away from "should I pay this much at all?" to "which of these two same-priced options is better?" — always resolving in favour of the target.

**Anchoring — how to implement**
1. Lead with your most expensive option (or the highest price in your tier structure), even if most customers will choose a lower tier.
2. If you have a premium/enterprise tier, show it first and most visibly. Its price anchors everything below it as a bargain.
3. Avoid letting the customer's first encounter with your pricing be your cheapest option — this anchors down.
4. In sales conversations, state the highest price first: "This would normally be £X/year; for your situation it's £Y."

**Zero Price Effect — how to implement**
1. If offering a free tier or trial, frame it as "free forever" or "start free" rather than "try for 14 days" — the word "free" carries stronger pull than "trial."
2. Use free elements (free report, free onboarding call, free first month) strategically as conversion triggers, not as standard inclusions that devalue the offer.
3. Be cautious: adding a price to a previously free element causes disproportionate resistance, even if the price is trivial.

**Price-quality signalling**
1. Do not price below your cost-basis floor just to compete. A price that seems "too cheap" undermines trust in quality.
2. For premium positioning, ensure your price is visibly in the upper portion of the category range — even if the delta above competitors is modest.
3. Pair price with visual quality signals (design, copy, photography) to reinforce the price-quality association.

**Tier design for relative comparison**
1. Three tiers almost always outperforms two or five.
2. The middle tier should be the target — price the top tier high enough that the middle feels reasonable, price the bottom tier stripped enough that the middle feels complete.
3. Highlight the middle tier visually ("Most Popular", "Best Value") — this is the contextual cue Ariely's research predicts will drive selection.

## Applicability to a UK 11+ app at pre-launch / early-launch stage

Ariely's frameworks are immediately applicable to the pricing page of a UK 11+ app. The decoy effect suggests a three-tier structure where the middle "Family" tier is the target: place a stripped "Single Child" tier below it and an inflated "School Licence" tier above it — the middle tier then looks obviously correct. Anchoring tells a founder to open the pricing page with the annual total (e.g. "£149.99/year") prominently, so that the monthly equivalent (£12.50/month) feels small by comparison. The zero-price effect is critical for freemium decisions: "Start free" will outperform "Free 14-day trial" in click-through rate, even if the actual offer is the same, because "free" crosses a psychological threshold that "trial" does not. And the price-quality link is a direct argument against launch pricing at £3.99/month — at that price point, parents in the UK 11+ market (who are paying £35+/hour for tutors) will assume the product is lightweight, regardless of its actual quality.
