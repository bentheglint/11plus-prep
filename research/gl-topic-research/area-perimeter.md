# GL Assessment Research: Area and Perimeter

## Format

GL Assessment tests Area and Perimeter within the **Shape and Space** strand of the 50-question, 50-minute multiple-choice maths paper. All questions offer 5 answer options (A-E) with one correct answer. No marks are deducted for wrong answers.

Area and perimeter questions are presented in two main styles:

1. **Diagram-based calculation** -- a shape is shown with labelled dimensions, and the child must calculate the area or perimeter. Some dimensions may be missing and must be deduced from the diagram.
2. **Word problem / real-world context** -- a scenario (garden, path, room, picture frame, fencing) where the child must extract the relevant measurements and apply the correct formula.

Questions typically progress from straightforward single-shape calculations early in the paper to multi-step compound shape problems later. GL papers are broadly arranged in increasing difficulty order, so area/perimeter questions may appear at various positions depending on their complexity.

**Time pressure is significant**: 1 minute per question means children cannot afford to re-derive methods from scratch -- fluency with formulae and rapid identification of the correct approach is essential.

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

Based on analysis of GL practice papers, tutor resources, and the KS2 curriculum, area and perimeter questions test across these categories:

| Category | Approx. GL Weight | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|-------|
| Perimeter of rectangles/squares | ~15% | Including missing side lengths |
| Perimeter of compound rectilinear shapes | ~15% | L-shapes, T-shapes, H-shapes with missing sides |
| Area of rectangles/squares | ~15% | Direct calculation |
| Area of triangles | ~12% | base x height / 2 |
| Area of parallelograms | ~8% | base x perpendicular height |
| Area of compound shapes | ~15% | Decomposition into rectangles, addition/subtraction |
| Reverse problems (given area, find dimension) | ~8% | Working backwards from area or perimeter |
| Real-world context problems | ~7% | Gardens, paths, borders, frames, fencing, painting |
| Same area/different perimeter (and vice versa) | ~5% | Conceptual understanding questions |

**Estimated frequency in a 50-question GL paper**: 2-4 questions will directly test area and/or perimeter. Shape and Space as a whole (including volume, angles, symmetry, coordinates) typically accounts for approximately 8-12 questions, but "number" questions dominate at roughly 5x the frequency of any other strand.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy -- approximately 30%)

Characteristics:
- Single standard shape (rectangle or square)
- All dimensions clearly labelled on the diagram
- Direct application of one formula
- Whole number dimensions, small values (single or low double digits)
- No unit conversion required
- The question clearly states whether area or perimeter is needed

**Example D1 question:**
> A rectangle has a length of 8 cm and a width of 5 cm. What is its perimeter?
> A) 13 cm &nbsp; B) 26 cm &nbsp; C) 40 cm &nbsp; D) 30 cm &nbsp; E) 20 cm

**What makes it easy**: One shape, all dimensions given, single operation (2 x (8+5) = 26), no ambiguity about what is being asked.

### D2 (Medium -- approximately 40%)

Characteristics:
- Compound rectilinear shapes (L-shape, T-shape) OR triangles/parallelograms
- One or two missing side lengths that must be deduced
- May require decomposing a shape into two rectangles
- May involve a simple real-world context (garden, room)
- Could involve decimals or slightly larger numbers
- May ask for area when perimeter is more intuitive (or vice versa)
- Two-step calculation required

**Example D2 question:**
> An L-shaped room is made from two rectangles. The overall length is 12 m, the overall width is 8 m. The cutout rectangle is 4 m by 3 m. What is the area of the room?
> A) 96 m^2 &nbsp; B) 84 m^2 &nbsp; C) 72 m^2 &nbsp; D) 80 m^2 &nbsp; E) 88 m^2

**What makes it medium**: Requires decomposition strategy, must calculate missing dimensions, two-step process (total area minus cutout, or sum of two rectangles).

### D3 (Hard -- approximately 30%)

Characteristics:
- Complex compound shapes requiring 3+ steps
- Reverse problems: given area, find a missing dimension, then calculate perimeter (or vice versa)
- Path/border problems: area of path = area of outer shape minus area of inner shape
- Mixed shape types within one problem (rectangle + triangle)
- Unit conversion within the problem (e.g., some dimensions in cm, some in m)
- Algebraic elements (side expressed as an expression)
- Requires choosing between multiple valid decomposition strategies
- The "obvious" answer is a distractor

**Example D3 question:**
> A rectangular garden is 15 m long and 10 m wide. A path 2 m wide runs all the way around the inside of the garden. What is the area of the path?
> A) 92 m^2 &nbsp; B) 84 m^2 &nbsp; C) 150 m^2 &nbsp; D) 66 m^2 &nbsp; E) 88 m^2

**What makes it hard**: Must visualise the inner rectangle (15-4=11 m by 10-4=6 m), calculate both areas (150 and 66), then subtract (150-66=84). Multiple places to make errors. Distractor A (92) catches those who only subtract 2 from each dimension instead of 4. Distractor D (66) catches those who give the inner area. Distractor C (150) catches those who give the outer area.

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Direct Perimeter Calculation
Calculate the perimeter of a shape with all or most sides labelled. For rectilinear shapes, the child adds all side lengths. For shapes with missing sides, they must deduce lengths first using the property that opposite sides of a rectangle are equal.

### Type 2: Direct Area Calculation
Calculate the area of a rectangle, square, triangle, or parallelogram using the appropriate formula. The diagram shows the shape with dimensions labelled.

### Type 3: Compound Shape Area (Addition)
An irregular rectilinear shape (L, T, H, U, or cross shape) must be split into simpler rectangles. The child calculates each rectangle's area and adds them together. Some dimensions must be deduced from the overall shape.

### Type 4: Compound Shape Area (Subtraction)
A shape is described as a large rectangle with a smaller rectangle (or triangle) removed. The child calculates the larger area and subtracts the smaller one. Common contexts: a picture frame, a lawn with a pond cut out, a wall with a window.

### Type 5: Perimeter of Compound Shapes
An irregular rectilinear shape where the child must find all side lengths (some missing) and add them. The key insight: for rectilinear shapes, the perimeter equals the perimeter of the bounding rectangle -- but GL often tests shapes where this shortcut does NOT apply, requiring careful addition of each side.

### Type 6: Reverse/Working Backwards
Given the area (or perimeter) of a shape, find a missing dimension. For example: "The area of a rectangle is 48 cm^2. Its length is 8 cm. What is its width?" Or: "A square has a perimeter of 36 cm. What is its area?"

### Type 7: Real-World Context
Word problems involving gardens, rooms, fencing, painting, carpet, wallpaper, picture frames, or paths. The child must extract the relevant information, choose the correct operation (area for covering/filling, perimeter for edging/fencing), and calculate.

### Type 8: Comparison/Reasoning
"Which shape has the larger area?" or "Two shapes have the same perimeter. Which has the greater area?" These test conceptual understanding rather than just formula application. The Year 6 curriculum explicitly requires children to understand that shapes with the same area can have different perimeters and vice versa.

### Type 9: Triangle and Parallelogram Area
Apply the formulae: triangle = 1/2 x base x height; parallelogram = base x perpendicular height. GL may present the height as a dotted line inside the shape, and children must identify the perpendicular height (not the slant side).

### Type 10: Multi-Step Context Problems
Problems combining area/perimeter with other maths skills: unit conversion, cost calculation (e.g., "Carpet costs 12 pounds per square metre. How much to carpet a 6m by 4m room?"), or division (e.g., "How many 1m x 1m tiles are needed to cover a 5m by 3m floor?").

## Distractor Design

GL constructs wrong answer options to catch specific misconceptions and procedural errors. For area and perimeter questions, the typical distractor patterns are:

### 1. Area/Perimeter Swap
The most common trap. If the answer is the area, one distractor will be the perimeter (and vice versa). This catches children who confuse which operation to use.
- **Correct (area)**: 8 x 5 = 40 cm^2
- **Distractor (perimeter)**: 2(8+5) = 26 cm

### 2. Forgot to Double (Perimeter)
For perimeter of a rectangle, a distractor will be length + width (i.e., half the perimeter). This catches children who do 8+5=13 instead of 2(8+5)=26.

### 3. Forgot to Halve (Triangle Area)
For triangle area, a distractor will be base x height (without dividing by 2). This catches children who calculate the area of the full rectangle instead of halving it.

### 4. Incorrect Missing Side
For compound shapes, a distractor uses an incorrectly deduced missing side length. For example, subtracting from the wrong overall dimension or using the wrong pair of sides.

### 5. Subtracted Wrong Way Round
For "path around" problems, a distractor subtracts the path width once instead of twice from each dimension (subtracting 2 instead of 4 when the path is 2m wide on both sides).

### 6. Partial Calculation
A distractor is the area of just one component rectangle (for compound shapes) rather than the total, or the area of the cutout rather than the remaining area.

### 7. Unit Error
One distractor applies the wrong unit conversion (e.g., treating 1 m^2 = 100 cm^2 instead of 10,000 cm^2).

### 8. Off-by-One Arithmetic
A distractor is close to the correct answer but with a simple arithmetic slip (e.g., 7 x 8 = 54 instead of 56). This catches children who rush.

### 9. Used Slant Height Instead of Perpendicular Height
For triangles and parallelograms, a distractor uses the slant side length instead of the perpendicular height.

### 10. Plausible Round Number
GL often includes a "neat" round number near the correct answer that has no mathematical basis but looks reasonable to a child who is guessing.

## Common GL Traps

1. **Confusing area and perimeter** -- the question asks for one but the child instinctively calculates the other. GL deliberately designs distractors to catch this.

2. **Missing sides on compound shapes** -- children assume a missing side is the same length as an adjacent labelled side, rather than deducing it from the overall dimensions.

3. **Path/border width applies to BOTH sides** -- a 2m path around a rectangle reduces each dimension by 4m (2m on each side), not 2m. This is the single most common error on path problems.

4. **Forgetting to halve for triangles** -- calculating base x height without the / 2 gives a distractor answer that GL will include.

5. **Using slant height for parallelogram area** -- the area formula requires the perpendicular height, not the length of the sloping side. GL diagrams label both to create confusion.

6. **Not reading the question** -- the question asks "What is the area of the shaded region?" but the child calculates the area of the whole shape, or vice versa.

7. **Perimeter of compound shapes is NOT always the same as the bounding rectangle** -- children sometimes assume the perimeter equals the outer rectangle's perimeter, ignoring internal edges that form part of the boundary.

8. **Unit confusion** -- mixing cm and m within one calculation, or writing cm^2 when the answer should be in m^2.

9. **Assuming regular shapes** -- a rectangle labelled with only two sides might be assumed to be a square, leading to incorrect calculations.

10. **Reverse problems: square root vs. divide** -- when working backwards from area to find a side of a square, children may divide by 4 (the perimeter method) instead of taking the square root.

11. **Decimal dimension errors** -- misreading 3.5 as 35, or failing to multiply decimals correctly.

12. **"Same area = same perimeter" misconception** -- children assume that if two shapes have the same area, they must have the same perimeter. GL may include a question specifically testing this Year 6 curriculum objective.

## Key Formulae Reference

Children must know these formulae fluently (they are not provided in the GL exam):

| Shape | Perimeter | Area |
|-------|-----------|------|
| Rectangle | 2 x (length + width) | length x width |
| Square | 4 x side | side x side (side^2) |
| Triangle | sum of all 3 sides | 1/2 x base x perpendicular height |
| Parallelogram | sum of all 4 sides | base x perpendicular height |
| Compound rectilinear | sum of ALL external sides | split into rectangles and add (or subtract from bounding rectangle) |

**Reverse formulae** (working backwards):
- Side of square from area: side = square root of area
- Side of square from perimeter: side = perimeter / 4
- Width of rectangle from area: width = area / length
- Width of rectangle from perimeter: width = (perimeter / 2) - length
- Base of triangle from area: base = (2 x area) / height

**Unit conversions**:
- 1 m = 100 cm = 1,000 mm
- 1 m^2 = 10,000 cm^2
- 1 cm^2 = 100 mm^2

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand and correctly use these terms:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Perimeter** | The total distance around the outside of a 2D shape |
| **Area** | The amount of space inside a 2D shape, measured in square units |
| **Rectilinear** | A shape made entirely of straight lines meeting at right angles |
| **Compound shape** / **Composite shape** | A shape made from two or more simpler shapes combined |
| **Perpendicular height** | The vertical distance from base to top, at right angles to the base |
| **Slant height** | The length of a sloping side (NOT used for area of parallelogram/triangle) |
| **Dimension** | A measurable extent (length, width, height) |
| **Square units** | cm^2, m^2, mm^2 -- the units used for area |
| **Linear units** | cm, m, mm, km -- the units used for perimeter/length |
| **Decompose** | To break a compound shape into simpler parts |
| **Base** | The bottom side of a shape (or any side chosen as the reference for height) |
| **Width / Breadth** | The shorter dimension of a rectangle |
| **Length** | The longer dimension of a rectangle |
| **Diagonal** | A line connecting two non-adjacent corners (not directly tested but may appear in diagrams) |
| **Regular** | All sides the same length (a regular rectangle is a square) |
| **Irregular** | Sides of different lengths |

## National Curriculum Alignment

The GL 11+ tests content aligned to the KS2 National Curriculum (Years 3-6). Here is what is taught at each stage:

### Year 3
- Measure the perimeter of simple 2D shapes by adding side lengths
- Use appropriate measuring tools and units

### Year 4
- Measure and calculate the perimeter of a rectilinear figure (including squares) in centimetres and metres
- Find the area of rectilinear shapes by counting squares on a grid

### Year 5
- Measure and calculate the perimeter of composite rectilinear shapes in centimetres and metres
- Calculate and compare the area of rectangles (including squares), using standard units: square centimetres (cm^2) and square metres (m^2)
- Estimate the area of irregular shapes by counting squares

### Year 6
- Recognise that shapes with the same areas can have different perimeters and vice versa
- Relate the area of rectangles to parallelograms and triangles, for example by dissection
- Calculate the area of parallelograms and triangles
- Solve problems involving the calculation and conversion of units of measure, using decimal notation up to 3 decimal places where appropriate

### Beyond Curriculum (11+ Stretch)
GL may test slightly beyond the standard Year 6 curriculum:
- More complex compound shapes requiring 3+ decomposition steps
- Reverse problems (given area, find missing side, then calculate perimeter)
- Multi-step word problems combining area/perimeter with cost calculations or unit conversions
- Path/border problems requiring subtraction of inner from outer area
- Algebraic expressions for unknown sides (e.g., side = 2x, perimeter = 36, find x)

**Note**: Circles (circumference and area using pi) are NOT part of the KS2 curriculum and are extremely unlikely to appear in a GL 11+ paper. They are KS3 content. However, some super-selective schools or non-GL papers may occasionally include a basic circle question.

## Teaching Notes

### Common Misconceptions (Research-Based)

1. **Area and perimeter are the same thing** -- many children struggle to distinguish between "the space inside" and "the distance around". Research shows this confusion persists even into secondary school because both concepts use the same shape and the same dimensions.

2. **"Make one bigger, the other gets bigger"** -- children intuitively believe that increasing area always increases perimeter (and vice versa). This is often true but not always. A long thin rectangle can have a larger perimeter than a more compact shape with the same area. The Year 6 curriculum explicitly addresses this.

3. **Addition = perimeter, multiplication = area** -- children over-simplify the operations. This leads to errors with triangles (where area involves division) and with perimeter of shapes that aren't rectangles.

4. **Counting squares is the only way to find area** -- children who learned area through grid counting in Year 4 may resist using formulae and try to "count" even when dimensions are large or the shape is not on a grid.

5. **Perpendicular height vs. slant height** -- when calculating the area of a triangle or parallelogram, children often use the slant side length instead of the perpendicular height. This is reinforced by diagrams where the slant side is more visually prominent.

6. **Compound shape perimeter includes internal edges** -- children sometimes add the internal dividing line (used for decomposition) to the perimeter, not realising it is not part of the outer boundary.

7. **Reverse problems feel unfamiliar** -- children who are fluent at "dimensions to area" struggle when asked to go from "area to dimensions". This requires flexible thinking and algebraic reasoning.

### Teaching Strategies for 11+ Preparation

- **Always draw and label**: even when a diagram is provided, encourage children to annotate it with deduced missing lengths before calculating.
- **Colour-code decomposition**: when splitting compound shapes, shade each sub-rectangle a different colour to avoid counting regions twice or missing one.
- **"Am I finding inside or around?"**: train children to ask this question before every calculation to avoid the area/perimeter swap.
- **Check units first**: before calculating, check whether the answer needs to be in cm, m, cm^2, m^2, etc. This prevents unit confusion.
- **Estimate first**: a rough mental estimate ("the rectangle is about 10 by 5, so area is about 50") helps children spot distractor answers that are wildly wrong.
- **Practice reverse problems explicitly**: these are high-value because they test deeper understanding and appear at D2/D3 difficulty.
- **Practise path/border problems**: these are the hardest common question type and the "subtract from both sides" rule needs to be drilled until automatic.
- **Time pressure practice**: at 1 minute per question, children need to recognise the question type and select the correct approach within seconds. Regular timed practice builds this fluency.
