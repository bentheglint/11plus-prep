# GL Assessment Research: Ratio and Proportion

**Researched:** March 2026
**Purpose:** Guide question quality, difficulty calibration, distractor design, and lesson content for the Ratio & Proportion topic in the 11+ prep app.

---

## Format

GL Assessment places **ratio** within the **Statistics** section of the maths paper, alongside averages, column graphs, pie charts, and coordinates. The official GL specification lists it as "simple ratio."

**Paper structure:**
- 50 multiple-choice questions in 50 minutes (1 minute per question average)
- 5 answer options (A-E) per question
- "Number" questions dominate the paper (~5x more than any other category)
- Ratio/proportion questions typically appear as word problems requiring calculation
- Answers recorded on a separate multiple-choice answer sheet

**Estimated question count for ratio/proportion:** 2-4 questions per paper. Ratio sits within the Statistics section, which also covers averages, graphs, pie charts, and coordinates. The Statistics section as a whole is a smaller portion of the paper compared to Number, but ratio questions can also appear embedded within Number problems (e.g., fractions-as-ratios, sharing amounts).

**Time pressure context:** At 1 minute per question, children must read, interpret, calculate, and select an answer quickly. Multi-step ratio problems (sharing in a three-part ratio, recipe scaling) are particularly time-pressured because they require several calculation steps within that minute.

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

GL tests ratio and proportion across several skill categories. Based on analysis of GL practice papers, tutor resources, and curriculum alignment:

| Category | Estimated GL Weight | Description |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Sharing in a ratio (2-part) | ~25% | Divide a total amount between two people/groups in a given ratio |
| Simplifying ratios | ~15% | Reduce ratios to simplest form using HCF |
| Equivalent ratios & scaling | ~15% | Find missing values by scaling ratios up or down |
| Recipe/ingredient scaling | ~10% | Direct proportion problems using recipes or mixtures |
| Ratio-to-fraction conversion | ~10% | Convert between ratio notation and fractions (part-to-whole) |
| Sharing in a ratio (3-part) | ~8% | Divide among three groups -- harder because more parts |
| Map scales & scale drawings | ~7% | Convert between map/model measurements and real distances |
| Proportion word problems | ~5% | "If 4 cost X, how much do 7 cost?" -- unitary method |
| Inverse proportion (basic) | ~3% | One quantity increases as another decreases |
| Best value/comparison | ~2% | Compare unit prices or rates to find best deal |

**Key point:** GL favours straightforward ratio-sharing and simplification problems. Complex multi-step proportion problems are less common but appear at D3 difficulty. Inverse proportion is rare at 11+ level but occasionally tested in simpler forms.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Simple two-part ratios with small, friendly numbers (e.g., 2:3, 1:4, 3:5)
- Total amounts that divide cleanly by the sum of ratio parts
- Single-step calculation only
- Ratios already in simplest form or require dividing by an obvious common factor (2, 5, 10)
- Clear, unambiguous wording with a familiar context

**Typical question formats:**
- "Share 20 sweets between Tom and Amy in the ratio 3:2. How many does Tom get?" (answer: 12)
- "Simplify the ratio 6:10" (answer: 3:5)
- "A recipe uses 2 eggs for every 3 cups of flour. How many eggs for 6 cups of flour?" (answer: 4)
- "What fraction of the total is represented by the smaller part of the ratio 1:3?" (answer: 1/4)

**Numbers used:** Totals up to 50, ratio parts that sum to 10 or less, clean division throughout.

**Distractors at D1:**
- One option is the other person's share (reversal trap)
- One option uses the ratio numbers directly without calculation
- One option divides by one part instead of the sum

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**Characteristics:**
- Two-part or three-part ratios with larger but still manageable numbers
- May require two calculation steps (find one share, then calculate the other)
- Ratios that need simplifying before use, or where HCF is not immediately obvious
- Contexts that add a layer of interpretation (recipes, maps, mixing)
- Totals that require careful division (not always multiples of 10)
- May involve converting between ratio and fraction notation
- The question may give one person's share and ask for the total (working backwards)

**Typical question formats:**
- "Share 108 in the ratio 5:4. How much does each person get?" (answer: 60 and 48)
- "On a map, 1 cm represents 5 km. Two towns are 3 cm apart. What is the real distance?" (answer: 15 km)
- "A paint mix uses red and blue in the ratio 3:7. If you use 21 ml of blue, how much red?" (answer: 9 ml)
- "Hetta, Ruby and Eva share 250 sweets in the ratio 5:3:2. How many does Ruby get?" (answer: 75)
- "The ratio of boys to girls is 3:4. What fraction of the class are boys?" (answer: 3/7)
- "Write 12:18:24 in its simplest form" (answer: 2:3:4)

**Numbers used:** Totals up to 250, ratio parts that sum to 10-15, HCF of 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6.

**Distractors at D2:**
- Plausible wrong answers from common calculation errors (dividing by wrong number)
- The "additive error" answer (adding instead of multiplying)
- Fraction confusion (writing 3:4 as 3/4 instead of 3/7 of the whole)
- Forgetting to convert units before applying scale

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Multi-step problems requiring 3+ calculations
- Three-part ratios with larger numbers
- Working backwards from one share to find the total or another share
- Combining ratio with other topics (percentages, fractions, money)
- Less obvious contexts requiring careful interpretation
- Ratios given in different forms that need converting (e.g., "for every 3 red there are 5 blue" rather than explicit ratio notation)
- Problems where the ratio must be inferred from the information given
- Scale factor problems with similar shapes
- Unit conversion required before ratio calculation

**Typical question formats:**
- "Amy and Ben share money in the ratio 5:3. Amy gets 30 more than Ben. How much money is shared in total?" (answer: 120)
- "A recipe for 8 cookies needs 200g flour, 100g butter, 50g sugar. How much flour for 20 cookies?" (answer: 500g)
- "In a class, the ratio of boys to girls is 2:3. If there are 12 boys, how many children are in the class?" (answer: 30)
- "A model car is built to a scale of 1:50. The real car is 4 m long. How long is the model in cm?" (answer: 8 cm -- requires m to cm conversion)
- "Three friends share a prize. Ali gets twice as much as Ben, and Ben gets three times as much as Cal. If the total prize is 200, how much does Cal get?" (answer: 20)
- "A bag has red and blue counters in the ratio 3:5. 10 more red counters are added and the ratio becomes 1:1. How many blue counters are there?" (answer: 25)

**Numbers used:** Totals up to 500+, multi-digit division, mixed units, ratio parts that sum to 15+.

**Distractors at D3:**
- Answers from incomplete working (stopping at step 2 of 3)
- Answers that confuse part-to-part with part-to-whole
- Answers from applying the ratio the wrong way round
- Plausible-looking round numbers that feel right but are wrong
- Answers from forgetting unit conversion

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Sharing in a Ratio (Most Common)
"Divide X between A and B in the ratio a:b"
- **Method:** Add ratio parts (a+b), divide total by sum, multiply by each part
- **Variant:** Three-part sharing (a:b:c)
- **Variant:** Given one person's share, find the total or the other share
- **GL favourite:** Money and sweet sharing -- relatable contexts for 10-year-olds

### Type 2: Simplifying Ratios
"Write the ratio a:b in its simplest form"
- **Method:** Find HCF, divide both parts
- **Variant:** Three-part ratios (a:b:c)
- **Variant:** Ratios with different units (must convert first: 2 km : 500 m = 4:1)
- **GL twist:** Including ratios where one part is already 1 (e.g., 1:7 is already simplified)

### Type 3: Equivalent Ratios / Missing Values
"Find the missing value: 3:5 = ?:20"
- **Method:** Identify scale factor, multiply/divide
- **Variant:** Tables of equivalent ratios
- **GL twist:** The scale factor is not always a whole number at D3

### Type 4: Ratio-to-Fraction Conversion
"The ratio of X to Y is 3:4. What fraction is X?"
- **Critical understanding:** 3:4 means X is 3/7 of the total (NOT 3/4)
- **This is the single most common error children make with ratios**
- **GL exploits this heavily** with fraction answers as distractors

### Type 5: Recipe / Mixture Scaling
"A recipe for 12 biscuits uses 300g flour. How much for 20 biscuits?"
- **Method:** Unitary method (find amount per 1, then scale) or scaling factor
- **Variant:** "You have 450g flour. How many biscuits can you make?"
- **GL context:** Baking, cooking, mixing paints, making squash/drinks

### Type 6: Map Scales & Scale Drawings
"On a map, 1 cm represents 5 km. Two towns are 3 cm apart on the map."
- **Method:** Multiply map distance by scale factor
- **Variant:** Working backwards (real distance to map distance)
- **Variant:** Scale factor for similar shapes (enlargement)
- **GL twist:** Unit conversion required (cm to m, m to km)

### Type 7: Proportion Word Problems (Unitary Method)
"5 oranges cost 1.25. How much do 8 oranges cost?"
- **Method:** Find cost of 1 (divide), then multiply by new quantity
- **Variant:** "How many can you buy with X?"
- **GL context:** Shopping, money, distance/time at simpler levels

### Type 8: Working Backwards from a Share
"Amy gets 24 sweets, and the ratio is 3:5. How many sweets in total?"
- **Method:** 24 represents 3 parts, so 1 part = 8, total = 8 parts = 64
- **This is harder because children must reverse the process**
- **GL uses this at D2-D3**

## Distractor Design

GL distractors for ratio questions are designed around **predictable calculation errors**. Each wrong answer corresponds to a specific mistake a child might make:

### Distractor Patterns

| Distractor Type | How It's Generated | Example |
|----------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Reversed share** | Give the other person's amount | Q: Tom's share of 20 in 3:2 → Distractor: 8 (Amy's share) |
| **Divide by one part, not sum** | Use a:b, divide total by a or b alone | Q: Share 20 in 3:2 → Distractor: 20/3 ≈ 7 or 20/2 = 10 |
| **Part-to-whole confusion** | Treat ratio as fraction of total | Q: Ratio 3:4, fraction of total? → Distractor: 3/4 instead of 3/7 |
| **Additive error** | Add instead of multiply | Q: 2:3 = 6:? → Distractor: 7 (added 4) instead of 9 (multiplied by 3) |
| **Incomplete calculation** | Stop before final step | Q: Multi-step → Distractor: intermediate value |
| **Wrong unit** | Forget to convert units | Q: Scale 1:50, real = 4m, model in cm → Distractor: 0.08 (forgot m→cm) |
| **Ratio applied backwards** | Swap which part belongs to whom | Q: Boys:Girls = 2:3, 12 boys → Distractor: 8 (treated 12 as the girls) |
| **Round number magnet** | Plausible-looking clean number near correct answer | Correct: 48 → Distractor: 50 |

### Five-Option Design Principle (A-E)
For a typical ratio sharing question "Share 35 in the ratio 2:5":
- **A:** 5 (divide total by ratio part -- common error)
- **B:** 10 (correct answer for smaller share)
- **C:** 14 (divide by one part only: 35/2.5 or similar reasoning error)
- **D:** 17.5 (divide total in half -- ignoring ratio entirely)
- **E:** 25 (correct answer for larger share -- but question asked for smaller)

The correct answer and its "reversed share" always both appear as options, because confusing which person gets which share is such a common error.

## Common GL Traps

1. **Part-to-whole vs part-to-part confusion:** The ratio 3:4 means 3 out of every 7 total, NOT 3 out of 4. GL exploits this by offering both 3/7 and 3/4 as options. This is the single most exploited misconception.

2. **Additive reasoning instead of multiplicative:** Children who haven't fully grasped proportional reasoning add a constant difference instead of multiplying by a scale factor. Example: If 2:3 becomes 6:?, they add 4 to get 7 instead of multiplying by 3 to get 9. Research (Ryan & Williams, 2000) identified this as the most persistent ratio misconception across all age groups.

3. **Order matters:** The ratio "boys to girls is 3:5" means 3 boys for every 5 girls. GL occasionally asks "what is the ratio of girls to boys?" and expects 5:3, trapping children who give 3:5 on autopilot.

4. **Unit conversion before ratio:** Scale and proportion questions sometimes give measurements in different units (cm and m, or m and km). Children must convert to the same unit BEFORE applying the ratio.

5. **"How many in total?" vs "how many does X get?":** Children who correctly work out one person's share may give that as the answer when the question asked for the total, or vice versa. Reading the question carefully is essential.

6. **Three-part ratio sum:** With ratios like 2:3:5, children sometimes add only two parts (e.g., 2+3=5) instead of all three (2+3+5=10). This gives completely wrong individual shares.

7. **Simplification not complete:** Children may simplify 12:18 to 6:9 (dividing by 2) and stop, not realising they should continue to 2:3 (dividing by 3). GL offers partially simplified ratios as distractors.

8. **Recipe scaling -- wrong direction:** "A recipe for 4 serves needs 200g. How much for 6?" Children who multiply 200 by 6 (instead of first finding the amount for 1, then multiplying) get 1200 instead of 300.

9. **Working backwards confusion:** When given one person's share and asked to find the total, children often multiply by the total parts instead of first finding one part's value. "Amy gets 15 in ratio 3:2. Total?" -- error: 15 x 5 = 75 instead of (15/3) x 5 = 25.

10. **Decimal/fraction ratios:** When a ratio involves decimals (e.g., 1.5:3), children must multiply both sides to get whole numbers (3:6 = 1:2). GL occasionally uses this to test understanding at D3.

## Key Concepts Reference

### Core Definitions
- **Ratio:** Compares two or more quantities using the notation a:b (read "a to b"). Shows how many times one quantity contains another.
- **Proportion:** A statement that two ratios are equal (a:b = c:d). Also used to mean "a part of the whole."
- **Part-to-part ratio:** Compares one part to another (3 boys to 5 girls = 3:5).
- **Part-to-whole ratio:** Compares one part to the total (3 boys out of 8 children = 3:8 or 3/8).
- **Equivalent ratios:** Ratios that represent the same relationship (2:3 = 4:6 = 6:9).
- **Simplest form:** A ratio where the HCF of all parts is 1 (e.g., 2:3 not 4:6).
- **Scale factor:** The multiplier used to enlarge or reduce. Scale 1:50 means real = 50 x model.
- **Unitary method:** Find the value for 1 unit first, then scale to the required amount.
- **Direct proportion:** As one quantity increases, the other increases at the same rate.
- **Inverse proportion:** As one quantity increases, the other decreases (rare at 11+ level).

### Method: Sharing in a Ratio
1. **Add** the ratio parts together (e.g., 3:5 -> 3+5 = 8 total parts)
2. **Divide** the total amount by the number of parts (e.g., 40 / 8 = 5 per part)
3. **Multiply** each ratio number by the value of one part (e.g., 3 x 5 = 15, 5 x 5 = 25)
4. **Check:** Do the shares add up to the total? (15 + 25 = 40 -- correct)

### Method: Simplifying a Ratio
1. **Find** the HCF of all parts
2. **Divide** every part by the HCF
3. **Check:** Can you divide further? If all parts are coprime, you're done.

### Method: Unitary Method
1. **Find** the value for 1 unit (divide the known quantity by its count)
2. **Multiply** the unit value by the required count
3. **Check:** Does the answer make sense in context?

### Method: Scale Conversion
1. **Identify** the scale (e.g., 1 cm : 5 km)
2. **Ensure** both measurements are in the same unit
3. **Multiply** or **divide** by the scale factor as needed
4. **Convert** the answer to the required unit if necessary

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand and be comfortable with these terms:

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Ratio | A comparison of two or more quantities | 3:5 |
| Proportion | A part relative to the whole, or two equal ratios | 3 out of 8 |
| Simplify / Simplest form | Reduce to lowest terms | 6:10 -> 3:5 |
| Equivalent | Same value expressed differently | 2:3 = 4:6 |
| Scale | The ratio between a drawing/model and real life | 1 cm : 5 km |
| Scale factor | How many times bigger or smaller | Scale factor of 3 |
| Share / Divide | Split an amount according to a ratio | Share 40 in ratio 3:5 |
| Parts | The numbers in a ratio represent "parts" of the total | 3 parts + 5 parts = 8 parts |
| For every | The language of ratio | "For every 2 red, there are 3 blue" |
| In every | The language of proportion | "3 in every 8 are blue" |
| Unitary method | Find the value for 1, then scale | "1 orange costs 25p" |
| HCF / Highest Common Factor | Largest number dividing all parts | HCF of 12 and 18 is 6 |
| Direct proportion | Both increase together | More oranges = more cost |
| Inverse proportion | One increases, other decreases | More workers = less time |
| Enlarge / Enlargement | Make bigger by a scale factor | Enlarge by scale factor 2 |
| Reduce | Make smaller by a scale factor | Reduce by scale factor 1/2 |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements (Ratio and Proportion)
The National Curriculum introduces ratio and proportion formally in Year 6 only. The four statutory objectives are:

1. **Solve problems involving the relative sizes of 2 quantities where missing values can be found by using integer multiplication and division facts.** -- This covers sharing in a ratio, equivalent ratios, and scaling.

2. **Solve problems involving the calculation of percentages [for example, of measures and such as 15% of 360] and the use of percentages for comparison.** -- Percentage problems are a form of proportion (per 100).

3. **Solve problems involving similar shapes where the scale factor is known or can be found.** -- Scale drawings, enlargements, and map scales.

4. **Solve problems involving unequal sharing and grouping using knowledge of fractions and multiples.** -- This is the most directly ratio-focused objective; it covers sharing in non-equal parts.

### Non-Statutory Guidance
"Pupils recognise proportionality in contexts when the relations between quantities are in the same ratio (e.g., similar shapes, recipes)."

### Prerequisite Knowledge (Year 5 and earlier)
Ratio and proportion builds heavily on prior learning. Children need fluency in:
- **Multiplication and division facts** (all tables to 12x12)
- **Fractions** (equivalent fractions, simplifying, fraction of an amount)
- **Multiples and factors** (finding HCF for simplifying ratios)
- **Percentages** (percentage of an amount, percentage comparison)
- **Measurement and units** (converting between cm/m/km for scale problems)

### Beyond Year 6 (GL can stretch into this)
GL occasionally tests at the upper boundary of KS2, which may touch on:
- Three-part ratios (not explicitly in the Year 6 curriculum but within its spirit)
- Multi-step proportion problems combining ratio with money or measurement
- Basic scale factor work that approaches Year 7 enlargement concepts

## Teaching Notes

### The Critical Misconception: Additive vs Multiplicative Thinking
Research consistently identifies the "additive error" as the most persistent misconception in ratio work (Ryan & Williams, 2000; Cambridge Mathematics Espresso 36). Children who think additively will, for example, see that 2 became 6 (added 4) and therefore add 4 to 3 to get 7, instead of recognising the multiplicative relationship (multiplied by 3) to get 9.

**How to address it:** Pose problems where the additive answer is obviously wrong. For example: "A rectangle is 1 cm by 4 cm. A similar rectangle has width 100 cm. What is its length?" Additive thinking gives 103 cm (almost square), which clearly cannot be similar to a long thin rectangle. This creates cognitive conflict that helps children shift to multiplicative reasoning.

### Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract Progression
Following research-backed pedagogy for 9-10 year olds:
1. **Concrete:** Use Cuisenaire rods, counters, or physical objects to build ratios
2. **Pictorial:** Bar models are the most powerful visual representation for ratio. Draw the ratio as bars of equal width, then label and calculate.
3. **Abstract:** Move to the a:b notation and algebraic manipulation only after conceptual understanding is secure.

### Bar Model Approach (Recommended for Lessons)
The bar model is the single most effective visual for ratio problems:
- Draw one bar per part of the ratio
- Label each bar with the same value (total / number of parts)
- This makes the relationship between parts and whole immediately visible
- Works for sharing, finding totals, working backwards, and fraction conversion

### Common Teaching Sequence
1. **Introduction to ratio language:** "for every", "to every", comparing groups
2. **Simplifying ratios** (using HCF, connecting to simplifying fractions)
3. **Equivalent ratios** (scaling up and down)
4. **Sharing in a two-part ratio** (the core skill)
5. **Ratio and fractions connection** (part-to-whole conversion)
6. **Three-part ratios** (extension of sharing)
7. **Recipe and mixture problems** (real-world direct proportion)
8. **Scale drawings and maps** (applied ratio)
9. **Unitary method** (proportion problem-solving strategy)
10. **Working backwards from one share** (harder reasoning)
11. **Multi-step problems combining ratio with other topics** (D3 preparation)

### What Makes Ratio Hard for 9-Year-Olds
- It requires **multiplicative reasoning**, which many children are still developing at this age
- The connection between ratio and fractions is not intuitive (3:4 feels different from 3/7)
- **Part-to-part vs part-to-whole** is genuinely confusing and needs repeated explicit teaching
- Real-world problems require careful reading to identify what's being asked
- Time pressure in the exam means children default to additive (quicker, more instinctive) reasoning

### Exam Strategy Tips
1. **Underline what's being asked:** "How many does Tom get?" vs "How many in total?" -- this catches the most common careless error
2. **Write the ratio clearly:** Always write out a:b and label which is which
3. **Add the parts first:** This is step 1 every time -- make it automatic
4. **Check the answer makes sense:** "Is my answer bigger/smaller than I'd expect?"
5. **Use the answer options:** If stuck, try working backwards from the options
6. **Watch for unit traps:** If the question mentions two different units, convert first
