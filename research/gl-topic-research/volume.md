# GL Assessment Research: Volume

## Format

GL Assessment tests volume within the **Measurement** strand of the 50-question, 50-minute multiple-choice maths paper. Volume questions are a minority of the paper — "number" questions appear roughly five times more often than any other strand — so expect **2-4 volume/capacity questions per paper**, occasionally fewer.

All questions are multiple choice with 5 options (A-E). The paper follows Key Stage 2 curriculum content, including some Year 6 material children may not yet have covered in school. Volume is explicitly listed by GL as a tested topic: "volume of cubes and cuboids" sits alongside money, time, metric system, perimeter and area, and speed/distance/time within their Measurement category.

Because volume appears infrequently, every question must count. GL uses volume questions to test both procedural fluency (can the child apply the formula?) and reasoning (can they work backwards from a volume, convert units, or handle multi-step real-world contexts?).

---

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

Based on GL practice papers, KS2 SATs analysis, and professional tutor resources, volume questions test these skills in roughly these proportions:

| Category | Estimated GL Weight | Description |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Volume of a cuboid (L x W x H) | ~25-30% | Direct calculation given three dimensions |
| Volume of a cube (a³) | ~10-15% | Edge length given or derived; understanding that all edges equal |
| Missing dimension (given volume + 2 dims) | ~15-20% | Reverse operation — division to find unknown length/width/height |
| Counting unit cubes / building cuboids | ~10-15% | Visual/spatial — counting cubes in a 3D diagram, including hidden cubes |
| Capacity & unit conversion (cm³ ↔ ml ↔ litres) | ~10-15% | Linking volume to liquid capacity; 1 cm³ = 1 ml, 1000 cm³ = 1 litre |
| Real-world word problems | ~10-15% | Tanks, boxes, containers — filling, emptying, "how much more to fill" |
| Comparing volumes | ~5-10% | Which shape has greater volume? Same volume, different dimensions? |
| Compound 3D shapes | ~5% | L-shaped or stepped solids split into two cuboids (D3 only) |
| How many small cubes fit inside a larger one | ~5% | Packing problems — divide per dimension, not by total volume |

**Note:** GL rarely tests surface area at 11+ level. Nets of 3D shapes may appear in the Geometry strand but are separate from volume calculation.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

Straightforward application of the volume formula with friendly numbers. No unit conversion. No multi-step reasoning.

**Characteristics:**
- Direct "calculate the volume" with three given whole-number dimensions
- Small, manageable numbers (single digits or small multiples of 10): e.g. 4 cm x 3 cm x 2 cm = 24 cm³
- Cube volume where edge length is given directly: e.g. "A cube has edges of 5 cm. What is its volume?"
- Counting visible unit cubes in a simple 3D diagram (small array, no hidden cubes)
- All dimensions in the same unit — no conversion needed
- Units provided in the question; answer options all in cm³
- One-step calculation only

**Example question types:**
- "A box is 6 cm long, 4 cm wide and 3 cm high. What is its volume?"
- "What is the volume of a cube with edges of 3 cm?"
- "How many 1 cm cubes would fill a box measuring 5 cm x 2 cm x 3 cm?"

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

Requires either a reverse operation, a unit conversion, a two-step calculation, or application in a word problem context.

**Characteristics:**
- Missing dimension: "A cuboid has a volume of 120 cm³. Its length is 10 cm and width is 4 cm. What is its height?"
- Two-step problems: calculate volume then convert to litres, or calculate volume then compare
- Word problem context requiring extraction of dimensions from text (not a diagram)
- Counting cubes in a 3D diagram where some cubes are hidden behind others
- Capacity problems: "A fish tank is 40 cm long, 25 cm wide and 30 cm tall. How many litres of water can it hold?"
- Numbers that require carrying or produce larger products (e.g. 12 x 8 x 5)
- Comparing two shapes: "Which box holds more?"

**Example question types:**
- "A water tank holds 60 litres. It is 50 cm long and 40 cm wide. How deep is the water in cm?" (requires 60,000 ml = 60,000 cm³, then ÷ 50 ÷ 40)
- "A cube has a volume of 64 cm³. What is the length of each edge?"
- "How many cubes are in this 3D shape?" (with hidden cubes to account for)

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

Multi-step reasoning, algebraic thinking, compound shapes, or problems that combine volume with other mathematical concepts (fractions, ratio, unit conversion chains).

**Characteristics:**
- Compound 3D shapes: L-shaped or stepped solids that must be split into two cuboids
- Algebraic missing dimension: "A cuboid is x cm long, 5 cm wide and 4 cm high. Its volume is 180 cm³. Find x."
- Packing problems with non-divisible dimensions: "How many 3 cm cubes fit in a box 10 cm x 9 cm x 7 cm?" (must think per-dimension: 3 x 3 x 2 = 18, not 630 ÷ 27 = 23.3...)
- Multi-step conversions: "A pool is 8 m long, 4 m wide and 1.5 m deep. How many litres does it hold?"
- Fractional volume: "A container is 3/4 full. It contains 600 ml. What is the total capacity?"
- Rate problems: "A tap fills 2 litres per minute. How long to fill a tank 50 cm x 40 cm x 30 cm?"
- Volume comparison where dimensions are given in different units
- Problems requiring knowledge that 1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³ = 1000 litres

**Example question types:**
- "An L-shaped room is 6 m long, 4 m wide and 3 m high, with a 2 m x 2 m x 3 m section removed. What is its volume?"
- "240 small cubes of edge 2 cm are melted and recast into a cuboid 20 cm x 12 cm. What is the height?"
- "A tank is 1/3 full with 12 litres of water. What is the tank's full capacity in cm³?"

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Direct Volume Calculation
"A cuboid measures 8 cm x 5 cm x 3 cm. What is its volume?"
- Most basic format. Tests formula application.
- D1 with friendly numbers; D2 with larger or decimal values.

### Type 2: Volume of a Cube
"A cube has edges of 7 cm. What is its volume?"
- Tests understanding that all three dimensions are equal.
- D1 with small edge lengths; D2 when edge must be derived from other information.

### Type 3: Missing Dimension
"A cuboid has a volume of 150 cm³. Its length is 10 cm and its width is 5 cm. What is its height?"
- Requires division: V ÷ (L x W) = H.
- D2 typically; D3 if the remaining dimension involves decimals or fractions.

### Type 4: Counting Unit Cubes
A 3D diagram shows a shape built from unit cubes. "How many cubes make this shape?"
- Tests spatial awareness and systematic counting.
- D1 with simple rectangular arrays; D2/D3 with irregular shapes or hidden cubes.

### Type 5: Capacity & Conversion
"A fish tank is 60 cm long, 30 cm wide and 40 cm tall. How many litres of water can it hold?"
- Links volume (cm³) to capacity (litres) via 1000 cm³ = 1 litre.
- D2 typically; D3 if combined with "how much more to fill" or partial filling.

### Type 6: Packing / "How Many Fit?"
"How many cubes with 2 cm edges can fit inside a box measuring 10 cm x 8 cm x 6 cm?"
- Critical trap: must divide each dimension separately (5 x 4 x 3 = 60), NOT total volumes (480 ÷ 8 = 60 — coincidentally same here, but not always).
- D2 with exact fits; D3 with remainders per dimension.

### Type 7: Real-World Word Problem
"A swimming pool is 25 m long, 10 m wide and 2 m deep. How many litres of water does it hold?"
- Multi-step: calculate volume in m³, convert to litres (x 1000).
- D3 due to large numbers and conversion chain.

### Type 8: Comparison
"Box A is 12 cm x 8 cm x 5 cm. Box B is 10 cm x 10 cm x 5 cm. Which holds more?"
- Calculate both volumes, compare.
- D2; D3 if dimensions are in different units.

### Type 9: Compound 3D Shape
An L-shaped or stepped solid shown with dimensions. "What is the volume?"
- Split into two cuboids, calculate each, add (or calculate the full cuboid and subtract the missing piece).
- D3 — rare at 11+ but appears in harder papers.

### Type 10: Algebraic / Reverse Problem
"A cuboid has volume 360 cm³. Its length is twice its width. Its height is 5 cm. Find its length."
- Sets up an equation: 2w x w x 5 = 360 → 10w² = 360 → w² = 36 → w = 6, L = 12.
- D3 — stretches into algebra territory.

## Distractor Design

GL designs wrong answers that exploit specific calculation errors. For volume, common distractor patterns include:

### 1. Area Instead of Volume (2D vs 3D confusion)
- **Correct:** 6 x 4 x 3 = 72 cm³
- **Distractor:** 6 x 4 = 24 (calculates area of one face only)
- **Why it works:** Children who forget the third dimension or confuse area with volume pick this.

### 2. Perimeter or Sum of Dimensions
- **Correct:** 6 x 4 x 3 = 72 cm³
- **Distractor:** 6 + 4 + 3 = 13 (adds instead of multiplying)
- **Why it works:** Weaker students default to addition when unsure which operation to use.

### 3. Off-by-One Multiplication Error
- **Correct:** 7 x 8 x 5 = 280
- **Distractors:** 270, 290, 275, 285
- **Why it works:** Times table errors with 7x8 (common weak spot) cascade into wrong answer.

### 4. Unit Conversion Error (x10 or ÷10 instead of x1000 or ÷1000)
- **Correct:** 72,000 cm³ = 72 litres
- **Distractor:** 7.2 litres (÷10,000) or 720 litres (÷100)
- **Why it works:** Children who don't know the cm³-to-litre conversion guess a power of 10.

### 5. Dividing Total Volumes for Packing Problems
- **Correct:** 10÷2 = 5, 8÷2 = 4, 6÷2 = 3, answer = 5 x 4 x 3 = 60
- **Distractor:** 480 ÷ 8 = 60 (coincidentally same here), but with non-exact fits: 10÷3 = 3, 9÷3 = 3, 7÷3 = 2, answer = 18, NOT 630÷27 = 23.3 rounded to 23
- **Why it works:** Children divide total volumes and round, missing the dimensional constraint.

### 6. Squared Instead of Cubed (for cubes)
- **Correct:** 5³ = 125 cm³
- **Distractor:** 5² = 25 (squares instead of cubes)
- **Why it works:** Children who know "squared" but haven't internalised "cubed" pick this.

### 7. Partial Calculation in Multi-Step Problems
- **Distractor** is the answer to step 1 (e.g., the volume in cm³ when the question asks for litres)
- **Why it works:** Children who do the calculation but forget the final conversion step.

### 8. Wrong Operation for Missing Dimension
- **Correct:** V ÷ (L x W) = H → 120 ÷ (10 x 4) = 3
- **Distractor:** 120 ÷ 10 = 12, or 120 - 10 - 4 = 106
- **Why it works:** Children unsure of inverse operations guess subtraction or incomplete division.

## Common GL Traps

1. **Area vs Volume confusion:** Question asks for volume but a face area is among the options. Children who multiply only two dimensions fall for this.

2. **Forgetting hidden cubes:** In 3D counting diagrams, cubes behind or beneath visible cubes are easy to miss. GL places "visible count only" as a distractor.

3. **cm³ vs litres confusion:** Forgetting that 1 litre = 1000 cm³ (not 100 or 10). The conversion 1 cm³ = 1 ml is the key bridge.

4. **Inconsistent units:** One dimension given in metres, others in centimetres. Children who don't convert first get an answer that's wildly wrong (often available as a distractor).

5. **Cube edge from volume:** "A cube has volume 64 cm³. What is the edge length?" Requires understanding cube roots (4 x 4 x 4 = 64). Children who try 64 ÷ 3 ≈ 21.3 or √64 = 8 pick wrong answers.

6. **"Twice as long":** Doubling one dimension doubles the volume — it does NOT multiply by 8 (which is what happens when all three dimensions double). GL tests whether children understand this relationship.

7. **Packing with remainders:** "How many 3 cm cubes fit in a 10 cm x 10 cm x 10 cm box?" Answer is 3 x 3 x 3 = 27, NOT 1000 ÷ 27 ≈ 37. The leftover space in each dimension cannot be used.

8. **Fraction of capacity:** "A tank is 2/5 full with 40 litres. What is the full capacity?" Children who multiply (40 x 2/5 = 16) instead of dividing (40 ÷ 2/5 = 100) get the wrong answer.

9. **Reading dimensions from a diagram vs text:** GL may show a diagram with dimensions labelled but provide additional information in the text. Children who use only the diagram (or only the text) may miss a dimension.

10. **"Volume" vs "capacity":** Volume is the space an object occupies; capacity is the amount a container can hold. At 11+ level these are treated as equivalent for cuboid containers, but the language shift can confuse children who haven't seen "capacity" used this way.

## Key Formulae Reference

| Formula | Expression | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Volume of a cuboid | V = length x width x height | All three dimensions must be in the same unit |
| Volume of a cube | V = a³ (= a x a x a) | a = edge length; all edges equal |
| Missing dimension | h = V ÷ (l x w) | Rearrange formula to isolate unknown |
| Capacity conversion | 1 cm³ = 1 ml | The fundamental bridge between volume and capacity |
| Capacity conversion | 1000 cm³ = 1 litre | Equivalent to: 1 litre = 1000 ml |
| Capacity conversion | 1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³ = 1000 litres | For larger real-world problems |
| Compound shape | V_total = V₁ + V₂ | Split into cuboids, calculate each, add |
| Compound shape (subtraction) | V = V_full - V_removed | Calculate full cuboid minus the cut-out |
| Packing cubes | N = (L÷a) x (W÷a) x (H÷a) | Floor each division — remainders don't fit |

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand these terms to access volume questions:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Volume** | The amount of 3D space inside a shape, measured in cubic units |
| **Capacity** | The amount a container can hold (used for liquids) |
| **Cubic centimetre (cm³)** | The volume of a 1 cm x 1 cm x 1 cm cube |
| **Cubic metre (m³)** | The volume of a 1 m x 1 m x 1 m cube |
| **Millilitre (ml)** | 1 ml = 1 cm³ |
| **Litre (l)** | 1 litre = 1000 ml = 1000 cm³ |
| **Cuboid** | A 3D shape with 6 rectangular faces (a box shape) |
| **Cube** | A special cuboid where all 6 faces are squares (all edges equal) |
| **Edge** | The line where two faces of a 3D shape meet |
| **Face** | A flat surface of a 3D shape |
| **Dimension** | A measurable extent — length, width, or height |
| **Length / Width / Height** | The three dimensions of a cuboid (interchangeable names depending on orientation) |
| **Depth** | Often used instead of "height" for containers holding liquid |
| **Net** | A 2D shape that folds into a 3D shape |
| **Cross-section** | The 2D shape you see when you cut through a 3D shape |
| **Compound shape** | A shape made by joining two or more simple shapes |
| **Unit cube** | A cube with edges of 1 unit (1 cm, 1 m, etc.) |
| **Estimate** | A sensible guess, often using rounding |

## National Curriculum Alignment

GL Assessment explicitly tests KS2 content. Volume spans Year 5 and Year 6:

### Year 5 Statutory Requirements
- **Estimate volume** (e.g., using 1 cm³ blocks to build cuboids including cubes) and **capacity** (e.g., using water)
- **Convert between units of metric measure** (e.g., kilometre and metre; centimetre and metre; centimetre and millimetre; gram and kilogram; litre and millilitre)
- **Identify 3-D shapes, including cubes and other cuboids, from 2-D representations**

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements
- **Calculate, estimate and compare volume of cubes and cuboids** using standard units, including cubic centimetres (cm³) and cubic metres (m³), and extending to other units (e.g., mm³ and km³)
- **Use, read, write and convert between standard units**, converting measurements of length, mass, volume and time from a smaller unit of measure to a larger unit, and vice versa, using decimal notation to up to three decimal places
- **Recognise, describe and build simple 3-D shapes, including making nets**
- Use all four operations to solve problems involving measure (including volume) using decimal notation

### What GL Adds Beyond Curriculum
GL 11+ questions go slightly beyond standard KS2 expectations:
- **Packing problems** (how many small cubes fit inside a larger cuboid) — tested in KS2 SATs but more complex at 11+
- **Multi-step capacity conversions** (cm³ → litres in real-world contexts) — Year 6 curriculum mentions conversion but GL embeds it in multi-step word problems
- **Compound 3D shapes** — KS2 covers this lightly; GL occasionally tests it at D3
- **Algebraic reverse problems** — not in KS2 measurement objectives but aligns with Year 6 algebra objectives
- **Cube roots by recognition** — "Volume = 125 cm³, find edge length" requires knowing 5³ = 125

## Teaching Notes

### Foundational Skills to Secure First
Before tackling 11+ volume questions, children must be fluent in:
1. **Times tables through 12 x 12** — multiplication errors are the #1 cause of wrong answers in volume
2. **Long multiplication** — for products exceeding 144 (e.g., 12 x 8 x 15)
3. **Division** — essential for missing dimension problems
4. **Place value** — understanding what happens when you multiply by 10, 100, 1000 (crucial for unit conversion)

### Building Conceptual Understanding
- Start with **physical cubes** (multilink cubes, sugar cubes) — build cuboids, count cubes, establish that volume = layers x cubes per layer
- Distinguish clearly between **1D (perimeter/edge), 2D (area/face), and 3D (volume/space inside)**
- Use the phrase "volume is the space inside a 3D shape" repeatedly until automatic
- Demonstrate that **the same volume can look different** — a 2x3x4 cuboid and a 1x6x4 cuboid both have volume 24 cm³

### Common Misconceptions to Address
1. **Confusing area and volume:** Children who've just learned area (L x W) try to apply it to 3D shapes. Explicitly teach: "Area = 2 numbers multiplied. Volume = 3 numbers multiplied."
2. **Forgetting hidden cubes:** When counting cubes in a diagram, children count only what they can see. Teach systematic counting by layers: "How many in the bottom layer? How many layers?"
3. **Units confusion (cm² vs cm³):** Reinforce that area uses "squared" (cm²) because it's 2D, volume uses "cubed" (cm³) because it's 3D. The superscript number matches the number of dimensions.
4. **Capacity ≠ volume of the container walls:** Capacity is the space INSIDE, not the total volume of the object including its material.
5. **Cube roots feel alien:** Unlike square roots (which children encounter in area), cube roots are new. Build familiarity with the key cubes: 1³=1, 2³=8, 3³=27, 4³=64, 5³=125, 6³=216, 7³=343, 8³=512, 9³=729, 10³=1000.
6. **Packing vs dividing:** When asked "how many 2 cm cubes fit in a 7 cm x 7 cm x 7 cm box?", children must check per dimension (3 x 3 x 3 = 27), NOT divide volumes (343 ÷ 8 = 42.875 → 42). The gap per dimension is wasted space.

### Key Conversion Facts to Memorise
| Conversion | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------------|
| 1 cm³ = 1 ml | The bridge between solid volume and liquid capacity |
| 1000 ml = 1 litre | Standard metric conversion |
| 1000 cm³ = 1 litre | Combines the above two facts — the single most important volume conversion |
| 1 m³ = 1,000,000 cm³ | Because 100 x 100 x 100 = 1,000,000 |
| 1 m³ = 1000 litres | For real-world pool/tank problems |

### Exam Strategy
- **Read the question twice:** Check whether it asks for cm³, litres, or a dimension (cm). Many marks are lost by giving volume when the question asks for a length.
- **Check units match:** Before multiplying, confirm all dimensions are in the same unit. If one is in metres and others in centimetres, convert first.
- **Estimate first:** For multi-step problems, a rough estimate helps eliminate obviously wrong options. E.g., "8 x 5 x 3 is about 120, so answers of 15 or 1200 are probably wrong."
- **Use the answer options:** In multiple choice, if you get 72 and the options are 24, 36, 72, 120, 360 — you can be confident. If your answer isn't there, recheck your working.
- **For packing problems:** Always divide each dimension separately, round DOWN each one, then multiply the results. Never divide total volumes.

### Progression Through Difficulty
1. **Start with D1:** Pure formula practice with friendly numbers until automatic (under 15 seconds per question)
2. **Move to D2:** Missing dimension problems, then word problems with extraction, then capacity conversion
3. **Finish with D3:** Compound shapes, packing with remainders, multi-step conversion chains, algebraic reverse

### Links to Other Topics
Volume questions often overlap with:
- **Decimals:** Dimensions given as decimals (e.g., 2.5 cm x 4 cm x 6 cm)
- **Fractions:** "The tank is 3/4 full" — need fraction operations
- **Ratio:** "Box A has twice the volume of Box B"
- **Algebra:** Missing dimension expressed as a variable
- **Unit conversion:** cm ↔ m, ml ↔ litres — overlaps with Place Value
- **Multiplication & division:** Core arithmetic fluency underpins everything
- **Area & perimeter:** Same shapes, different measures — must not confuse
