## What this covers

Annual prepay is the practice of offering a subscription at a discounted rate in exchange for a full year's payment upfront. David Sacks, co-founder of Craft Ventures and a recurring commentator on SaaS capital efficiency, has written extensively on this mechanic as a tool for improving cash flow, reducing churn, and compressing CAC payback periods. This file covers the canonical discount band, the working capital mechanism, and the three-part playbook Sacks associates with making annual prepay work.

## The pattern / mechanic

**The discount band:**

The industry-standard range for annual prepay discounts is 15–20% off the equivalent monthly rate. Sacks and most SaaS practitioners treat this as a floor and ceiling respectively. Below 15%, the incentive is too weak to drive meaningful behaviour change. Above 20%, you are giving away future revenue unnecessarily.

Heap's mathematical analysis (based on churn modelling) confirms the economics: at 3% monthly churn, the maximum rational prepay discount is approximately 15%. At higher churn rates, the discount ceiling rises because the risk of losing the customer mid-year is higher. The formula: maximum discount = (annual committed revenue) minus (expected revenue accounting for monthly churn across 12 months).

**Real-world examples — UK consumer subscription:**

| Product | Monthly price | Annual equivalent | Effective discount |
|---|---|---|---|
| Atom Learning Exam Prep | £59.99/mo | £47.99/mo (£575.90/yr) | ~20% |
| Atom Learning Exam Prep Plus | £69.99/mo | £55.99/mo (£671.90/yr) | ~20% |
| Atom Learning EMS | £39.99/mo | £31.99/mo (£383.90/yr) | ~20% |
| Doodle Family Bundle | £12.99/mo | £119.99/yr (= £10.00/mo) | ~23% |
| Spotify Premium Individual | £11.99/mo | ~£9.99/mo equivalent | ~17% |

Atom Learning sits squarely in the 20% band. Doodle runs slightly above it on the family bundle (23%), suggesting they weight cash flow over margin optimisation at that price point.

**The working capital effect:**

When a customer pays annually upfront, the business collects cash before it has delivered the majority of the service. Under accounting standards, this creates deferred revenue (a liability) but a real cash asset. For a startup without a credit facility, this is the closest equivalent to interest-free debt: the customer has effectively lent you money at 0% to fund your growth. Sacks frames this explicitly — annual prepay is not just a retention tool; it is a funding mechanism.

At scale, if 50% of your subscriber base is on annual plans, you carry roughly six months of forward revenue on your balance sheet at any given time. This materially changes your burn multiple and can enable a company to grow without external capital.

**Sacks' three-move playbook:**

The playbook is documented across his Craft Ventures writing and SaaStr appearances:

1. **Shorten time-to-value** — customers will only commit to a year if they believe they'll get value. Guided onboarding that delivers a clear win within the first session removes the hesitation to prepay.
2. **Qualify out faster** — stop trying to convert unconvinced prospects. Annual prepay only works on customers who already believe; wasting sales effort on fence-sitters increases payback period.
3. **Push annual prepay** — actively price, frame, and default to the annual option. Do not bury it. Sacks notes that the monthly plan should feel like a conscious downgrade, not the natural default.

**CAC payback compression:**

A company with a 12-month CAC payback period on monthly billing can compress that to 1–2 months by converting customers to annual prepay. The cash arrives in month one; the acquisition cost is already spent. This is the mechanism Sacks describes as making annual prepay "one of the highest-leverage moves in SaaS."

## Applicability to UK 11+ app

An 11+ app has a natural annual anchor: the exam itself. Year groups are fixed — a child preparing for September 2027 entry has a defined prep horizon of 12–18 months. Framing the annual plan around "full prep covered until your exam" makes the commitment feel rational rather than open-ended, which removes the primary psychological barrier to prepay. A 20% discount (matching Atom Learning's structure) at a £9.99/month monthly price would price the annual plan at approximately £95.90/year — a one-payment commitment that the research suggests is more retentive than monthly billing.
