# GL Assessment Research: Prime Numbers, Factors, and Multiples

> **Purpose:** Deep reference for auditing, writing, and improving 11+ questions on this topic.
> **Sources:** GL practice papers, Atom Learning, Exam Happy, 11PluseHelp, PiAcademy, Third Space Learning, NRICH, Oak National Academy, KS2 SATs materials, National Curriculum statutory guidance.
> **Last updated:** March 2026

---

## Format

- **Paper-based, multiple-choice** — 5 options per question (A-E)
- **50 questions in 50 minutes** (1 minute per question average)
- **Non-adaptive** — all children sit the same paper; difficulty is fixed, not tailored
- **Answer booklet** — children shade answers on a separate sheet, marked electronically
- **Non-calculator** — all mental or written methods
- **Number questions dominate** — roughly 5x more number questions than any other category (geometry, measurement, data, algebra), meaning ~30+ of the 50 questions test number skills
- **Difficulty progression** — easier questions at the start, harder towards the end
- Questions on prime numbers, factors, and multiples typically appear as 2-4 questions per paper, scattered across the difficulty range

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

GL draws from the KS2 National Curriculum (Years 5-6) but pushes beyond it at the top end. The topic broadly splits into these skill areas:

| Skill Area | Approx. Weighting | What GL Tests |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Identifying primes** | ~15% | Recognise primes up to 100; recall primes to 19; understand that 1 is not prime; know 2 is the only even prime |
| **Finding factors** | ~20% | List all factor pairs of a number; find how many factors a number has; systematic factor-finding |
| **Common factors & HCF** | ~15% | Find common factors of two numbers; identify the highest common factor; apply to word problems (sharing/dividing equally) |
| **Multiples** | ~15% | Identify multiples of given numbers; understand multiples as "times tables extended"; distinguish multiples from factors |
| **Common multiples & LCM** | ~10% | Find common multiples of two numbers; identify the lowest common multiple; apply to scheduling/timing word problems |
| **Prime factorisation** | ~10% | Break a number into its prime factors using factor trees; express as a product of primes (e.g. 72 = 2^3 x 3^2) |
| **Square & cube numbers** | ~10% | Recognise perfect squares (1-144) and cubes (1-1000); use squared/cubed notation; relate to factors |
| **Divisibility rules** | ~5% | Apply rules for 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 9, 10 to quickly test whether numbers are factors/multiples |

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**What it looks like:**
- Direct recall or recognition — no multi-step reasoning
- Numbers are small (under 50) and familiar
- The question tests a single concept in isolation

**Typical questions:**
- "Which of these is a prime number?" with options like 12, 15, 17, 21, 25
- "What is the smallest prime number?" (testing the 1-is-not-prime and 2-is-prime knowledge)
- "How many factors does 12 have?"
- "Which of these is a multiple of 6?" with straightforward options
- "What is 5 squared?"
- "Which number is a factor of both 10 and 15?"

**Distractor profile:**
- Wrong answers are composite numbers that children commonly confuse with primes (e.g. 9, 15, 21, 27, 51)
- For factor questions, distractors include numbers that are close to correct but not actual factors
- One distractor is typically a "near miss" (e.g. including 1 as a prime, or 51 as a prime)

**What a child needs to know:**
- Definition of prime (exactly two factors: 1 and itself)
- That 1 is NOT prime and 2 IS prime
- Times tables up to 12x12 fluently
- How to list factors in pairs

---

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**What it looks like:**
- Two-step reasoning or applying a concept in context
- Numbers are larger (50-100) or the question combines two ideas
- Word problems with a real-life scenario requiring factor/multiple knowledge

**Typical questions:**
- "Emma wants to arrange 18 chairs in equal rows with no chairs left over. Which of these is NOT a possible number of rows?" (factors in context)
- "What is the highest common factor of 24 and 36?"
- "Which number is both a factor of 30 and a factor of 45?" (common factors)
- "A number is a multiple of both 4 and 6. Which of these could it be?" (common multiples)
- "Which number between 50 and 60 is prime?" (testing primes in a higher range)
- "What is 7 cubed?"
- "How many prime numbers are there between 20 and 40?"

**Distractor profile:**
- For HCF questions, distractors include other common factors (not the highest) and factors of only one number
- For "which is prime" questions with larger numbers, distractors include odd composites that look prime (51, 57, 87, 91)
- For common multiples, distractors include multiples of only one number
- One distractor is the result of a common calculation error (e.g. confusing HCF with LCM)

**What a child needs to know:**
- Efficient factor-finding (pair method: 1 x n, 2 x ?, 3 x ?, etc.)
- How to find common factors by comparing lists
- How to spot primes up to 100 using divisibility checks
- Square numbers up to 12^2 = 144
- Cube numbers: 1, 8, 27, 64, 125

---

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**What it looks like:**
- Multi-step reasoning, often combining prime/factor/multiple knowledge with another concept
- Requires deduction, not just recall
- Abstract or unusual phrasing
- Numbers may be larger (100+) or the question involves algebraic thinking

**Typical questions:**
- "A number has exactly 3 factors. What could it be?" (must know: only squares of primes have exactly 3 factors — 4, 9, 25, 49)
- "What is the LCM of 12 and 18?" (requires systematic method, not guessing)
- "The prime factorisation of a number is 2^2 x 3 x 5. What is the number?" (working backwards)
- "Two buses leave the station at 9:00 am. Bus A runs every 8 minutes, Bus B every 12 minutes. When will they next leave together?" (LCM in context)
- "What is the smallest number that is a multiple of both 15 and 20?"
- "A number between 80 and 100 has exactly 2 factors. What is it?" (prime in a range — 83, 89, 97)
- "The sum of two prime numbers is 50. One of them is 3. What is the other?" (requires knowing 47 is prime)
- "Tom says all odd numbers are prime. Give an example to show he is wrong." (counter-example/proof)
- Venn diagram questions: sets defined as "multiples of 3" and "multiples of 4" within a universal set, asking what goes in the intersection

**Distractor profile:**
- Exploit the HCF/LCM confusion (offering the HCF when LCM is asked, and vice versa)
- Include numbers that satisfy some but not all conditions
- For prime factorisation, offer products with one non-prime factor included
- For "exactly n factors" questions, include numbers with n-1 or n+1 factors

**What a child needs to know:**
- Prime factorisation using factor trees
- LCM by listing multiples or using prime factorisation
- HCF by listing factors or using prime factorisation
- Relationship between squares of primes and numbers with exactly 3 factors
- How to verify large numbers are prime (check divisibility by 2, 3, 5, 7)
- Connecting abstract number properties to real-world contexts

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Identify/Select
> "Which of these numbers is prime / a factor of X / a multiple of X?"

The most common format. Child selects from 5 options. Tests recognition and recall.

**Variations:**
- Which is prime?
- Which is NOT prime?
- Which is a factor of 36?
- Which is NOT a factor of 48?
- Which is a common factor of 24 and 36?
- Which is a multiple of both 6 and 8?

### Type 2: Count/List
> "How many factors does 24 have?" / "How many prime numbers are between 20 and 50?"

Tests systematic enumeration. Easy to make errors by missing factor pairs or miscounting.

### Type 3: Word Problem (Real-Life Context)
> "Jake has 24 sweets to share equally into bags. How many different numbers of bags could he use?"
> "Buses leave every 12 minutes and trains every 8 minutes. Both leave at 10:00. When do they next leave together?"

GL loves embedding factor/multiple concepts into scenarios. Common contexts:
- **Sharing equally** (factors) — sweets, chairs, tiles, stickers
- **Arranging in rows/groups** (factors) — sports teams, chairs, classroom arrangements
- **Scheduling/timing** (LCM) — buses, flashing lights, running laps
- **Buying in packs** (LCM) — hotdogs and buns, pens and pencils in different pack sizes

### Type 4: True/False/Proof
> "Tom says all odd numbers are prime. Is he correct? Explain why."
> "Priya says 91 is prime. Is she correct?"

Tests deeper understanding. The child must construct a counter-example or verify a claim.

### Type 5: Factor Trees / Prime Factorisation
> "Complete the factor tree for 60."
> "Express 84 as a product of its prime factors."

Tests the method of breaking numbers into prime building blocks. In multiple-choice format, this becomes "What is the prime factorisation of 72?" with options showing different products.

### Type 6: Properties and Relationships
> "A number has exactly 3 factors. What could it be?"
> "I am thinking of a number. It is a common multiple of 3 and 5. It is less than 40. It is odd. What is the number?"

Tests the ability to combine multiple constraints. Requires systematic thinking.

### Type 7: Venn Diagram
> Numbers sorted into "multiples of 3" and "factors of 24" within a universal set.

Tests set-based thinking. The child must correctly classify numbers that belong to one set, both sets, or neither.

### Type 8: Square and Cube Recognition
> "Which of these is both a square number and a cube number?" (Answer: 64 = 8^2 = 4^3)
> "A square number added to a cube number equals 35. What are the two numbers?" (Answer: 8 + 27 = 35)

Tests knowledge of perfect squares and cubes and ability to combine them.

## Distractor Design

GL distractors are never random. Each wrong answer targets a specific mistake or misconception:

### Prime Number Questions
| Distractor Type | Example | What Mistake It Targets |
|----------------|---------|------------------------|
| Odd composite | 9, 15, 21, 27, 33, 39, 49, 51, 57, 63, 87, 91 | "All odd numbers are prime" |
| Number 1 | 1 as an option | "1 is the smallest prime" |
| Even composite | 4, 6, 8, 14 | Confusion about definitions |
| Near-miss composite | 51 (3x17), 57 (3x19), 91 (7x13) | Numbers that "look prime" because they're odd and not in times tables |

### Factor Questions
| Distractor Type | Example | What Mistake It Targets |
|----------------|---------|------------------------|
| Multiple instead of factor | For "factor of 12": offering 24, 36 | Confusing factors with multiples |
| Near-miss | For "factor of 24": offering 5, 7 | Not checking division properly |
| Factor of only one number | For "common factor of 12 and 18": offering 4 (factor of 12 only) | Not checking both numbers |
| Counting errors | For "how many factors": off by 1 or 2 | Missing 1 and/or the number itself, or missing a factor pair |

### HCF/LCM Questions
| Distractor Type | Example | What Mistake It Targets |
|----------------|---------|------------------------|
| HCF when LCM asked (or vice versa) | HCF(12,18)=6 vs LCM(12,18)=36 | The most common confusion |
| Product of the two numbers | For LCM(12,18): offering 216 (12x18) | Just multiplying the numbers together |
| A common factor/multiple, but not the H/L | For HCF(12,18): offering 3 (a common factor, but not the highest) | Stopping too early |

### Square/Cube Questions
| Distractor Type | Example | What Mistake It Targets |
|----------------|---------|------------------------|
| Doubled instead of squared | For 7^2: offering 14 instead of 49 | Confusing squaring with doubling |
| Tripled instead of cubed | For 4^3: offering 12 instead of 64 | Confusing cubing with tripling |
| Adjacent square/cube | For "which is a square number": offering 15, 26, 50 | Numbers near perfect squares |

## Common GL Traps

1. **"Is 1 prime?"** — GL loves testing this. 1 is NOT prime (it has only one factor, not two). Many children include 1 when listing primes.

2. **"Is 2 prime?"** — 2 IS prime and is the ONLY even prime. Children who think "all primes are odd" will miss this.

3. **"All odd numbers are prime"** — The classic trap. Counter-examples: 9 (3x3), 15 (3x5), 21 (3x7), 25 (5x5), 27 (3x9), 33 (3x11), 35 (5x7), 39 (3x13), 49 (7x7), 51 (3x17).

4. **51 and 91 look prime but aren't** — 51 = 3 x 17. 91 = 7 x 13. These are the two most commonly mistaken composites. GL uses them deliberately.

5. **Confusing factors and multiples** — Factors go INTO a number (they're smaller or equal). Multiples come OUT of a number (they're larger or equal). Children who mix these up get the wrong answer every time.

6. **Forgetting 1 and the number itself as factors** — When listing factors of 24, children sometimes forget to count 1 and 24. This leads to undercounting.

7. **Forgetting factor pairs** — Children often list factors sequentially (1, 2, 3, 4...) and miss one. The pair method (1x24, 2x12, 3x8, 4x6) is more reliable.

8. **HCF/LCM swap** — Children who learn both at the same time often confuse which is which. "Highest Common Factor" = biggest shared factor. "Lowest Common Multiple" = smallest shared multiple.

9. **Assuming LCM = product** — LCM(6, 8) is NOT 48. It's 24. Children who just multiply the two numbers together will always get the wrong answer when the numbers share a common factor.

10. **Squared vs doubled** — 5^2 = 25, not 10. Children who read "squared" as "times 2" will pick the doubling distractor.

11. **Cubed vs tripled** — 4^3 = 64, not 12. Same misconception as above but for cubes.

12. **Not checking all primes up to the square root** — To verify 83 is prime, you only need to check 2, 3, 5, 7 (since 9^2 = 81 < 83 < 100 = 10^2). Children who don't know this shortcut waste time or give up.

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand and use these terms fluently:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Factor** | A whole number that divides exactly into another number | 6 is a factor of 24 (24 / 6 = 4) |
| **Factor pair** | Two factors that multiply to give the number | (3, 8) is a factor pair of 24 |
| **Multiple** | The product of a number and any positive integer | 15 is a multiple of 5 (5 x 3 = 15) |
| **Common factor** | A factor shared by two or more numbers | 4 is a common factor of 12 and 20 |
| **Highest Common Factor (HCF)** | The largest factor shared by two or more numbers | HCF of 12 and 18 is 6 |
| **Common multiple** | A multiple shared by two or more numbers | 24 is a common multiple of 6 and 8 |
| **Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)** | The smallest multiple shared by two or more numbers | LCM of 6 and 8 is 24 |
| **Prime number** | A number with exactly two factors: 1 and itself | 13 is prime (factors: 1 and 13) |
| **Composite number** | A number with more than two factors | 15 is composite (factors: 1, 3, 5, 15) |
| **Prime factor** | A factor that is also a prime number | The prime factors of 30 are 2, 3, and 5 |
| **Prime factorisation** | Expressing a number as a product of its prime factors | 72 = 2^3 x 3^2 |
| **Factor tree** | A diagram showing how a number breaks down into prime factors | Used to find the prime factorisation |
| **Square number** | A number made by multiplying an integer by itself | 49 = 7 x 7 = 7^2 |
| **Cube number** | A number made by multiplying an integer by itself three times | 27 = 3 x 3 x 3 = 3^3 |
| **Divisible** | Can be divided exactly with no remainder | 36 is divisible by 9 |
| **Product** | The result of multiplying numbers together | The product of 4 and 7 is 28 |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 — Statutory Requirements
From the National Curriculum for England (Mathematics):

- **5C5a:** Identify multiples and factors, including finding all factor pairs of a number, and common factors of two numbers
- **5C5b:** Know and use the vocabulary of prime numbers, prime factors and composite (non-prime) numbers
- **5C5c:** Establish whether a number up to 100 is prime and recall prime numbers up to 19
- **5C6:** Recognise and use square numbers and cube numbers, and the notation for squared (^2) and cubed (^3)
- **5C7:** Solve problems involving multiplication and division, including using their knowledge of factors and multiples, squares and cubes

**Non-statutory guidance:** Pupils use and understand the terms factor, multiple and prime, square and cube numbers. They use them to construct equivalence statements.

### Year 6 — Statutory Requirements

- **6C5:** Identify common factors, common multiples and prime numbers
- **6C7:** Solve problems involving addition, subtraction, multiplication and division

**Non-statutory guidance:** Common factors can be related to finding equivalent fractions.

### What GL Adds Beyond the Curriculum
The National Curriculum requires knowledge up to "whether a number up to 100 is prime" and "common factors of two numbers." GL pushes further:
- HCF and LCM are not explicitly named in the KS2 curriculum but appear in GL papers
- Prime factorisation as a formal method (factor trees) goes slightly beyond KS2 statutory requirements
- Word problems applying LCM to real-life scheduling scenarios require higher-order application
- Questions combining multiple concepts (e.g. "a number with exactly 3 factors") require deductive reasoning beyond simple recall

This means a child who has only covered the statutory curriculum may struggle with 2-3 questions per paper. Targeted preparation on HCF, LCM, and prime factorisation is essential.

## Teaching Notes

### Critical Foundations (Must Be Secure Before This Topic)
- **Times tables to 12x12** — without fluent recall, factor-finding is painfully slow
- **Division facts** — understanding that "6 is a factor of 24" means 24 / 6 = 4 exactly
- **Even/odd recognition** — essential for divisibility rules and prime checking

### Common Misconceptions to Address Early
1. **"1 is prime"** — Drill this. 1 has only ONE factor (itself). Primes have exactly TWO.
2. **"All odd numbers are prime"** — Use 9, 15, 21, 25 as counter-examples immediately.
3. **"Factors are always smaller"** — Actually, a number IS a factor of itself. 24 is a factor of 24.
4. **Confusing factors and multiples** — Use physical metaphor: factors FIT INSIDE the number, multiples GROW OUT from it.
5. **"Squared means times 2"** — Show the area model: 5^2 means a 5x5 square = 25 squares, not 5+5.

### Efficient Methods to Teach
1. **Factor pairs method:** Start from 1, work up. 1x24, 2x12, 3x8, 4x6. Stop when pairs start to repeat.
2. **Prime checking shortcut:** Only test prime divisors up to the square root. For any number under 100, checking 2, 3, 5, 7 is sufficient.
3. **Factor trees for prime factorisation:** Start with any factor pair, keep splitting until all branches end in primes.
4. **LCM by listing:** List multiples of each number until you find a match. For small numbers this is faster than prime factorisation.
5. **HCF by listing:** List all factors of both numbers, find the largest in common.
6. **Divisibility rules as a shortcut:** Teach the rules for 2, 3, 5, 10 first (most useful). Add 4, 6, 9 later.

### Time Management Tips for the Exam
- Prime/factor questions should take 30-60 seconds each
- If a factor question involves a large number, use divisibility rules rather than trial division
- For HCF/LCM, listing method is usually faster than factor trees for numbers under 100
- For "is X prime?" questions, check 2, 3, 5, 7 in order — if none divide, it's prime (for numbers up to 120)
- If stuck on a multi-step question, eliminate obviously wrong answers first

### What Makes This Topic "Score Well" on GL
- It's one of the more predictable topics — question formats repeat across papers
- A child who can reliably identify primes to 100, list factors in pairs, and find HCF/LCM will pick up 2-4 marks per paper
- The hardest questions (D3) reward children who understand WHY number properties work, not just HOW to apply them
- Speed matters: a child who hesitates over whether 51 is prime loses 30+ seconds that could go elsewhere

### Recommended Practice Progression
1. **Week 1:** Prime number recognition (identify, count, list primes to 50, then to 100)
2. **Week 2:** Factors (list all factors, factor pairs, "how many factors" questions)
3. **Week 3:** Multiples, common factors, common multiples
4. **Week 4:** HCF and LCM (both by listing and by prime factorisation)
5. **Week 5:** Square and cube numbers (recognition, notation, combined problems)
6. **Week 6:** Mixed questions and word problems combining all concepts
7. **Ongoing:** Timed practice papers to build speed and accuracy under pressure
