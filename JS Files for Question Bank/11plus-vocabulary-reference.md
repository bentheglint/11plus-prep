## 12. Literary/Figurative Language Terms

### Key Terms for 11+ English

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Alliteration | Repetition of the same consonant sound at the start of nearby words | "Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers" |
| Assonance | Repetition of vowel sounds within nearby words | "The rain in Spain falls mainly on the plain" |
| Cliche | An overused expression that has lost its impact | "At the end of the day" |
| Consonance | Repetition of consonant sounds within or at the end of words | "Mike likes his bike" |
| Emotive language | Words chosen to create an emotional response | "The innocent child was abandoned" |
| Euphemism | A milder or less direct word used in place of a harsh one | "Passed away" instead of "died" |
| Foreshadowing | Hints or clues about what will happen later in a story | Dark clouds before a disaster in a story |
| Hyperbole | Deliberate exaggeration for effect | "I've told you a million times!" |
| Idiom | A phrase whose meaning is different from the literal words | "It's raining cats and dogs" |
| Imagery | Descriptive language that appeals to the senses | "The golden sun melted into the crimson horizon" |
| Irony | When words or events convey the opposite of expectation | A fire station burning down |
| Metaphor | Comparing two things directly by saying one IS the other | "The classroom was a zoo" |
| Narrator | The person telling the story | First person (I), third person (he/she) |
| Onomatopoeia | A word that imitates the sound it describes | "Buzz, crash, sizzle, pop, hiss, bang, whoosh" |
| Oxymoron | Two contradictory words placed together | "Deafening silence", "bittersweet" |
| Pathetic fallacy | Using weather or nature to reflect mood or emotions | A storm during an argument scene |
| Personification | Giving human qualities to non-human things | "The wind whispered through the trees" |
| Pun | A play on words with double meaning | "I'm reading a book about gravity - I can't put it down!" |
| Rhetorical question | A question asked for effect, not expecting an answer | "Who wouldn't want to be happy?" |
| Rhyme | Words that have the same ending sound | "cat" and "hat", "moon" and "June" |
| Rhythm | A regular pattern of beats or stressed syllables | The beat in poetry |
| Sarcasm | Using irony to mock or convey contempt | Saying "Oh great!" when something goes wrong |
| Sibilance | Repetition of 's' sounds | "The snake slithered silently" |
| Simile | Comparing two things using "like" or "as" | "As brave as a lion", "She ran like the wind" |
| Symbolism | Using an object or action to represent something deeper | A dove representing peace |
| Tone | The writer's attitude towards the subject | Formal, informal, serious, humorous, sarcastic |

### Poetry-Specific Terms

| Free verse | Poetry that does not follow a regular rhyme scheme or rhythm |
| Refrain | A repeated line or group of lines in a poem |
| Stanza | A group of lines in a poem (like a paragraph) |
| Verse | A single line of poetry, or a stanza |
