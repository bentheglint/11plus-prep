# GL Assessment Research: Algebra

**Compiled:** March 2026
**Purpose:** Master reference for building and auditing 11+ algebra questions. Covers GL format, skills tested, difficulty criteria, distractor design, traps, and national curriculum alignment.

---

## Format

GL Assessment tests algebra as part of the 50-question, 50-minute maths paper. All questions are multiple-choice with 5 options (A-E). Algebra is one of five topic strands (Number, Measurement, Data, Algebra, Geometry), but Number questions dominate — there are typically five times more Number questions than any other single strand.

**Estimated algebra weighting:** Based on practice paper analysis and tutor resources, algebra accounts for approximately 3-6 questions per paper (6-12% of the 50 questions). This is consistent with KS2 SATs data showing algebra makes up 4-9% of the national assessment. However, many "Number" questions involve algebraic thinking (sequences, missing numbers, function machines) without being categorised as pure algebra, so the actual algebraic reasoning demand is higher than the raw count suggests.

**Pacing:** At 1 minute per question, algebra questions must be solvable quickly. GL designs them so that a child who understands the concept can reach the answer in 30-60 seconds. Multi-step algebra questions at D3 may take up to 90 seconds.

**Question presentation:** Questions appear as either:
- A direct algebraic statement ("If 3n + 5 = 20, what is the value of n?")
- A word problem requiring translation into algebra ("I think of a number, double it, and add 7. The answer is 23. What was my number?")
- A visual/diagrammatic format (function machines, shape equations, sequences)

| Skill | Approximate GL Weight | Description |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|
| Substitution into expressions | ~20% | Given values for variables, calculate the result |
| Solving one-step equations | ~20% | Find the unknown in equations like 4x = 28 or x + 15 = 42 |
| Solving two-step equations | ~15% | Find the unknown in equations like 3x + 5 = 20 |
| Function/number machines | ~15% | Follow or reverse a chain of operations |
| Linear sequences (find next term) | ~10% | Continue a sequence by identifying the rule |

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

Easy algebra questions have these characteristics:
- **Single operation:** Only one step needed (e.g. x + 8 = 15, or 5x = 30)
- **Small, friendly numbers:** Single-digit or simple multiples (values up to 20-30)
- **No negative numbers:** All values positive
- **Direct substitution with one variable:** "If a = 4, what is 3a?" (answer: 12)
- **Obvious sequences:** Common patterns like adding 3, adding 5, or times tables sequences
- **Function machines with one operation:** Input 7, multiply by 3, output = ?
- **No brackets or order-of-operations complexity**
- The unknown is isolated or trivially isolable
- "Think of a number" problems with a single operation

**Example D1 questions:**
- "If x + 9 = 17, what is x?" (A: 6, B: 7, C: 8, D: 9, E: 26)
- "What is the value of 5a when a = 6?" (A: 11, B: 25, C: 30, D: 35, E: 56)
- "What are the next two numbers in this sequence? 4, 9, 14, 19, __, __" (A: 23, 27; B: 24, 29; C: 24, 30; D: 25, 30; E: 24, 28)

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

Medium difficulty questions introduce:
- **Two-step equations:** 3x + 5 = 20 or 2x - 7 = 11
- **Substitution with two variables:** "If a = 3 and b = 5, what is 2a + 3b?" (answer: 21)
- **Larger numbers:** Values into the tens or low hundreds
- **Function machines with two operations:** Input -> multiply by 4 -> subtract 3 -> output
- **Reverse function machines:** Given the output, work backwards to find the input
- **Sequences requiring calculation:** Finding the 10th term, or working with less obvious common differences
- **Simple word problems:** "I think of a number, multiply it by 3, then add 8. The answer is 29. What was my number?"
- **BIDMAS awareness needed:** Expressions where order of operations matters
- **Finding a rule from a table of values**

**Example D2 questions:**
- "If 4n - 3 = 25, what is n?" (A: 5, B: 6, C: 7, D: 8, E: 22)
- "A function machine doubles the input then subtracts 5. If the output is 13, what was the input?" (A: 4, B: 8, C: 9, D: 16, E: 18)
- "If p = 4 and q = 7, what is 3p + 2q - 5?" (A: 21, B: 23, C: 26, D: 31, E: 33)

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

Hard questions feature:
- **Multi-step solving:** Three or more operations, or unknowns on both sides
- **Negative numbers in expressions or answers:** "If x = -3, what is 2x + 10?"
- **Substitution into complex expressions:** Expressions with brackets, powers, or multiple terms
- **Forming equations from word problems:** Child must construct the equation themselves, then solve
- **Two unknowns with constraints:** "x + y = 20 and x is three times y. Find x."
- **Sequences with non-obvious rules:** Quadratic-feeling sequences, alternating operations, or rules like "multiply by 2 then subtract 1"
- **Nested function machines:** Multiple machines in series, or finding the missing operation
- **Algebraic reasoning with shapes/perimeters:** "A rectangle has a perimeter of 38 cm. The length is 5 cm more than the width. Find the length."
- **Symbol/picture equations:** Shapes representing unknowns across multiple simultaneous equations
- **Enumerate possibilities with constraints:** Finding all pairs that satisfy two conditions simultaneously

**Example D3 questions:**
- "A rectangle's length is (2x + 3) cm and width is (x - 1) cm. If the perimeter is 34 cm, what is x?" (A: 3, B: 4, C: 5, D: 6, E: 7)
- "If a = -2 and b = 5, what is the value of 3a^2 - 2b?" (A: -22, B: 2, C: 7, D: 22, E: 32)
- "In the sequence 2, 5, 11, 23, 47, ..., what is the rule?" (A: add 3; B: multiply by 2 then add 1; C: multiply by 2 then subtract 1; D: add double the previous difference; E: multiply by 3 then subtract 1)

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Direct Substitution
"If a = 5 and b = 3, what is the value of 4a - 2b?"
- Provide values, ask child to compute
- D1: one variable, one operation (3a when a = 4)
- D2: two variables, mixed operations (2a + 3b when a = 3, b = 5)
- D3: brackets, powers, or negatives (a^2 + 2b when a = -3, b = 4)

### Type 2: Solve the Equation
"What is the value of x in 3x + 7 = 22?"
- One-step at D1 (x + 5 = 12)
- Two-step at D2 (3x + 7 = 22)
- Multi-step or unknowns on both sides at D3 (5x - 3 = 2x + 12)

### Type 3: Function/Number Machines
"A number machine multiplies the input by 3 then adds 4. If the input is 6, what is the output?"
- Forward operation at D1
- Reverse (find the input from output) at D2
- Find the missing operation, or chain of 3+ machines at D3

### Type 4: "Think of a Number" Word Problems
"I think of a number, multiply it by 4, then subtract 9. The answer is 27. What was my number?"
- Single operation at D1
- Two operations at D2
- Three operations or with negative result at D3

### Type 5: Linear Sequences
"What is the next number in the sequence: 7, 12, 17, 22, ...?"
- Constant addition/subtraction at D1 (add 5 each time)
- Find the rule and apply it to find a distant term at D2 (what is the 20th term?)
- Express the rule algebraically at D3 (write the nth term formula: 5n + 2)

### Type 6: Forming Equations from Context
"Sarah is x years old. Her father is 4 times her age minus 3 years. If her father is 37, how old is Sarah?"
- Simple contexts at D2
- Multi-variable or multi-constraint at D3

### Type 7: Shape/Picture Equations
"Triangle + Triangle + Circle = 17. Triangle + Circle = 9. What is Triangle?"
- Two shapes, simple relationship at D2
- Three shapes, three equations at D3

### Type 8: Pairs of Unknowns
"Find two numbers that add up to 24 where one number is twice the other."
- Direct relationship at D2
- Multiple constraints, enumerate all solutions at D3

### Type 9: Using Formulae
"The formula for the area of a triangle is A = 1/2 x b x h. If b = 12 cm and h = 8 cm, what is A?"
- Straightforward substitution into given formula
- May involve rearranging to find a missing dimension at D3

## Distractor Design

GL constructs wrong answer options based on predictable errors children make. Each distractor corresponds to a specific mistake, not random numbers.

### Algebra-Specific Distractor Patterns

| Distractor Type | How It's Generated | Example |
|----------------|-------------------|---------|
| **Operation reversal** | Added instead of subtracted (or vice versa) | For 3x - 5 = 16: distractor uses 3x + 5 = 16, giving x = 3.67 or rounded |
| **BIDMAS error** | Operations done left-to-right ignoring order | For "2 + 3 x 4": distractor = 20 (not 14) |
| **Forgot the second step** | Only completed first operation | For 3x + 5 = 20: child solves 3x = 20, gets x = 6.67, rounds to 7 |
| **Applied operation to wrong side** | Inverse operation error | For x + 7 = 15: distractor x = 22 (added 7 to 15) |
| **Sign error with negatives** | Dropped or flipped a negative sign | For "2(-3) + 10": distractor = 16 (treating -3 as +3) |
| **Coefficient confusion** | Treated 3x as 3 + x instead of 3 x x | For "3x when x = 5": distractor = 8 (3 + 5) |
| **Incomplete substitution** | Substituted for one variable but not another | For "2a + 3b when a=4, b=2": distractor uses only one substitution |
| **Sequence extension error** | Used wrong common difference | For 3, 7, 11, 15, ...: distractor uses +3 instead of +4 |
| **"Off by one" on sequences** | Correct rule but counted position wrong | nth term formula applied with n starting at 0 instead of 1 |
| **The raw number trap** | Answer is a component of the calculation | For 3x + 5 = 20: distractors include 20, 15, and 3 |

### Distractor Placement Strategy
- GL typically places distractors in plausibility order — the most tempting wrong answer often appears near the correct answer in the options list
- At least one distractor is always close to the correct answer (e.g. off by 1)
- At least one distractor is a "gross error" for children who guess or completely misunderstand
- The correct answer position (A-E) is distributed roughly evenly across the paper — no bias toward any position

## Common GL Traps

1. **The "equals means answer" trap:** Questions like 8 + ? = 5 + 7 where children write 13 in the box (adding 8 + 5) instead of finding what makes both sides equal (answer: 4)

2. **The BIDMAS trap:** Expressions where left-to-right calculation gives a different answer from correct order-of-operations. E.g. "3 + 4 x 5" — many children say 35, correct answer is 23

3. **The invisible multiplication trap:** "3a" means "3 times a" but some children read it as "3 and a" (i.e. 3 + a or the two-digit number 3a)

4. **The negative substitution trap:** When substituting a negative number, forgetting brackets. E.g. -3^2: children may calculate as -(3^2) = -9 when the question means (-3)^2 = 9

5. **The reverse function machine trap:** Children apply operations forwards when they should be working backwards, or apply the inverse operations in the wrong order

6. **The "both sides" trap:** In equations like 3x + 5 = 20, children subtract 5 from the right but forget it affects the relationship, or divide only part of an expression by the coefficient

7. **The sequence position trap:** When a sequence rule is "3n + 2", the 1st term is 5 (not 2). Children who start at n=0 get wrong answers

8. **The word problem translation trap:** "5 less than a number" is written as n - 5, not 5 - n. GL often includes both as options

9. **The enumeration completeness trap:** When asked "how many pairs of whole numbers satisfy x + y = 10 where both are positive?", children forget that order matters (or doesn't, depending on context) or miss boundary cases

10. **The formula rearrangement trap:** Given A = l x w and told A = 36, w = 4, children may multiply instead of divide to find l

11. **The "carried-forward error" trap:** In multi-step problems, an arithmetic slip in step 1 produces an answer that appears as one of the distractors — GL builds this deliberately

12. **The coefficient distribution trap:** For 2(x + 3), children calculate 2x + 3 instead of 2x + 6

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand these terms to navigate GL algebra questions:

### Core Terms
| Term | Meaning at 11+ Level |
|------|---------------------|
| **Variable** | A letter representing an unknown or changing number |
| **Expression** | A combination of numbers, variables, and operations (e.g. 3x + 5) — no equals sign |
| **Equation** | A statement that two expressions are equal (e.g. 3x + 5 = 20) |
| **Formula** | A rule written with letters and numbers (e.g. A = l x w) |
| **Unknown** | A specific value we need to find |
| **Term** | A single number or variable, or a number multiplied by a variable (e.g. 3x is a term) |
| **Coefficient** | The number in front of a variable (in 5x, the coefficient is 5) |
| **Substitute** | Replace a letter with a given number |
| **Simplify** | Collect like terms to make an expression shorter |
| **Solve** | Find the value of the unknown that makes the equation true |
| **Inverse operation** | The opposite operation (+ and -, x and /) used to undo steps |
| **Function machine** | A diagram showing input -> operation(s) -> output |
| **Linear sequence** | A sequence where the same amount is added or subtracted each time |
| **Common difference** | The fixed amount added between consecutive terms in a linear sequence |
| **nth term** | A formula that gives any term in a sequence from its position number |

### GL Command Words in Algebra Questions
- "What is the value of..." — substitute and calculate
- "Find the value of x..." — solve the equation
- "What is the next number in..." — continue the sequence
- "Write an expression for..." — form the algebra (rarer in MC format)
- "What is the output when..." — function machine forward
- "What was the input if..." — function machine reverse
- "Which of these is correct?" — identify the right equation/expression

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements (Algebra)

Pupils should be taught to:
1. **Use simple formulae** — substitute values into formulae expressed in words or symbols
2. **Generate and describe linear number sequences** — find terms and express the term-to-term rule
3. **Express missing number problems algebraically** — use letters for unknowns in equations
4. **Find pairs of numbers that satisfy an equation with 2 unknowns** — e.g. if a + b = 12 and a = 2b, find a and b
5. **Enumerate possibilities of combinations of 2 variables** — list all solutions that satisfy given constraints

### Year 6 Non-Statutory Guidance

"Pupils should be introduced to the use of symbols and letters to represent variables and unknowns in mathematical situations that they already understand, such as:
- Missing numbers, lengths, coordinates and angles
- Formulae in mathematics and science
- Equivalent expressions (for example, a + b = b + a)
- Generalisations of number patterns
- Number puzzles (for example, what 2 numbers can add up to...)"

### Earlier Year Group Foundations
- **Year 1-2:** Missing number problems (7 = ? - 9), recognising patterns in objects and shapes
- **Year 3-4:** Extending number patterns, identifying rules in sequences, missing number problems with all four operations
- **Year 5:** Linear sequences with term-to-term rules described in words, more complex missing number problems

### What GL Adds Beyond the Curriculum
GL algebra questions are based firmly on the KS2 curriculum but often push into the harder end:
- Substitution into expressions with multiple variables and operations (curriculum says "simple formulae")
- Two-step and occasionally three-step equations (curriculum focuses on single unknowns)
- Sequence questions that require identifying the nth term formula (curriculum says "describe" the sequence)
- Forming equations from word problems (curriculum says "express missing number problems algebraically")

The 11+ sits at the boundary between Years 5-6 content, with D3 questions sometimes touching early Year 7 expectations.

## Teaching Notes

### What Children Find Hardest

1. **The equals sign misconception:** The most persistent barrier. Many Year 6 children think "=" means "the answer is" rather than "both sides are balanced." Research shows that when presented with 8 + 4 = __ + 5, a significant proportion of Year 6 pupils answer 12 or 17, rather than the correct answer of 7.

2. **Letters as objects, not quantities:** Children may think "a" stands for "apple" rather than "the number of apples." This leads to errors like writing d = 7w for "there are d days in w weeks" (instead of d = 7w being correct because it means d equals 7 times w).

3. **Inverse operations in the wrong order:** When reversing a function machine with "multiply by 3, then add 5", children often subtract 5 first then divide by 3 — which is correct. But many do divide first, then subtract. The order of inverse operations is the reverse of the original order.

4. **Anxiety about algebra:** Research notes that children inherit negative preconceptions about algebra from older siblings or parents. Building confidence with missing-number problems before introducing letter notation helps significantly.

5. **The invisible multiplication sign:** Understanding that 3n means 3 x n, not "thirty-something" or "3 and n." This is a notational convention that needs explicit teaching.

6. **BIDMAS with substitution:** After substituting values, children must apply order of operations correctly. The expression 2a + 3 when a = 4 is 11 (not 14), but children often compute 2 x 4 + 3 correctly. The trouble comes with expressions like 2(a + 3) where brackets are involved.

7. **Forming equations from words:** Translating "5 less than a number" into n - 5 (not 5 - n) is a common stumbling block. GL exploits this by offering both forms as answer options.

### Effective Teaching Strategies

Based on research evidence (Kieran 2004; Knuth et al. 2006; Carraher et al. 2006):

- **Balance model:** Present equations as a balance scale — what you do to one side, you must do to the other
- **Bar models:** 3x + 5 = 20 shown as a bar of length 20, split into a section of 5 and three equal sections
- **Function machines:** Input -> operation -> output. Work forwards and backwards. Build from concrete to abstract
- **Arithmetic to algebra bridge:** Start with 7 + ? = 12, then evolve to 7 + x = 12
- **Pattern-based introduction:** "Position 1 = 4, Position 2 = 7, Position 3 = 10... What's the rule? Can you write it?"
- **Real-life contexts:** Formulae for converting miles to km, calculating costs with fixed + per-item pricing

### Priority Skills for 11+ Preparation

In order of importance for GL algebra questions:
1. Substitution into expressions (appears most frequently)
2. Solving one-step and two-step equations
3. Function machines (forward and reverse)
4. Continuing linear sequences
5. Translating word problems into equations
6. Working with simple formulae
7. Finding pairs satisfying two-unknown equations
8. Expressing sequence rules algebraically
