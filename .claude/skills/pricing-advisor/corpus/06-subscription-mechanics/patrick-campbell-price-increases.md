## What this covers

Patrick Campbell is the founder of ProfitWell (acquired by Paddle), the subscription analytics platform that processed pricing data for thousands of SaaS and subscription businesses. His body of work on price increases is grounded in empirical data from that dataset rather than in theory. His central finding — that most companies are underpriced, that shock causes churn not price itself, and that price increases should be a regular annual practice — provides a counterweight to the caution advocated by Lemkin.

## The pattern / mechanic

**Most companies are underpriced:**

Campbell's research consistently finds that companies systematically underprice their products, particularly at the early stage. The mechanism is psychological: founders price based on what feels "fair" or what they imagine customers can afford, rather than on willingness-to-pay data. The result is a price that customers would have paid more for without complaint.

His research across thousands of subscription companies found that the average time between pricing reviews is approximately 2.7 to 3 years. Companies that wait this long accumulate a compounding underpricing gap — each year of product improvement and customer base maturation represents value delivered that was never captured in the price.

**The shock hypothesis:**

The reason most companies fear price increases — customer churn — is real but is caused by the wrong variable. Campbell's finding is: "Price increases are always a shock, regardless of how much customers love you or the value you've provided."

What causes churn is not the higher price per se, but the surprise of seeing it. The practical implication: the communication and framing of a price increase is at least as important as its magnitude. A well-communicated 15% increase will generate less churn than a poorly-communicated 5% increase.

He draws an analogy to receipt emails: the day a company sends a receipt notification is always the day of highest churn, because the receipt is a reminder that a payment is occurring. Price increase announcements function the same way. The spike is real but temporary — "afterward, churn typically reverses as price-sensitive customers depart, followed by improved retention overall."

**The five-part communication framework:**

When executing a price increase, Campbell recommends a structured customer communication:

1. **Show delivered value** — use real data (e.g. "your child has completed 847 practice questions since joining")
2. **Highlight new features** built since the customer joined, particularly ones they requested
3. **Connect price to value** with explicit language: "To continue investing in the product, we need to reflect the value it now delivers"
4. **Reward loyalty** with a grace period at the old price (6 months recommended minimum)
5. **Include a compassionate P.S.** — "If this materially impacts your budget, reach out and we'll work something out"

His instruction: "Be very transparent, be very clear, put it on their invoice... and then after the six months, it drops off." After announcement, he explicitly advises: "Don't go overboard with it... stop making it a thing."

**Annual price iteration:**

Campbell's prescriptive advice is to review pricing at least annually and treat small increases as routine rather than exceptional events. The logic: below 10%, increases are rarely worth the effort at small scale, but they establish a norm of annual price iteration that reduces the shock of any future increase. Companies that never raise prices, then raise by 20% in one move, generate far more churn than companies that have raised 5% per year for four years.

**The 20–30% reactivation tail:**

One under-appreciated statistic from ProfitWell's dataset: 20–30% of customers who cancel following a price increase reactivate within 12 months. This means the true churn cost of a price increase is lower than it appears immediately after the event. Campbell frames this as evidence that "growth requires viewing customer relationships as a multi-move game" — a customer who leaves is not necessarily lost.

## Applicability to UK 11+ app

Campbell's framework suggests an 11+ app should plan annual pricing reviews from year one, even if no increase is taken. The act of reviewing establishes internal pricing discipline and surfaces underpricing early. When an increase is taken, the five-part communication should be personalised to the child's actual progress data — "Sophie has completed 1,200 questions and improved her accuracy from 61% to 78%" is a far more compelling case for a price increase than generic value language. In a market where parents are already paying £30–£60/month to Atom Learning, an app priced below that benchmark is almost certainly leaving money on the table.
