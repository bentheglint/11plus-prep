# GL Assessment Research: Fractions

## Format

- **Paper structure:** 50 multiple-choice questions in 50 minutes (1 minute per question average)
- **Answer format:** 5 options (A-E), marked on a separate answer sheet with pencil
- **Fractions coverage:** Fractions questions appear throughout the paper, both as standalone calculations and embedded in multi-step word problems. Number-type questions (which include fractions) are the most common category in GL papers — roughly five times more frequent than any other type.
- **Difficulty progression:** GL papers are not adaptive. All children sit the same test. Questions generally progress from easier to harder through the paper, with simpler 1-mark questions early and more complex multi-step questions later.
- **Cross-topic integration:** Fractions rarely appear in pure isolation. GL frequently combines fractions with decimals, percentages, money, measurement, data interpretation (pie charts), and ratio/proportion within a single question.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**What makes it easy:**
- Unit fractions of round numbers (1/2, 1/4, 1/5, 1/10 of multiples)
- Numbers that divide evenly with no remainder
- Single-step calculation only
- Fractions already have common denominators
- Denominators are small and familiar (2, 3, 4, 5, 8, 10)
- Visual/diagram support (shaded shapes)

**Typical D1 questions:**
- "What is 1/5 of 125?" → 25
- "What fraction of this shape is shaded?" (simple grid, clear division)
- "Which is bigger: 3/4 or 2/4?"
- "Convert 1/2 to a decimal" → 0.5
- "What is 3 3/5 as an improper fraction?" → 18/5
- "Simplify 6/8" → 3/4

**D1 characteristics:**
- The correct answer is immediately obtainable from one operation
- Denominators are factors of the total (no messy remainders)
- Common benchmark fractions that children encounter daily (halves, quarters, fifths, tenths)
- If visual, the shape is regularly divided and the shaded portion is obvious

---

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**What makes it medium:**
- Non-unit fractions of amounts requiring two steps (divide then multiply)
- Adding/subtracting fractions with different denominators (need to find LCD)
- Converting between fractions, decimals and percentages
- Mixed number operations
- Word problems with one layer of context
- Answers need simplifying

**Typical D2 questions:**
- "What is 3/8 of 240?" → 90
- "3/4 + 1/3 = ?" → 1 1/12
- "Put these in order: 35%, 0.3, 34/100" → 0.3, 34/100, 35%
- "What is 9/12 - 1/3?" → 5/12
- "Harry lost three-quarters of half the money his aunt gave him. Half was £64. How much did Harry lose?" → £48
- "What is 18/4 as a mixed fraction in its simplest form?" → 4 1/2

**D2 characteristics:**
- Two or three steps to reach the answer
- Requires finding a common denominator or converting between forms
- Word problems with a realistic but straightforward context (money, sharing, recipes)
- The answer may need simplifying, and an unsimplified version appears as a distractor
- Denominators may require the child to find the LCM (e.g., 3 and 8 → 24)

---

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**What makes it hard:**
- Multi-step word problems requiring fractions combined with other operations
- Reverse/working-backwards problems ("2/3 of the original amount is 40 — what was the original?")
- Fraction of a fraction ("What is four-fifths of five-sixteenths?")
- Division by fractions or dividing fractions by whole numbers
- Mixed representations in one question (fractions + percentages + decimals)
- Questions where the "whole" is not explicitly stated
- Algebraic thinking with fractions
- Counter-intuitive results (multiplying makes smaller, dividing makes bigger)

**Typical D3 questions:**
- "An eighth of an eighth is the same as what fraction of a fourth?" → 1/16
- "There are 360 degrees in a circle. If you walk 5/8 of the circumference, how many degrees have you turned?" → 225
- "How many 6/15 are there in 4/5?" → 2
- "If 17/30 of the class voted against extra homework, how many voted in favour?" → 13/30 of the class
- "Ava spent 1/4 of her money on a book and 30% on lunch. She had £9 left. How much did she start with?" → £20
- "A bath is 2/3 full with 20 litres of water. Given there is room for another 12 litres, find the capacity of the bath." → 96 litres

**D3 characteristics:**
- Three or more steps, often with a conceptual twist
- Requires understanding what "the whole" is and that it can change mid-problem
- Working backwards from a result to find the starting value
- Combining fraction skills with other maths areas (geometry, data, algebra)
- The obvious/intuitive answer is wrong — careful working is essential
- May require interpreting fractions of fractions or nested fractional relationships

## Common Question Types

### 1. Fraction of an Amount
"What is 3/5 of £450?"
Method: Divide by denominator, multiply by numerator (450 / 5 = 90, 90 x 3 = 270).
**GL twist:** The amount may come from a previous calculation, or the question asks for the remainder after the fraction is taken.

### 2. Equivalent Fractions & Simplifying
"Which of these fractions is equivalent to 3/4?"
Options might include: 6/8, 9/12, 12/15, 15/20, 75/100.
"Simplify 24/36 to its lowest terms" → 2/3.
**GL twist:** Multiple options may be equivalent — only one is fully simplified.

### 3. Adding & Subtracting Fractions
"What is 2/5 + 3/4?"
Requires LCD = 20: 8/20 + 15/20 = 23/20 = 1 3/20.
**GL twist:** Distractors include the result of adding numerators and denominators directly (5/9).

### 4. Comparing & Ordering
"Put these fractions in order from smallest to largest: 2/3, 3/5, 7/10."
Requires converting to a common denominator (LCD = 30): 20/30, 18/30, 21/30.
Order: 3/5, 2/3, 7/10.
**GL twist:** With same numerators (e.g., 2/3, 2/5, 2/10), the counter-intuitive rule applies — larger denominator means smaller fraction.

### 5. Converting Between Fractions, Decimals & Percentages
"Convert 3/8 to a decimal" → 0.375.
"Write 0.35 as a fraction in its simplest form" → 7/20.
"What percentage is equivalent to 2/5?" → 40%.
**GL twist:** Ordering a mix of fractions, decimals and percentages in one question.

### 6. Mixed Numbers & Improper Fractions
"Convert 5 4/8 to an improper fraction in its simplest form" → 11/2.
"Write 39/4 as a mixed number" → 9 3/4.
**GL twist:** The answer must be in simplest form — unsimplified versions are distractors.

### 7. Multiplying & Dividing Fractions
"What is 4/5 x 2/3?" → 8/15.
"What is 7/12 / 4?" → 7/48.
"What is 3/5 x 7?" → 21/5 = 4 1/5.
**GL twist:** Multiplying a fraction by a fraction produces a smaller result (counter-intuitive for children used to multiplication making things bigger).

### 8. Word Problems (Multi-step)
"Sophie reads 2/5 of a 150-page book on Monday and 1/3 of the remainder on Tuesday. How many pages are left?"
(150 x 2/5 = 60; remainder = 90; 90 x 1/3 = 30; left = 60 pages)
**GL twist:** "Of the remainder" — the whole changes mid-problem.

### 9. Reverse/Working Backwards Problems
"After spending 3/5 of her money, Mia has £24 left. How much did she start with?"
(£24 = 2/5 of total; total = £24 / 2 x 5 = £60)
**GL twist:** Children must identify which fraction is left over, not given.

### 10. Visual/Diagram Fractions
"What fraction of this shape is shaded?" with a diagram showing a divided shape.
May involve irregular divisions, overlapping shading, or composite shapes.
**GL twist:** The shape may not be divided into equal parts, requiring the child to identify the unit.

### 11. Fractions in Data Interpretation
"The pie chart shows how 120 pupils travel to school. What fraction walk?" (reading from a pie chart sector).
**GL twist:** Requires combining fraction reading with data skills.

## Distractor Design

GL Assessment designs distractors (wrong answers) that exploit predictable errors. For fractions, the main distractor patterns are:

### Computational Error Distractors
| Error Type | Example | Correct Answer | Distractor |
|-----------|---------|----------------|------------|
| Adding denominators as well as numerators | 1/3 + 1/4 | 7/12 | 2/7 |
| Forgetting to simplify | Simplify 8/12 | 2/3 | 4/6 or 8/12 |
| Multiplying instead of dividing (or vice versa) | 1/3 of 60 | 20 | 180 |
| Wrong common denominator | 2/3 + 1/4 using 7 instead of 12 | 11/12 | 3/7 |
| Numerator/denominator swap | Convert 3 2/5 to improper | 17/5 | 5/17 |
| Forgetting to convert mixed number | 3 2/5 as improper | 17/5 | 32/5 |
| Not simplifying to lowest terms | 96/8 as mixed number simplified | 39/4 → 9 3/4 | 72/8 or 39/2 |

### Conceptual Error Distractors
| Error Type | Example | Correct Answer | Distractor |
|-----------|---------|----------------|------------|
| Larger denominator = larger fraction | Order 1/3 and 1/8 | 1/3 > 1/8 | 1/8 > 1/3 |
| Treating fraction parts as independent whole numbers | 3/4 vs 5/8 | 3/4 > 5/8 | 5/8 > 3/4 (because 5 > 3) |
| "Of the remainder" missed | 1/3 of 150, then 1/4 of remainder | 25 | 37.5 (1/4 of 150) |
| Multiplication makes bigger | 2/3 x 1/2 | 1/3 | "Bigger than 2/3" option |
| Division makes smaller | 4 / 1/2 | 8 | 2 |

### Distractor Placement Principles
- The correct answer is distributed roughly evenly across positions A-E
- The most plausible distractor (from the most common error) is typically placed adjacent to the correct answer
- At least one distractor is "wildly wrong" to give weaker students a reasonable elimination starting point
- At least one distractor is very close to the correct answer (e.g., unsimplified form)

## Common GL Traps

1. **"Of the remainder" trap:** A fraction of the total is taken, then a second fraction is taken "of the remainder." Children who take the second fraction of the original total get the wrong answer. This is GL's most reliable fractions trap.

2. **Simplification trap:** The unsimplified answer appears as an option. Children who calculate correctly but don't reduce to lowest terms pick the wrong option.

3. **Mixed representation trap:** Ordering 0.3, 35%, 7/20 — requires converting all to the same form. Children who compare digits without converting are caught out.

4. **Counter-intuitive ordering:** When fractions share a numerator (e.g., 2/3, 2/5, 2/7), the largest denominator gives the smallest fraction. Children instinctively order by denominator size and get it backwards.

5. **Implicit "whole" trap:** "A class has 30 pupils. 2/5 are boys. 1/3 of the boys play football. How many boys play football?" The child must find 2/5 of 30 first (= 12), then 1/3 of 12 (= 4), not 1/3 of 30.

6. **Reverse working trap:** "After eating 3/4 of the sweets, Tom has 12 left." Children who calculate 3/4 of 12 (= 9) instead of recognising that 12 is 1/4 of the total get trapped.

7. **Denominator addition trap:** When adding 1/3 + 1/4, children who add both top and bottom get 2/7 instead of 7/12. GL always includes this error as a distractor.

8. **Mixed number conversion trap:** Converting 5 4/8 to an improper fraction — children who concatenate to get 54/8 instead of calculating (5 x 8) + 4 = 44/8 = 11/2.

9. **Unit confusion in word problems:** Questions that switch between units (litres/ml, metres/cm) while using fractions. Children who don't convert units first calculate the wrong fraction.

10. **"What fraction is NOT shaded" trap:** Visual questions where children identify the shaded fraction but the question asks for the unshaded portion.

## Key Concepts Reference

### Essential Fraction Operations

**Finding a fraction of an amount:**
- To find a/b of N: divide N by b, then multiply by a
- Example: 3/8 of 240 = 240 / 8 x 3 = 90

**Adding/subtracting fractions:**
1. Find the LCD (lowest common denominator)
2. Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions with the LCD
3. Add/subtract numerators, keep denominator
4. Simplify if possible
- Example: 2/3 + 3/4 = 8/12 + 9/12 = 17/12 = 1 5/12

**Multiplying fractions:**
- Multiply numerators together, multiply denominators together
- Simplify (cancel common factors before or after multiplying)
- Example: 4/5 x 2/3 = 8/15

**Dividing fractions:**
- "Keep, Change, Flip" — keep the first fraction, change division to multiplication, flip the second fraction (use its reciprocal)
- Example: 8/9 / 2/3 = 8/9 x 3/2 = 24/18 = 4/3 = 1 1/3

**Converting mixed numbers to improper fractions:**
- Multiply whole number by denominator, add numerator, keep denominator
- Example: 3 2/5 = (3 x 5 + 2)/5 = 17/5

**Converting improper fractions to mixed numbers:**
- Divide numerator by denominator: quotient = whole part, remainder = new numerator
- Example: 23/4 = 5 remainder 3 = 5 3/4

### Simplifying Fractions
- Find the HCF (highest common factor) of numerator and denominator
- Divide both by the HCF
- Example: 24/36 → HCF = 12 → 2/3
- Quick check: if both are even, halve both. If both end in 0 or 5, divide by 5. Repeat until no common factors remain.

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand and correctly use these terms:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Numerator** | The top number — how many parts you have |
| **Denominator** | The bottom number — how many equal parts the whole is divided into |
| **Proper fraction** | Numerator < denominator (less than 1), e.g., 3/4 |
| **Improper fraction** | Numerator >= denominator (1 or more), e.g., 7/4 |
| **Mixed number** | Whole number + proper fraction, e.g., 1 3/4 |
| **Equivalent fractions** | Different fractions representing the same value, e.g., 2/4 = 1/2 |
| **Simplify / cancel / lowest terms** | Reduce by dividing numerator and denominator by their HCF |
| **Common denominator** | A shared denominator for comparing or adding fractions |
| **Lowest common denominator (LCD)** | The smallest common denominator (= LCM of denominators) |
| **Unit fraction** | A fraction with numerator 1, e.g., 1/3, 1/8 |
| **Non-unit fraction** | A fraction with numerator > 1, e.g., 3/8 |
| **Reciprocal** | The fraction flipped — reciprocal of 3/4 is 4/3 |
| **Highest common factor (HCF)** | Largest number that divides into both numerator and denominator |
| **Lowest common multiple (LCM)** | Smallest number that is a multiple of both denominators |
| **"Of" in fractions** | Means multiply — "1/4 of 60" = 1/4 x 60 |
| **Remainder** | What is left after a fraction is taken |
| **Convert** | Change from one form to another (fraction ↔ decimal ↔ percentage) |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 Statutory Requirements (Fractions, Decimals & Percentages)
- Compare and order fractions whose denominators are all multiples of the same number
- Identify, name and write equivalent fractions of a given fraction, represented visually, including tenths and hundredths
- Recognise mixed numbers and improper fractions and convert from one form to the other
- Add and subtract fractions with the same denominator, and denominators that are multiples of the same number
- Multiply proper fractions and mixed numbers by whole numbers, supported by materials and diagrams
- Read and write decimal numbers as fractions (e.g., 0.71 = 71/100)
- Recognise and use thousandths and relate them to tenths, hundredths and decimal equivalents
- Round decimals with 2 decimal places to the nearest whole number and to 1 decimal place
- Read, write, order and compare numbers with up to 3 decimal places
- Recognise the per cent symbol (%) and understand that per cent relates to "number of parts per hundred"
- Write percentages as a fraction with denominator 100, and as a decimal
- Solve problems which require knowing percentage and decimal equivalents of 1/2, 1/4, 1/5, 2/5, 4/5 and those with a denominator of a multiple of 10 or 25

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements (Fractions, Decimals & Percentages)
- Use common factors to simplify fractions; use common multiples to express fractions in the same denomination
- Compare and order fractions, including fractions > 1
- Add and subtract fractions with different denominators and mixed numbers, using the concept of equivalent fractions
- Multiply simple pairs of proper fractions, writing the answer in its simplest form
- Divide proper fractions by whole numbers (e.g., 1/3 / 2 = 1/6)
- Associate a fraction with division and calculate decimal fraction equivalents (e.g., 0.375 for 3/8)
- Identify the value of each digit in numbers given to 3 decimal places and multiply and divide numbers by 10, 100 and 1,000
- Multiply one-digit numbers with up to 2 decimal places by whole numbers
- Use written division methods in cases where the answer has up to 2 decimal places
- Solve problems which require answers to be rounded to specified degrees of accuracy
- Recall and use equivalences between simple fractions, decimals and percentages, including in different contexts

### Year 6 Ratio & Proportion (fraction-adjacent)
- Solve problems involving the calculation of percentages (e.g., 15% of 360) and the use of percentages for comparison

### GL 11+ Beyond Curriculum
GL Assessment tests are designed to challenge the top 25% of the year group and may include:
- Content from Year 6 that has not yet been formally taught at the time of sitting (exams typically in September of Year 6)
- Multi-step problems combining multiple curriculum objectives in a single question

## Teaching Notes

### Foundational Understanding Before Procedures
Research consistently shows that children who learn fraction procedures without understanding the concept behind them make persistent errors. Key foundations:
- A fraction represents a **relationship** between parts and a whole — not two separate numbers
- The denominator names the type of part (fifths, eighths); the numerator counts how many of those parts
- "The whole" can be one object, a set of objects, or a quantity — and understanding what "the whole" is in any given problem is the single most important fraction skill

### The Four Most Common Misconceptions (from research)

1. **Applying whole-number thinking to fractions:** Children treat numerator and denominator as independent whole numbers. They believe 3/8 > 1/2 because 3 and 8 are both bigger than 1 and 2. Fix: Extensive use of fraction walls, bars, and number lines before any abstract work.

2. **Larger denominator = larger fraction:** Children think 1/8 > 1/4 because 8 > 4. This is the single most persistent fraction misconception. Fix: Physical sharing activities — sharing one pizza among 4 people vs 8 people makes it viscerally clear who gets more.

3. **Adding/subtracting by treating numerators and denominators independently:** 1/3 + 1/4 = 2/7. The child has added the tops and added the bottoms. Fix: Explain that denominators are "names" — you cannot add thirds and quarters just as you cannot add apples and oranges. You must rename them as the same thing first (twelfths).

4. **Confusion about "the whole":** When asked "what fraction of the class are boys?", children may count boys and girls separately (4 boys, 6 girls) and write 4/6 instead of 4/10. Fix: Always ask "what is the whole here?" before any fraction calculation.

### GL-Specific Preparation Priorities

**Priority 1 — Fraction of an amount (highest frequency):**
Drill both methods: (a) divide by denominator, multiply by numerator; (b) multiply then divide. Use money, measurement, and real-world contexts. Include "what's left?" questions (find the fraction, subtract from total).

**Priority 2 — FDP conversions (appears in almost every paper):**
Memorise the key equivalences table above. Practise ordering mixed representations (e.g., 0.6, 58%, 3/5 — convert all to same form). Understand that a fraction IS a division (3/8 means 3 divided by 8).

**Priority 3 — Adding/subtracting with unlike denominators:**
Master the LCD method. Practise with denominators that are NOT simple multiples of each other (e.g., 3 and 7, not just 3 and 6). Always simplify the result.

**Priority 4 — Word problems and reverse fractions:**
Practise identifying "what is the whole?" in every problem. Work backwards from a result: "2/5 of the original is 30 — what was the original?" Practise "of the remainder" problems where the whole changes mid-question.

**Priority 5 — Simplifying (tested indirectly in almost every question):**
Children must habitually check whether their answer can be simplified. GL nearly always includes the unsimplified version as a distractor. Teach the "divide by 2, 3, 5, 7" checking sequence.

### Time Management for Fractions Questions
- Simple fraction-of-an-amount: aim for 30-45 seconds
- Adding/subtracting with different denominators: aim for 45-60 seconds
- Multi-step word problems: allow 60-90 seconds
- If stuck, eliminate obviously wrong options first (e.g., the "added denominators" answer) and make an educated guess — no negative marking in GL

### Common Errors to Watch For in Practice
- Not simplifying final answers
- Adding denominators when adding fractions
- Forgetting to find a common denominator before comparing
- Converting mixed numbers incorrectly (concatenating instead of calculating)
- Not reading whether the question asks for the fraction taken or the fraction remaining
- Losing track of "the whole" in multi-step problems
- Unit conversion errors in measurement-based fraction problems
