# GL Assessment Research: Compound Words (VR)

## Format

### Question Type Classification
- **GL Type 12**: "Compound Words" (also referred to as "Type Q" in the older IPS Method classification)
- One of 21 GL verbal reasoning question types
- Falls under the **Word Manipulation** category in GL's taxonomy

### How It Appears on Paper

**Standard GL format — Two Groups:**

The child is given two groups of words (typically 3 words in each group). They must select one word from the first group and one word from the second group that combine to form a single valid compound word. The word from the first group always comes first.

**Instruction wording (from GL familiarisation materials):**
> "In each of the following questions, find the two words, one from each group, that together make a new, real word. The word from the group on the left always comes first."

**Presentation format:**
```
(early  book  read)    (shelf  skim  king)
```
Answer: **book** + **shelf** = **bookshelf**

```
(miss  throw  imp)    (air  take  pair)
```
Answer: **imp** + **air** = **impair**

**Answer format:**
- Bournemouth/Dorset uses multiple-choice format (all three papers are multiple-choice, electronically marked)
- The child marks TWO answers on the answer sheet — one from each group
- Some practice materials present the answer as a single compound word from 5 options instead

### Our App's Adapted Format — Word Link

Our app uses a "word link" variant which is closely related but structured for 5-option multiple choice:

> "Which word can go in front of both 'light' and 'rise' to make two new words?"
> Options: Day, Sun, Cloud, Star, Flash

This tests the same underlying skill (compound word formation) but in a slightly different presentation. The child finds a single linking word that creates valid compound words with two given words. This is arguably harder because the child must test the linking word in two directions simultaneously.

Both formats appear across GL practice materials and are considered valid compound word questions.

### Paper Structure
- GL verbal reasoning paper: **80 questions** in **50 minutes**
- 21 question types can appear, but not all 21 appear in every paper
- GL does not disclose in advance which types will appear
- Typically **3-5 compound word questions per paper** when the type is included

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

Compound words sit in GL's **Word Manipulation** category, testing vocabulary breadth, word formation knowledge, and systematic problem-solving.

| Skill Tested | GL Weight | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Vocabulary breadth — knowing compound words exist | ~35% | Child must recognise the target word is real |
| Systematic combination testing | ~25% | Methodically trying left + right pairs |
| Spelling accuracy / visual word recognition | ~20% | Distinguishing real words from near-misses |
| Pronunciation flexibility | ~10% | Recognising words that sound different when combined |
| Speed under pressure | ~10% | 9 combinations to test mentally per question |

### What Makes This Different From Other VR Types
- Unlike synonyms/antonyms, this does NOT test word meaning — it tests word structure
- Unlike hidden words or letter move, the component words are given openly
- The challenge is recognising valid combinations among many plausible-looking ones
- Strong readers with wide vocabulary have a significant advantage

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Common, high-frequency compound words that children use daily
- Both component words are familiar and obviously form part of the compound
- The compound word sounds like its parts said together (no pronunciation shift)
- Distractors clearly don't form real words with any partner
- Child recognises the answer almost immediately

**Example compounds:**
- sun + light = **sunlight**
- rain + bow = **rainbow**
- foot + ball = **football**
- bed + room = **bedroom**
- tooth + brush = **toothbrush**
- class + room = **classroom**
- black + berry = **blackberry**
- sun + rise = **sunrise**
- day + light = **daylight**
- snow + flake = **snowflake**

**Example question (two-group format):**
```
(rain  cloud  storm)    (bow  sky  wet)
```
Answer: rain + bow = rainbow

**Example question (word-link format):**
> Which word can go in front of both 'rise' and 'set'?
> Sun, Moon, Star, Day, Cloud → **Sun** (sunrise, sunset)

**Why it's easy:** "Rainbow" and "sunrise" are words children have known since infant school. No pronunciation tricks, no obscure vocabulary.

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**Characteristics:**
- Less obvious compound words — child knows the word but might not immediately see it as a compound
- Component words may have unrelated individual meanings to the compound
- May require testing several combinations before finding the answer
- One or two distractors form partial matches (work with one partner word but not the other)
- Some pronunciation shift when words combine

**Example compounds:**
- house + wife = **housewife**
- arm + chair = **armchair**
- book + mark = **bookmark**
- under + stand = **understand**
- day + dream = **daydream**
- dead + line = **deadline**
- court + yard = **courtyard**
- wheel + house = **wheelhouse**
- farm + yard = **farmyard**
- spear + mint = **spearmint**

**Example question (two-group format):**
```
(heap  wide  long)    (right  wine  spread)
```
Answer: wide + spread = widespread

**Example question (word-link format):**
> Which word can go after both 'arm' and 'wheel'?
> Rest, Chair, Band, Pit, Less → **Chair** (armchair, wheelchair)

**Why it's medium:** "Widespread" doesn't sound like "wide" + "spread" when spoken naturally. The child needs to try combinations systematically. Distractors like "longwine" or "heapright" are clearly wrong, but the child may hesitate between plausible-sounding options.

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Obscure compound words the child may never have encountered
- Significant pronunciation shift — the compound sounds completely different from its parts
- Component words seem unrelated to the compound's meaning
- Multiple distractors form plausible-looking combinations
- Near-miss spelling traps (almost looks like a word but isn't)
- May use less common vocabulary in the word groups
- Homophones in the options create confusion (metre/meter, sight/site/cite)

**Example compounds:**
- habit + at = **habitat**
- feat + her = **feather**
- be + cause = **because**
- cup + board = **cupboard**
- ham + burger = **hamburger**
- them + selves = **themselves**
- fore + thought = **forethought**
- fore + sight = **foresight**
- here + after = **hereafter**
- war + path = **warpath**

**Example question (two-group format):**
```
(examine  thought  be)    (nation  cause  full)
```
Answer: be + cause = because

**Example question (word-link format):**
> Which word can go in front of both 'ward' and 'right'?
> Up, Out, Down, Back, For → **Out** (outward, outright)

**Why it's hard:** "Because" doesn't sound like "be" + "cause". "Cupboard" doesn't sound like "cup" + "board". The child must think about spelling, not pronunciation. Multiple distractors look tempting — "thoughtful" seems obvious but "-full" is not the correct suffix spelling ("-ful").

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Two-Group Selection (Standard GL Format)
Two groups of 3 words. Pick one from each to form a compound word.
```
(fresh  habit  mixed)    (acted  glass  at)
```
Answer: habit + at = habitat

This is the primary GL format. Creates 9 possible combinations (3 x 3) that must be tested mentally.

### Type 2: Word-Link / Shared Word (Used in Practice Materials)
Find a single word that goes before or after two given words to make two compound words.
> Which word can go after both 'book' and 'suit'?
Answer: case (bookcase, suitcase)

This is common in practice books and our app. Tests the same skill but in reverse — child must generate compound words from a linking word rather than combine two given words.

### Type 3: Three-Group Selection (Rare, Advanced)
Three groups of words — pick one from each to form a three-part compound word.
```
(high  low  wide)    (road  load  way)    (boy  girl  man)
```
Answer: high + way + man = highwayman

This appears occasionally at D3 difficulty. Very challenging due to the exponential increase in combinations (3 x 3 x 3 = 27).

## Distractor Design

GL designs distractors for compound word questions using several deliberate strategies:

### 1. Partial-Match Distractors
Words that form a valid compound with ONE partner but not the other.
> Which word goes in front of both 'light' and 'rise'?
> **Day** works with "light" (daylight) but NOT "rise" (*dayrise is not a word)
> **Star** works with "light" (starlight) but NOT "rise" (*starrise is not a word)
> Only **Sun** works with BOTH (sunlight, sunrise)

This is the most common distractor strategy. Forces the child to check BOTH combinations, not just one.

### 2. Two-Word Phrase Confusion
Distractors that form a two-word phrase but not a compound word.
```
(electricity  farm  work)    (meter  metre  yard)
```
"Electricity meter" is a real phrase (two separate words) but NOT a compound word.
"Farmyard" IS a single compound word. Answer: farm + yard.

Children who confuse phrases with compounds get caught here.

### 3. Near-Miss Spelling Traps
Combinations that almost spell a real word but don't quite.
```
(miss  throw  imp)    (air  take  pair)
```
- "mistake" → tempting, but the combination gives "misstake" (extra s)
- "impair" → correct: imp + air = impair

GL exploits the fact that children sound out combinations rather than checking letter-by-letter.

### 4. Homophone Confusion
Using words that sound the same but are spelled differently.
```
(four  fore  for)    (sight  site  cite)
```
- Only "fore + sight = foresight" is correct
- "four + sight" looks plausible but "foursight" is not a word
- "fore + site" sounds right but "foresite" is not a word

### 5. Suffix Spelling Traps
Using near-correct suffixes that are actually misspelled.
```
(examine  thought  be)    (nation  cause  full)
```
- "Thoughtful" seems obvious — but the suffix is "-ful" (one L), and the option says "full" (two Ls)
- The combination "thoughtfull" is a misspelling
- The actual answer is be + cause = because

### 6. Unfamiliar Correct Answers
The correct compound word may be one the child doesn't know, making them doubt their answer and gravitate toward a wrong but familiar-sounding combination.
- "Wheelhouse" (helm area on a ship) — child may not know this word
- "Warpath" — less common in everyday speech
- "Hereafter" — somewhat formal/literary

## Common GL Traps

### Trap 1: Pronunciation Shift
The biggest trap in compound words. When two words combine, the resulting word often sounds completely different from the individual parts.

| Parts | Compound | Why It's Tricky |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| cup + board | cupboard | Sounds like "cubberd" not "cup-board" |
| be + cause | because | Sounds like "bicoz" not "be-cause" |
| habit + at | habitat | Stress shifts entirely |
| feat + her | feather | Sounds like "fether" not "feat-her" |
| break + fast | breakfast | Sounds like "brekfast" not "break-fast" |
| ham + burger | hamburger | Nobody says "ham-burger" |

**Defence:** Look at the LETTERS, not the sounds. Write out combinations if unsure.

### Trap 2: Instinct Jumping
Children spot what looks like a familiar word and mark it without checking all options. GL designs questions where the first plausible-looking answer is wrong and the correct answer appears later.

**Defence:** Always check every combination. The obvious answer is often a trap.

### Trap 3: Double Letter Confusion
When combining words, children may add or remove letters.
- "reap" + "ear" → "reapear" (NOT a word)
- "reap" + "pear" → "reappear" (IS a word, but wait — that's "reap" + "pear", the letters are r-e-a-p-p-e-a-r)

Children get confused about whether the boundary letters overlap or concatenate.

**Defence:** Write out the EXACT letters of both words joined together. Don't guess.

### Trap 4: The "Both Must Work" Rule (Word-Link Format)
In the word-link format, children often find a word that works with one partner and assume it works with both.
- "Day" works with "light" (daylight) → child marks "Day" without checking "dayrise" (not a word)
- "Finger" works with "print" (fingerprint) → child marks "Finger" without checking "fingerstep" (not a word)

**Defence:** Always test the linking word with BOTH partner words before answering.

### Trap 5: Open Compound vs Closed Compound
Some word pairs form open compounds (two words) rather than closed compounds (one word).
- "Ice cream" → two words, NOT a compound word for this question type
- "Full time" → two words (though "full-time" is hyphenated)

GL only accepts closed compound words as correct answers in this question type. If the result is two separate words, it's wrong.

### Trap 6: False Confidence from Partial Knowledge
A child who knows "thoughtful" exists may confidently mark "thought" + "full" — but the option says "full" (the standalone word) not "-ful" (the suffix). The concatenation "thoughtfull" is a misspelling.

## Key Compound Word Patterns

### By First-Word Category

**Body parts (very common):**
- foot + ball, footprint, footstep, footpath, foothold, footwear, foothill, footnote
- hand + shake, handsome, handbook, handmade, handwriting, handstand
- head + ache, headline, headquarters, headband, headlight, headmaster, headphone
- eye + brow, eyelash, eyelid, eyesight, eyewitness
- finger + print, fingertip, fingernail

**Nature/weather:**
- sun + light, sunrise, sunset, sunflower, sunburn, sunshine, sunscreen
- rain + bow, raincoat, raindrop, rainfall, rainforest, rainstorm
- snow + ball, snowfall, snowflake, snowman, snowstorm
- water + fall, waterproof, watermelon, watercolour, watermill

**Home/buildings:**
- house + hold, housework, housewife, housekeeper
- bed + room, bedtime, bedside, bedspread
- door + bell, doorstep, doorway, doorknob

**Direction/position:**
- out + side, outward, outright, outcome, outdoor, outlook, output, outstanding
- over + look, overcome, overflow, overnight, overseas, overtake
- under + stand, underground, underline, underneath, undertake, underwater
- up + stairs, update, upgrade, uphill, upon, upset, upward

**Time/sequence:**
- day + break, daydream, daylight, daytime
- night + fall, nightmare, nighttime
- after + noon, afterlife, aftermath, afterthought
- fore + sight, forethought, forecast, forehead, foreground

**Animals/nature (less common, often tricky):**
- butter + fly = butterfly
- lady + bird = ladybird
- dragon + fly = dragonfly
- sea + horse = seahorse
- jelly + fish = jellyfish

### By Difficulty Pattern

**Transparent compounds (D1):** Both parts obviously relate to meaning
- sunflower, football, bedroom, toothbrush, raincoat

**Semi-transparent compounds (D2):** Connection visible if you think about it
- bookmark, deadline, armchair, widespread, courtyard

**Opaque compounds (D3):** Parts seem unrelated to the whole word's meaning
- cupboard, breakfast, habitat, butterfly, understand, because

## Teaching Notes

### Core Strategy: Systematic Combination Testing
Teach children to work through combinations methodically rather than guessing:
1. Take the FIRST word from Group 1
2. Try it with EACH word from Group 2 (left to right)
3. If no match, move to the SECOND word from Group 1
4. Repeat until a valid compound word is found
5. ALWAYS check remaining combinations to confirm there's only one answer

### The "Write It Out" Rule
For any combination that seems plausible but uncertain, write out the exact letters joined together. This catches:
- Double letter errors (reap + ear ≠ reappear)
- Suffix spelling mistakes (thought + full ≠ thoughtful)
- Near-miss misspellings

### Pronunciation Awareness
Explicitly teach children that compound words often DON'T sound like their parts:
- Say "cupboard" → then show it's "cup" + "board"
- Say "breakfast" → then show it's "break" + "fast"
- Say "because" → then show it's "be" + "cause"

This builds the mental model that they should trust SPELLING over SOUND.

### Building Compound Word Vocabulary
The single most effective preparation strategy is exposure to a wide range of compound words:
- Read compound word lists regularly (aim for familiarity with 200+ compounds)
- Group by first word (all "sun-" words, all "hand-" words, etc.)
- Play compound word matching games (cards with first halves and second halves)
- Point out compound words during everyday reading

### The "Both Must Work" Drill (Word-Link Format)
For the word-link variant, drill the habit of testing BOTH combinations:
1. Read the linking word option
2. Combine with partner word 1 — is it a real word?
3. Combine with partner word 2 — is it a real word?
4. BOTH must be real words — if only one works, it's wrong

### Speed Tips
- Start with the shortest words — they tend to form compounds more often
- Common first-half words (sun, rain, foot, hand, bed, day, out, over, under) are high-probability starting points
- If nothing works going left-to-right, try the most unusual word in each group — GL often hides the answer there
- Trust your first instinct for D1 questions — don't overthink simple compounds

### Common Mistakes to Watch For
1. **Rushing** — marking the first plausible combination without checking all options
2. **Sounding out** — relying on pronunciation rather than spelling
3. **Missing the unfamiliar** — rejecting the correct answer because the compound word is unknown
4. **Two-word phrases** — accepting "electricity meter" as a compound when it's two separate words
5. **Not checking both directions** — in word-link format, testing only one partner word
6. **Letter confusion** — getting muddled about where one word ends and the next begins

### Time Management
- D1 questions should take 10-15 seconds
- D2 questions should take 20-30 seconds
- D3 questions should take 30-45 seconds maximum
- If stuck after 45 seconds, make best guess and move on — the VR paper has 80 questions in 50 minutes (37.5 seconds average per question)

**Must-know (appear constantly):**
