# GL Assessment Research: Angles and Shapes

## Format

GL Assessment tests angles and shapes as part of the **50-question, 50-minute maths paper**. All questions are **multiple choice** with 5 answer options (A-E). The paper is non-adaptive and paper-based — every child sits the same questions.

Geometry questions (covering properties of shapes, angles, symmetry, coordinates, nets, and transformations) typically account for **6-10 questions per paper** (~12-20% of the paper). Number topics dominate the paper at roughly 5x the frequency of any other strand, so geometry is a smaller but consistent component.

Geometry questions appear throughout the paper, not clustered together. Diagrams accompany most angle and shape questions, always labelled **"Not drawn to scale"** — children must calculate rather than measure. Diagrams are clear, uncluttered line drawings showing the essential geometric features.

The content is drawn from the **Key Stage 2 National Curriculum** (Years 5-6), though GL deliberately includes some advanced Year 6 content that children may not have studied in school yet. The paper targets the **top 25%** of learners, so the harder geometry questions require multi-step reasoning and confident knowledge of shape properties.

## Skills Tested and Category Weightings

| Skill Category | Approx. GL Weight | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Angle calculation** | ~25-30% | Finding missing angles using angle sum rules (triangle = 180deg, quadrilateral = 360deg, straight line = 180deg, point = 360deg, vertically opposite) |
| **Properties of 2D shapes** | ~20-25% | Identifying shapes from properties, knowing sides/angles/symmetry of triangles, quadrilaterals, and regular polygons |
| **Symmetry** | ~10-15% | Lines of symmetry in 2D shapes, rotational symmetry order, completing symmetrical patterns |
| **3D shapes and nets** | ~10-15% | Identifying 3D shapes from descriptions/nets, counting faces/edges/vertices, matching nets to solids |
| **Coordinates** | ~10-15% | Reading and plotting coordinates in the first quadrant (and occasionally all four quadrants with negative values) |
| **Reflection and translation** | ~5-10% | Reflecting shapes across mirror lines, translating shapes on grids, describing transformations |
| **Rotation** | ~5% | Rotating shapes about a point, understanding clockwise/anticlockwise turns |

**Note:** These weightings are approximate, derived from analysis of GL practice papers and tutor resources. The exact distribution varies between papers, but angle calculation and shape properties consistently dominate the geometry content.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

- **Single-step angle calculation**: Given two angles in a triangle, find the third (straightforward subtraction from 180deg)
- **Direct shape identification**: "Which shape has exactly 4 right angles and 4 equal sides?" (answer: square)
- **Simple symmetry**: Identifying lines of symmetry on familiar shapes (rectangle, equilateral triangle)
- **Basic coordinate reading**: Reading coordinates of a point clearly marked on a first-quadrant grid
- **Familiar 3D shapes**: "How many faces does a cube have?"
- **Key characteristics**: Uses common, well-known shapes. Numbers are friendly (e.g., angles of 90deg, 60deg, 45deg). Only one rule or property needed. The diagram clearly shows what is being asked.

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

- **Two-step angle calculation**: Finding an angle in an isosceles triangle where you must first work out the base angles, OR finding a missing angle on a straight line then using it in a triangle
- **Shape properties reasoning**: "Which of these clues is incorrect for this shape?" requiring knowledge of multiple properties
- **Rotational symmetry**: Determining the order of rotational symmetry for less familiar shapes (parallelogram, regular pentagon)
- **Net identification**: Matching a net to a 3D shape, or determining which corner meets which when folded
- **Coordinate plotting**: Drawing a shape on a grid using given coordinates, or finding the fourth vertex of a rectangle
- **Reflection across diagonal lines**: Reflecting shapes across lines that are not simply horizontal or vertical
- **Key characteristics**: Requires combining two pieces of knowledge. May involve less common shapes (rhombus, trapezium, kite). Numbers may be less friendly (e.g., 73deg, 118deg). The child must select the right rule before calculating.

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

- **Multi-step angle chains**: Angles involving vertically opposite angles combined with triangle/quadrilateral angle sums, or using exterior angle properties
- **Complex shape reasoning**: Identifying properties of irregular polygons, or deducing a shape from a set of clues that require elimination
- **Compound angle problems**: Finding angles in shapes made of overlapping triangles or shapes with parallel lines
- **Advanced nets**: Predicting which face is opposite another when a net is folded, or which corners will touch
- **Coordinates with transformations**: Describing a shape's position after combined reflection and translation, or working in all four quadrants
- **Interior angles of regular polygons**: Using (n-2) x 180deg formula to find interior angles of pentagons, hexagons, etc.
- **Key characteristics**: Three or more steps. Requires confident recall of multiple rules. May use unfamiliar shape orientations or embed geometry within word problems. The diagram may show a complex figure where you must identify relevant sub-shapes. Red-herring information may be present.

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Find the Missing Angle
The most common geometry question type. A diagram shows a shape (usually a triangle or quadrilateral) with some angles labelled and one unknown. The child must use angle sum rules.

**Example style:** "In the triangle below, angle A = 65deg and angle B = 48deg. What is angle C?"
Options: A) 57deg  B) 67deg  C) 77deg  D) 113deg  E) 180deg

### Type 2: Shape Properties / Identification
A shape is shown or described, and the child must identify it or verify statements about its properties.

**Example style:** "Which pair of shapes both have at least one right angle?"
Options show pairs of shapes drawn in varying orientations.

**Example style:** "Dee is trying to guess Fran's shape. Which clue is INCORRECT?" with statements about angles, sides, and parallel lines.

### Type 3: Angle Types and Classification
Questions asking children to identify, count, or classify angles within shapes.

**Example style:** "How many of these shapes contain an interior reflex angle?"
Shows 5-6 shapes, child counts those with reflex angles.

### Type 4: Symmetry
Questions about lines of symmetry or rotational symmetry, often with a diagram.

**Example style:** "Which of these shapes has exactly 2 lines of symmetry?"
Options: A) Square  B) Rectangle  C) Equilateral triangle  D) Regular pentagon  E) Circle

**Example style:** "What is the order of rotational symmetry of this shape?" with a regular hexagon shown.

### Type 5: Nets of 3D Shapes
A net is shown and the child must identify the 3D shape it makes, or determine which face/corner meets another.

**Example style:** "Which corner will touch the corner marked X when this net is folded?"

### Type 6: Coordinates
A grid with plotted points or shapes, requiring the child to read or give coordinates.

**Example style:** "A rectangle has three corners at (2, 1), (2, 5), and (7, 5). What are the coordinates of the fourth corner?"

### Type 7: Reflection and Translation
Shapes on grids that must be reflected or translated, or the result of a transformation identified.

**Example style:** "Triangle P is reflected in the mirror line shown. Which triangle shows the correct reflection?"

### Type 8: Isosceles Triangle Problems
A specific GL favourite. Given one angle of an isosceles triangle, find another — but the child must consider which angle is given (the unique angle or one of the equal base angles).

**Example style:** "A, B and C are the angles of an isosceles triangle. Angle A = 70deg. Angle B is greater than 60deg. What is the size of angle C?"

### Type 9: Angles on Lines and at Points
Diagrams showing intersecting lines or angles around a point, with missing angles to find.

**Example style:** A diagram shows two straight lines crossing. One angle is labelled 125deg. "What is the size of angle x?"

### Type 10: Compass Directions and Bearings
Questions involving turns (quarter turn = 90deg, half turn = 180deg), compass directions (N, S, E, W, NE, etc.), and simple bearings.

**Example style:** "Jack faces North and turns clockwise through 270deg. Which direction is he now facing?"

## Distractor Design

GL designs wrong answers to catch specific misconceptions and common calculation errors. For angles and shapes, the distractor patterns are:

### Angle Calculation Distractors
1. **Subtracted from wrong total**: Child subtracts from 360deg instead of 180deg (or vice versa) — e.g., for a triangle question, one option is the answer you get if you use 360deg instead of 180deg
2. **Forgot to account for equal angles**: In isosceles triangle problems, one distractor is the answer if you assume the given angle is the unique angle when it is actually a base angle (or vice versa)
3. **Off-by-one-step error**: One distractor is the supplementary angle (180deg minus the correct answer) — catches children who find the angle on the other side of the line
4. **Simple arithmetic slip**: A distractor that is 10deg or a small amount off the correct answer, catching mental arithmetic errors
5. **The "obvious" wrong answer**: 180deg or 360deg itself appears as an option, catching children who write down the total instead of the missing angle

### Shape Properties Distractors
1. **Confusing similar shapes**: Options include shapes that share some but not all properties (e.g., rhombus vs square, parallelogram vs rectangle)
2. **Orientation confusion**: A correct shape shown in an unusual orientation — children who only recognise shapes in "standard" position may reject it
3. **Property overcounting**: For symmetry questions, common errors include counting too many or too few lines (e.g., saying a rectangle has 4 lines of symmetry instead of 2)

### Net and 3D Shape Distractors
1. **Mirror-image net**: A net that looks similar but is reflected, which would not fold into the same shape
2. **Extra/missing face**: A net with one face too many or too few
3. **Wrong orientation**: The correct shape but rotated so markings do not match

### Coordinate Distractors
1. **Swapped x and y**: The coordinates with x and y values reversed — catches children who confuse "along the corridor, up the stairs"
2. **Wrong quadrant**: A point reflected into the wrong quadrant (sign error)
3. **Off-by-one on grid**: Misreading the grid scale by one unit

## Common GL Traps

1. **"Not drawn to scale" trap**: The diagram deliberately shows an angle that looks like 90deg but is actually 85deg or 95deg. Children who estimate from the picture instead of calculating get caught.

2. **Isosceles ambiguity**: Giving one angle of an isosceles triangle without specifying whether it is the unique angle or a base angle. GL resolves this with an extra clue (e.g., "angle B is greater than 60deg") but children who miss the clue choose the wrong interpretation.

3. **Square-rectangle conflation**: A question asks about rectangles, and a square is one of the options. Many children reject the square because they do not know a square IS a rectangle. GL exploits this misconception.

4. **Non-standard orientation**: Showing a triangle "upside down" or a square rotated 45deg (appearing as a diamond). Children who only recognise shapes in standard orientation fail to identify them.

5. **Reflex angle blindness**: Asking about interior angles when one is reflex (>180deg). Children who have not practised reflex angles may not even consider this possibility.

6. **The "all angles" red herring**: Providing side length information that is not needed for an angle calculation. Children waste time or get confused trying to use irrelevant data.

7. **Vertically opposite angles ignored**: In intersecting line diagrams, children calculate the supplementary angle (on a straight line) when the question asks for the vertically opposite angle — which is simply equal to the given angle.

8. **Exterior vs interior angle confusion**: Questions about the exterior angle of a triangle where children use the interior angle sum rule incorrectly, forgetting that the exterior angle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.

9. **Net folding mismatch**: Showing a net with marked corners or faces and asking which pairs meet. Children who cannot mentally fold the net often guess based on proximity on the flat net rather than actual 3D adjacency.

10. **Symmetry overcounting**: Children count axes of symmetry that do not actually work (e.g., diagonal "symmetry" of a non-square rectangle) or confuse line symmetry with rotational symmetry.

11. **Coordinate axis confusion**: Mixing up x-axis and y-axis, or reading the wrong scale when grid lines are not every unit.

12. **Clockwise vs anticlockwise**: Rotation questions where the direction matters. Children who default to one direction without reading the question get the wrong answer.

## Key Shape Properties Reference

### Triangles
| Type | Sides | Angles | Lines of Symmetry | Rotational Symmetry |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Equilateral | 3 equal | All 60deg | 3 | Order 3 |
| Isosceles | 2 equal | 2 equal | 1 | Order 1 |
| Scalene | All different | All different | 0 | Order 1 |
| Right-angled | Varies | One 90deg | 0 (or 1 if isosceles right) | Order 1 |

**Key rule:** Angles in any triangle sum to **180deg**.

### Quadrilaterals
| Shape | Sides | Angles | Parallel Sides | Lines of Symmetry | Rotational Symmetry |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Square | 4 equal | 4 x 90deg | 2 pairs | 4 | Order 4 |
| Rectangle | 2 pairs equal | 4 x 90deg | 2 pairs | 2 | Order 2 |
| Rhombus | 4 equal | 2 pairs equal (not 90deg) | 2 pairs | 2 | Order 2 |
| Parallelogram | 2 pairs equal | 2 pairs equal | 2 pairs | 0 | Order 2 |
| Trapezium | Varies | Varies | 1 pair | 0 (or 1 if isosceles) | Order 1 |
| Kite | 2 pairs adjacent equal | 1 pair equal | 0 | 1 | Order 1 |

**Key rule:** Angles in any quadrilateral sum to **360deg**.

**Important class inclusions:**
- A square IS a rectangle (special case with 4 equal sides)
- A square IS a rhombus (special case with 4 right angles)
- A rectangle IS a parallelogram (special case with 4 right angles)
- A rhombus IS a parallelogram (special case with 4 equal sides)

### Regular Polygons
| Shape | Sides | Interior Angle Sum | Each Interior Angle | Each Exterior Angle | Lines of Symmetry |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Equilateral triangle | 3 | 180deg | 60deg | 120deg | 3 |
| Square | 4 | 360deg | 90deg | 90deg | 4 |
| Regular pentagon | 5 | 540deg | 108deg | 72deg | 5 |
| Regular hexagon | 6 | 720deg | 120deg | 60deg | 6 |
| Regular octagon | 8 | 1080deg | 135deg | 45deg | 8 |

**Key formula:** Interior angle sum = **(n - 2) x 180deg** where n = number of sides.
**Key rule:** Exterior angles of ANY polygon sum to **360deg**.

### 3D Shapes
| Shape | Faces | Edges | Vertices |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cube | 6 | 12 | 8 |
| Cuboid | 6 | 12 | 8 |
| Triangular prism | 5 | 9 | 6 |
| Square-based pyramid | 5 | 8 | 5 |
| Triangular pyramid (tetrahedron) | 4 | 6 | 4 |
| Cylinder | 3 (2 flat + 1 curved) | 2 | 0 |
| Cone | 2 (1 flat + 1 curved) | 1 | 1 |
| Sphere | 1 (curved) | 0 | 0 |

**Euler's formula:** Faces + Vertices - Edges = 2 (for all polyhedra)

### Essential Angle Facts
- Angles on a **straight line** sum to **180deg**
- Angles at a **point** sum to **360deg**
- **Vertically opposite** angles are **equal**
- Angles in a **triangle** sum to **180deg**
- Angles in a **quadrilateral** sum to **360deg**
- **Exterior angle** of a triangle = sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles
- **Complementary** angles sum to **90deg**
- **Supplementary** angles sum to **180deg**

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand and use these terms fluently:

**Angle types:** acute, right angle, obtuse, straight angle, reflex, full turn

**Angle relationships:** vertically opposite, supplementary, complementary, interior angle, exterior angle

**Triangle types:** equilateral, isosceles, scalene, right-angled

**Quadrilateral types:** square, rectangle, rhombus, parallelogram, trapezium, kite

**Polygon names:** triangle, quadrilateral, pentagon, hexagon, heptagon, octagon

**Shape properties:** side, vertex/vertices, edge, face, diagonal, parallel, perpendicular

**Symmetry:** line of symmetry (mirror line), rotational symmetry, order of rotational symmetry

**3D terms:** face, edge, vertex, net, prism, pyramid, cross-section

**Position and direction:** coordinates, x-axis, y-axis, origin, quadrant, reflect, translate, rotate, clockwise, anticlockwise

**Measurement of angles:** degrees, protractor, right angle (90deg), straight angle (180deg), full turn (360deg)

**Other:** regular (all sides and angles equal), irregular, congruent (same shape and size), similar (same shape, different size), perpendicular, parallel, diagonal

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 (Age 9-10) — Geometry: Properties of Shapes
- Know angles are measured in degrees; estimate and compare acute, obtuse, and reflex angles
- Draw and measure angles in degrees using a protractor
- Identify angles at a point, angles on a straight line, and vertically opposite angles
- Use the properties of rectangles to find missing lengths and angles
- Distinguish between regular and irregular polygons based on reasoning about equal sides and angles

### Year 5 — Geometry: Position and Direction
- Identify, describe, and represent the position of a shape following a reflection or translation, using the appropriate language, and know that the shape has not changed
- Work with coordinates in the first quadrant

### Year 6 (Age 10-11) — Geometry: Properties of Shapes
- Draw 2D shapes using given dimensions and angles
- Recognise, describe, and build simple 3D shapes, including making nets
- Compare and classify geometric shapes based on their properties and sizes
- Find unknown angles in any triangle, quadrilateral, or regular polygon
- Recognise angles where they meet at a point, are on a straight line, or are vertically opposite, and find missing angles
- Illustrate and name parts of circles: radius, diameter, circumference, and know that diameter = 2 x radius

### Year 6 — Geometry: Position and Direction
- Describe positions on the full coordinate grid (all four quadrants)
- Draw and translate simple shapes on the coordinate plane
- Reflect shapes in the axes

### What GL adds beyond the curriculum
- GL questions are set at the **top end of Year 5 to middle of Year 6** difficulty
- Some questions push into early Year 7 territory: interior angles of regular polygons using the (n-2) x 180deg formula, compound angle problems with overlapping shapes
- The multiple-choice format means children must be confident enough to select from plausible-looking options rather than showing working
- Time pressure (1 minute per question average) means angle calculations must be fluent, not slow and methodical

## Teaching Notes

### What Children Find Hardest
1. **Isosceles triangle angle problems** — by far the most common stumbling block. Children forget there are two possible configurations (the given angle could be the unique angle OR one of the equal pair) and default to the simpler interpretation
2. **Recognising shapes in non-standard orientations** — a triangle pointing downward, a square rotated to look like a diamond, or a right-angled triangle with the right angle at the top rather than bottom-left
3. **Mental folding of nets** — children who cannot visualise 3D from 2D find net questions extremely difficult. This is a spatial reasoning skill that needs explicit practice
4. **Remembering which angle sum applies where** — 180deg for triangles, 360deg for quadrilaterals, 180deg for a straight line, 360deg at a point. Under pressure, children mix these up
5. **Multi-step angle problems** — any question requiring 2+ steps (e.g., find one angle using straight line rule, then use it to find another in a triangle) has a significant drop in success rate
6. **Coordinates with negative numbers** — working in all four quadrants, particularly reflections that cross axes
7. **Reflex angles** — many children simply have not encountered or practised reflex angles and do not know what they are
8. **Class inclusion of shapes** — that a square is a special rectangle, a rectangle is a special parallelogram. Most children treat these as entirely separate categories.

### Common Misconceptions (Specific to 10-11 Year Olds)
- "A square is not a rectangle" — the most persistent shape misconception
- "A diamond is a shape" — actually a square or rhombus in non-standard orientation
- A rectangle has 4 lines of symmetry (it has 2 — children count the diagonals, which are NOT lines of symmetry for a non-square rectangle)
- A right-angled triangle has three right angles (confusion from the name)
- Vertically opposite angles add up to 180deg (they are actually equal)
- All triangles have a horizontal base (orientation dependence)
- If you know one angle of a triangle is 40deg, the other two must be 70deg each (assumes isosceles with no basis)
- Parallel lines must be horizontal (they can be at any angle, as long as they never meet)
- Perpendicular means "vertical" (it actually means "at right angles to")
- On a coordinate grid, (3, 5) and (5, 3) are the same point

### Effective Practice Strategies
1. **Drill angle sum rules** until they are automatic — flashcard-style: "Angles in a triangle?" "180deg!" No hesitation.
2. **Always show shapes in varied orientations** — never let children only see triangles pointing upward or squares with horizontal bases
3. **Practise isosceles triangles both ways** — give the unique angle sometimes, give a base angle other times. Make children identify which case they are in.
4. **Use physical nets** — cutting and folding actual paper nets builds the spatial reasoning that no amount of diagram work can replicate
5. **Timed practice** — 1 minute per question is tight. Children need to be able to identify the question type, recall the rule, and calculate within this window.
6. **Expose reflex angles early** — do not leave them as an afterthought. Children need to know they exist and how to calculate them (360deg minus the non-reflex angle).
7. **Practise reading "Not drawn to scale" diagrams** — explicitly train children NOT to estimate from diagrams and to always calculate.
