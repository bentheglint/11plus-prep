# GL Assessment Research: Long Multiplication

## Format

- **Paper:** GL 11+ Maths (50 questions, 50 minutes, all multiple-choice)
- **Options:** 5 per question (A-E) in our app; GL official papers use 4-5
- **Calculator:** Not permitted
- **Topic weighting:** "Number" questions dominate the GL maths paper -- typically five times more number questions than any other category. Long multiplication falls within this dominant number/arithmetic strand.
- **Question style:** GL rarely tests long multiplication as a naked calculation ("What is 347 x 26?"). Instead, multiplication is almost always embedded in a context: word problems, multi-step problems, area calculations, money, scaling, or combined with other operations (BODMAS). A child who can only do column multiplication but cannot identify when to multiply will struggle.
- **Time pressure:** 1 minute per question means children cannot afford to use the full column method for every calculation. Mental strategies, estimation, and efficient written methods are essential. Questions are designed so that a child with strong number sense can solve them faster than one who relies purely on procedural methods.
- **Difficulty progression:** GL papers are fixed-difficulty (not adaptive). Questions generally progress from easier to harder within the paper, though this is not strictly guaranteed. The final 10-15 questions are typically the most challenging.

---

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

Long multiplication in the GL 11+ tests a blend of procedural fluency, conceptual understanding, and application. The questions rarely test one skill in isolation -- they combine multiplication with reasoning.

| Category | Approx. Weight | Description |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Formal written method** | ~15-20% | Straightforward column multiplication: 3-4 digits by 1-2 digits. Tests procedure accuracy. |
| **Word problems (single-step)** | ~20-25% | "A box holds 48 pencils. A school orders 35 boxes. How many pencils?" Requires identifying multiplication as the operation. |
| **Multi-step problems** | ~25-30% | Multiplication combined with addition, subtraction, or division. E.g., "Find total cost then calculate change" or "Find area then subtract." |
| **Mental multiplication strategies** | ~10-15% | Problems designed for mental calculation: multiplying by 10/100/1000, doubling/halving, using near-multiples, partitioning. |
| **Estimation and checking** | ~5-10% | "Which is the best estimate for 387 x 19?" or using inverse operations to verify. |
| **Multiplication within other topics** | ~15-20% | Area/perimeter calculations, scaling recipes, unit conversions, speed-distance-time, ratio -- where multiplication is a tool, not the focus. |

### Specific Skills Expected

1. **Times tables fluency (up to 12 x 12)** -- instant recall, no counting up
2. **Multiply up to 4 digits by a 1-digit number** (short multiplication)
3. **Multiply up to 4 digits by a 2-digit number** (long multiplication / column method)
4. **Multiply by 10, 100, 1000** -- and by multiples of 10 (e.g., x 40 = x 4 x 10)
5. **Multiply decimals** -- understanding place value when multiplying with decimal numbers
6. **Use commutativity** -- 4 x 12 x 5 = 4 x 5 x 12 = 20 x 12 = 240
7. **Use the distributive law** -- 39 x 7 = (30 x 7) + (9 x 7) = 210 + 63 = 273
8. **Use associativity** -- (2 x 3) x 4 = 2 x (3 x 4) = 2 x 12 = 24
9. **Estimate products using rounding** -- 387 x 19 is roughly 400 x 20 = 8,000
10. **Use inverse operations (division) to check** -- if 347 x 26 = 9,022, then 9,022 / 26 should = 347
11. **Recognise square and cube numbers** -- 7 x 7 = 49, 5 x 5 x 5 = 125
12. **Apply BODMAS/order of operations** -- 2 + 3 x 4 = 14, not 20

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**What it looks like:**
- Single-step problems with straightforward context
- Numbers that align with familiar times tables (multiples of 2, 5, 10)
- 2-3 digit number multiplied by a single digit
- Context is simple and unambiguous ("There are 6 bags with 24 sweets in each")
- No carrying required, or carrying is minimal (one carry at most)
- Mental calculation is feasible for a well-prepared child

**Typical question formats:**
- "What is 125 x 4?"
- "A packet contains 15 biscuits. How many biscuits are in 6 packets?"
- "What is 230 x 3?"
- "Multiply 56 by 10"
- "What is 7 x 8 x 5?"

**Number characteristics:**
- Products up to ~2,000
- Multiplier is a single digit (2-9) or a multiple of 10
- Multiplicand is 2-3 digits, often with zeros (150, 230, 400)
- Times tables facts up to 12 x 12 tested directly

**Distractor profile:**
- One option is a common addition error (added instead of multiplied)
- One option reflects a times table slip (e.g., 6 x 8 = 42 instead of 48)
- Options are spread enough that estimation can eliminate 2-3

---

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**What it looks like:**
- Two-step problems requiring multiplication plus another operation
- 3-4 digit number multiplied by a 2-digit number
- Context requires the child to identify which operation is needed
- Some carrying required in the column method
- May involve money (pounds and pence), measurements, or simple scaling
- Cannot easily be done entirely mentally -- written method needed

**Typical question formats:**
- "A school trip costs GBP12.50 per child. There are 28 children going. What is the total cost?"
- "A rectangular garden is 15 m long and 23 m wide. What is its area?"
- "Ben saves GBP35 per week. How much does he save in 14 weeks?"
- "348 x 27 = ?"
- "A factory produces 1,250 widgets per day. How many does it produce in 24 days?"
- "Pencils cost 45p each. How much do 36 pencils cost? Give your answer in pounds."

**Number characteristics:**
- Products in the range ~2,000-50,000
- Multiplier is 2 digits (not a multiple of 10)
- Multiple carries required
- May involve converting units (pence to pounds, cm to m)

**Distractor profile:**
- One option reflects a place value error (forgot the placeholder zero)
- One option reflects a carrying error (didn't carry, or carried wrong digit)
- One option is the result of a different operation (addition or subtraction)
- Options are closer together, so estimation alone won't distinguish them
- Partial product errors feature (e.g., correctly multiplied by ones but not tens)

---

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**What it looks like:**
- Multi-step problems (3+ steps) combining multiplication with other operations
- Requires careful reading and extraction of relevant information from wordy contexts
- Numbers that don't simplify neatly -- no helpful patterns
- May require working backwards (inverse operations)
- Contexts include speed-distance-time, area with missing dimensions, scaling recipes, compound problems
- May embed multiplication within BODMAS expressions
- Estimation is insufficient -- precise calculation required
- Time pressure is a factor: the calculation is lengthy

**Typical question formats:**
- "A hall has 32 rows of chairs. Each row has 28 chairs. For a concert, 156 extra chairs are added. How many chairs are there in total?"
- "A gardener needs 175 bulbs per flower bed. She has 12 flower beds but only has 1,800 bulbs. How many more does she need?"
- "Train A travels at 85 mph for 3 hours. Train B travels at 65 mph for 4 hours. How much further does Train B travel?"
- "What is 2,347 x 46?"
- "A recipe for 4 people uses 350g of flour. How much flour is needed for 14 people?"
- "The product of two numbers is 3,024. One number is 48. What is the other number?"

**Number characteristics:**
- Products can exceed 100,000
- 4-digit multiplicand with 2-digit multiplier
- Multiple carries with awkward digits (7s, 8s, 9s)
- May involve decimals or require unit conversion as part of the answer
- Numbers chosen to maximise the chance of carrying errors

**Distractor profile:**
- All five options are plausible -- each represents a specific error pathway
- Differences between options may be small (e.g., 9,022 vs 9,032 vs 9,122)
- One option is "nearly right" -- correct partial product but wrong final addition
- One option reverses the multiplier digits (x 46 vs x 64)
- Estimation can only eliminate 1-2 options at best

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Direct Calculation
"What is 456 x 37?"
- Tests procedural fluency
- Appears at all difficulty levels (number size determines difficulty)
- GL typically embeds this in a word problem rather than presenting it bare

### Type 2: Cost/Price Problems
"Tickets cost GBP14.75 each. How much do 8 tickets cost?"
- Very common GL format
- Tests multiplication + decimal handling + unit awareness
- Often involves money (pounds/pence conversion)
- May add a multi-step element: "How much change from GBP200?"

### Type 3: Area and Measurement
"A rectangular field is 125 m long and 48 m wide. What is its area?"
- Multiplication is the tool; area is the context
- D2-D3: may require converting between units (cm/m)
- Can combine with perimeter: "What is the difference between the area and perimeter?"

### Type 4: Scaling and Proportion
"A recipe for 6 people uses 450g of sugar. How much sugar is needed for 15 people?"
- Requires identifying the scale factor (15/6 = 2.5) then multiplying
- D2-D3: scale factors may not be whole numbers
- Often involves recipes, map scales, or model dimensions

### Type 5: Multi-Step with Mixed Operations
"A school bought 24 boxes of pens. Each box contains 36 pens. 58 pens were broken. How many working pens are there?"
- Step 1: 24 x 36 = 864
- Step 2: 864 - 58 = 806
- Tests whether child completes all steps and in the right order

### Type 6: Inverse/Missing Number
"___ x 23 = 1,587. What is the missing number?"
- Tests understanding that division is the inverse of multiplication
- Child must divide: 1,587 / 23 = 69
- GL may frame as: "The product of two numbers is 1,587. One number is 23. What is the other?"

### Type 7: Estimation
"Which of these is closest to 389 x 21?"
A) 6,000  B) 7,000  C) 8,000  D) 9,000  E) 10,000
- Tests rounding and approximation: 389 ~ 400, 21 ~ 20, so ~ 8,000
- May ask "approximately" or "roughly" to signal estimation
- D1-D2: round both numbers. D3: one number rounds up, one down, making it less obvious.

### Type 8: BODMAS/Order of Operations
"What is 5 + 3 x 12 - 4?"
- Tests whether the child multiplies before adding/subtracting
- Common error: working left to right (5 + 3 = 8, 8 x 12 = 96)
- Correct: 5 + 36 - 4 = 37
- May include brackets: "(5 + 3) x 12 = 96"

### Type 9: Function Machines / Number Patterns
"A function machine multiplies the input by 7 then adds 15. If the input is 23, what is the output?"
- Tests multiplication as part of a sequence of operations
- May ask for the input given the output (inverse thinking)

## Distractor Design

GL designs wrong answers to catch specific, predictable errors. Every distractor represents a real mistake a child might make. This is how they build distractors for multiplication questions:

### 1. Place Value / Placeholder Error
**The mistake:** Forgetting to write the placeholder zero when multiplying by the tens digit.
**Example:** 347 x 26
- Correct: 347 x 6 = 2,082; 347 x 20 = 6,940; total = 9,022
- Error: 347 x 6 = 2,082; 347 x 2 = 694 (no zero); total = 2,776
- Distractor: **2,776**

### 2. Carrying Error
**The mistake:** Forgetting to add the carried digit, or carrying the wrong amount.
**Example:** 456 x 7
- Correct: 6x7=42 (write 2 carry 4), 5x7=35+4=39 (write 9 carry 3), 4x7=28+3=31 -> 3,192
- Error: 6x7=42 (write 2 carry 4), 5x7=35 (forgot carry) -> 3,152
- Distractor: **3,152**

### 3. Times Table Slip
**The mistake:** Getting a times table fact wrong, especially in the 6, 7, 8 range.
**Example:** 7 x 8 = 54 (instead of 56), 6 x 7 = 48 (instead of 42), 9 x 6 = 56 (instead of 54)
- These propagate through the entire calculation, producing answers that are close but wrong
- GL uses the "difficult middle" (6, 7, 8) deliberately

### 4. Operation Confusion
**The mistake:** Adding instead of multiplying (or vice versa), especially in word problems.
**Example:** "6 bags of 24 sweets"
- Correct: 6 x 24 = 144
- Error: 6 + 24 = 30
- Distractor: **30**

### 5. Partial Completion
**The mistake:** Only completing part of a multi-step problem.
**Example:** "24 boxes of 36 pens, 58 broken. How many working pens?"
- Correct: (24 x 36) - 58 = 806
- Error 1: 24 x 36 = 864 (forgot to subtract) -> Distractor: **864**
- Error 2: 36 - 58 = ... (tried to subtract from wrong number)

### 6. Unit Conversion Error
**The mistake:** Forgetting to convert pence to pounds or cm to m.
**Example:** "45p each, buy 36. Answer in pounds."
- Correct: 45 x 36 = 1,620p = GBP16.20
- Error: 1,620 (left in pence) or GBP162.0 (decimal point wrong)
- Distractor: **GBP1,620** or **GBP162.00**

### 7. Digit Reversal
**The mistake:** Reversing the multiplier or reading numbers carelessly.
**Example:** 347 x 26 vs 347 x 62
- Correct: 9,022
- Error: 347 x 62 = 21,514
- Distractor: **21,514**

### 8. Off-by-One in Context
**The mistake:** Misinterpreting "how many more" or inclusive/exclusive counting.
**Example:** "She needs 2,100 bulbs but has 1,800. How many more?"
- Correct: 300
- Error: 2,100 (gives total instead of difference)

## Common GL Traps

1. **The "nearly right" answer:** GL places a distractor that is within 10-50 of the correct answer, catching children who make a single carrying error. These are devastatingly effective because the child feels confident in their working.

2. **Wordy problems that bury the numbers:** The question includes irrelevant information or a long narrative. The child must extract only the relevant numbers and decide which operation to use. Children who rush misidentify the operation.

3. **Unit mismatch in the question:** The question gives values in different units (e.g., "3.5 metres" and "75 centimetres") and the child must convert before multiplying. Children who don't convert get an answer that appears as a distractor.

4. **The "too easy" trap:** A D2/D3 question where an obvious but incomplete answer is available. E.g., "How much change from GBP100 after buying 7 items at GBP12 each?" -- children who stop at 7 x 12 = 84 select that answer instead of calculating 100 - 84 = 16.

5. **Multiplying by zero confusion:** Questions involving numbers with internal zeros (e.g., 3,060 x 24) where children mishandle the zero in the middle.

6. **Decimal multiplication traps:** "0.5 x 36" -- children expect multiplication to make numbers bigger and may write 180 instead of 18. The misconception that "multiplication always makes bigger" is a deliberate GL target.

7. **BODMAS bait:** Expressions like "5 + 3 x 4" where the addition appears first. Children who work left-to-right get 32 instead of 17. GL always includes the left-to-right error as a distractor.

8. **The "clever" mental shortcut gone wrong:** For example, multiplying by 25 by dividing by 4 and multiplying by 100 -- if the number isn't divisible by 4, children get stuck or make errors. GL sometimes designs questions where an obvious shortcut doesn't work cleanly.

9. **Reversed roles in word problems:** "How many groups of 15 can be made from 180?" requires division (180 / 15 = 12), not multiplication. But the word problem mentions both numbers and children instinctively multiply.

10. **Two-part questions where step 1 is multiplication:** "Find the total cost, then work out how many GBP20 notes are needed." Children who stop after multiplication (step 1) find their answer among the options.

## Key Concepts Reference

### The Column Method (Long Multiplication)

```
    3 4 7
  x   2 6
  -------
  2 0 8 2    <- 347 x 6 (multiply by ones)
  6 9 4 0    <- 347 x 20 (multiply by tens, placeholder zero)
  -------
  9 0 2 2    <- add partial products
```

**Critical steps children must master:**
1. Line up digits by place value
2. Multiply by the ones digit first, carrying as needed
3. Write a placeholder zero before multiplying by the tens digit
4. Multiply by the tens digit, carrying as needed
5. Add the two partial products carefully

### Mental Multiplication Strategies

| Strategy | Example | When to Use |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Multiply by 10/100/1000** | 47 x 100 = 4,700 | Shift digits left |
| **Multiply by multiples of 10** | 36 x 40 = 36 x 4 x 10 = 1,440 | Break into x factor x 10 |
| **Partitioning (distributive law)** | 23 x 7 = (20 x 7) + (3 x 7) = 140 + 21 = 161 | Any 2-digit x 1-digit |
| **Doubling and halving** | 25 x 48 = 50 x 24 = 1,200 | When one factor is near 25 or 50 |
| **Near multiples** | 99 x 7 = (100 x 7) - 7 = 693 | Factor close to a round number |
| **Multiply by 5** | 5 x 74 = 74/2 x 10 = 37 x 10 = 370 | Halve then x 10 |
| **Multiply by 11** | 11 x 36 = 360 + 36 = 396 | x 10 then add one more lot |
| **Commutativity** | 4 x 13 x 25 = 4 x 25 x 13 = 100 x 13 = 1,300 | Reorder to find friendly pairs |
| **Associativity** | 8 x 15 = (8 x 5) x 3 = 40 x 3 = 120 | Break a factor into parts |

### Square and Cube Numbers (Year 5-6)

| Square numbers | Cube numbers |
|---------------|-------------|
| 1, 4, 9, 16, 25 | 1, 8, 27, 64, 125 |
| 36, 49, 64, 81, 100 | 216, 343, 512, 729, 1000 |
| 121, 144 | |

Children should recognise these on sight and know the notation (5^2 = 25, 4^3 = 64).

### Factor Pairs

Knowing factor pairs supports efficient multiplication and division:
- 24: 1x24, 2x12, 3x8, 4x6
- 36: 1x36, 2x18, 3x12, 4x9, 6x6
- 48: 1x48, 2x24, 3x16, 4x12, 6x8

### BODMAS / Order of Operations

**B**rackets, **O**rders (powers/roots), **D**ivision, **M**ultiplication, **A**ddition, **S**ubtraction

- Division and Multiplication are equal priority (left to right)
- Addition and Subtraction are equal priority (left to right)
- Example: 3 + 4 x 5 = 3 + 20 = 23 (NOT 35)
- Example: (3 + 4) x 5 = 7 x 5 = 35

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand and respond to all of these terms in GL questions:

| Term | Meaning | GL Usage |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Product** | The result of multiplying two or more numbers | "What is the product of 15 and 23?" |
| **Multiply / Times** | The operation itself | "Multiply 347 by 26" |
| **Factor** | A number that divides exactly into another | "What are the factors of 36?" |
| **Multiple** | A number in a given times table | "What is the smallest multiple of 7 greater than 50?" |
| **Multiplicand** | The number being multiplied | 347 in 347 x 26 |
| **Multiplier** | The number you multiply by | 26 in 347 x 26 |
| **Partial product** | The intermediate result when multiplying by each digit | 2,082 and 6,940 in the example above |
| **Estimate** | An approximate answer using rounding | "Estimate the product of 389 and 21" |
| **Inverse** | The opposite operation | "Division is the inverse of multiplication" |
| **Commutative** | Order doesn't matter | "5 x 3 = 3 x 5" |
| **Square number** | A number multiplied by itself | "49 is a square number (7 x 7)" |
| **Cube number** | A number multiplied by itself three times | "27 is a cube number (3 x 3 x 3)" |
| **Array** | An arrangement of objects in rows and columns | Visual representation of multiplication |
| **Total / Altogether** | Signal words for multiplication (or addition) in word problems | "How many altogether?" |
| **Each / Every / Per** | Signal words indicating multiplication in word problems | "GBP3.50 per ticket" |
| **Groups of** | Multiplication context | "7 groups of 12" |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 Statutory Requirements (Multiplication & Division)
- Identify multiples and factors, including finding all factor pairs of a number, and common factors of two numbers
- Know and use the vocabulary of prime numbers, prime factors, and composite numbers
- Establish whether a number up to 100 is prime and recall prime numbers up to 19
- **Multiply numbers up to 4 digits by a one- or two-digit number using a formal written method, including long multiplication for two-digit numbers**
- Multiply and divide numbers mentally, drawing upon known facts
- **Multiply and divide whole numbers and those involving decimals by 10, 100, and 1,000**
- Recognise and use square numbers and cube numbers, and the notation for squared (^2) and cubed (^3)
- Solve problems involving multiplication and division, including using their knowledge of factors and multiples, squares and cubes

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements (Multiplication & Division)
- **Multiply multi-digit numbers up to 4 digits by a two-digit whole number using the formal written method of long multiplication**
- Divide numbers up to 4 digits by a two-digit whole number using the formal written method of long division, and interpret remainders
- Perform mental calculations, including with mixed operations and large numbers
- Identify common factors, common multiples, and prime numbers
- **Use their knowledge of the order of operations to carry out calculations involving the four operations**
- Solve problems involving addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division
- **Use estimation to check answers to calculations and determine, in the context of a problem, an appropriate degree of accuracy**

### Year 6 Non-Statutory Guidance
- Pupils should "explore the order of operations using brackets; for example, 2 + 1 x 3 = 5 and (2 + 1) x 3 = 9"
- "Use the distributive law: 39 x 7 = 30 x 7 + 9 x 7"
- "Use the associative law: (2 x 3) x 4 = 2 x (3 x 4)"
- "Develop efficient mental methods: 4 x 12 x 5 = 4 x 5 x 12 = 20 x 12 = 240"

### Progression from Earlier Years
| Year | Multiplication Objective |
|------|------------------------|
| Y3 | Recall and use multiplication facts for 3, 4, 8 tables; write and calculate using the multiplication sign |
| Y4 | Recall all multiplication tables to 12 x 12; use formal written method for 2-3 digits x 1 digit |
| Y5 | Multiply up to 4 digits x 1-2 digits using formal written methods including long multiplication |
| Y6 | Multiply multi-digit numbers up to 4 digits x 2 digits using long multiplication; order of operations |

The 11+ exam (typically sat at start of Year 6 or during Year 5) tests everything up to and including Year 6 objectives. Children need to be working confidently at Year 6 standard before the exam.

## Teaching Notes

### Times Tables Are Non-Negotiable
The single biggest predictor of success in GL multiplication questions is times tables fluency. A child who hesitates on 7 x 8 or 6 x 9 will:
- Make more carrying errors in long multiplication
- Take longer per question, losing time across the paper
- Be more susceptible to times table slip distractors

**Target:** Instant recall (under 3 seconds) for any fact up to 12 x 12, in any order. The "difficult middle" of 6, 7, 8 times tables needs particular attention -- these are the facts GL exploits most.

### Teach the Method, Then Teach When NOT to Use It
Column multiplication is essential, but children also need to recognise when a mental strategy is faster:
- 25 x 36: doubling and halving gives 50 x 18 = 900 (faster than column method)
- 99 x 14: near-multiple gives (100 x 14) - 14 = 1,386
- 15 x 40: partitioning gives 10 x 40 + 5 x 40 = 400 + 200 = 600

GL's time pressure rewards children who choose the right method for each question.

### Common Errors to Watch For and Drill

| Error | Example | How It Manifests |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Forgetting placeholder zero** | 347 x 26: writes 694 instead of 6,940 for tens row | Answer far too small |
| **Not carrying** | 7 x 8 = 56, writes 6 but forgets to carry 5 | Answer slightly wrong |
| **Carrying the wrong digit** | 7 x 8 = 56, carries 6 instead of 5 | Answer slightly wrong in opposite direction |
| **Multiplying tens by tens** | In 64 x 17, multiplies 6x1=6 and 4x7=28 separately | Completely wrong method |
| **Adding instead of multiplying** | Word problem: "5 bags of 20" -> 5 + 20 = 25 | Answer far too small |
| **Forgetting to add partial products** | Writes 2,082 and 6,940 but doesn't add them | Gives a partial product as answer |
| **Misreading the question** | Multiplies when should divide, or uses wrong number from question | Common in wordy problems |
| **Unit conversion omission** | Gives 1,620p instead of GBP16.20 | Gets calculation right but answer wrong |

### Estimation as a Safety Net
Teach children to estimate BEFORE calculating:
- 347 x 26: roughly 350 x 25 = 8,750. If their answer is 2,776 (placeholder error), they should recognise it's too small.
- This catches the most catastrophic errors and can eliminate 2-3 multiple-choice options immediately.

### Practice Recommendations for GL Preparation
1. **Daily times tables practice** -- random order, timed (aim for under 3 seconds per fact)
2. **Column method with checking** -- do the multiplication, then estimate to verify
3. **Word problem interpretation** -- practice identifying which operation(s) are needed before calculating
4. **Mixed operations** -- problems that combine multiplication with addition/subtraction/division
5. **Money problems** -- multiplying prices by quantities, giving change, converting pence to pounds
6. **Timed practice papers** -- simulate the 1-minute-per-question pressure
7. **Error spotting exercises** -- show a worked multiplication with an error and ask the child to find it
8. **Mental strategies** -- explicitly teach and practise all strategies in the table above

### What Separates a Pass from a Top Score
- **Pass-level children** can do column multiplication accurately but slowly, and handle straightforward word problems
- **Top-scoring children** choose between mental and written methods efficiently, estimate to check, rarely make carrying errors, read multi-step problems carefully, and manage their time so they attempt all 50 questions
