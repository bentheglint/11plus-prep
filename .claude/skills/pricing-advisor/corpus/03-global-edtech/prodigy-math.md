## Overview

Prodigy Math is a Canadian edtech platform (founded 2011, Toronto) offering curriculum-aligned maths games for students in grades 1–8 (UK equivalent: Years 2–9). It has over 150 million registered users globally and is embedded in a significant number of North American schools. The product is structured as an adaptive maths RPG: students battle monsters and progress through a fantasy world by answering curriculum-aligned maths questions. Prodigy's core model is aggressively parent-funded: all academic content is free (Prodigy works with schools to ensure no student is educationally disadvantaged by inability to pay), and paid memberships unlock cosmetic and gameplay content — additional outfits, pets, worlds — rather than additional maths. This makes it one of the clearest examples of a "free curriculum, paid engagement layer" model in global edtech.

## Pricing tiers

**Free (Student)**
- Price: £0 / $0
- Billing: Ongoing
- Included: Full access to all curriculum-aligned maths content, adaptive algorithm, full game access at a basic cosmetic level, teacher and school integration, progress tracking for teachers.

**Core Membership**
- Price: $59/year (approx. £47/year)
- Billing: Annual only
- Included: 100+ exclusive member items (outfits, wands, pets), member-exclusive zones and quests, early access to new content, monthly members-only events, member badge and username tag. No academic content advantage over free.

**Plus Membership**
- Price: $89/year (approx. £71/year)
- Billing: Annual only
- Included: Everything in Core, plus accelerated item collection, more exclusive items, dedicated Plus events, priority feature access.

**Ultimate Membership**
- Price: $119/year (approx. £95/year)
- Billing: Annual only
- Included: Everything in Plus, plus the largest selection of exclusive items, ultimate-only events, maximum cosmetic customisation.

**Parent Dashboard (Separate)**
- The parent dashboard with detailed progress reporting — question-level data, time-on-task, curriculum coverage, areas of weakness — is available as a separate paid add-on (pricing varies; approximately $9.95–$15/month or bundled into membership tiers in some configurations). This is a notable design decision: the academic insight layer is partially paywalled even though the academic content is free.

**Multi-child discount**
- 25% discount when purchasing memberships for multiple children under one parent account. This is applied at checkout rather than being a separate plan type.

## Free vs paid (the core question for global edtech)

Prodigy's philosophy is explicit: no child should be academically disadvantaged because their family cannot afford a membership. All maths content, all adaptive difficulty adjustments, all teacher tools, and all school integrations are completely free. Paid tiers sell game experience and cosmetics — the equivalent of an in-game store in a children's video game.

This creates an interesting ethical and commercial tension. The free academic content drives school adoption (teachers advocate for the product), which brings students onto the platform. Parents see engaged children doing maths and feel social pressure to buy the cosmetic membership so their child doesn't feel left out compared to friends with member perks. Critics have noted this creates peer pressure dynamics within classrooms, where free students visibly lack cosmetic items that paid students display.

## Family / multi-user pricing

The 25% multi-child discount is applied automatically when purchasing memberships for siblings. There is no formal "family plan" with a single subscription covering all children — instead, each child has an individual membership at a discounted rate. For a family with two children, this means approximately $89.25/year for two Core memberships (25% off the second), versus $118 at full price. Annual billing only — there is no monthly option for any membership tier.

## Unusual mechanics

- **Cosmetics-only paywall**: Prodigy is one of very few edtech products globally where the paid tier has zero academic content value. This is a deliberate ethical and commercial choice that enables school integration (no teacher can be accused of creating educational disadvantage) while still monetising parent willingness to pay for a child's happiness.
- **Annual-only billing**: All membership tiers are annual only, with no monthly option. This reduces churn dramatically — once purchased, a membership typically runs for the full year.
- **Parent dashboard partial paywalling**: The detailed academic insight layer (progress data, weakness identification) is paywalled or bundled into memberships, which is the one area where paying parents get academic value. This is the monetisation seam worth noting — insight, not content.
- **School partnership model**: Prodigy works directly with schools and districts, which creates a distribution channel independent of paid marketing. Schools are the acquisition channel; parents are the revenue channel.
- **No ads in free tier**: The free student experience contains no advertising, which is essential for school and parent trust.

## Positioning copy

Prodigy's membership marketing targets parents' desire for their child to have the best experience: "Give your child the ultimate Prodigy adventure." Membership benefits are framed in terms of the child's enjoyment and status within the game rather than academic outcomes. The parent dashboard is marketed separately with outcome language: "Understand exactly how your child is progressing." Free-tier messaging emphasises breadth: "100% free maths curriculum, forever — no strings attached."

## Lessons for a UK 11+ app at smaller scale

The cosmetics-only paywall is a genuine innovation: it enables school and educator buy-in (no academic gating) while still capturing parent willingness to pay via a child's desire for in-game status. For a 11+ app, the equivalent might be a free curriculum with paid engagement layers (custom avatars, badge collections, leaderboard access, bonus practice packs). The insight-layer monetisation — paywalling detailed progress data for parents — is directly applicable and arguably the highest-value premium differentiator a small UK edtech product can offer. The 25% multi-child discount applied at checkout (rather than requiring a separate plan) is operationally simple and worth replicating.
