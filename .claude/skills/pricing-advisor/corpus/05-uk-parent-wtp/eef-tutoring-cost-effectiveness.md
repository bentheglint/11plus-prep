## Source

The Education Endowment Foundation (EEF) is an independent charity funded by the DfE and Sutton Trust to evaluate what works in improving educational outcomes for disadvantaged children. It ran and evaluated the National Tutoring Programme (NTP) from 2020 to 2024 and maintains the Teaching and Learning Toolkit, which rates cost-effectiveness of interventions.

Key publications:
- *EEF Teaching and Learning Toolkit* — ongoing evidence synthesis
- *National Tutoring Programme launch* (2020) — establishing the NTP's cost model
- *Tutor Trust — Affordable Tutoring* (second trial) — evaluation of low-cost tutoring models
- *Small Group Tuition* — systematic evidence review

## Key findings (the headline numbers)

- **NTP cost to schools (Year 1, 2020–21):** Subsidised tutoring available at **£70–£100 per pupil per 15-hour block** for small group sessions (1:2 or 1:3 ratio); online delivery was at the lower end of this range. (EEF, NTP launch 2020)
- The subsidy covered **up to 50%** of tutoring costs for pupil-premium-eligible students in 2023–24. (DfE NTP data, 2024)
- **Mainstream schools** received a minimum of **£67.50 per pupil-premium-eligible pupil** toward NTP tutoring in 2023–24; non-mainstream schools received **£176.25**. (DfE/EEF NTP final year data)
- EEF's Teaching and Learning Toolkit rates small group tuition as providing up to **+5 months of additional progress** — "moderate impact for moderate cost."
- **Tutor Trust affordable tutoring trial**: training university students and recent graduates to give disadvantaged primary pupils small-group tuition boosted maths results by **three months' progress** over the course of a year, across 105 primary schools.
- **Online tutoring pilot**: a project delivering specialist one-to-one tutoring online to Year 6 pupils found **no statistically significant impact** on KS2 maths test scores compared to business-as-usual teaching. (EEF, online tuition pilot evaluation)
- The NTP over 2020–2024 delivered **6.17 million total tutoring course starts** across four years. In its final year (2023–24): **1.07 million pupils** received tutoring; **16.4 million hours** delivered; average course duration **10.9 hours**.
- **45.2%** of NTP pupils were FSM-eligible (free school meals in previous 6 years); **28.1%** had SEN. (DfE NTP final year statistics, 2024)

## Sample / methodology (if known)

EEF evaluations use randomised controlled trial (RCT) methodology wherever possible. The Tutor Trust second trial involved 105 primary schools. The NTP national statistics are administrative data from DfE. The Teaching and Learning Toolkit synthesises evidence from across multiple studies.

## Direct quotes

1. "There is strong evidence that high-quality tuition is a cost-effective way to enable pupils to catch up." — EEF Teaching and Learning Toolkit summary on small group tuition.

2. "Training university students and recent graduates to give disadvantaged primary pupils small-group tuition can boost their maths results by three months over the course of a year." — EEF, *Tutor Trust Affordable Tutoring — Second Trial* evaluation.

3. "The small groups allow the children to experience very targeted and personal support, something that I cannot offer (no matter how much I want to or try to) as a class teacher." — Year 6 teacher, quoted in Action Tutoring's analysis of EEF/Sutton Trust 2026 research.

4. Note: The EEF's online tutoring pilot found no significant impact on KS2 maths outcomes, an important nuance — **format and quality** matter, not just digital delivery.

## Applicability to UK 11+ app pricing

The EEF cost data anchors the floor for what tutoring "should" cost if it is affordable. The NTP delivered tutoring at £70–£100 per pupil per 15-hour block — roughly **£5–£7 per hour** for state-subsidised small group tutoring. Private market rates (£40–£90/hour) are 8–15× higher. An 11+ app at £40–£70/month delivering adaptive, personalised practice sits between these extremes: substantially cheaper than the private market, more engaging and scalable than the state minimum. The EEF evidence that online tutoring alone did not improve KS2 outcomes suggests a hybrid model (app + some parental involvement) would be a stronger positioning claim.
