## 1. Your Role as a Parent

### Coach, Cheerleader, or Homework Enforcer?

Here is the single most important thing to understand: your job is not to teach your child the 11+ curriculum. Your job is to create the conditions in which they can learn it.

There are three roles parents tend to fall into during 11+ preparation, and only one of them works well long-term:

**The Tutor** — sits with the child, explains every question, corrects every mistake, and slowly takes over the learning process. The child becomes dependent. When they sit in the exam room alone, they freeze — because Mum or Dad isn't there to explain the tricky ones.

**The Homework Enforcer** — "Have you done your 11+ practice? You need to do your 11+ practice. Why haven't you done your 11+ practice?" This creates a dynamic where the 11+ becomes something done *to* avoid parental displeasure rather than *for* their own benefit. The child associates practice with nagging, and the whole thing becomes a battle of wills.

**The Coach/Cheerleader** — sets up the structure, provides the resources, celebrates effort, stays calm when things go wrong, and trusts the process. This is the one that works.

### What Good Coaching Looks Like in Practice

- **You set the timetable together.** Not imposed from above — agreed collaboratively. "When do you think would be a good time to do some practice this week?"
- **You provide the resources** but you don't hover. Set them up with the right materials, check in afterwards, but don't sit over their shoulder for every question.
- **You mark papers and identify weak areas** — but frame it as detective work, not judgement. "Interesting — you got every fractions question right but the ratio ones tripped you up. Shall we look at those?"
- **You celebrate effort, not just scores.** "You stuck with that really hard paper for 45 minutes without giving up. That's brilliant."
- **You stay calm when they can't do something.** If you show frustration when they get something wrong, they'll start hiding mistakes rather than learning from them.

### The Danger of Becoming the Enforcer

This is worth dwelling on because it's the trap most parents fall into. It happens gradually. You start by gently suggesting practice. Then you're reminding. Then you're insisting. Then you're arguing about it every evening. Before you know it, the 11+ has become the biggest source of conflict in your household, and your child associates the exam with misery.

Research consistently shows that children pick up on their parents' anxiety immediately. If you're stressed about it, they'll be stressed about it — and stressed children don't learn well.

The antidote is to replace "You must do this!" with "Let's tackle this together." Frame yourself as being on their team, not as their manager.

### When Your Child Is Struggling

There will be days — possibly weeks — when your child genuinely cannot do what's being asked of them. Maybe the topic is too hard. Maybe they're tired. Maybe they're nine years old and would rather be playing Minecraft.

In those moments, the most useful thing you can do is:

1. **Acknowledge it.** "This is really hard, isn't it? I can see you're frustrated."
2. **Normalise it.** "Everyone finds some topics difficult. That's completely normal."
3. **Adjust.** Switch to an easier topic, shorten the session, or stop entirely. A bad 10 minutes of practice is worse than no practice at all.
4. **Come back to it later.** The topic that seems impossible in Year 4 often clicks naturally in Year 5 when their brain has matured a bit more.

## 2. Building a Preparation Plan

### The 18-Month Overview

If you're reading this 18 months before the exam (which, for a September 2026 exam, means starting around March 2025), you have the luxury of a proper phased approach. If you're starting later, don't panic — compress the phases rather than skipping them.

#### Phase 1: Foundation Building (Months 1-6)

**Goal:** Fill curriculum gaps and build core skills. No practice papers yet.

This is the phase most parents skip, and it's the most important one. Jumping straight to practice papers is like entering a marathon without training — you'll just confirm what your child can't do without giving them the tools to improve.

- Work through age-appropriate material in maths, English, and (if required) verbal/non-verbal reasoning
- Identify weak areas early — not through testing, but through gentle exploration
- Build reading habits (more on this in Section 8)
- Introduce verbal and non-verbal reasoning concepts gradually — these aren't taught in school, so they'll be completely new

**Time commitment:** 20-30 minutes per day, 4-5 days per week. That's it. At this stage, consistency matters far more than volume.

#### Phase 2: Skill Development (Months 7-10)

**Goal:** Strengthen weak areas, introduce exam-style questions, build speed.

- Start working through topic-specific practice material
- Introduce timed elements gradually — "Let's see how many you can do in 15 minutes" rather than full timed papers
- Revisit weak areas regularly — spacing out revision is far more effective than cramming
- Begin familiarising your child with the specific format of the exam they'll sit (GL Assessment, CEM, or independent school papers all have different formats)

**Time commitment:** 30-45 minutes per day, 4-5 days per week.

#### Phase 3: Mock Tests and Exam Practice (Months 11-15)

**Goal:** Build exam technique, stamina, and confidence under timed conditions.

- Full-length practice papers under timed conditions
- Mock exams (many tutoring centres, libraries, and online providers run these)
- Detailed review of every mock — not just the score, but *why* they got things wrong
- Focus on exam technique: time management, checking answers, not getting stuck on one question

**Time commitment:** 45-60 minutes on practice days, plus weekend mock papers. Still keeping 2-3 days per week completely free.

#### Phase 4: Final Preparation (Last 2-3 Months)

**Goal:** Consolidate, build confidence, reduce anxiety.

- Tighten timings — practise finishing papers with time to spare for checking
- Light revision of any remaining weak spots
- Focus on mental preparation and stress management
- Gradually reduce intensity in the final 2 weeks — your child needs to arrive at the exam rested, not exhausted

**Time commitment:** Maintain steady practice but actively wind down in the final fortnight.

### Sample Weekly Schedule (Phase 2/3)

| Day | Activity | Duration |
|-----|----------|----------|
| Monday | Maths topic practice | 30-40 mins |
| Tuesday | Reading / free time | — |
| Wednesday | English comprehension or VR | 30-40 mins |
| Thursday | Free time / hobbies / sport | — |
| Friday | Maths or NVR practice | 30-40 mins |
| Saturday | Timed practice paper (from Phase 3) | 45-60 mins |
| Sunday | Completely free | — |

This is a guide, not a commandment. Adjust it to fit your family's life. The key principles are:

- **Alternate subjects** to keep things interesting
- **At least two completely free days** per week
- **Short, focused sessions** rather than marathon study blocks
- **Weekend papers** when your child is more rested and alert
- **Consistency over intensity** — four 30-minute sessions beat one two-hour Sunday cramming session every time

### How Much Is Enough?

This is the question every parent asks, and the honest answer is: it depends on the child. But here are some rough guidelines that align with what tutoring professionals and education researchers recommend:

- **Year 4 (early prep):** 15-25 minutes, 3-4 times per week
- **Year 5 (core prep):** 30-45 minutes, 4-5 times per week
- **Summer before Year 6:** Can increase to 45-60 minutes on practice days
- **Year 6 (if exam is in September):** Maintain steady practice, don't suddenly triple the workload

If your child is routinely spending more than an hour a day on 11+ preparation, that's probably too much for a 9 or 10-year-old. Quality of focus matters far more than quantity of time.

## 3. Managing Exam Stress and Anxiety

### This Is a Big Deal — Take It Seriously

We're asking 9 and 10-year-olds to sit a high-stakes exam that they know will determine which school they go to. That's a lot of pressure for someone who still has a bedtime. Research from the NHS and child psychology literature consistently shows that exam-related anxiety in primary-age children is both real and increasing.

Parental pressure is one of the largest sources of stress for children aged 10-11 working towards exams. Children can pick up on their parents' attitudes and associated behaviour and feel under pressure to make them proud — even when parents think they're hiding their anxiety well.

### Recognising Stress in 9-11 Year Olds

Children this age often can't articulate that they're stressed. Instead, it comes out sideways. Watch for:

**Physical signs:**
- Stomach aches, headaches, or feeling sick — especially on practice days or before mocks
- Trouble sleeping, nightmares, or wanting to sleep in your bed again
- Changes in appetite — eating much more or much less
- Complaints of tiredness that seem disproportionate
- Picking at skin, biting nails, or other nervous habits

**Emotional signs:**
- Tearfulness or emotional outbursts that seem out of proportion
- Withdrawal — going quiet, spending more time alone
- Clinginess or not wanting to be separated from you
- Saying things like "I'm stupid" or "I can't do anything"
- Sudden anger or irritability, especially around practice time

**Behavioural signs:**
- Refusing to do practice or actively avoiding it
- Regression to younger behaviours (thumb sucking, baby talk)
- Loss of interest in activities they usually enjoy
- Perfectionism — rubbing out answers repeatedly, refusing to move on until something is exactly right
- Rushing through practice just to get it over with

### Anxiety-Reduction Techniques That Actually Work

**1. Normalise the feelings.** "It's completely normal to feel nervous about a test. Even adults feel like that. It means you care about doing well, and that's a good thing." The NHS specifically advises acknowledging feelings rather than dismissing them or expecting children to "push through."

**2. Breathing exercises.** Teach your child box breathing: breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4, out for 4, hold for 4. Practise it when they're calm so it's automatic when they need it. This isn't woo — it activates the parasympathetic nervous system and genuinely reduces the physical symptoms of anxiety.

**3. Reframe the exam.** Help them see it as "a test to find out which school would suit you best" rather than something they pass or fail. This is language that matters enormously.

**4. Visualisation.** Have them imagine themselves in the exam room, feeling calm, reading the questions carefully, and working through them steadily. Athletes use this technique because it works.

**5. Physical activity.** Exercise is one of the most effective anxiety reducers for children. A run around the park, a bike ride, a swim — anything that gets them moving and out of their head. The NHS recommends this specifically for exam stress.

**6. Maintain routine.** Anxiety thrives on unpredictability. Keep mealtimes, bedtimes, and family activities consistent. The exam preparation should fit into normal life, not replace it.

**7. The "worry time" technique.** Designate 10 minutes a day as "worry time" — they can worry about whatever they want. Outside that window, worries get written on a piece of paper and put in a "worry box" to be dealt with during worry time. This sounds simple but is a technique used in cognitive behavioural therapy.

### When to Push Through vs When to Stop

**Push through when:**
- They're mildly reluctant but engage once they start
- They're finding something difficult but making progress
- They're simply in a "can't be bothered" mood (every child has these)

**Stop when:**
- They're in tears or visibly distressed
- They've been going for more than the agreed time and are losing focus
- Physical symptoms appear (headache, stomach ache)
- The session is creating genuine conflict between you
- They're so tired or upset that nothing is going in

Pushing through genuine distress doesn't build resilience — it builds anxiety. A child who learns that practice always ends in tears will dread it, and dread is the enemy of learning.

### When to Seek Professional Help

If your child's anxiety is:
- Persistent (lasting weeks, not just the occasional bad day)
- Interfering with daily life (not sleeping, not eating, not wanting to go to school)
- Causing them to say things that worry you about their self-worth
- Not responding to your support and reassurance

Then speak to your GP. They can refer to CAMHS (Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services) if needed. There is absolutely no shame in this — it's the responsible thing to do. YoungMinds (youngminds.org.uk) also has an excellent parents' helpline: 0808 802 5544.

**A reality check:** if the 11+ preparation is causing your child genuine mental health difficulties, it's worth asking whether the goal is worth the cost. No school place is worth your child's wellbeing. That's not giving up — that's good parenting.

## 4. Motivation Over the Long Haul

### The 18-Month Problem

Eighteen months is a very long time in the life of a 9-year-old. Motivation that burns brightly in the spring of Year 4 can be completely extinguished by the autumn of Year 5. This is normal, and it doesn't mean your child isn't cut out for grammar school. It means they're a child.

### Reward Systems That Work

**The marble jar.** Every completed practice session earns a marble. When the jar is full, they choose a reward — a day out, a new book, a special meal. This works because progress is visible and tangible.

**The sticker chart** (for younger children). Surprisingly effective even for children who claim to be "too old" for stickers.

**Experience rewards over material rewards.** "If you complete all your practice this week, we'll go to the cinema on Saturday" works better than "I'll buy you a toy." Experiences create memories and don't set up an escalating pattern of material expectations.

**Choice and autonomy.** Let your child have some control over their rewards. "What would you like to work towards this month?" Children who feel they have agency over the reward system are more motivated by it.

### Reward Systems That Backfire

**Tying rewards to scores.** "If you get 80% on your mock, you'll get..." This creates anxiety about performance and punishes children for finding something difficult. Tie rewards to effort and completion instead — "If you do all your practice sessions this week" not "If you get them all right."

**Escalating material rewards.** If you start with sweets and work up to a PlayStation, you've created a motivation system that requires ever-larger bribes. Start small, stay small.

**Punishing poor results.** "No iPad until your marks improve" turns the 11+ into a punishment dynamic. If results are poor, the answer is to investigate why and adjust the approach — not to take things away.

**Comparisons with siblings or friends.** "Your brother managed it, why can't you?" This is devastating and never, ever motivates.

### The "I Don't Want To" Conversation

At some point — probably multiple points — your child will say "I don't want to do 11+ practice." Here's how to handle it:

**First, listen.** There's usually a reason behind the resistance. Are they tired? Is the material too hard? Are they bored? Have they been doing too much? Are they anxious? The solution depends on the cause.

**If they're bored:** Change the format. Switch from workbooks to online practice. Do a quiz together instead of a written paper. Use games — Scrabble for vocabulary, card games for mental maths.

**If they're overwhelmed:** Scale back. Shorter sessions, easier material, more frequent breaks. Build back up gradually.

**If they're questioning the whole thing:** Have an honest conversation. "Do you want to try for grammar school? It's OK if you're not sure." Some children are genuinely not interested and are only doing it because their parents want them to. If that's the case, forcing it will make everyone miserable.

**If it's a bad day:** Let it go. One missed session won't make the difference between passing and failing. "OK, let's skip it today. We'll do it tomorrow instead." This isn't giving in — it's being flexible.

### Keeping It Fresh

- **Vary the activities.** Don't do the same type of practice every day. Rotate between maths, English, VR, and NVR.
- **Make it social.** If friends are also preparing, organise group practice sessions. Children often work better with peers than alone.
- **Celebrate milestones.** Completed all the fractions questions? That's worth recognising. Finished a whole practice paper? Mark it.
- **Use real-world connections.** Shopping trips for percentages. Cooking for fractions. Road trips for speed, distance, and time. Learning that connects to real life sticks better.
- **Let them teach you.** "Can you explain how you worked that out?" is one of the most powerful learning techniques there is. If they can teach it, they've truly understood it.

## 5. The Comparison Trap

### The School Gate Problem

If you live in an area where grammar schools exist, the 11+ becomes a defining feature of Year 5 and 6 social life — for parents more than children. School gate conversations become dominated by tutoring schedules, mock test results, and competitive one-upmanship.

"Oh, Olivia's been tutored since Year 3."
"Jake got 95% on his last mock."
"We're doing three hours a day now."

This is toxic, and you need to protect both yourself and your child from it.

### What the Competition Does to You

Hearing about other children's preparation creates anxiety that you're not doing enough. This anxiety transfers to your child through increased pressure, more practice sessions, and a general atmosphere of panic. The child senses that their parent is worried, which makes *them* worried.

Social media amplifies this. Tutoring companies post success stories. Parent groups share "how to guarantee a pass." Facebook threads become competitive displays of how much preparation people are doing.

### What the Competition Does to Your Child

Children talk to each other. If your child's friends are also preparing, they'll inevitably compare. "I got 85% on my mock, what did you get?" For the child who got 65%, this is crushing.

When friends who are also preparing get their results, some will pass and some won't. If your child doesn't pass but their best friend does, that's a genuinely painful experience that needs careful handling.

### How to Protect Your Family

**1. Limit school gate conversations about the 11+.** You don't owe anyone information about your child's preparation. "We're just taking it steady" is a perfectly adequate response to nosy questions.

**2. Don't compare your child to anyone else's.** Not out loud, and not in your own head if you can help it. Every child is different. The child who scores highest in mocks doesn't always score highest on the day.

**3. Frame it as personal progress.** "You got 70% last time and 75% this time — that's great improvement" matters infinitely more than "Sophie got 80%."

**4. Be honest about what you don't know.** Parents who claim their child is "definitely going to pass" are guessing. Nobody knows until results day.

**5. Shield your child from adult anxiety.** Don't discuss tutoring costs, pass rates, or your worries about their chances in front of them. They don't need that weight.

**6. Remember that other parents are also anxious.** The parent who brags about their child's mock scores is usually managing their own fear. Compassion is more useful than competition.

### The Sibling Dimension

If you have more than one child and one is preparing for the 11+ (or has already sat it), comparison becomes an internal family issue too.

- Never compare siblings' abilities, even implicitly
- If an older sibling passed, don't create an expectation that the younger one will too
- If an older sibling didn't pass, don't transfer your disappointment onto the younger child's preparation
- Each child's journey is their own. Say it, mean it, and live it

## 6. Balancing Preparation with Childhood

### How Much Is Too Much?

There is no universally agreed number of hours that constitutes "too much" 11+ preparation. But research on childhood development, play, and over-scheduling gives us strong indicators.

The American Academy of Pediatrics states that play is essential to development because it contributes to the cognitive, physical, social, and emotional well-being of children. The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child classifies play as a fundamental right. Research from the University of Cambridge demonstrates that free play develops executive functions including working memory, cognitive flexibility, and impulse control — precisely the skills needed for exam performance.

In other words: the time your child spends playing isn't wasted time. It's time that builds the brain capacity they need for the exam.

### Signs of Burnout

Watch for these warning signs that your child has tipped from "working hard" into "burning out":

- **Chronic tiredness** that doesn't improve with sleep
- **Declining performance** — getting worse, not better, despite more practice
- **Loss of interest** in things they used to enjoy
- **Increased illness** — stress weakens the immune system, and frequent colds or stomach bugs can be a sign
- **Emotional flatness** — not upset, just... blank. Disengaged. Going through the motions.
- **Resistance that feels different** — not the ordinary "I can't be bothered" but a deeper refusal that seems to come from exhaustion rather than laziness
- **Sleep disturbances** — difficulty falling asleep, nightmares, or waking very early
- **Irritability and meltdowns** that seem disproportionate to the trigger

If you're seeing three or more of these consistently, your child needs less practice, not more. The counter-intuitive truth is that stepping back often leads to better performance once they've recovered.

### The Non-Negotiables

These things should not be sacrificed for 11+ preparation:

- **Sleep.** A 9-10 year old needs 9-12 hours per night. Practice sessions that eat into bedtime are counterproductive — sleep is when the brain consolidates learning.
- **Physical activity.** At least an hour of active play or exercise every day. This isn't a luxury — it directly supports concentration, mood, and learning.
- **Social time with friends.** Playdates, clubs, hanging out — children need peer interaction for healthy development.
- **Family time.** Meals together, weekend activities, bedtime stories. The 11+ shouldn't colonise every family moment.
- **Hobbies and interests.** If your child loves football, swimming, drama, or art — keep it going. These activities build confidence, provide stress relief, and develop skills that matter beyond any exam.
- **Downtime.** Unstructured time where they can be bored, daydream, potter about. This is when creativity and self-regulation develop.

### A Useful Rule of Thumb

11+ preparation should occupy no more than about 10-15% of your child's waking hours during the week. For a child who's awake for roughly 12 hours a day, that's 1-1.5 hours maximum — and that includes commuting to tutoring sessions.

If the 11+ has become the dominant feature of your child's life, something has gone wrong. It should be one part of a full, happy childhood — not the headline act.

## 7. Working with Schools

### What Schools Can and Can't Do

This is an area where parents often feel frustrated, so let's be clear about the landscape.

**State primary schools are not obliged to prepare children for the 11+.** The 11+ is not part of the national curriculum. Verbal reasoning and non-verbal reasoning — which feature in many 11+ exams including the GL Assessment — are not taught in state schools at all. Your child will not encounter these subjects in the classroom unless their school makes a specific choice to include them.

This means that for VR and NVR preparation, you're entirely on your own. Don't wait for school to cover it.

**What teachers *can* tell you:**
- Whether they think your child is working at a level that suggests the 11+ is appropriate
- Which areas of maths and English your child is strong or weak in
- Whether your child seems happy and coping, or stressed and struggling

**What teachers typically *won't* do:**
- Provide specific 11+ preparation in class (this is outside their remit)
- Recommend tutors (most schools have policies against this)
- Give you a prediction of whether your child will pass

### When Your School Doesn't Support 11+ Preparation

Some schools are actively unsupportive of the 11+ — either because they don't have grammar schools in the area, because they have a philosophical objection to selective education, or because they've seen the negative effects of 11+ pressure on children.

If your school falls into this category:
- **Don't take it personally.** Their job is to educate all children, not to prepare a subset for a selective exam.
- **Don't expect them to change.** Work around it rather than against it.
- **Do keep communication open.** Even if they're not supporting 11+ prep specifically, they can still tell you how your child is progressing academically and emotionally.
- **Do be sensitive to other children.** Not everyone in your child's class will be taking the 11+, and creating a visible divide between "11+ children" and others is harmful to all.

### Private Tutoring

Many parents hire private tutors. If you go this route:
- Choose someone with specific experience of the exam your child will sit (GL, CEM, or school-specific)
- Ask for references from parents whose children have already taken the exam
- Make sure the tutor's approach aligns with your values — if they're creating more stress, they're not helping
- One hour per week is standard and usually sufficient alongside home practice
- Don't assume that paying for a tutor means you can outsource all responsibility — you still need to oversee the broader picture

## 8. The Role of Reading

### The Single Biggest Thing You Can Do

If there's one piece of advice that every education professional, tutoring company, and 11+ veteran agrees on, it's this: **children who read widely perform significantly better in the 11+, particularly in English and verbal reasoning.**

This isn't opinion — it's backed by extensive research. Over 55% of mistakes in 11+ verbal reasoning tests relate to weak vocabulary, and the primary route to strong vocabulary is extensive reading. Children who read many books across a range of genres learn new words faster and have much wider vocabularies than children who seldom read.

Reading develops comprehension skills, inference, deduction, understanding of tone and register, exposure to complex sentence structures, and cultural literacy — all of which are tested directly in 11+ English papers.

### What to Read

**Variety is key.** The 11+ comprehension passage could be anything — a Victorian novel extract, a newspaper article, a poem, a piece of travel writing, a historical account. Children who only read one genre will be thrown by unfamiliar styles.

**Aim for a mix of:**
- Modern children's fiction (David Walliams, Roald Dahl, Jacqueline Wilson, Jeff Kinney)
- Classic children's literature (The Wind in the Willows, The Secret Garden, The Hobbit, Narnia, Treasure Island)
- Non-fiction (nature books, history, science, biographies)
- Newspapers and magazines (First News is excellent for this age group)
- Poetry (doesn't have to be long — short poems introduce rich vocabulary and figurative language)
- Challenging texts that stretch them a bit (The Phantom Tollbooth, The Giver, Northern Lights)

**Don't ban "easy" books.** If your child loves Diary of a Wimpy Kid, let them read it. Reading for pleasure builds the habit; you can gradually introduce more challenging material alongside.

### Making Reading a Habit, Not a Chore

- **Model it.** Children who see their parents reading are more likely to read themselves. Put down your phone and pick up a book.
- **Bedtime reading.** Even if your child can read independently, reading aloud together builds comprehension and shared experience. Alternate — you read a page, they read a page.
- **Audiobooks count.** Listening to well-narrated books builds vocabulary and comprehension. It's not "cheating." Use them in the car, at bedtime, on long journeys.
- **Library visits.** Make them routine. Let your child choose whatever they want. The library is free and full of books you'd never think to buy.
- **Book as reward.** When they complete a milestone, let them choose a new book. This reinforces reading as a treat rather than a task.
- **Talk about what they're reading.** "What's happening in your book?" "Who's your favourite character?" "What do you think will happen next?" These casual conversations build comprehension skills naturally.
- **Never punish with reading or reward with the absence of it.** "You can stop reading and watch TV" teaches them that reading is the bad thing and TV is the good thing.

### The Reading Gap

Research consistently shows that the vocabulary gap between children who read regularly and those who don't is enormous and widening. By age 11, a child who has been read to regularly and reads independently could have been exposed to millions more words than a child who hasn't. This isn't something you can cram in the final few months — it's built over years.

Start now. If your child isn't a keen reader, find what they *are* interested in and find books about that. Football-mad? There are brilliant football novels. Loves animals? The natural world section of the library is enormous. Obsessed with space? There's a reading list for that.

The goal isn't to read "11+ appropriate" books. The goal is to read. Everything else follows.

## 9. Vocabulary Building

### Why Vocabulary Matters So Much

The vocabulary required for the 11+ goes significantly beyond what's taught in the primary curriculum. Verbal reasoning questions in particular rely on knowing words like "abundant," "diminish," "reluctant," "hastily," and "obstinate" — words that many 10-year-olds simply haven't encountered.

The gap between children who pass and those who narrowly miss out often comes down to vocabulary. It's the hidden variable that underpins performance in English comprehension, verbal reasoning, and even maths (understanding what a word problem is actually asking).

### Strategies Beyond Word Lists

Learning vocabulary from lists — "Learn these 50 words by Friday" — is the least effective way to build word knowledge. Words learned in isolation, without context, are quickly forgotten.

Here's what works better:

**1. Reading widely (see Section 8).** This is the foundation. Words encountered in context — in stories, articles, and poems — stick in memory because they're connected to meaning, emotion, and narrative.

**2. Word roots, prefixes, and suffixes.** This is the vocabulary equivalent of learning to fish rather than being given a fish. If your child knows that "pre-" means before, "un-" means not, "micro-" means small, and "-tion" makes a noun, they can decode thousands of unfamiliar words.

A surprisingly small set of Latin and Greek roots unlocks an enormous proportion of English vocabulary. Twenty common prefixes and fourteen common roots can help decode over 100,000 words. Key ones to learn:

- **Prefixes:** un-, re-, pre-, mis-, dis-, over-, under-, anti-, inter-, trans-
- **Roots:** duct (lead), port (carry), ject (throw), scrib/script (write), vis/vid (see), aud (hear), bene (good), mal (bad)
- **Suffixes:** -able/-ible, -ment, -tion/-sion, -ful, -less, -ous, -ive

**3. Word-of-the-day.** Pick one interesting word each morning. Use it in conversation during the day. Write it on a sticky note on the fridge. By the end of the week, that's seven new words — 365 over a year.

**4. Context clue practice.** Read a passage together and stop at an unfamiliar word. Instead of looking it up, work out its meaning from the surrounding text. "What clues does the sentence give us about what this word means?" This mirrors exactly what they'll need to do in the exam.

**5. Word games.** Scrabble, Boggle, Bananagrams, crosswords, word searches — all build vocabulary in a way that feels like play rather than work. Bonus: they also practise spelling.

**6. Synonyms and antonyms.** This is specifically tested in many VR papers. Play "What's another word for...?" and "What's the opposite of...?" as car games, mealtime conversations, or quick-fire challenges.

**7. A vocabulary notebook.** When your child encounters a new word — in reading, conversation, or practice — write it in a dedicated notebook with its meaning and a sentence using it. Review the notebook weekly. The act of writing reinforces memory.

**8. Conversation.** Simply talking to your child using rich vocabulary — and explaining words when they ask — is one of the most natural and effective vocabulary builders. Don't dumb down your language. If they ask "What does that mean?", that's a learning moment.

## 10. Dealing with Setbacks

### Bad Mock Results

Your child will, at some point, come home with a mock test result that's worse than expected. Maybe much worse. How you react in that moment will shape their entire attitude to failure and learning.

**What to do:**

1. **Stay calm.** Your face is the first thing they look at. If you look disappointed, they've already learned that this result means they've let you down.

2. **Acknowledge their feelings.** "How are you feeling about that score?" Let them tell you before you react.

3. **Normalise it.** "Mock tests are supposed to show us what we need to work on. That's literally their job. If you got everything right, we wouldn't need to practise any more."

4. **Investigate together.** Look at which questions they got wrong and why. Was it carelessness? Running out of time? Not understanding the topic? Each cause has a different solution.

5. **Make a plan.** "OK, so ratio questions are tricky for you. Let's spend a bit of time on those this week."

6. **Focus on trajectory, not snapshots.** One bad result in a series of improving results is an anomaly. A pattern of declining results needs attention. Help your child see the trend, not just the single data point.

**What NOT to do:**
- Show disappointment or frustration
- Compare their result to anyone else's
- Increase practice time as a punishment
- Catastrophise ("You'll never pass at this rate")
- Dismiss their feelings ("It's just a mock, it doesn't matter")

### The Difficult Practice Session

Some sessions just don't work. They can't focus, everything seems too hard, and you're both getting frustrated. This is not a sign that your child is failing or that you're a bad parent. It's a Tuesday.

When a session is going badly:
- **Cut it short.** "Let's stop here and come back to this tomorrow with fresh eyes."
- **Switch to something easier.** A confidence boost from easier questions can reset their mood.
- **Do something physical.** A 10-minute run around the garden can completely change their energy.
- **Be honest.** "I can see this isn't working today. That's fine. We'll try again another time."

### When Siblings Have Different Results

If one child passed and another is struggling, or if one sibling is academically stronger than the other, this requires real sensitivity.

- Never say "Your sister found this easy" or anything remotely similar
- Celebrate each child's individual strengths
- Make it clear that the 11+ measures a narrow set of skills, not overall intelligence or worth
- If one child passes and another doesn't, both children need support — the one who passed may feel guilty, and the one who didn't needs to know they're equally valued

### Reframing Failure as Learning

Research on growth mindset shows that children whose parents view failure positively — as a learning opportunity rather than a disaster — develop greater academic resilience. This isn't about pretending failure doesn't hurt. It's about modelling a healthy response:

"That was disappointing. But now we know exactly what to focus on."
"You didn't know how to do that one. *Yet*. That 'yet' is important."
"Making mistakes is how your brain grows. Literally — the neural pathways get stronger when you work through something difficult."

## 11. Exam Day Parenting

### The Night Before

- **Normal bedtime.** Don't send them to bed two hours early — they'll just lie there worrying.
- **No last-minute revision.** It won't help, and it sends the message that they're not ready.
- **Prepare everything they need** — pencils, eraser, water bottle, snack, any required documentation — and put it by the door.
- **Calm, normal evening.** Watch a film together. Play a board game. Read a book. The goal is relaxation, not preparation.

### The Morning

**Your mood sets the tone.** Children mirror their parents' emotions. If you're rushing, stressed, and snappy, they'll absorb every bit of it. Plan to be calm. That means getting up early enough that nothing is rushed.

- **Normal breakfast.** Whatever they usually eat. This is not the morning to introduce a superfood smoothie they've never had before. Slow-release carbohydrates — toast, porridge, cereal — are ideal. Avoid sugary cereals that cause an energy spike and crash.
- **Leave early.** Build in time for traffic, parking, and a comfort break. Arriving flustered is the worst possible start.
- **Light conversation.** Talk about normal things. What they want to do at the weekend. Not the exam.

### What to Say

- "Just do your best. That's all anyone can ask."
- "We're proud of you whatever happens."
- "Remember your breathing if you feel nervous."
- "If you get stuck on a question, skip it and come back."

### What NOT to Say

- "This is your big chance — don't waste it"
- "Remember how hard you've worked — make it count"
- "Everyone's going to ask how you did"
- "Just think how good it'll feel when you pass"
- Any variation of "Don't be nervous" (this never works and actually increases anxiety)
- Last-minute tips or advice (this implies they're not ready)

### The School Gate on Exam Day

The atmosphere outside the exam venue can be intense. Other parents will be anxious, some children will be visibly stressed, and there may be a competitive tension in the air.

- **Arrive, stay calm, and keep your child close.**
- **Don't engage in last-minute comparisons** with other parents about preparation.
- **If possible, take a short walk** before going in rather than standing in a crowd of nervous families.
- **A quick hug, a smile, and "See you afterwards"** is better than a long emotional goodbye.

### After the Exam

When they come out:

1. **Don't ask "How did it go?"** as the first thing you say. Give them a hug. Ask if they're hungry. Let them decompress.

2. **When they do want to talk, listen.** They might say "It was easy" (great), "It was OK" (fine), or "It was horrible" (stay calm).

3. **Don't do a post-mortem.** Going through the paper question by question is stressful and pointless — the exam is done. There will be questions they remember getting wrong that they actually got right, and vice versa.

4. **Celebrate the effort.** "You did it. I'm really proud of you for going in there and giving it your best. Let's go and do something nice."

5. **Move on.** The exam is over. Now it's about waiting for results, not rehashing the experience.

## 12. If They Don't Get In

### Preparing for All Outcomes

Before results day, have a conversation with your child about what might happen. Not a heavy, scary conversation — just an honest one.

"When results come out, there are two things that might happen. You might get offered a place at [grammar school], or you might not. Either way, we'll be absolutely fine, because there are brilliant schools for you either way, and we're proud of you no matter what."

This conversation serves two purposes: it reduces the shock if the result is disappointing, and it genuinely takes the pressure off.

### The Research You Should Know About

This is important, so pay attention to it.

A landmark 2023 study by UCL and Durham University, published in the peer-reviewed journal *Educational Review*, analysed GCSE data for approximately 500,000 pupils. The findings were striking:

- **Pupils with the highest scores at age 11 were *less* likely to achieve five top GCSE grades if they attended grammar school than if they attended a comprehensive**, once other background factors were accounted for.
- Grammar school pupils had about **90% of the odds** of achieving five A/A* grades compared to equivalent pupils in non-selective areas.
- Grammar school attendees were **no more likely to attend university** — including Russell Group universities — than comprehensive school attendees from the same background.
- The Education Policy Institute found that the **poverty attainment gap was greater in areas with selective education** than in comprehensive areas.

Researchers suggested the "big fish, little pond" effect: pupils in selective schools may see themselves as less able compared to high-achieving classmates, and the stress of a competitive environment may actually hinder performance.

This doesn't mean grammar schools are bad. But it does mean that the widespread belief that grammar school guarantees better outcomes is **not supported by the evidence.** Your child can thrive anywhere.

As one Mumsnet parent put it: "My son was the only one of three primary school friends to get into a selective school. One of the friends who didn't is now a doctor and another is a lawyer, both earning more than him. The 11+ result doesn't define their life."

### Handling the Disappointment

If your child doesn't get offered a place:

**In the first hours:**
- Let them feel their feelings. They might cry, withdraw, or be angry. All of these are normal.
- Don't rush to offer solutions or silver linings. Just be present.
- Absolutely do not show your own disappointment. If you need to be upset, do it privately. Your child is watching how you respond, and if you treat it as a disaster, they'll internalise that they've failed catastrophically.

**In the first days:**
- Acknowledge that it's disappointing without being negative about the process, the school, or others.
- Remind them that only around 25-30% of children get grammar school places. The majority of bright, hardworking children don't. This isn't because they're not good enough.
- Point out that the effort has value in itself. Everything they've learned during preparation will benefit them at whatever school they attend.
- Buy them a small treat — not as a consolation prize, but as recognition of the months of hard work they put in.

**In the longer term:**
- Help them get excited about their actual school. Visit, learn about clubs and activities, focus on what's positive.
- Don't keep bringing it up. Once you've processed it together, let it go.
- Watch for signs of lasting distress — most children bounce back quickly, but some may need more support.

### The Appeal Process

If your child narrowly missed the cut-off, you may be able to appeal. Contact the grammar school directly for their appeals process. Appeals can be successful, particularly if there are mitigating circumstances (illness on exam day, special educational needs not adequately accommodated).

## 13. Special Circumstances

### Supporting Children with Dyslexia

Dyslexia doesn't mean a child can't pass the 11+ — but it does mean they'll need adapted preparation:

**What to know:**
- Reading speed and processing speed may be significantly slower
- Written expression may not reflect their actual understanding
- They may find verbal reasoning particularly challenging because of word recognition difficulties
- Fatigue sets in faster due to the extra cognitive effort required for reading

**How to adapt preparation:**
- **Use multisensory methods.** Tracing letters in sand, colour-coded word cards, audio resources alongside written ones
- **Shorter, more frequent sessions** rather than longer ones — 15-20 minutes with breaks rather than 45 minutes straight through
- **Read questions aloud** during practice so you're testing their reasoning, not just their reading speed
- **Build in extra time during practice** so they experience success before adding time pressure
- **Focus on verbal reasoning strategies** that work around weak decoding — word association, elimination of wrong answers, using context
- **Use coloured overlays or reading rulers** if these help — and make sure they're available in the exam

**Access arrangements:** You can apply for extra time (typically 25%) and other arrangements. A dyslexia diagnosis alone isn't always sufficient — you'll need evidence that your child works significantly slower than peers. Start the process early, ideally 6+ months before the exam. See the access arrangements section below.

### Supporting Children with ADHD

Children with ADHD can absolutely succeed in the 11+, but the standard preparation approach may not suit them:

**What to know:**
- Sustained concentration on a single task is genuinely harder — it's neurological, not laziness
- They may rush through questions impulsively without reading them properly
- Sitting still for 45-60 minutes in an exam is a significant challenge
- Medication timing may affect exam performance

**How to adapt preparation:**
- **Chunk sessions into 10-15 minute blocks** with physical activity between them. Five 10-minute sessions with movement breaks can be more productive than one 50-minute session.
- **Use variety.** Switch between topics, formats, and activities frequently.
- **Build in physical activity** before practice sessions — this can significantly improve focus.
- **Practice the skill of slowing down.** Teach them to underline key words in questions, to physically point to each answer option before choosing, to check their answer before moving on.
- **Use a timer visually** — not to create pressure, but to build awareness of time passing. Time blindness is a common feature of ADHD.
- **Practise the exam environment.** The more familiar they are with sitting still and working through a paper, the less the novelty will distract them on the day.
- **If your child takes medication,** discuss exam day timing with their paediatrician. The exam should ideally fall within the medication's effective window.

### Supporting Children with Anxiety

Some children experience clinical anxiety that goes beyond normal exam nerves:

**What to know:**
- They may freeze during tests even when they know the material
- Physical symptoms (nausea, shaking, inability to think) can be severe
- Test anxiety can create a cycle where fear of failure causes the failure they fear
- They may be perfectionists who would rather not try than risk getting something wrong

**How to adapt preparation:**
- **Gradual exposure.** Start with low-pressure practice and very slowly increase the formality. Going straight to timed mock exams can be overwhelming.
- **Relaxation techniques** taught and practised regularly — not just for the exam but as life skills. Box breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided visualisation.
- **Cognitive reframing.** "I can't do this" becomes "I'm finding this difficult right now." "I'm going to fail" becomes "I'm going to try my best."
- **Desensitisation to the exam environment.** Visit the exam venue if possible. Sit in an unfamiliar room and do practice papers. Familiarise them with every aspect of the day so there are no surprises.
- **Professional support.** If anxiety is significantly impacting your child's life, consider CBT (cognitive behavioural therapy) — it's evidence-based and often very effective for children. Your GP can refer through CAMHS, or you can access private child psychologists.

### Access Arrangements — How to Apply

Access arrangements ensure that children with special educational needs can access the exam fairly. For the GL Assessment 11+, the process typically works as follows:

**What's available:**
- Extra time (usually 25%)
- Supervised rest breaks
- Separate or smaller exam room
- Large print papers
- Coloured overlays
- A reader (someone who reads questions aloud)
- A scribe (someone who writes answers)

**How to apply:**
1. **Start early** — ideally 6-12 months before the exam. Applications submitted at the last minute are far less likely to succeed.
2. **Speak to your child's school.** The application typically needs to come through or be supported by the school. The school must demonstrate that the arrangement reflects your child's "normal way of working" — i.e., they already receive similar support in the classroom.
3. **Gather evidence.** You'll need a formal diagnosis (from a paediatrician, educational psychologist, or CAMHS) and evidence of how the condition affects your child in educational settings. For extra time specifically, evidence of slow processing speed (such as standardised reading speed tests) is usually required.
4. **Contact the exam administrators.** Each local authority or grammar school consortium has its own process. Contact them directly to find out deadlines and requirements. Don't assume your school will do this automatically.
5. **Children with an Education, Health and Care Plan (EHCP)** typically qualify automatically for extra time.

**Important:** The criteria for 11+ access arrangements may differ from those for SATs or GCSEs. Don't assume that because your child gets extra time at school, they'll automatically get it for the 11+. Check the specific requirements for your exam.

## A Final Word

The 11+ is one exam on one day. It tests a specific set of skills under specific conditions, and it produces a score that determines eligibility for one type of school. That's all it is.

It is not a measure of your child's intelligence, potential, character, or worth. It is not a measure of your parenting. It is not a prediction of their future success or happiness.

The children who do best in the 11+ — and more importantly, who emerge from the process happiest and most resilient — are those whose parents kept perspective. They're the children who were supported but not pressured, challenged but not overwhelmed, and loved unconditionally regardless of the result.

Your child needs to know, deeply and unshakably, that you will be proud of them whatever happens. Not proud of them *if* they pass. Proud of them for trying, for learning, for growing, and for being who they are.

If you can give them that, you've already given them something more valuable than any school place.
