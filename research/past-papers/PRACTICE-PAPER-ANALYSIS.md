**Time:** 50 minutes (just over 1 minute per question, minus passage reading time)

### 1.1 Comprehension (Q1-Q18)

#### Passage Characteristics
- **Length:** ~900-1,100 words across 2 pages
- **ONE passage per paper** (not multiple short passages)
- **Paragraphs:** 10-14 numbered paragraphs (questions reference paragraph numbers)
- **Genre mix across papers:**
  - Test A: "The Story of Jean Lafitte" -- historical non-fiction (pirates, treasure, Louisiana)
  - Test B: "Michelle's Holiday" -- contemporary realistic fiction (family holiday, humour)
  - Test C: "The Story of Robin Hood" -- legend/narrative non-fiction (medieval, adventure)
  - Test D: "The Story of Ancient Rome" -- historical non-fiction (Roman Empire, facts & dates)
- **Mix:** 3 non-fiction, 1 fiction. Non-fiction dominates but both genres appear.
- **Readability:** Challenging for Year 5/6 but accessible. Rich vocabulary embedded naturally.

#### Comprehension Question Types (within Q1-Q18)

| Type | Description | Typical Count | Difficulty |
|------|------------|---------------|------------|
| **Retrieval** | Direct fact from the passage | 4-5 per paper | Easy-Medium |
| **Simple Inference** | Deduce from evidence | 3-4 per paper | Medium |
| **Character Inference** | Feelings, motivation, personality | 2-3 per paper | Medium-Hard |
| **Negative Retrieval** | "Which is NOT mentioned/suggested" | 1-2 per paper | Hard |
| **Author's Purpose/Viewpoint** | Writer's intent, opinion, tone | 1-2 per paper | Hard |
| **Effect on Reader** | Impact of language choices | 0-1 per paper | Hard |
| **Text Type/Genre** | "Where would you read this?" | 1 per paper | Easy-Medium |
| **Multi-statement Combo** | "Which statements are true?" with combined answers like "A and C" | 0-1 per paper | Hard |
| **Prediction** | What happens next / what does ending suggest | 0-1 per paper | Medium-Hard |

#### Difficulty Progression
- **Q1-Q4:** Easy (straightforward retrieval, opener questions)
- **Q5-Q9:** Medium (simple inference, close distractors)
- **Q10-Q14:** Medium-Hard (character analysis, paragraph synthesis, negative retrieval)
- **Q15-Q18:** Hard (author viewpoint, prediction, complex inference, high vocabulary in options)

### 1.2 Vocabulary in Context (Q19-Q22)

- Always reference a specific paragraph number: "In paragraph seven, what does 'persecution' mean?"
- Target word/phrase shown in quotation marks
- 5 single-word or short-phrase options
- Tests: synonyms, phrase meaning, figurative language, advanced vocabulary
- Distractors include: antonyms, words from same semantic field, vaguely related words
- **One variant (seen in Test C):** Reverse definition -- "Which phrase from the passage means 'a method of disguise'?"
- Words tested: peered, persecution, escapades, romanticised, deposed, fictitious, golden age, merged, ruthless, tenderly, fully co-ordinated, outweighed

### 1.3 Word Class & Grammar (Q23-Q25)

Three question sub-types:

1. **Word class identification (group):** Four words displayed, identify their shared class
   - Example: "story, men, swamps, profit" -- Answer: Nouns
   - Example: "suspected, disguised, surrounded, returned" -- Answer: Verbs
   - Example: "Pantheon, Rome, Colosseum, Vespasian" -- Answer: Proper nouns

2. **Word class identification (in sentence):** Identify a specific word class within a quoted sentence
   - Example: "'Catalon lived to the ripe old age of 94...' Which word is a verb?" -- Answer: lived
   - Example: "'Michelle knew that he would accept.' Which word is a pronoun?" -- Answer: he

3. **Literary/punctuation terminology:** Identify sentence type or punctuation feature
   - Example: "'Did he know where the treasure was?' is a:" -- Answer: Rhetorical question
   - Example: "What is the punctuation called at the end of this quotation?" -- Answer: Ellipsis

### 1.4 Spelling Error-Spotting (Q26-Q33)

#### Format (CRITICAL -- different from standard MC)
- A **short passage** (~100-120 words) with a title, different from the comprehension passage
- Passage is split line-by-line, one line per question
- Each line divided into **4 segments labelled A, B, C, D**
- Segments are visually separated by grey dividers/boxes
- The 5th option is **N** (No mistake)
- Each line has either ONE spelling mistake in one segment, or NO mistake at all

#### Passage Topics (all fiction/narrative):
- Test A: "The Journey" (man travelling to distant lands)
- Test B: "The Knight's Journey" (medieval quest for treasure)
- Test C: "The Big Match" (football cup final)
- Test D: "The Sea Journey" (ship's captain searching for new lands)

#### Types of Spelling Errors
| Error Type | Examples | Frequency |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Homophones** | road/rode, their/there, new/knew, pane/pain, hoarse/horse, plaice/place, then/than, sea/see, where/were | Most common |
| **Phonetic misspellings** | qwest/quest, fealt/felt, scrole/scroll, jurney/journey, desicive/decisive | Common |
| **Similar-sounding** | relaying/relying, distance/distant, grate/great | Common |
| **Extra/missing letters** | indeeed/indeed, wating/waiting, glome/gloom | Occasional |
| **No mistake lines** | Answer is N | 1-2 per paper |

### 1.5 Punctuation Error-Spotting (Q34-Q41)

#### Format: IDENTICAL to spelling (4 segments A-D + N)

#### Passage Topics (all fiction/narrative):
- Test A: "Fred's Holiday" (boy wanting a game, finding money)
- Test B: "Lucie's Bike Ride" (girl learning to ride)
- Test C: "Hide and Seek" (children playing in a forest)
- Test D: "The First Day" (starting at a new school)

#### Types of Punctuation Errors
| Error Type | Examples | Frequency |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Missing capital after full stop** | "book. she went" → "book. She went" | Most common |
| **Missing apostrophe (contraction)** | "Im" → "I'm", "didnt" → "didn't" | Common |
| **Missing apostrophe (possession)** | "Emmas home" → "Emma's home", "schools tie" → "school's tie" | Common |
| **Wrong end punctuation** | "Where are you." → "Where are you?" | Common |
| **Missing comma** | "dark damp" → "dark, damp"; before "but" | Common |
| **Incorrect capitalisation** | "Be spending" mid-sentence → "be spending" | Occasional |
| **Speech punctuation** | Missing/misplaced speech marks | Occasional |
| **No mistake lines** | Answer is N | 1-2 per paper |

### 1.6 Cloze / Grammar Gap-Fill (Q42-Q49)

#### Format
- A **short passage** (~80-100 words) with blanks
- Each blank has **5 options (A-E)** displayed in a horizontal row of boxes
- Options are inline within the passage text
- The passage is a continuous narrative read as a story

#### Passage Topics:
- Test A: "Painting for Money" (artist clearing up, selling paintings)
- Test B: "The Concert" (boys forming a band for school prom)
- Test C: "The Secret" (explorer finding a hidden temple in Peru)
- Test D: "The Spooky House" (children visiting an abandoned house)

#### Types of Grammar/Cloze Questions
| Error Type | Examples | Frequency |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Homophones** | to/too/two, their/there/they're, who's/whose, witch/which | Most common |
| **Verb tenses** | chose/choose/choosed, decided/deicided, brought/brung/buyed | Very common |
| **Modal verbs** | should have/should of, might of/could have | Common |
| **Conjunctions** | but/because/although/however | Common |
| **Prepositions** | of/off/from/in/to | Common |
| **Comparative/superlative** | best/better/good/well | Occasional |
| **Passive voice** | been shown/been showed/been show | Occasional |
| **Fixed phrases** | "in order to", "different from" | Occasional |

#### Distractor Patterns in Cloze
- **Made-up verb forms:** "knewed", "choosed", "bringed", "buyed"
- **Common grammatical errors as options:** "should of" (not "should have")
- **Near-homophones as word options:** rots/rotes/routes/roots/rites
- **Real words that don't fit context:** defied/derided/decried alongside decided

### 1.7 Distractor Construction Patterns (All Sections)

1. **Close but imprecise:** Option is partially correct but another is more precise
2. **Partially true:** Contains some accurate info but misses the key point
3. **Same semantic field:** All options relate to the topic but only one fits
4. **Vocabulary traps:** Sophisticated words that say the same thing (analogous = similar), making the odd-one-out correct
5. **Too extreme:** "It is all lies" vs "History has made it hard to know"
6. **Literal misreading traps:** "told BY a pirate" vs "a story ABOUT a pirate"
7. **Real-world knowledge traps:** True in real life but not stated in the passage
8. **Combination answers:** "Answers A, B and C" / "Answers A and C" (distinctive GL format)

### 2.2 Detailed Format for Each Question Type

#### TYPE 1: Letter Move (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "Take one letter from the word on the left and add it to the word on the right to make two new, proper words. The order of the letters must not be changed."
**Example:** paint + men → pint + mean (the letter "a" moves)
**Format:** Two words shown. Child identifies the single letter that transfers.
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: short common words (fleet + fat)
- Hard: longer words with multiple plausible letters (crease + sever)

#### TYPE 2: Odd Two Out (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "Find the two words that are different from the rest."
**Example:** March, Monday, July, December, Christmas → Monday & Christmas (others are months)
**Format:** Five words listed. Child marks the TWO that don't belong. Three words share a category; two are outsiders.
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: concrete categories (clothing types, vegetables)
- Hard: abstract/nuanced (words meaning "big" vs words meaning "amazing"; measurement terms)
**Vocabulary demands:** magenta, sapphire, circumference, girth, placid, tenacious, erratic, coppice

#### TYPE 3: Letter Sums (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "The letters stand for numbers. Work out the answer to each sum as a letter."
**Example:** If A=1, B=2, C=4, D=10, E=8, then B × C + B = ? → Answer: D (2×4+2=10)
**Format:** 5 letter-to-number mappings, then an arithmetic expression. Answer is one of the letters.
**Tests:** BODMAS, mental arithmetic, letter-number translation
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: single operation (A × C)
- Hard: 3-4 operations with large numbers, some papers use decimals (A=3.5, B=5.5)

#### TYPE 4: Verbal Analogies (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "Choose one word from each set which best completes the sentences."
**Example:** Black is to (dark, white, old) as good is to (new, young, bad) → white, bad
**Format:** "X is to (a, b, c) as Y is to (d, e, f)" — pick one from each bracket.
**Relationship types tested:**
- Synonyms/antonyms (fresh:stale as sweet:sour)
- Category membership (piano:keys as viola:strings)
- Young animal names (swan:cygnet as hare:leveret)
- Subject-field (astronomy:stars as meteorology:weather)
- Part-whole, tool-material, cause-effect
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: common relationships (summer:winter as day:night)
- Hard: advanced vocabulary (campanology:bells, consecutively:following)

#### TYPE 5: Compound Words (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "Find two words, one from each set, which combine to make one new word. Left word always comes first."
**Example:** (red, garden, green) + (jumper, house, rose) → greenhouse
**Format:** Two bracketed sets of 3 words. Pick one from each to form a compound word.
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: common compounds (cupboard, strawberry)
- Hard: less obvious (bandage from "band"+"age", copyright from "copy"+"right")

#### TYPE 6: Hidden Words (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "A four-letter word is hidden between two words that are next to each other."
**Example:** "The child fell over the stone." → LOVE (fe-LL OV-Er)
**Format:** A sentence. The hidden 4-letter word spans two adjacent words. Mark the two words.
**Always exactly 4 letters.**
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: common hidden word, obvious word boundary
- Hard: unusual hidden word, less obvious split

#### TYPE 7: Missing Three Letters (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "One word in capital letters has had three letters removed. These three missing letters make a three-letter word."
**Example:** "There was a strange CR by the pond." → OAK (CR+OAK = CROAK)
**Format:** Sentence with an incomplete capitalised word. The 3 missing letters form a word.
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: FYARD → FARMYARD (missing ARM)
- Hard: DEDENT → DEPENDENT (missing PEN), LANDSE → LANDSCAPE (missing CAP)

#### TYPE 8: Synonyms / Closest Meaning (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "Choose the two words, one from each set, that are closest in meaning."
**Example:** (race, shop, start) + (flag, end, begin) → start, begin
**Format:** Two bracketed sets of 3 words. Pick the synonym pair (one from each).
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: friend/companion, hungry/famished
- Hard: apparition/spectre, courage/valour, sensitive/responsive

#### TYPE 9: Letter Pair Series (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "Work out the pattern and mark the correct pair of letters to continue the sequence."
**Alphabet provided on the page.**
**Example:** AZ, CY, EX, GW, ? → IV (first letter +2, second letter -1)
**Format:** Series of 4-5 letter pairs. Find the next pair.
**Difficulty range:**
- Easy: both letters shift by constant amounts
- Hard: mixed operations (one forward, one backward, different step sizes)

#### TYPE 10: Word-Code Analogies (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "The three words on the right go together the same way as the three on the left. Work out the missing word."
**Example:** duck (cat) flat : mate (?) chip → The middle word is derived from the outer words by a letter-extraction rule.
**Format:** LEFT: word1 (word2) word3 | RIGHT: word1 (?) word3
**These are the HARDEST questions on the paper.** Always placed near the end.
**The child must figure out which letters from the outer words combine to form the middle word.**

#### TYPE 11: Number Series (7 per paper)
**Instruction:** "What number continues the pattern?"
**Example:** 2, 4, 6, 8, ? → 10
**Pattern types seen:**
- Simple arithmetic (+3, +5, -4)
- Doubling (3, 6, 12, 24)
- Triangular numbers (3, 6, 10, 15, 21)
- Fibonacci-like (1, 3, 4, 7, 11)
- Square numbers descending (81, 64, 49, 36)
- Alternating/interleaved sequences (two separate patterns interwoven)
- Halving and adding (100, 50, 60, 30, 40, 20)
- Decreasing gaps (99, 98, 94, 85, 69)
**Difficulty range:** Enormous — from add-3 to multi-pattern interleaved sequences

#### TYPE 12: Logic - Short (1 per paper)
**Format:** Short factual scenario with 5 options (A-E). Mark one answer.
**Example types:**
- "A crow is a large black bird. There was a large black bird in Sally's garden..." → Which statement MUST be true?
- "All pizzas are covered with cheese..." → Which statement must be true?
- "There are 5 balls in a bag with 3 different colours..." → Which CANNOT be true?
**Tests:** Logical deduction, avoiding assumptions beyond stated facts

#### TYPE 13: Logic - Long (1 per paper)
**Format:** Longer scenario requiring systematic deduction. Often involves ordering/ranking.
**Example types:**
- 5 children with different ages, given clues → Which two are the same age?
- 5 people in numbered houses, given position clues → Which statement must be true?
- Swimming race times with ordering clues → Whose time was 28 seconds?
**Tests:** Multi-step logical reasoning, constraint satisfaction

#### TYPE 14a: Letter Codes (6 per paper, Tests A/B only)
**Instruction:** "Some words are written in code. The first code word is worked out for you. Using the same code, work out the second code word."
**Alphabet provided.**
**Example:** If SHOP = RGNO, what does QNZC mean? → ROAD (each letter shifted -1)
**Code types:**
- Constant shift (+1, -1, +2, etc.)
- Variable shift per position (+2, +3, +4, +5 for successive letters)
- Both encoding (word → code) and decoding (code → word)

#### TYPE 14b: Shared/Missing Letter (6 per paper, Tests C/D only)
**Instruction:** "One letter can be used to end the first word and start the second word. The same letter must complete both pairs."
**Example:** MEA(?)ABLE / COS(?)ENNIS → T (MEAT/TABLE, COST/TENNIS)
**Format:** Two pairs of incomplete words. One letter fits all four gaps.
**Tests:** Vocabulary breadth, systematic letter testing

1. **Convert to 5-option MC** (simpler, matches our existing framework)
2. **Support dual-answer selection** (more authentic, better exam prep)

### 2.6 Difficulty Within Each Section

Every section follows a consistent difficulty curve:
- **Q1-Q2 of section:** Accessible, uses common words/simple patterns
- **Q3-Q5 of section:** Medium, requires careful thought
- **Q6-Q7 of section:** Hard, uses advanced vocabulary or complex patterns

This maps to our difficulty levels:
- **Difficulty 1 (Easy):** First 2-3 questions of each section
- **Difficulty 2 (Medium):** Middle 2-3 questions
- **Difficulty 3 (Hard):** Final 2 questions of each section

## PART 4: DIFFICULTY CALIBRATION

### How to Map to Our 3 Difficulty Levels

Based on the consistent difficulty progression observed in ALL papers:

**Difficulty 1 (Easy) -- ~30% of questions:**
- Comprehension: Q1-Q4 style (direct retrieval, obvious answers)
- Spelling: Common homophones (their/there, to/too)
- Punctuation: Missing capital after full stop
- Cloze: Obvious homophones (to/too, their/there)
- VR: First 2 questions of each section, common vocabulary

**Difficulty 2 (Medium) -- ~40% of questions:**
- Comprehension: Q5-Q12 style (inference, close distractors)
- Spelling: Less obvious homophones, phonetic errors
- Punctuation: Apostrophes, speech marks
- Cloze: Verb tenses, modal verbs
- VR: Middle questions, requires good vocabulary and careful thinking

**Difficulty 3 (Hard) -- ~30% of questions:**
- Comprehension: Q13-Q18 style (author viewpoint, negative retrieval, prediction, advanced vocab in options)
- Spelling: Unusual misspellings, "no mistake" lines
- Punctuation: Subtle comma rules, "no mistake" lines
- Cloze: Passive voice, complex conjunctions, fixed phrases
- VR: Final 2 questions of each section, advanced vocabulary, complex patterns

## PART 5: KEY PRINCIPLES FOR QUESTION WRITING

### English

1. **Comprehension passages must be ~900-1000 words** with 10-14 paragraphs. Mix fiction and non-fiction. British setting/context.
2. **Questions progress from easy retrieval to hard inference** within each passage set.
3. **Always include at least 1 "NOT mentioned" question** (negative retrieval) per passage.
4. **Vocabulary questions must reference paragraph numbers** and show the target word in quotes.
5. **Spelling errors should primarily be homophones** embedded in a continuous narrative passage.
6. **Punctuation errors focus on capitals, apostrophes, commas, and speech marks.**
7. **The "N" (no mistake) option in error-spotting is essential** -- at least 1-2 per section.
8. **Cloze passages should test homophones, verb tenses, and conjunctions** in a flowing story.
9. **ALL content is 100% original** -- no published passages, no published questions.

### Verbal Reasoning

1. **Word Manipulation is the largest category (33-40%)** -- prioritise these types.
2. **Many questions require TWO answers** -- the app needs to support this or convert to MC.
3. **Vocabulary demands are high** -- children need words like cygnet, leveret, spectre, valour, campanology.
4. **Number-based questions require BODMAS** and mental arithmetic with numbers up to ~100 (occasionally larger).
5. **Code/pattern questions are the hardest** -- word-code analogies are placed last for a reason.
6. **Each section has 7 questions with consistent difficulty progression** (easy → hard).
7. **Logic questions are rare (2 per paper)** but test genuine deductive reasoning.
8. **ALL content is 100% original** -- new words, new patterns, new scenarios.
