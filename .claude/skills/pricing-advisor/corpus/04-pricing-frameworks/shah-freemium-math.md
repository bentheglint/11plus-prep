## Who / what

Hiten Shah is a serial product entrepreneur who co-founded Crazy Egg, KISSmetrics, and FYI, and writes the Product Habits newsletter. He is one of the most cited practitioners on the economics of freemium and product-led growth. Unlike many commentators who theorise about freemium, Shah has built and operated free-tier products for over a decade across different market sizes, giving him a ground-level view of where the model works and where it bleeds cash. He is also notable for being in explicit, documented tension with co-founders who rejected freemium (his *Startup Chat* co-host Steli Efti built Close.io as paid-only, arguing that paid expectations drive better customers) — a tension that sharpens Shah's thinking considerably.

## Core ideas (their key beliefs / frameworks)

- **Freemium is a marketing strategy, not a pricing strategy**: the fundamental reframe. Freemium's function is customer acquisition, not monetisation. "Freemium isn't a way of monetising your customers, it's a way of acquiring them." Treating it as a revenue model misunderstands the unit economics.
- **The fastest-growing SaaS companies offer free plans**: Shah's observation across the market is that the companies with the steepest growth curves (Slack, Dropbox, Notion, Figma) built their acquisition engine on a free tier. The free tier creates a pool of future customers and a word-of-mouth distribution mechanism.
- **Freemium is a volume game**: to generate meaningful revenue from a 2–5% conversion rate, you need thousands of active free users. The model only works if unit economics support this ratio, or if free users generate network value for paid customers. Shah: "You need thousands of active free users to generate hundreds of paid customers."
- **Free users provide compounding value beyond conversion**: even users who never pay can generate referrals, create content, post screenshots, recommend to colleagues, and create social proof. This non-paying value must be factored into the economics, not discounted to zero.
- **Freemium raises the stakes on retention**: the same low switching costs that allow free users to adopt your product allow them to leave it and adopt a competitor. Shah is direct: "The easier it gets for your company to acquire customers, the harder it gets to retain them." Freemium success depends on a product that is genuinely sticky, not just accessible.
- **Free tier design requires discipline**: the free tier should limit functionality, user count, or storage in a way that prepares users for eventual upgrade. A badly designed free tier either gives away too much (no upgrade trigger) or too little (no genuine value, no habit formed).
- **Founders must stay close to sales**: even in PLG/freemium models, Shah believes founders should be involved in early sales conversations. The signal from why free users do or don't convert is irreplaceable.
- **Freemium may delay initial revenue but expands long-term potential**: the trade-off is clear and must be made consciously. Founders who choose freemium without runway to survive the delay are making a structural mistake.

## Method / how to apply

**The freemium viability test (synthesised from Shah's body of work)**

Before launching a free tier, answer all four:
1. Do you have enough runway to survive 6–12 months of acquiring non-paying users before meaningful conversion?
2. Is there a natural usage ceiling that creates a genuine upgrade trigger (storage, seats, feature depth, report frequency)?
3. Does the free tier create genuine habit? (Can a user accomplish something meaningful in the free tier — or does it just frustrate them?)
4. Is the product viral or shareable by nature, such that free users become a distribution mechanism?

If you cannot answer yes to all four, consider a time-limited free trial instead.

**The conversion math (industry benchmarks Shah works within)**

| Funnel stage | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| Visitor to free signup | ~6% (PLG median) |
| Free signup to active free user | ~20–40% (depends on activation design) |
| Active free user to paid (within 6 months) | 3–8% (self-serve) / 5–15% (sales-assist) |
| Net: visitors to paying customers | ~0.4–0.5% |

**Practical implication**: to reach 100 paying customers, a freemium product needs approximately 20,000 monthly visitors with a functional PLG funnel. If traffic is lower, a free trial or direct paid model may produce faster revenue with less dilution.

**Retention as the sustainability driver**

Shah's retention framework:
- Acquisition is now cheap and commoditised. Retention is the moat.
- Design for sticky value first (what keeps users coming back in week 2, week 4?).
- Align marketing messaging to attract users who match the retention pattern of your best customers.
- Expanding value to existing customers (features, depth, integrations) is 4× cheaper than acquiring new ones.

## Applicability to a UK 11+ app at pre-launch / early-launch stage

Shah would force a 11+ app founder to answer the freemium viability test before anything else. A children's exam prep app has a natural free-tier ceiling (limited topics, limited practice questions, no detailed analytics) and a clear upgrade trigger (the parent wants the full curriculum before the exam date). However, the volume requirement is real: without meaningful organic or word-of-mouth traffic from parent communities (Mumsnet, school groups, tutor referrals), a freemium model at launch will produce too few paying customers too slowly to survive. Shah's implicit advice: if you cannot project 5,000+ free signups in the first three months through a credible channel, launch with a time-limited trial instead and revisit freemium once you have distribution.
