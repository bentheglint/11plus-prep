## Source

The Sutton Trust is the UK's leading social mobility research charity. It has produced a continuous series of polling and research on private tutoring uptake since 2005. The two most relevant recent publications are:

- **Tutoring: The New Landscape** (March 2023) — surveys conducted via Ipsos Young People Omnibus and the COSMO (COVID Social Mobility & Opportunities) study tracking a Year 11 cohort from 2020/21.
- **Private Tutoring 2026** (March 2026) — follow-on polling, incorporating separate teacher polling conducted in 2025. The 2026 report updates all headline figures.

Both are freely available on suttontrust.com.

## Key findings (the headline numbers)

- **29%** of secondary pupils (aged 11–16) in England and Wales had received private tutoring as of 2026, up from 27% pre-pandemic and 18% in 2005. (Sutton Trust, *Private Tutoring 2026*, March 2026)
- **Year 11 pupils** are the most likely to receive tutoring at **25%**; Year 10 at **10%**. (Sutton Trust, 2026)
- **London: 45%** of pupils had received private tutoring vs **27%** in the rest of England, **24%** in Wales, **33%** in urban areas and **19%** in rural areas. (Sutton Trust, 2026)
- **Socioeconomic gap**: 30% of pupils from the best-off households vs 23% from the worst-off households had tutoring. This gap has persisted since 2019 (28% vs 21%). (Sutton Trust, 2026)
- **Ethnic disparities**: Black pupils 64%, Asian pupils 50%, White pupils 20%. In deprived areas specifically: Black 65%, Asian 43%, White 10%. (Sutton Trust, 2026)
- **Income quartile (2023 data)**: 32% of those in the top income quartile had private tutoring vs 13% in the bottom quartile. (Sutton Trust, *Tutoring: The New Landscape*, 2023)
- **Professional/managerial households**: 24% tutoring rate vs 11% for routine/manual households. (Sutton Trust, 2023)
- **Graduate parents' children**: 26% tutoring rate vs 13% for non-graduate parents. (Sutton Trust, 2023)
- The market is estimated at **£2 billion per year**. (Sutton Trust, 2026 news release)
- **58%** of schools reduced their tutoring offer compared to the previous year (2025 teacher polling). In-school tutoring declined after the National Tutoring Programme ended in 2024. (Sutton Trust, 2026)
- School-based tutoring narrowed inequality: when both school and private tutoring were counted, the gap between professional and manual households narrows to **just 1 percentage point**. (Sutton Trust, 2023)

## Sample / methodology (if known)

- *Tutoring: The New Landscape* (2023): Ipsos Young People Omnibus survey; cross-checked against the COSMO longitudinal study tracking a Year 11 cohort (2020/21 academic year). Nationally representative sample.
- *Private Tutoring 2026*: Updated polling using consistent methodology. Separate polling of teachers conducted in 2025 via representative teacher panels.

## Applicability to UK 11+ app pricing

The Sutton Trust data establishes that private tutoring is now a mainstream purchase for UK parents — affecting nearly 1 in 3 secondary-age children and nearly half of all London families. The socioeconomic data shows that the core paying audience (professional/managerial, graduate parents, London/South East) represents a group already spending significantly on tutoring. For an 11+ prep app, these are exactly the families in the target market; a monthly subscription of £20–£70 sits comfortably below what they already spend on private tutoring. The data also signals a political and consumer tailwind: as in-school tutoring declines post-NTP, parents are actively filling the gap with private provision, creating demand for affordable digital alternatives.
