## Overview

Brilliant is an interactive learning platform focused on STEM — maths, science, and computer science — for adults and older teens. Founded in 2012, it serves over 10 million users globally. Unlike most edtech platforms, Brilliant has no meaningful free tier: almost all content is behind a paywall. It competes on depth and interactivity, positioning itself as an alternative to passive video learning. Its courses are built around problem-solving and visual, interactive explanations rather than lectures. The platform is web-first with strong mobile apps. Brilliant targets intellectually curious adults and is not a curriculum-aligned product, though it overlaps with 11+ content areas (maths, logical reasoning, verbal reasoning analogues in science).

## Pricing tiers

**Free**
- Price: £0 / $0
- Included: A very limited selection of introductory lessons to demonstrate the product; no meaningful ongoing content. This functions as a trial/demo rather than a usable free tier. Brilliant does not position this as "free forever" — it is a taster.

**Premium (Monthly)**
- Price: $27.99/month (approx. £22.40/month)
- Billing: Monthly, cancel anytime
- Included: Full access to all courses and content, progress tracking, daily challenges, mobile apps.

**Premium (Annual)**
- Price: $13.49/month billed annually ($161.88/year, approx. £129.50/year)
- Billing: Annual upfront
- Included: Everything in monthly Premium. Annual discount is approximately 52% off the monthly rate — one of the largest annual discounts in edtech.

**Group / Team Plan**
- Price: From $11.33/seat/month (billed annually for teams of 5+); pricing scales down with larger teams
- Billing: Annual
- Included: All Premium content, team management dashboard, progress reporting for administrators.

**30-Day Free Trial**
- Available on Premium (annual). Trial converts automatically to paid at end of period. No credit card required on some promotional flows.

## Free vs paid (the core question for global edtech)

Brilliant's philosophy is the inverse of Duolingo's: the product is premium-only, and the "free tier" is effectively a marketing demo. This works because Brilliant's target user (an intellectually curious adult who wants to improve STEM skills) has higher willingness to pay and lower price sensitivity than a language learner. Brilliant does not try to serve the entire market — it serves a specific segment willing to pay for quality.

The absence of a genuine free tier means Brilliant relies heavily on its trial, word-of-mouth, and content marketing (blog, social) to drive top-of-funnel. Conversion from trial to paid is reportedly high (Brilliant does not publish figures, but the trial is generous enough to create genuine attachment before the paywall hits).

## Family / multi-user pricing

Brilliant does not offer a consumer family plan. The group plan (from $11.33/seat annually) is designed for corporate teams and educational institutions rather than households. A parent wanting two children on Brilliant would pay two full Premium subscriptions, making the household cost $27/month (annual) — significantly higher than competitors with family tiers. This is a structural gap in Brilliant's consumer offering.

## Unusual mechanics

- **52% annual discount**: The gap between monthly ($27.99) and annual ($13.49/month) pricing is unusually large. This is a deliberate anchoring technique: the monthly price feels expensive, making the annual deal seem like a bargain. It also strongly incentivises annual commitment, improving retention and revenue predictability.
- **No grandfathering or legacy pricing**: Brilliant has raised prices over time without grandfathering existing subscribers, signalling confidence in retention at higher price points.
- **Daily challenge mechanic**: Free and paid users receive a daily problem, which serves as a retention and re-engagement loop. This is a lightweight habit mechanic borrowed from language apps.
- **"Try it for free" CTA**: Brilliant's primary conversion CTA emphasises the 30-day trial, not the price. Price is secondary in all marketing copy.
- **No ads, no hearts, no streaks**: Brilliant deliberately avoids gamification mechanics that other edtech platforms use. Its positioning is explicitly "serious learning" rather than "habit formation."

## Positioning copy

Brilliant positions itself as anti-passive: "Don't just watch — do." The core message is that interactive problem-solving is more effective than video lectures. Pricing copy emphasises access and breadth: "Unlock thousands of lessons in maths, science, and computer science." The annual plan CTA typically reads "Most popular" with the monthly price crossed out. Testimonials emphasise "I actually understand this now" outcomes rather than credentials or certificates.

## Lessons for a UK 11+ app at smaller scale

Brilliant demonstrates that a premium-only model (no genuine free tier) works when the target user has clear willingness to pay and the product quality justifies it — but it requires a robust trial to replace the trust-building role of a free tier. The 52% annual discount trick is directly portable: anchor high on monthly pricing, make annual look like a rescue. The absence of a family plan is a learnable mistake — a UK 11+ app serving multiple-child households should treat family pricing as a first-class tier, not an afterthought.
