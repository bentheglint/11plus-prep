# GL Assessment Research: Long Division

## Format

### Paper Structure
- GL 11+ maths paper: **50 questions in 50 minutes** (1 minute per question average)
- All questions are **multiple choice with 5 options (A-E)**
- Answers marked on a separate answer sheet, electronically scanned
- Number/arithmetic questions appear most frequently — roughly **5x more number questions than any other category** on a typical GL paper
- Division questions fall under the "Number" strand and appear alongside multiplication, fractions, decimals, and percentages
- Estimated **2-4 pure division questions per paper**, plus additional questions where division is a required step within multi-step problems (e.g., finding averages, unit costs, sharing quantities)

### How Division Questions Appear
- Always presented as a **word problem or calculation in context** — GL rarely (if ever) presents bare "calculate 4,576 / 32" questions
- The question stem describes a real-world scenario requiring division
- Five plausible numerical answers are provided
- No working space is provided on the answer sheet — children must work on the question booklet or mentally
- Questions are **not grouped by topic** — a division question might sit between a geometry question and a fractions question

### Time Pressure
- At 1 minute per question, children cannot afford to spend 3-4 minutes on a single long division
- GL expects fluency: children who need to laboriously work through every step of long division will run out of time
- Many division questions can be solved more efficiently through estimation, known facts, or inverse multiplication rather than formal long division
- The multiple-choice format means estimation + elimination is a valid and often faster strategy

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

### Core Division Skills (Direct)

| Skill | Frequency | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Division by 1-digit number (short division) | Common | Year 5 curriculum; expected to be fluent |
| Division by 2-digit number (long division) | Common | Year 6 curriculum; the main "long division" skill |
| Interpreting remainders in context | Common | Round up, round down, express as fraction — depends on scenario |
| Division as inverse of multiplication | Common | "What number multiplied by 15 gives 375?" |
| Division in multi-step problems | Very common | Often combined with other operations |
| Dividing by 10, 100, 1000 | Common | Place value shifts; often a sub-step |
| Dividing decimals | Occasional | E.g., "Share £45.60 equally among 8 children" |
| Estimating division answers | Implicit | Needed for time-efficient answering and checking |

### Related Skills (Division as a Tool)

| Skill | How Division is Used |
|-------|---------------------|
| Finding averages (mean) | Sum / count = mean |
| Unit cost / unit rate | Total cost / number of items |
| Converting units | E.g., "How many metres in 4,500 cm?" (divide by 100) |
| Fraction of an amount | E.g., "Find 3/4 of 480" requires 480 / 4 first |
| Ratio / sharing in ratio | Total / (sum of ratio parts) then multiply |
| Speed, distance, time | Distance / time = speed, etc. |
| Finding factors | Testing divisibility |
| Simplifying fractions | Dividing numerator and denominator by GCD |

### Approximate Weighting
- **Pure division questions**: ~5-8% of the paper (2-4 questions out of 50)
- **Questions requiring division as a step**: ~15-20% of the paper (7-10 additional questions)
- Combined, division fluency affects roughly **20-25% of a child's maths score**

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Division by a **single-digit** number or a "friendly" 2-digit number (10, 12, 15, 20, 25, 50)
- Dividend up to **3 digits**
- **No remainder**, or remainder is not relevant to the answer
- Straightforward word problem with a single step
- Relies on **known multiplication facts** (times tables to 12x12)
- Context is simple and familiar (sharing sweets, packing boxes)

**Number ranges:**
- Dividends: 48 to 999
- Divisors: 2-12, 15, 20, 25, 50
- Quotients: typically single or 2-digit whole numbers

**Example question types:**
- "A teacher shares 144 pencils equally among 12 tables. How many pencils does each table get?" (144 / 12 = 12)
- "There are 180 children split into groups of 6. How many groups are there?" (180 / 6 = 30)
- "A bag contains 225 marbles shared equally among 5 friends. How many marbles does each friend receive?" (225 / 5 = 45)

**What makes it D1:**
- Can be solved using times table recall
- No ambiguity in what to do
- Clean division (no remainder)
- Single operation needed

---

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**Characteristics:**
- Division by a **2-digit number** (13-50 range, not "friendly" multiples)
- Dividend up to **4 digits**
- May have a **remainder that needs interpreting** (round up for "how many buses needed", round down for "how many complete boxes")
- May be a **two-step problem** where division is one step
- Requires formal long division method or strong estimation skills
- Context may require identifying that division is the correct operation

**Number ranges:**
- Dividends: 100 to 9,999
- Divisors: 13-50 (non-friendly numbers like 17, 23, 28, 34, 43)
- Quotients: 2-digit numbers, possibly with remainders

**Example question types:**
- "A baker makes 375 biscuits and packs them into boxes of 15. How many boxes does he fill?" (375 / 15 = 25)
- "96 pupils go on a trip. Each minibus holds 15 passengers. How many minibuses are needed?" (96 / 15 = 6 r6, so **7** minibuses — round up)
- "A farmer has 672 apples. He packs 21 into each crate. How many crates does he fill?" (672 / 21 = 32)
- "A school raises £828 for charity. They share it equally among 23 charities. How much does each charity receive?" (828 / 23 = £36)

**What makes it D2:**
- Requires formal written method (cannot rely on times tables alone)
- May need remainder interpretation
- May need to identify that division is the correct operation from the word problem
- Two plausible approaches exist (e.g., repeated subtraction vs formal method)

---

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Division by a **2-digit number** with larger dividends or awkward divisors
- **Multi-step problems** requiring division combined with other operations
- Remainder must be interpreted in a **non-obvious way** (e.g., as a fraction or decimal, or requiring further calculation)
- May involve **dividing decimals** or money amounts that don't divide cleanly
- Problem may be presented in a way that **disguises** the need for division
- May require **working backwards** (given the answer, find the original number)
- May involve **estimation** to choose between close answer options

**Number ranges:**
- Dividends: up to 9,999 (occasionally higher)
- Divisors: 2-digit numbers (17-99)
- Answers may be decimals, fractions, or require multi-step reasoning

**Example question types:**
- "A roll of ribbon is 8.4 metres long. Pieces of 0.35 m are cut from it. How many pieces can be cut?" (8.4 / 0.35 = 24 — requires adjusting decimals)
- "Tom thinks of a number. He multiplies it by 17, then adds 45. His answer is 249. What was his original number?" (249 - 45 = 204, then 204 / 17 = 12)
- "A factory produces 7,392 widgets in a 32-hour week. On average, how many widgets per hour?" (7,392 / 32 = 231)
- "4 friends share the cost of a holiday equally. The holiday costs £2,356. How much does each person pay?" (2,356 / 4 = £589 — or if the question adds a twist like "one friend pays double")
- "Which of these divisions gives a remainder of 5?" (testing understanding of the division algorithm)
- "A number divided by 14 gives 23 remainder 8. What is the number?" (14 x 23 + 8 = 330)

**What makes it D3:**
- Multiple operations needed
- Division is disguised or embedded in a complex scenario
- Requires strong conceptual understanding (not just procedural fluency)
- Answer options are close together, requiring precision
- May involve working backwards or algebraic thinking
- Decimal division or non-standard contexts

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Equal Sharing / Grouping
The most common format. A quantity is shared equally among groups, or items are grouped into fixed-size sets.

**Sharing:** "504 stickers are shared equally among 24 children. How many stickers does each child get?"
**Grouping:** "A factory packs 1,248 tins into boxes of 16. How many boxes are needed?"

**GL trap:** The sharing and grouping versions use the same calculation but children sometimes struggle to identify grouping as division.

### Type 2: Remainder Interpretation
A division that doesn't work out exactly, and the child must decide what the remainder means.

**Round up:** "85 people need taxis. Each taxi holds 4 passengers. How many taxis are needed?" (85 / 4 = 21 r1, answer: **22** taxis)
**Round down:** "A baker has 500g of flour. Each biscuit needs 35g. How many biscuits can she make?" (500 / 35 = 14 r10, answer: **14** biscuits)
**State the remainder:** "A box holds 8 eggs. Jamie has 50 eggs. He fills as many boxes as he can. How many eggs are left over?" (50 / 8 = 6 r2, answer: **2** eggs)
**Remainder as fraction:** "Share 47 metres of rope equally among 5 people. How many metres does each person get?" (47 / 5 = 9.4 or 9 2/5 metres)

### Type 3: Inverse / Missing Number
Division is used as the inverse of multiplication to find a missing value.

- "What number multiplied by 23 gives 621?" (621 / 23 = 27)
- "_ x 15 = 450" (450 / 15 = 30)
- "I think of a number, multiply by 8, and get 296. What was my number?" (296 / 8 = 37)

### Type 4: Multi-Step Problems
Division is one step in a problem requiring two or more operations.

- "A shop sells pencils in packs of 12. Each pack costs £1.80. What is the cost per pencil?" (£1.80 / 12 = 15p — division + unit conversion)
- "A school has 648 pupils. 1/3 are in KS1 and the rest are in KS2. KS2 pupils are split into 12 equal classes. How many pupils in each KS2 class?" (648 x 2/3 = 432, then 432 / 12 = 36)

### Type 5: Division with Decimals / Money
Division involving decimal numbers, typically in a money context.

- "Four friends share a restaurant bill of £97.60 equally. How much does each pay?" (£97.60 / 4 = £24.40)
- "A piece of wood is 3.78 m long. It is cut into 6 equal pieces. How long is each piece?" (3.78 / 6 = 0.63 m)

### Type 6: Working Backwards
The answer to a division is given, and the child must find the original number.

- "A number divided by 14 gives 23 remainder 8. What is the number?" (14 x 23 + 8 = 330)
- "When I divide my number by 25, I get exactly 36. What is my number?" (25 x 36 = 900)

### Type 7: Estimation and Approximation
Requires rounding or estimating to find an approximate answer.

- "Which of these is closest to 7,843 / 39?" (Estimate: 7,800 / 40 = 195)
- "A farmer harvests 4,897 potatoes. About how many bags of 25 can he fill?" (Estimate: 5,000 / 25 = 200)

### Type 8: Divisibility and Factor-Based
Tests understanding of when division results in a whole number.

- "Which of these numbers is exactly divisible by 16?" (Test each option)
- "Tom has some stickers. When he shares them equally among 7 friends, he has 3 left over. When he shares them among 5 friends, he has 1 left over. How many stickers could Tom have?" (Find common solution)

## Distractor Design

GL Assessment designs distractors (wrong answers) based on **predictable errors children make**. For division questions, the typical distractor patterns are:

### Pattern 1: Off-by-One Errors
The correct answer surrounded by +1 and -1 variants.
- If correct answer is 24, options might include 23 and 25
- Exploits small calculation errors in the long division process

### Pattern 2: Remainder Mishandling
- **Including the remainder in the answer:** 96 / 15 = 6 r6, distractor: **6** (forgot to round up)
- **Dropping the remainder:** When the question asks "how many left over?", distractor is the quotient (6) instead of the remainder (6)
- **Adding remainder to quotient:** 96 / 15 = 6 r6, distractor: **12** (6 + 6)
- **Using remainder as a decimal digit:** 96 / 15 = 6 r6, distractor: **6.6** (treating r6 as .6)

### Pattern 3: Place Value / Zero Errors
- Forgetting to write a zero in the quotient when the divisor doesn't go into a partial dividend
- E.g., 2,016 / 8 = **252**, but a child might write **252** correctly or miss the zero and write **252** — more commonly, 3,024 / 8 where missing the zero gives 38 instead of 378
- Distractor is a number with a missing or extra digit

### Pattern 4: Wrong Operation
- **Multiplying instead of dividing:** "How many boxes of 15 from 375?" — distractor might be a multiplication-based number
- **Subtracting instead of dividing:** Distractor is 375 - 15 = 360

### Pattern 5: Calculation Errors in Long Division
- Wrong multiple chosen during the division process
- E.g., when dividing by 23, child uses 23 x 3 = 69 instead of 23 x 4 = 92, leading to an answer that's slightly off
- Distractor is the answer you'd get if you made one specific multiplication error

### Pattern 6: Close Plausible Values
- All five options are within a narrow range (e.g., 21, 22, 23, 24, 25)
- Forces precise calculation — estimation alone won't suffice
- This is GL's most common approach: options are **tightly clustered around the correct answer**

### Pattern 7: Misreading the Question
- Answer to a different interpretation of the problem
- E.g., "How many complete boxes?" vs "How many items left over?" — distractor is the answer to the wrong question

## Common GL Traps

### Trap 1: "How Many Needed?" vs "How Many Complete?"
The single most important distinction in division word problems. GL deliberately uses both phrasings:
- "How many minibuses are **needed**?" → Round UP (you need an extra bus for the leftover people)
- "How many boxes can be filled **completely**?" → Round DOWN (you can't fill an incomplete box)
- Children who don't read carefully will choose the wrong answer — and GL will have it as an option

### Trap 2: Zero in the Quotient
Divisions like 3,024 / 14 = 216, or 4,032 / 16 = 252 — where a zero appears in the working but not the final answer. Conversely, 2,040 / 12 = 170 — where the zero IS part of the answer. Children who mishandle the zero get a completely wrong answer.

### Trap 3: "Friendly" Divisors That Aren't
A divisor like 25 or 50 seems friendly, but the dividend doesn't divide cleanly. E.g., 637 / 25 = 25 r12. Children assume it'll be a nice answer because 25 is a "nice" number.

### Trap 4: Hidden Division
The word "divide" or "share" doesn't appear in the question. Instead:
- "How many groups of 15 can be made from 360 children?"
- "A rope is cut into pieces each 0.75 m long. The rope is 6 metres long. How many pieces?"
- "What fraction of 480 is 60?" (60/480 = 1/8, requires recognising the division relationship)

### Trap 5: Decimal Answers in Whole-Number Contexts
- "Share £14.50 among 4 children" = £3.625 → need to round to £3.63 (or the question asks for the nearest penny)
- The question may ask "how much does each child get if they share equally?" when equal sharing gives a recurring decimal

### Trap 6: Division Disguised as Fractions
- "Find 1/8 of 456" is really 456 / 8 = 57
- Children who don't connect fractions with division may use an incorrect method

### Trap 7: Working Backwards Requires Multiplication
- "A number divided by 15 gives 24 remainder 6. What is the number?"
- Children must understand: number = divisor x quotient + remainder
- They might just do 15 x 24 = 360 and forget to add the remainder (360 + 6 = 366)

### Trap 8: Close Answer Options Force Precision
- GL regularly sets options like: 31, 32, 33, 34, 35
- One small calculation error pushes you to an adjacent wrong answer
- This is by design — it separates children who are procedurally accurate from those who make small errors

## Key Concepts Reference

### The Division Algorithm
```
Dividend = Divisor x Quotient + Remainder
```
Where the remainder must be less than the divisor (0 <= remainder < divisor).

### Short Division (Bus Stop Method)
- Used when dividing by a **1-digit number**
- Compact method: remainders carried mentally to the next column
- Year 5 curriculum expectation
- Example: 847 / 3 → 8/3 = 2 r2, 24/3 = 8, 7/3 = 2 r1 → Answer: 282 r1

### Long Division (Formal Written Method)
- Used when dividing by a **2-digit number**
- Steps: **Divide, Multiply, Subtract, Bring down** (repeat)
- Mnemonic: "Does McDonald's Sell Cheese Burgers?" (DMSCB) or "Dad, Mum, Sister, Brother" (DMSB)
- Year 6 curriculum expectation
- Pre-writing the first 9 multiples of the divisor saves time and reduces errors

### Checking Division
- **Inverse check:** Quotient x Divisor + Remainder = Dividend
- **Estimation check:** Round both numbers to get an approximate answer first
- **Digit sum check:** Use casting out nines as a quick error-detection method

### Divisibility Rules (Mental Shortcuts)
| Divisor | Rule |
|---------|------|
| 2 | Last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, 8) |
| 3 | Sum of digits divisible by 3 |
| 4 | Last two digits divisible by 4 |
| 5 | Last digit is 0 or 5 |
| 6 | Divisible by both 2 and 3 |
| 8 | Last three digits divisible by 8 |
| 9 | Sum of digits divisible by 9 |
| 10 | Last digit is 0 |
| 11 | Alternating sum of digits divisible by 11 |
| 25 | Last two digits are 00, 25, 50, or 75 |

### Equivalent Division Strategies
Children should know multiple approaches for speed:
- **Halving:** Dividing by 4 = halve twice; dividing by 8 = halve three times
- **Factor pairs:** 432 / 36 = 432 / 4 / 9 = 108 / 9 = 12
- **Scaling:** 3,600 / 12 = 360 / 1.2 = 300 (multiply both by 10: 36,000 / 120)
- **Known facts:** If you know 25 x 4 = 100, then 2,500 / 25 = 100
- **Compensation:** 396 / 4 → think of 400 / 4 = 100, minus 4/4 = 1, so 99

### Remainder Interpretation Guide
| Context | What to Do | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| "How many **needed**?" | Round UP | 85 / 4 = 21 r1 → 22 taxis |
| "How many **complete**?" | Round DOWN | 500 / 35 = 14 r10 → 14 biscuits |
| "How many **left over**?" | State remainder | 50 / 8 = 6 r2 → 2 eggs left |
| "How much **each**?" (money) | Express as decimal | £47 / 5 = £9.40 |
| "How long **each**?" (measurement) | Express as decimal or fraction | 47m / 5 = 9.4m or 9 2/5 m |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 Statutory Requirements (Division-Related)
1. Divide numbers up to 4 digits by a **one-digit number** using the formal written method of **short division** and interpret remainders appropriately for the context
2. Multiply and divide whole numbers and those involving decimals by **10, 100 and 1,000**
3. Identify **multiples and factors**, including finding all factor pairs of a number, and common factors of two numbers
4. Know and use the vocabulary of **prime numbers, prime factors and composite numbers** (related: testing primality involves trial division)
5. Solve problems involving multiplication and division, including using their knowledge of factors and multiples, squares and cubes

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements (Division-Related)
1. Divide numbers up to 4 digits by a **two-digit whole number** using the formal written method of **long division**, and interpret remainders as whole number remainders, fractions, or by rounding, as appropriate for the context
2. Divide numbers up to 4 digits by a two-digit number using the formal written method of **short division** where appropriate (i.e., when the divisor is small enough)
3. Perform **mental calculations**, including with mixed operations and large numbers
4. Identify **common factors, common multiples and prime numbers**
5. Use their knowledge of the order of operations to carry out calculations involving the four operations (**BODMAS**)
6. Solve problems involving addition, subtraction, multiplication and division
7. Use estimation to check answers to calculations and determine, in the context of a problem, an appropriate degree of accuracy

### Key Non-Statutory Guidance
- Pupils should practise long division with 2-digit divisors (13-99)
- Pupils should interpret remainders as: **whole number remainders**, **fractions** (e.g., 98 / 4 = 24 1/2), or **decimals** (e.g., 98 / 4 = 24.5), or by **rounding** (up or down depending on context)
- By end of Year 6, pupils should be **fluent** in written methods for all four operations
- Mental calculation strategies should be developed alongside written methods

### GL 11+ vs National Curriculum
The GL 11+ tests up to and including Year 6 content, even though many children sit the exam in September of Year 6 (before they've covered all Year 6 material in school). This means:
- **Short division** should be completely secure (Year 5 content — should be mastered)
- **Long division by 2-digit numbers** is the key skill being tested (Year 6 content — may not yet be taught in school)
- Children preparing for the 11+ must learn long division **ahead of schedule** if their school hasn't covered it yet
- GL expects children to handle remainders in all three forms: whole number, fraction, and by rounding

## Teaching Notes

### Priority Skills to Drill

**1. Times tables fluency (non-negotiable foundation)**
Long division is impossible without instant recall of multiplication facts to 12x12. Every hesitation on a times table fact adds seconds to a division question. Under exam time pressure, this compounds.

**2. Generating multiples of 2-digit numbers quickly**
The hardest part of long division by a 2-digit number is knowing multiples of that divisor. Children should practise writing out the first 9 multiples of numbers like 13, 17, 23, 28, 34, etc. before starting the division. This front-loaded effort prevents errors during the algorithm.

**3. The DMSB cycle until automatic**
Divide, Multiply, Subtract, Bring down — this cycle must become automatic. Children who hesitate on which step comes next make procedural errors. Use the mnemonic consistently.

**4. Zero in the quotient**
Explicitly teach what happens when the divisor doesn't go into the current number: you write a zero in the quotient and bring down the next digit. This is the single most common procedural error.

**5. Remainder interpretation (context reading)**
Practise the three types of remainder question until the child can instantly categorise: "Am I rounding up, rounding down, or stating the remainder?"

### Common Errors to Watch For

| Error | What Happens | How It Manifests |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Forgetting to bring down** | Child subtracts but doesn't bring down the next digit | Answer has fewer digits than it should |
| **Missing the zero** | Divisor doesn't go into partial dividend; child skips the zero | Answer is roughly 10x too small or has wrong digits |
| **Wrong multiple chosen** | During "Divide" step, picks wrong number of times | Answer off by 1-3 (caught by close distractors) |
| **Subtraction error** | Makes arithmetic mistake in the subtract step | Cascading error — all subsequent digits wrong |
| **Misaligned place values** | Digits written in wrong columns | Completely wrong answer |
| **Remainder > Divisor** | Doesn't realise the remainder must be less than the divisor | Should have divided one more time |
| **Forgetting the remainder** | Gets the quotient right but ignores the remainder | Wrong answer when context requires remainder interpretation |
| **Round up vs round down** | Applies wrong remainder interpretation | Off by 1 — and GL will have both options available |

### Exam Strategy Tips

1. **Estimate first:** Before starting formal division, round both numbers and estimate. If 4,576 / 23, think "4,600 / 23 ≈ 200". This immediately eliminates 2-3 answer options.

2. **Use the answer options:** Since it's multiple choice, try multiplying an answer option by the divisor. If one of the options times the divisor equals the dividend, that's the answer. This is often faster than doing formal long division.

3. **Check with inverse multiplication:** After dividing, multiply quotient by divisor (+ remainder) to verify it equals the dividend.

4. **Factor pairs for speed:** If dividing by 36, divide by 4 then by 9 (or by 6 then by 6). This turns one hard division into two easy ones.

5. **Know the "friendly" equivalents:** Dividing by 25 is the same as multiplying by 4 and dividing by 100. Dividing by 50 is the same as multiplying by 2 and dividing by 100. These shortcuts save significant time.

6. **Read the question twice:** Specifically for remainder questions — the difference between "how many needed?" and "how many complete?" is the difference between right and wrong.

7. **Write multiples first:** For any 2-digit divisor, write the first 9 multiples before starting the algorithm. The 20-30 seconds invested prevents multiplication errors during the division process.

### Cross-Topic Links
Division doesn't exist in isolation on the GL paper. Children should understand how it connects to:

- **Fractions:** 1/4 of 360 = 360 / 4 = 90. Finding a fraction of an amount IS division.
- **Decimals:** Dividing gives decimal results (e.g., 7 / 4 = 1.75). Converting fractions to decimals requires division.
- **Percentages:** Finding 1% means dividing by 100. Finding any percentage often starts with division.
- **Ratio:** Sharing in ratio requires dividing the total by the sum of ratio parts.
- **Averages:** Mean = sum / count. Every average question is a division question.
- **Speed/Distance/Time:** Speed = distance / time. All three formulae involve division.
- **Unit costs:** Price per item = total cost / number of items.
- **Scaling:** Enlarging/reducing by a scale factor involves division.

### What Makes GL Division Questions Hard (Beyond Difficulty Level)
1. **Time pressure** — not the calculation itself, but doing it accurately in under 60 seconds
2. **Identifying the operation** — the word "divide" often doesn't appear in the question
3. **Close answer options** — no room for "roughly right" answers
4. **Multi-step problems** — division is buried inside a larger problem
5. **Unfamiliar contexts** — children who only practise with "share sweets" struggle with rate/unit/average contexts
6. **Decimal dividends or answers** — more cognitive load than whole numbers
7. **Working backwards** — requires conceptual understanding, not just procedural fluency
