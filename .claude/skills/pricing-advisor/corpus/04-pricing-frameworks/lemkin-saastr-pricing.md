## Who / what

Jason Lemkin founded EchoSign (sold to Adobe for $400 million) before establishing SaaStr, the largest community for SaaS founders and executives globally, which he runs as a blog, podcast, annual conference, and fund. He writes prolifically and directly — sometimes abrasively — about everything a SaaS founder needs to know, with pricing as one of his most frequently revisited topics. His authority comes from having been an operator who made real pricing decisions under pressure, combined with a decade of watching hundreds of SaaStr portfolio and community companies do the same. His pricing views are practical, occasionally contrarian, and grounded in observed founder behaviour rather than academic theory.

## Core ideas (their key beliefs / frameworks)

- **In early days, use comparables — don't innovate on pricing**: "95 times out of 100, don't innovate on pricing. Especially in the early days. That's too clever by half." Find products delivering similar value to yours and price them similarly. Customers already have cognitive anchors from SaaS products they use; matching those anchors removes buying friction.
- **Offer both annual and monthly — the "false choice" principle**: the annual vs. monthly debate is a false choice. "It is almost always better to let the customer buy how they are most comfortable buying." Removing monthly options creates friction, not commitment; commitment comes from product stickiness, not billing structure.
  - Small businesses and individuals often prefer monthly even at a 20%+ premium.
  - Enterprise buyers prefer annual for accounting predictability.
  - Zoom had 26% monthly customers at $1B ARR; during COVID peak this hit 50%.
- **Double your pricing on your next big deal**: once you have a reference customer at a given price, quote double to the next comparable prospect. The logic: if a customer justified £X, the next comparable customer justifies 2× — and the exercise of doubling forces you to think about providing a genuine "solution to a big problem" rather than a tool. A £1,000/month tool is expensive; £12,000/year that eliminates the need to hire is "dirt cheap."
- **Raise prices on new customers, not existing ones**: "Raise prices on existing customers? Doesn't help if you are growing quickly. Just makes them unhappy. Raise prices on new customers? Forces you to deliver more value. Often makes everyone more happy."
- **Pricing power is a consequence of value, not strategy**: "Whether you raise prices or not, if you don't increase customer count, you'll quietly go into terminal decline." Pricing power follows product quality and market position; it cannot be engineered from the pricing page alone.
- **Price increases have become a standard SaaS growth lever at scale**: at mature SaaS companies, price increases of 10–15% per year have become normalised. Salesforce's model shows 72% of forward growth coming from price increases rather than new customer acquisition. For early-stage founders, the lesson is to build with pricing headroom — set an initial price that leaves room to raise.
- **Annual plan psychology**: when communicating annual discount, dollar amounts outperform percentages. "Save £30" is more motivating than "Save 17%."
- **Transparent pricing almost always leaves money on the table**: particularly in enterprise or semi-enterprise contexts, published pricing removes negotiation leverage. For B2C consumer apps, transparency may be appropriate; for B2B, Lemkin is sceptical.

## Method / how to apply

**Early-stage pricing (pre-PMF)**

1. Identify 2–3 directly comparable products (similar value, similar buyer, similar use case).
2. Note their published pricing and tier structure.
3. Price your product in the same range. Do not attempt to price lower to "win on price" — this trains buyers that you are cheap and makes future increases harder. Do not price materially higher without a clear, communicable differentiation.
4. Make both monthly and annual available from day one. Suggest annual with a modest discount (10–20%).
5. Set the initial price at a point that leaves room to increase it. Starting at £4.99/month and raising to £9.99 feels like a doubling; starting at £9.99 and raising to £12.99 feels like a modest annual adjustment.

**The double-pricing rule (for B2B / semi-B2B deals)**

- Close your current largest deal at your current price.
- That price becomes your new baseline.
- Quote double to the next comparable prospect, with full confidence.
- If they say yes, double again for the one after.
- If they push back, you learn what they value enough to negotiate around.

**Annual vs. monthly decision tree**

| Buyer type | Preference | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Individual / SMB | Monthly | Offer monthly at full price |
| Mid-market | Both | Default to annual, monthly available |
| Enterprise | Annual | Annual-only is defensible |

**Price increase cadence**

- Year 1: establish your initial price; do not change it.
- Year 2: make a modest increase to new customers only (10–15%).
- Year 3+: consider price increases to existing customers if NPS is strong and churn is low. Warn 60+ days in advance.

## Applicability to a UK 11+ app at pre-launch / early-launch stage

Lemkin's comparables rule is immediately applicable: a founder building an 11+ app should look at what Atom Learning, Explore Learning, and MyMaths charge for subscription access, use those as anchors, and price in a comparable range rather than inventing a novel model. His "double on the next deal" logic translates for B2C as: if early beta users happily paid £9.99/month, test £14.99 at public launch with confidence — you already have proof of value. His most important warning for a consumer app is structural: make both monthly and annual available from day one, because parents vary widely in how they budget for educational spend, and removing monthly removes the conversion path for a meaningful slice of your market.
