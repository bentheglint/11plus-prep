# GL Assessment Research: Place Value and Rounding

## Format

- **Paper structure:** 50 multiple-choice questions in 50 minutes (1 minute per question)
- **Options:** 5 choices (A-E) per question on a dedicated answer sheet
- **Topic weighting:** "Number" questions dominate the GL maths paper -- typically five times more number questions than any other topic category. Place value and rounding sit within this dominant "Number" strand alongside arithmetic, fractions, decimals, and percentages.
- **Estimated place value/rounding questions per paper:** 2-4 questions directly testing place value or rounding, plus additional questions where place value understanding is the underlying skill (e.g. ordering decimals, interpreting large numbers in word problems, estimation)
- **Difficulty progression:** Papers generally progress from easier questions at the start to harder questions towards the end. Place value questions can appear at any point depending on difficulty.
- **Non-calculator:** All GL 11+ maths papers are completed without a calculator

**Note:** These weightings are estimated from analysis of practice papers and tutor resources. GL does not publish exact category breakdowns. The skills overlap heavily -- a single question may test rounding AND place value understanding simultaneously.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**Place value:**
- Identifying the value of a digit in a 3-4 digit whole number (e.g. "What is the value of the 7 in 3,742?" Answer: 700)
- Reading and writing numbers up to 10,000 in words and figures
- Simple partitioning (e.g. "Which of these equals 2,345?" Options include 2,000 + 300 + 40 + 5)
- Basic Roman numerals (I, V, X, L, C -- numbers below 100)

**Rounding:**
- Rounding 2-3 digit numbers to the nearest 10 (e.g. "Round 63 to the nearest 10" -- Answer: 60)
- Rounding 3-4 digit numbers to the nearest 100 (e.g. "Round 845 to the nearest 100" -- Answer: 800)
- Clear-cut cases where the deciding digit is well above or below 5

**Ordering:**
- Ordering 3-4 whole numbers by size (all positive, no decimals)
- Comparing two numbers using < or >

**Characteristics:**
- Numbers are manageable (up to low thousands)
- Only one skill tested per question
- Distractors are clearly wrong to a child who understands the concept
- No context/word problem wrapping -- pure numerical questions

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**Place value:**
- Identifying digit values in 5-7 digit numbers (e.g. "What is the value of the 4 in 2,648,301?" Answer: 40,000)
- Identifying digit values in decimal numbers (e.g. "What is the digit in the hundredths place in 12.3876?" Answer: 8)
- Multiplying/dividing by 10, 100, 1,000 and stating the effect on digits
- Roman numerals involving subtraction principle (IV, IX, XL, XC, CD, CM -- numbers up to 1,000)

**Rounding:**
- Rounding larger numbers to the nearest 1,000 or 10,000 (e.g. "Round 34,562 to the nearest 1,000" -- Answer: 35,000)
- Rounding decimals to 1 decimal place (e.g. "Round 3.47 to 1 decimal place" -- Answer: 3.5)
- Cases where the deciding digit is exactly 5 (must round up)
- Rounding where trailing zeros matter (e.g. rounding 2.96 to 1 d.p. gives 3.0, not 3)

**Ordering:**
- Ordering a mix of decimals (e.g. 0.45, 0.405, 0.5, 0.54 -- ascending)
- Ordering negative numbers (e.g. -3, -7, 2, -1, 5 -- ascending)
- Ordering numbers that cross zero

**Characteristics:**
- Numbers are larger or include decimals
- Two skills may be combined (e.g. round then compare)
- Distractors exploit common misconceptions (e.g. thinking 0.45 > 0.5 because 45 > 5)
- Some questions embedded in light context (money, measurement)

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**Place value:**
- Identifying values in numbers up to 10,000,000 with decimal components
- "Which number is ten times larger than 0.035?" or "What is 4.7 divided by 100?"
- Multi-step questions: "The digit 3 in the number 5,321 is worth how many times more than the digit 3 in the number 7,038?"
- Reverse questions: "A number has 6 in the ten thousands place, 0 in the thousands place, and is between 60,000 and 61,000. Which could it be?"

**Rounding:**
- Rounding to 2 or 3 decimal places (e.g. "Round 1.7245 to 2 decimal places" -- Answer: 1.72)
- Rounding very large numbers to 100,000 or 1,000,000
- Contextual rounding where mathematical rounding rules do not apply (e.g. "347 children need buses. Each bus holds 50. How many buses are needed?" -- must round UP to 7, not down to 6.94)
- Estimation questions: "Which calculation gives the best estimate for 4,891 x 19?" (round to 5,000 x 20 = 100,000)
- "A number rounds to 4,500 to the nearest 100. What is the smallest/largest it could be?"

**Ordering:**
- Ordering a mix of fractions, decimals, and percentages by converting to common form
- Ordering negative decimals (e.g. -0.3, -0.12, -0.7, -0.09)
- "Which of these numbers is closest to 3.5?" with very close options

**Characteristics:**
- Multiple steps or skills combined in one question
- Context-heavy word problems requiring interpretation before calculation
- Distractors are plausible and exploit specific misconceptions
- May require working backwards from a rounded answer
- Time pressure becomes a significant factor

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Digit Value Identification
"What is the value of the 8 in 483,962?"
- Options: 8, 80, 800, 8,000, 80,000
- Tests: understanding that position determines value
- GL variation: may ask "which digit is in the ten thousands place" (reverse direction)

### Type 2: Direct Rounding
"What is 486,385 rounded to the nearest 10,000?"
- Options: 480,000, 486,000, 490,000, 496,000, 500,000
- Tests: identifying the correct rounding boundary and applying the rule
- GL variation: may give the rounded answer and ask what the original could have been

### Type 3: Ordering Numbers
"Put these numbers in order from smallest to largest: 0.35, 0.4, 0.305, 0.53"
- Options show different orderings
- Tests: decimal place value understanding (not treating decimals like whole numbers)

### Type 4: Multiplying/Dividing by Powers of 10
"What is 3.45 x 100?"
- Options: 0.0345, 3.45, 34.5, 345, 3450
- Tests: understanding digit movement when multiplying/dividing by 10, 100, 1,000

### Type 5: Estimation
"Estimate 3,892 x 21 by rounding each number to 1 significant figure"
- Options include the correct estimate (4,000 x 20 = 80,000) and traps
- Tests: ability to round strategically and compute the simplified calculation

### Type 6: Partitioning / Expanded Form
"Which of these is the same as 45,327?"
- Options: 40,000 + 5,000 + 300 + 20 + 7 (correct) alongside wrong partitions
- GL variation: "45,327 = 40,000 + ? + 300 + 20 + 7. What is the missing number?"

### Type 7: Roman Numerals
"What is XLIV in numbers?" or "Write 99 in Roman numerals"
- Tests: addition and subtraction rules for Roman numerals
- Key symbols: I(1), V(5), X(10), L(50), C(100), D(500), M(1,000)

### Type 8: Contextual Rounding
"A school needs 347 chairs. Chairs come in packs of 50. How many packs must they buy?"
- Tests: understanding when to round up regardless of the digit rule
- Also: "About how many..." estimation in real-world context

### Type 9: Negative Number Ordering
"Which of these temperatures is the coldest? -3C, -7C, 2C, -1C, 0C"
- Tests: understanding that -7 < -3 < -1 < 0 < 2
- GL variation: "The temperature was -4C. It rose by 7 degrees. What is it now?"

### Type 10: Comparison Statements
"Which statement is correct?"
- A: 0.45 > 0.5  B: 0.305 < 0.35  C: 0.6 = 0.06  D: 0.09 > 0.1  E: 0.4 < 0.04
- Tests: precise decimal comparison skills

## Distractor Design

GL Assessment designs distractors (wrong answer options) to catch specific misconceptions. Each wrong option represents a predictable error, not a random number.

### Place Value Distractors
| Misconception | Example | Distractor |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| Confusing digit with value | "Value of 6 in 364,285" | Offering 6 instead of 60,000 |
| Wrong column identified | "Value of 4 in 2,648,301" | Offering 400 (hundreds) instead of 40,000 (ten thousands) |
| Adjacent place confusion | "Digit in hundredths place of 3.4562" | Offering 4 (tenths) or 6 (thousandths) instead of 5 |
| Reversed decimal places | "What is 0.037 x 10?" | Offering 0.0037 (divided instead of multiplied) |

### Rounding Distractors
| Misconception | Example | Distractor |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| Rounding to wrong place | "Round 34,562 to nearest 1,000" | 34,600 (rounded to nearest 100 instead) |
| Rounding down at 5 | "Round 2,385 to nearest 10" | 2,380 (should be 2,390 -- the 5 rounds up) |
| Changing wrong digits | "Round 142,785 to nearest 10,000" | 142,000 or 143,000 (rounded to nearest 1,000) |
| Truncating instead of rounding | "Round 3.47 to 1 d.p." | 3.4 (truncated) instead of 3.5 (rounded) |
| Decimal confusion | "Round 2.96 to 1 d.p." | 2.9 (truncated) or 3 (missing the .0) instead of 3.0 |

### Ordering Distractors
| Misconception | Example | Distractor |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| Treating decimals as whole numbers | "Order: 0.9, 0.45, 0.6" | Placing 0.45 before 0.6 (because 45 > 6 as whole numbers) |
| Longer decimal = bigger | "Order: 0.5, 0.405, 0.51" | Placing 0.405 > 0.5 (more digits = bigger) |
| Negative number reversal | "Order: -3, -7, -1" | Ordering as -1, -3, -7 (treating negatives like positives) |
| Ignoring decimal point | "Order: 1.5, 1.25, 1.3" | Placing 1.25 as largest (25 > 5 > 3 as whole numbers) |

### Estimation Distractors
| Misconception | Example | Distractor |
|---------------|---------|------------|
| Exact answer offered | "Estimate 3,892 x 21" | 81,732 (exact answer -- not an estimate) |
| Over-rounding | "Estimate 4,891 x 19" | 5,000 x 10 = 50,000 (rounded too aggressively) |
| Under-rounding | "Estimate 3,892 x 21" | 3,900 x 21 (not rounded enough for quick estimation) |

## Common GL Traps

1. **The "5 rounds up" trap:** Questions deliberately place the digit 5 in the deciding position. Children who think 5 rounds down will lose the mark. Example: "Round 4,350 to the nearest 100" -- answer is 4,400, not 4,300.

2. **Decimal ordering trap:** Mixing decimals with different numbers of decimal places exploits the misconception that more digits after the point means a larger number. Example: 0.5 vs 0.45 -- children think 0.45 > 0.5 because "45 is bigger than 5".

3. **Adjacent place value trap:** Questions about the value of a digit where adjacent columns are offered as distractors. The child must count columns precisely, especially in 6-7 digit numbers or decimal numbers with leading zeros (e.g. 0.0264).

4. **Rounding to the wrong level:** The question asks to round to the nearest 1,000 but a distractor shows the number rounded to the nearest 100 or 10,000. Under time pressure, children misread "thousand" as "hundred".

5. **Contextual rounding override:** Real-world problems where you must round UP even though the digit rule says round down. Buses, packs, containers, tables, etc. "If 247 people need seats and each row has 30, how many rows?" -- answer is 9 (not 8.23 rounded to 8).

6. **Negative number magnitude trap:** Children assume -3 > -7 is wrong because "7 is bigger than 3". Temperature and debt contexts are used to test this. The number line visual helps -- further left = smaller.

7. **The trailing zero trap:** "Round 2.96 to 1 decimal place" gives 3.0, not 3. Both represent the same value but 3.0 explicitly shows it has been rounded to 1 decimal place. GL may offer 3 as a wrong option.

8. **Powers of 10 direction trap:** When multiplying by 10, digits move LEFT (number gets bigger). When dividing by 10, digits move RIGHT (number gets smaller). Children reverse this, especially with decimals. "0.35 x 100 = ?" -- answer is 35, not 0.0035.

9. **Roman numeral subtraction trap:** IV = 4 (not 6), IX = 9 (not 11), XL = 40 (not 60). Children who always add will get these wrong. Also: a numeral can be repeated up to 3 times (III = 3) but not 4 times (IIII is not valid -- it should be IV).

10. **"Closest to" questions:** "Which number is closest to 500?" with options like 489, 511, 498, 505, 492. Requires calculating the difference from 500 for each option -- not just gut feeling.

## Key Concepts Reference

### Place Value Grid (Whole Numbers)

| Millions | Hundred Thousands | Ten Thousands | Thousands | Hundreds | Tens | Ones |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|-----------|----------|------|------|
| 1,000,000 | 100,000 | 10,000 | 1,000 | 100 | 10 | 1 |

### Place Value Grid (Decimals)

| Ones | . | Tenths | Hundredths | Thousandths | Ten Thousandths |
|------|---|--------|------------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1 | . | 0.1 | 0.01 | 0.001 | 0.0001 |

### Rounding Rules
1. Identify the rounding digit (the place you are rounding TO)
2. Look at the digit immediately to the RIGHT of it
3. If that digit is 0, 1, 2, 3, or 4 -- the rounding digit stays the same (round down)
4. If that digit is 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9 -- the rounding digit goes up by 1 (round up)
5. Replace all digits to the right with zeros (for whole numbers) or remove them (for decimals)

**Special case:** When rounding up causes a "carry" (e.g. rounding 9,960 to the nearest 100 gives 10,000 not 9,900+100=10,000)

### Multiplying/Dividing by Powers of 10
- x10: each digit moves ONE place to the LEFT (3.45 becomes 34.5)
- x100: each digit moves TWO places to the LEFT (3.45 becomes 345)
- x1000: each digit moves THREE places to the LEFT (3.45 becomes 3,450)
- /10: each digit moves ONE place to the RIGHT (345 becomes 34.5)
- /100: each digit moves TWO places to the RIGHT (345 becomes 3.45)
- /1000: each digit moves THREE places to the RIGHT (345 becomes 0.345)

**Key principle:** The digits move, not the decimal point. The decimal point is always between the ones and tenths.

### Roman Numerals Rules
- **Seven symbols:** I(1), V(5), X(10), L(50), C(100), D(500), M(1,000)
- **Addition:** Smaller after larger = add (VI = 5+1 = 6)
- **Subtraction:** Smaller before larger = subtract (IV = 5-1 = 4)
- **Repetition:** A symbol can be repeated up to 3 times (III = 3, XXX = 30)
- **Subtractive pairs:** Only I, X, and C can be subtracted. I before V or X only. X before L or C only. C before D or M only.
- **Key conversions to memorise:** IV=4, IX=9, XL=40, XC=90, CD=400, CM=900

### Negative Numbers
- Numbers less than zero, written with a minus sign
- On a number line: further LEFT = smaller (so -7 < -3)
- Crossing zero: from -4, adding 7 gives +3 (count: -3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3)
- Common contexts in 11+: temperature, sea level (above/below), bank balance

| Term | Meaning | Example in Question |

| Descending order | Largest to smallest | "Arrange in descending order" |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 Statutory Requirements (Number and Place Value)
- Read, write, order and compare numbers to at least 1,000,000 and determine the value of each digit
- Count forwards or backwards in steps of powers of 10 for any given number up to 1,000,000
- Interpret negative numbers in context; count forwards and backwards with positive and negative whole numbers, including through zero
- Round any number up to 1,000,000 to the nearest 10, 100, 1,000, 10,000 and 100,000
- Solve number problems and practical problems that involve all of the above
- Read Roman numerals to 1,000 (M) and recognise years written in Roman numerals

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements (Number and Place Value)
- Read, write, order and compare numbers up to 10,000,000 and determine the value of each digit
- Round any whole number to a required degree of accuracy
- Use negative numbers in context, and calculate intervals across zero
- Solve number and practical problems that involve all of the above

### Year 6 Non-Statutory Guidance
- Pupils use the whole number system, including saying, reading and writing numbers accurately
- Revision of previously learned concepts; identifying and addressing gaps through varied problem-solving contexts

### 11+ Extension Beyond Curriculum
GL papers may push slightly beyond the standard Year 6 curriculum:
- Numbers up to 10,000,000 (in line with Y6 curriculum)
- Decimal place value to thousandths and occasionally ten-thousandths
- Rounding decimals to specified decimal places (not just whole numbers)
- Estimation requiring rounding to 1 significant figure
- Multi-step problems combining place value with other operations
- Contextual problems requiring reasoning about when to round up vs down

## Teaching Notes

### Top Misconceptions to Address
1. **"Longer decimals are bigger"** -- 0.45 is NOT bigger than 0.5. Teach children to add trailing zeros for comparison: 0.45 vs 0.50. Now it is clear 50 hundredths > 45 hundredths.

2. **"5 rounds down"** -- The rule is 5 or above rounds UP. Drill this with specific examples: 45 to nearest 10 is 50, 350 to nearest 100 is 400, 2,500 to nearest 1,000 is 3,000.

3. **Digit vs value confusion** -- Children say "the 6" when asked for value, instead of "6,000" or "600". Practice both directions: "What digit is in the hundreds place?" vs "What is the VALUE of the digit 6?"

4. **Rounding to the wrong degree** -- Under time pressure, children round to the nearest 100 when asked for the nearest 1,000. Teach them to underline the key word in the question before calculating.

5. **Negative number ordering** -- Use a number line drawn horizontally. Always show that moving LEFT makes numbers smaller. -7 is further left than -3, so -7 is smaller. Thermometer contexts work well for British children.

6. **Decimal place value** -- The first place after the decimal point is tenths, not "oneths". Each place is 10 times SMALLER than the one to its left, just as each whole number place is 10 times BIGGER.

7. **Roman numeral subtraction** -- Only I, X, and C can be used subtractively, and only before specific larger numerals. VL is NOT valid for 45 (it should be XLV). Teach the six subtractive combinations: IV, IX, XL, XC, CD, CM.

### Speed Strategies for Exam Conditions
- **Rounding shortcut:** Identify the rounding digit, look at the ONE digit to its right, decide up or down -- done. Do not look at any other digits.
- **Decimal comparison:** Add zeros to make all decimals the same length, then compare as whole numbers.
- **Powers of 10:** Count the zeros in 10/100/1,000 -- that is how many places the digits move. Direction: multiply = left, divide = right.
- **Estimation:** Round each number to 1 significant figure FIRST, then do the simple calculation.
- **Roman numerals:** Build from the largest values first. 1,994 = M(1000) + CM(900) + XC(90) + IV(4) = MCMXCIV.

### Links to Other Topics
Place value understanding is foundational for:
- **Decimals:** Reading and comparing decimal values
- **Fractions:** Understanding tenths, hundredths as fractions
- **Percentages:** Converting between forms (0.25 = 25%)
- **Long multiplication/division:** Column alignment depends on place value

- **Money:** Understanding pounds and pence (2 decimal places)
