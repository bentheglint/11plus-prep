## 1. Mental Maths Shortcuts

The GL Assessment maths paper gives 50 questions in 50 minutes — roughly one minute per
question. Mental shortcuts are not optional; they are essential for finishing the paper. The
goal is to replace slow written methods with fast, reliable mental techniques.

### 1.1 Multiplying by 5

**The trick:** Multiply by 10, then halve the result (or halve first, then multiply by 10).

This works because 5 = 10 ÷ 2.

**Worked examples:**

```
36 × 5
  → 36 × 10 = 360
  → 360 ÷ 2 = 180  ✓

5 × 54
  → 54 × 10 = 540
  → 540 ÷ 2 = 270  ✓

Even number shortcut (halve first):
5 × 48
  → 48 ÷ 2 = 24
  → 24 × 10 = 240  ✓
```

**Why halve first for even numbers?** If the number is even, halving gives a whole number,
then you just tag a zero. No division of odd numbers needed.

### 1.2 Multiplying by 25

**The trick:** Multiply by 100, then divide by 4 (because 25 = 100 ÷ 4). To divide by 4,
halve twice.

**Worked examples:**

```
25 × 36
  → 36 × 100 = 3,600
  → 3,600 ÷ 2 = 1,800
  → 1,800 ÷ 2 = 900  ✓

25 × 48
  → 48 × 100 = 4,800
  → 4,800 ÷ 2 = 2,400
  → 2,400 ÷ 2 = 1,200  ✓

Or halve first (even numbers):
25 × 64
  → 64 ÷ 4 = 16
  → 16 × 100 = 1,600  ✓
```

### 1.3 Multiplying by 11

**The trick (two-digit numbers):** Split the digits, add them together, and place the sum
in the middle.

**Worked examples:**

```
42 × 11
  → Split: 4 _ 2
  → Add: 4 + 2 = 6
  → Place in middle: 462  ✓

63 × 11
  → Split: 6 _ 3
  → Add: 6 + 3 = 9
  → Result: 693  ✓
```

**What if the middle digits add to more than 9?** Carry the 1 to the left digit.

```
48 × 11
  → Split: 4 _ 8
  → Add: 4 + 8 = 12
  → Place 2 in the middle, carry 1: (4+1) 2 8 = 528  ✓

77 × 11
  → Split: 7 _ 7
  → Add: 7 + 7 = 14
  → Place 4 in the middle, carry 1: (7+1) 4 7 = 847  ✓
```

### 1.4 Multiplying by 9

**The trick:** Multiply by 10 and subtract the original number (because 9 = 10 − 1).

**Worked examples:**

```
9 × 7
  → 10 × 7 = 70
  → 70 − 7 = 63  ✓

9 × 34
  → 10 × 34 = 340
  → 340 − 34 = 306  ✓

9 × 56
  → 10 × 56 = 560
  → 560 − 56 = 504  ✓
```

**Finger trick (single digits only):** Hold up 10 fingers. To find 9 × n, fold down the
nth finger. Count fingers before the fold (tens) and after the fold (ones).

```
9 × 4 → fold 4th finger → 3 fingers before, 6 after → 36  ✓
9 × 8 → fold 8th finger → 7 fingers before, 2 after → 72  ✓
```

### 1.5 Finding Percentages Quickly (Building Blocks Method)

Start from three building blocks and combine them to make any percentage:

| Building block | How to find it | Example (of 360) |
|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **10%** | Divide by 10 | 360 ÷ 10 = 36 |
| **5%** | Half of 10% | 36 ÷ 2 = 18 |
| **1%** | Divide by 100 | 360 ÷ 100 = 3.6 |

**Building any percentage from these blocks:**

```
Find 35% of 240:
  → 10% = 24
  → 30% = 24 × 3 = 72
  → 5% = 24 ÷ 2 = 12
  → 35% = 72 + 12 = 84  ✓

Find 17% of 500:
  → 10% = 50
  → 5% = 25
  → 1% = 5
  → 2% = 10
  → 17% = 50 + 25 + 10 = 85  ✓

Find 70% of 120:
  → 10% = 12
  → 70% = 12 × 7 = 84  ✓

Find 15% of 80:
  → 10% = 8
  → 5% = 4
  → 15% = 8 + 4 = 12  ✓
```

**Quick 50% shortcut:** 50% is always just halving the number.

### 1.7 Quick Divisibility Tests

These let you instantly check whether a number divides evenly — essential for simplifying
fractions, finding factors, and checking prime numbers.

| Divisor | Test | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| **2** | Last digit is even (0, 2, 4, 6, 8) | 574 → last digit 4 → divisible ✓ |
| **3** | Sum of digits divisible by 3 | 246 → 2+4+6 = 12 → 12÷3 = 4 ✓ |
| **4** | Last two digits divisible by 4 | 1,324 → 24 ÷ 4 = 6 ✓ |
| **5** | Last digit is 0 or 5 | 735 → last digit 5 ✓ |
| **6** | Divisible by both 2 AND 3 | 234 → even ✓ and 2+3+4=9 (÷3) ✓ |
| **8** | Last three digits divisible by 8 | 2,120 → 120 ÷ 8 = 15 ✓ |
| **9** | Sum of digits divisible by 9 | 729 → 7+2+9 = 18 → 18÷9 = 2 ✓ |
| **10** | Last digit is 0 | 450 → ends in 0 ✓ |

**Worked example (simplifying a fraction):**

```
Simplify 36/48:
  → Both divisible by 2? 36 (even) ✓, 48 (even) ✓ → 18/24
  → Both divisible by 2 again? ✓ → 9/12
  → Both divisible by 3? 9÷3=3, 12÷3=4 → 3/4  ✓
```

### 1.8 Squaring Numbers Ending in 5

**The trick:** Take the digit(s) before the 5, multiply by the next number up, then tag
"25" on the end.

**Worked examples:**

```
35²
  → Digit before 5: 3
  → 3 × 4 = 12
  → Tag 25: 1,225  ✓

75²
  → Digit before 5: 7
  → 7 × 8 = 56
  → Tag 25: 5,625  ✓

15²
  → 1 × 2 = 2
  → Tag 25: 225  ✓

95²
  → 9 × 10 = 90
  → Tag 25: 9,025  ✓
```

**Why it works:** If the number is (10a + 5), then (10a + 5)² = 100a(a+1) + 25. The
first part gives you a × (a+1) followed by two zeros, plus 25 fills those zeros.

### 1.9 Adding and Subtracting Near-Round Numbers

**The trick:** Round to the nearest convenient number, then adjust.

**Worked examples for addition:**

```
367 + 99
  → 367 + 100 = 467
  → Adjust: 467 − 1 = 466  ✓

245 + 198
  → 245 + 200 = 445
  → Adjust: 445 − 2 = 443  ✓

56 + 38
  → 56 + 40 = 96
  → Adjust: 96 − 2 = 94  ✓
```

**Worked examples for subtraction:**

```
543 − 99
  → 543 − 100 = 443
  → Adjust: 443 + 1 = 444  ✓
  (We subtracted 1 too many, so add 1 back)

782 − 297
  → 782 − 300 = 482
  → Adjust: 482 + 3 = 485  ✓

400 − 198
  → 400 − 200 = 200
  → Adjust: 200 + 2 = 202  ✓
```

**Key rule to remember:**
- Adding a number that's been rounded UP → subtract the difference
- Subtracting a number that's been rounded UP → add the difference

### 1.10 Doubling and Halving Strategies

**The trick:** When multiplying two numbers, you can halve one and double the other without
changing the answer. Keep going until the calculation is easy.

**Worked examples:**

```
25 × 16
  → Double 25 → 50, halve 16 → 8
  → 50 × 8 = 400  ✓

35 × 4
  → Double 35 → 70, halve 4 → 2
  → 70 × 2 = 140  ✓

15 × 24
  → Double 15 → 30, halve 24 → 12
  → 30 × 12 = 360  ✓

125 × 8
  → Double 125 → 250, halve 8 → 4
  → Double 250 → 500, halve 4 → 2
  → 500 × 2 = 1,000  ✓
```

**When does this work best?** When one factor is even (so it halves cleanly) and the other
is a "friendly" number when doubled (like 5→10, 25→50, 15→30).

**Repeated doubling for ×4, ×8:**

```
37 × 4 = 37 × 2 × 2
  → 37 × 2 = 74
  → 74 × 2 = 148  ✓

13 × 8 = 13 × 2 × 2 × 2
  → 13 × 2 = 26
  → 26 × 2 = 52
  → 52 × 2 = 104  ✓
```

### 1.11 Compensation Method for Subtraction

**The trick:** Adjust both numbers by the same amount to make the subtraction easier.
Subtracting the same value from both numbers does not change the difference.

**Worked examples:**

```
92 − 37
  → Add 3 to both: 95 − 40 = 55  ✓

83 − 28
  → Add 2 to both: 85 − 30 = 55  ✓

604 − 286
  → Add 14 to both: 618 − 300 = 318  ✓

1,000 − 673
  → Subtract 673 from 1,000 is awkward
  → Instead: "What do I add to 673 to reach 1,000?"
  → 673 + 7 = 680, + 20 = 700, + 300 = 1,000
  → Total added: 7 + 20 + 300 = 327  ✓
```

The last example uses "counting up" (also called the "shopkeeper's method"), which is
particularly effective when subtracting from round numbers.

## 2. Word Problem Strategies

Word problems are the most common source of marks lost on the GL paper — not because the
maths is harder, but because children misread what the question is asking.

### 2.1 The RUCSAC Method

A systematic six-step approach for tackling any word problem:

| Step | Letter | Action | What to do |
|------|--------|--------|------------|
| 1 | **R** | Read | Read the whole question carefully. Do NOT start calculating yet. |
| 2 | **U** | Understand | Underline key numbers and words. What is the question actually asking? |
| 3 | **C** | Choose | Decide which operation(s) to use (add, subtract, multiply, divide). |
| 4 | **S** | Solve | Do the calculation. Show your working. |
| 5 | **A** | Answer | Write the answer with correct units. Does it answer the actual question? |
| 6 | **C** | Check | Does the answer make sense? Use estimation or inverse operation. |

**Worked example:**

> A school orders 15 boxes of pencils. Each box contains 24 pencils. The pencils are shared
> equally among 6 classes. How many pencils does each class get?

```
R — Read: 15 boxes, 24 pencils per box, 6 classes, pencils per class?
U — Understand: I need total pencils, then divide by classes.
C — Choose: Multiply (total), then divide (share equally).
S — Solve: 15 × 24 = 360 pencils total. 360 ÷ 6 = 60.
A — Answer: Each class gets 60 pencils.
C — Check: 60 × 6 = 360. 360 ÷ 24 = 15 boxes. ✓
```

### 2.2 Operation Keywords

These words signal which operation to use. **Warning:** Keywords are a guide, not a
guaranteed rule — always think about what the problem is actually describing.

| Operation | Keywords |
|-----------|----------|
| **Addition (+)** | total, sum, altogether, combined, in all, more than, increased by, plus, and, together |
| **Subtraction (−)** | difference, less than, fewer, minus, decrease, reduce, left, remain, take away, change, lost, fell, dropped |
| **Multiplication (×)** | times, of, product, twice, double, triple, every, each (when grouping), per (sometimes), area, groups of |
| **Division (÷)** | share, split, each (when distributing), per (sometimes), divided, equally, average, half, quarter, ratio |

**Trap to watch for:** "How many more" looks like addition (the word "more") but it
actually means find the difference — it is subtraction.

```
"Tom has 45 marbles. Sara has 28 marbles. How many more does Tom have?"
  → 45 − 28 = 17 (NOT 45 + 28)  ✓
```

### 2.3 Bar Models

Bar models are visual diagrams that represent quantities as rectangular bars. They make
the structure of a problem visible before you calculate.

**Part-whole model (for addition/subtraction):**

```
Problem: Sam has 35 red and 47 blue marbles. How many altogether?

|←————— Total: ? —————→|
|——— 35 ———|——— 47 ———|

Answer: 35 + 47 = 82
```

**Comparison model (for difference problems):**

```
Problem: A book costs £12. A game costs £35. How much more is the game?

|——— £12 ———|
|——— £35 ———————————|
              |— ? —|

Answer: £35 − £12 = £23
```

**Equal parts model (for multiplication/division):**

```
Problem: 4 bags, each with 15 sweets. How many sweets altogether?

|—— 15 ——|—— 15 ——|—— 15 ——|—— 15 ——|
|←————————— Total: ? ————————————————→|

Answer: 4 × 15 = 60
```

**Fraction bar model:**

```
Problem: 3/5 of 45 children chose pizza. How many?

|—— 9 ——|—— 9 ——|—— 9 ——|—— 9 ——|—— 9 ——|
|←—— pizza (3 parts) ——→|←— rest ——→|
                          |←— 45 total ————→|

45 ÷ 5 = 9 per part. 3 parts = 27 children chose pizza.
```

### 2.4 Working Backwards from the Answer

When a problem describes a chain of events and gives the final result, start from the end
and reverse every operation.

**Key rule:** Addition becomes subtraction. Multiplication becomes division. And vice versa.

**Worked example:**

> I think of a number. I double it, then add 5. The answer is 31. What was my number?

```
Start from the end:
  31
  → Undo "add 5": 31 − 5 = 26
  → Undo "double": 26 ÷ 2 = 13

My number was 13.
Check: 13 × 2 = 26, 26 + 5 = 31  ✓
```

**Worked example (multi-step):**

> Sarah had some pens. She bought 34 more. She threw away 29. She now has 64.
> How many did she start with?

```
Start from the end:
  64 pens now
  → Undo "threw away 29": 64 + 29 = 93
  → Undo "bought 34": 93 − 34 = 59

She started with 59 pens.
Check: 59 + 34 = 93, 93 − 29 = 64  ✓
```

### 2.5 Estimation Before Calculation

Before solving any word problem, make a rough estimate. This catches major errors —
especially pressing the wrong button on a calculator or misreading a number.

**Technique:** Round each number to 1 significant figure, then calculate.

```
Problem: 389 × 21

Estimate: 400 × 20 = 8,000
Actual: 389 × 21 = 8,169

The actual answer is close to 8,000, so it's likely correct.
If you'd accidentally got 81,690, the estimate would flag it immediately.
```

## 3. Geometry and Spatial Reasoning

### 3.1 The "Angle Hunt" Method

When a geometry question asks for a missing angle, work through these rules in order until
you find what you need. Think of it as a treasure hunt — each rule reveals the next clue.

**The angle facts to apply (in order of usefulness):**

| Rule | Total | When to use |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Angles on a straight line | 180° | Two or more angles sitting on a straight line |
| Angles at a point | 360° | Angles that meet at a single point |
| Angles in a triangle | 180° | Any triangle — always |
| Angles in a quadrilateral | 360° | Any four-sided shape |
| Vertically opposite angles | Equal | Where two straight lines cross |
| Isosceles triangle | Two angles equal | When two sides are marked as equal |
| Equilateral triangle | All 60° | When all sides are marked as equal |

**Worked example:**

```
A triangle has angles of 65° and 50°. Find the third angle.

Step 1: Angles in a triangle = 180°
Step 2: 65° + 50° = 115°
Step 3: 180° − 115° = 65°

The third angle is 65°.
(Bonus: two angles are 65°, so this is an isosceles triangle.)
```

**Worked example (multi-step angle hunt):**

```
A straight line has an angle of 130° on one side. The other side
is split into two equal angles. Find each angle.

Step 1: Angles on a straight line = 180°
Step 2: Other side = 180° − 130° = 50°
Step 3: Two equal angles: 50° ÷ 2 = 25° each.
```

**The strategy:**
1. Write down EVERY angle fact you know from the diagram
2. Look for angles you CAN calculate (even if they're not the answer)
3. Use those calculated angles to find the one you need
4. Always check: do all your angles add up correctly?

### 3.2 Properties of Shapes — Systematic Checklist

When a question asks about shape properties, mentally run through this checklist:

| Property | What to check |
|----------|---------------|
| Sides | How many? Equal? Parallel? |
| Angles | Right angles? Equal angles? Obtuse/acute? |
| Symmetry | How many lines of symmetry? |
| Diagonals | Do they bisect? Are they equal? Do they cross at right angles? |
| Special types | Is it regular? Is it a special case (e.g. square is a special rectangle)? |

**Key shape facts for the 11+:**

| Shape | Sides | Angles | Lines of symmetry |
|-------|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Equilateral triangle | 3 equal | 3 × 60° | 3 |
| Isosceles triangle | 2 equal | 2 equal | 1 |
| Square | 4 equal, all parallel | 4 × 90° | 4 |
| Rectangle | 2 pairs equal, all parallel | 4 × 90° | 2 |
| Rhombus | 4 equal, 2 pairs parallel | Opposite pairs equal | 2 |
| Parallelogram | 2 pairs equal, 2 pairs parallel | Opposite pairs equal | 0 |
| Trapezium | 1 pair parallel | Varies | 0 (or 1 if isosceles) |
| Regular pentagon | 5 equal | 5 × 108° | 5 |
| Regular hexagon | 6 equal | 6 × 120° | 6 |

### 3.3 Visualising Nets and 3D Shapes

A net is a 2D pattern that folds into a 3D shape. The 11+ tests this in two ways: "Which
net folds into this shape?" and "What shape does this net make?"

**Cube nets:** There are exactly 11 different nets that fold into a cube. The key insight
is that a cube net always has exactly 6 squares, and no more than 4 squares can be in a
row.

**Strategy for net questions:**
1. **Identify the base** — pick one face as the base
2. **Mark opposite faces** — in a cube net, faces separated by exactly one square will be
   opposite when folded
3. **Check for overlaps** — fold mentally. If two faces would land on the same position,
   it is not a valid net
4. **Use identifying features** — if faces have patterns or numbers, track which direction
   they face relative to their neighbours

**3D visualisation tips:**
- For "view from above" questions: imagine yourself floating directly above the shape
- For "view from the side" questions: imagine squashing the shape flat from that direction
- Count blocks systematically: go row by row, column by column
- Watch for hidden blocks that are obscured by blocks in front

### 3.4 Mirror Lines and Symmetry

**How to check a reflection:**
1. Each point must be the **same distance** from the mirror line on both sides
2. Use the "count squares" method: count squares from the original point to the mirror
   line, then count the same number on the other side
3. The reflected shape must be the **same size** — just flipped
4. The shape should NOT be rotated, only reflected

**Checking for lines of symmetry:**
- Mentally fold the shape along the proposed line
- Both halves must match exactly
- Common trap: children assume all regular shapes have a vertical line of symmetry, but
  forget about diagonal and horizontal lines

**Mirror line worked example:**

```
Reflect point A, which is 3 squares to the left of a vertical mirror line.

→ Count 3 squares to the RIGHT of the mirror line
→ Place the reflected point there
→ Repeat for every vertex of the shape
→ Join the reflected vertices in the same order
```

## 4. Data Handling Strategies

### 4.1 Scale Reading Technique (The 3-Step Check)

Before reading ANY value from a graph or chart:

```
Step 1: Read the axis TITLE — what is being measured?
Step 2: Read the SCALE — what does each small division represent?
Step 3: THEN read the value.
```

**Common trap:** The scale might not go up in ones. It could be 2s, 5s, 10s, 20s, or
even 25s. Children who skip Step 2 will misread every value.

**Worked example:**

```
A bar chart y-axis shows: 0, 20, 40, 60, 80, 100
There are 4 small divisions between each labelled line.

Step 2 — Each small division = 20 ÷ 4 = 5
Step 3 — A bar reaching 3 small divisions above 60 = 60 + 15 = 75
```

### 4.2 Pie Chart Estimation

Pie charts on the 11+ rarely require exact calculation. Instead:

**Benchmark fractions to recognise at a glance:**

| Fraction of circle | Angle | What it looks like |
|--------------------|---------|--------------------|
| 1/2 | 180° | Exactly half the circle |
| 1/4 | 90° | A right angle — like a clock at 3:00 |
| 3/4 | 270° | Three-quarters — like a clock at 9:00 |
| 1/3 | 120° | Slightly more than a quarter |
| 1/8 | 45° | Half of a quarter |
| 1/10 | 36° | A thin slice |

**Strategy:**
1. Start with the biggest slice — estimate what fraction it is
2. Compare other slices to it and to known benchmarks
3. If the total is given (e.g. "120 people were surveyed"), multiply the fraction by
   the total

**Worked example:**

```
A pie chart shows 4 sections. The total is 200 people.
Section A is clearly half the circle → 200 × 1/2 = 100 people
Section B is about a quarter → 200 × 1/4 = 50 people
Section C is slightly bigger than section D
If C + D = 200 − 100 − 50 = 50, then C ≈ 30, D ≈ 20
```

### 4.3 Mean, Median, Mode — Quick Identification

| Measure | Definition | Quick method |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| **Mean** | Total ÷ number of values | Add everything up, divide by how many |
| **Median** | Middle value when in order | Put in order, cross off from both ends |
| **Mode** | Most frequent value | Look for repeats |
| **Range** | Biggest − smallest | Not an average — it measures spread |

**Finding the median efficiently:**

```
Data: 3, 7, 2, 9, 5, 1, 8

Step 1 — Order: 1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 8, 9
Step 2 — Cross off from both ends:
  ✗1, 2, 3, [5], 7, 8, ✗9
  ✗1, ✗2, 3, [5], 7, ✗8, ✗9
  ✗1, ✗2, ✗3, [5], ✗7, ✗8, ✗9

Median = 5
```

**Even number of values:** Take the mean of the two middle numbers.

```
Data (ordered): 3, 5, 8, 12

Middle two: 5 and 8
Median = (5 + 8) ÷ 2 = 6.5
```

### 4.4 "Backwards Mean" Problems

These give you the mean and ask you to find a missing value. They are a favourite 11+
question type.

**The method:**

```
Step 1: Mean × number of values = TOTAL
Step 2: Add up the values you know
Step 3: Missing value = Total − sum of known values
```

**Worked example:**

> The mean of 5 numbers is 8. Four of the numbers are 6, 10, 3, and 12.
> Find the fifth number.

```
Step 1: Mean × count = Total → 8 × 5 = 40
Step 2: Sum of known values = 6 + 10 + 3 + 12 = 31
Step 3: Missing value = 40 − 31 = 9

The fifth number is 9.
Check: (6 + 10 + 3 + 12 + 9) ÷ 5 = 40 ÷ 5 = 8  ✓
```

**Worked example (harder):**

> After a sixth score is added, the mean increases from 8 to 9.
> What was the sixth score?

```
Original total: 8 × 5 = 40
New total: 9 × 6 = 54
Sixth score: 54 − 40 = 14
```

## 5. Algebra for Year 6

Algebra in the 11+ is not GCSE-level equation solving. It is about finding unknown
values using logical thinking, often with visual support.

### 5.1 Function Machines as a Gateway

A function machine takes an input, applies operations, and produces an output. This is
the simplest form of algebra — it builds the idea that operations can be chained and
reversed.

**Forward (finding the output):**

```
Input: 7
Operations: × 3 → + 4
Output: (7 × 3) + 4 = 21 + 4 = 25
```

**Backward (finding the input):**

```
Output: 19
Operations: × 2 → + 5
Reverse: 19 → undo "+ 5" → 19 − 5 = 14 → undo "× 2" → 14 ÷ 2 = 7
Input: 7

Check: 7 × 2 + 5 = 19  ✓
```

**Key idea:** Working backwards through a function machine uses inverse operations — the
same skill needed for solving equations.

### 5.2 Bar Model Method for Equations

Bar models make abstract equations visual. The unknown is represented by a bar with a
question mark.

**Worked example: x + 8 = 23**

```
|——— x ———|— 8 —|
|←——— 23 ————————→|

x = 23 − 8 = 15
```

**Worked example: 3x + 5 = 26**

```
|— x —|— x —|— x —|— 5 —|
|←————— 26 ——————————————→|

3x = 26 − 5 = 21
x = 21 ÷ 3 = 7
```

**Worked example: Two unknowns**

> Amy and Ben have 40 marbles together. Amy has 6 more than Ben.

```
Ben:  |————— b —————|
Amy:  |————— b —————|— 6 —|
Total: ←——— 40 ———————————→

2b + 6 = 40
2b = 34
b = 17 (Ben), Amy = 23

Check: 17 + 23 = 40 ✓ and 23 − 17 = 6 ✓
```

### 5.3 Substitution — "Replace and Calculate"

Substitution means replacing a letter with a number, then working out the result. Frame
it simply: "Wherever you see the letter, write the number instead."

**Worked examples:**

```
If a = 4 and b = 3, find the value of:

2a + b = 2(4) + 3 = 8 + 3 = 11  ✓
a² − b = 4² − 3 = 16 − 3 = 13  ✓
3(a + b) = 3(4 + 3) = 3 × 7 = 21  ✓
```

**Common trap:** 2a means 2 × a, NOT 2 followed by a. Children sometimes write 24
instead of 8 when a = 4.

### 5.4 Trial and Improvement

When you cannot easily work out the answer, make a sensible guess, check it, then adjust.

**Worked example:**

> Find a number where n × (n + 1) = 72

```
Try n = 7:  7 × 8 = 56  → too small
Try n = 9:  9 × 10 = 90  → too big
Try n = 8:  8 × 9 = 72  → perfect ✓
```

**Strategy:**
1. Start with an estimate that seems roughly right
2. Check: is it too big or too small?
3. Adjust in the right direction
4. Narrow down until you find the answer
5. Always verify by substituting back

## 6. Checking Strategies

On a 50-question paper, even small errors accumulate. These strategies catch mistakes
without taking much time.

### 6.1 Estimation to Catch Major Errors

**Round to 1 significant figure** and do a rough calculation in your head.

```
Question: 48 × 32
Your answer: 1,536

Quick estimate: 50 × 30 = 1,500
1,536 is close to 1,500 → probably correct ✓

If you'd accidentally written 15,360, the estimate would catch it.
```

**For division:**

```
Question: 756 ÷ 12
Your answer: 63

Quick estimate: 800 ÷ 10 = 80. A bit high because we rounded up the
dividend and down the divisor, so 63 is reasonable ✓
```

### 6.2 Inverse Operations

Use the opposite operation to check your answer:

| If you did... | Check with... |
|---------------|---------------|
| Addition | Subtraction |
| Subtraction | Addition |
| Multiplication | Division |
| Division | Multiplication |

```
Question: 347 + 285 = 632
Check: 632 − 285 = 347  ✓

Question: 840 ÷ 24 = 35
Check: 35 × 24 = 840  ✓
```

### 6.3 Units Check

Always check that your answer has the right units and that the units make sense.

**Common unit errors on the 11+:**
- Calculating area but writing "cm" instead of "cm²"
- Calculating perimeter but writing "cm²" instead of "cm"
- Mixing metres and centimetres in the same calculation
- Forgetting to convert units when the question uses different ones

```
Question: A rectangle is 2 m by 50 cm. Find the area.

Common error: 2 × 50 = 100 cm²  ✗  (mixed units!)
Correct: 200 cm × 50 cm = 10,000 cm²  ✓
Or: 2 m × 0.5 m = 1 m²  ✓
```

### 6.4 "Does the Answer Make Sense?" Check

After calculating, pause for one second and ask: is this answer reasonable?

**Red flags that signal an error:**
- A child's age that comes out as 150 years
- A price that is negative
- A percentage over 100% (when it should not be)
- A fraction answer when the question asks "how many people"
- An angle in a triangle greater than 180°
- A distance in kilometres when you are measuring a pencil

```
Question: "A train travels 240 miles in 4 hours. What is its average speed?"
Your answer: 960 mph

Sense check: 960 mph is faster than a commercial aeroplane.
A train doing 60 mph makes much more sense.
Correct: 240 ÷ 4 = 60 mph  ✓
```

## 7. Number Sense Development

Number sense is the deep, intuitive understanding of how numbers work. It is what allows
a child to "feel" that an answer is wrong before they can explain why. All the shortcuts
above rely on number sense — without it, they are just memorised tricks that break down
under pressure.

### 7.1 Subitising and Rapid Estimation

**Subitising** (from Latin: "suddenly") is the ability to instantly see how many items are
in a group without counting. For small quantities (1–4), this is innate. For larger
quantities (5–10), it develops with practice.

**Why it matters for the 11+:** Children who subitise well make faster, more accurate
estimates. They "see" that 48 is close to 50, that 7 × 8 is "about 56" rather than
needing to recalculate from scratch.

**How to build it:**
- Quick-flash exercises: show a dot pattern for 1 second, then ask "how many?"
- Dice and domino games (recognising patterns without counting)
- Estimation jars: guess how many objects, then count to check

### 7.2 Place Value — Deep Understanding

Place value is the foundation of all written and mental arithmetic. A child who truly
understands place value knows that in 4,725:
- The 4 is worth 4,000 (not "just 4")
- The 7 is worth 700
- Moving a digit one place left makes it 10× bigger
- Moving a digit one place right makes it 10× smaller

**Key skill for the 11+:** Multiplying and dividing by 10, 100, 1,000. This is NOT
"adding zeros" — it is shifting every digit to the left or right.

```
3.45 × 10 = 34.5  (every digit moves one place LEFT)
3.45 × 100 = 345   (every digit moves two places LEFT)
3.45 ÷ 10 = 0.345  (every digit moves one place RIGHT)
```

**Why "add a zero" is dangerous:** 3.45 × 10 ≠ 3.450. The "add a zero" shortcut only
works for whole numbers, and even then it masks the real understanding.

### 7.3 Proportional Reasoning

Proportional reasoning is the ability to see and use relationships between quantities.
It underpins ratio, percentages, fractions, scaling, and many word problems.

**Building blocks:**
- If 1 costs 30p, then 5 cost 150p (scaling up)
- If 4 cost £2, then 1 costs 50p (finding the unit)
- If a recipe for 4 people uses 200g of flour, a recipe for 6 people uses... (scaling)

**The unitary method (find the value of 1, then multiply):**

```
Problem: 8 pencils cost £2.40. How much do 5 pencils cost?

Step 1: Find the cost of 1 pencil: £2.40 ÷ 8 = £0.30
Step 2: Find the cost of 5: £0.30 × 5 = £1.50  ✓
```

**Scaling method (find a useful multiplier):**

```
Problem: A recipe for 4 people uses 300g of flour.
How much flour for 10 people?

4 people → 300g
2 people → 150g (halve)
10 people → 5 × 150g = 750g  ✓

(Or: 10 ÷ 4 = 2.5, so 300g × 2.5 = 750g)
```

### 7.4 Pattern Recognition

Sequences and pattern questions appear on every GL paper. The skill is spotting the rule.

**Strategy — always look for these in order:**
1. **Constant difference:** Goes up or down by the same amount each time
   (e.g. 3, 7, 11, 15 → +4 each time)
2. **Constant ratio:** Each term is multiplied by the same number
   (e.g. 2, 6, 18, 54 → ×3 each time)
3. **Two-step rule:** Multiply then add/subtract
   (e.g. 1, 3, 7, 15 → ×2 then +1)
4. **Alternating pattern:** The rule alternates
   (e.g. 2, 6, 4, 12, 10 → alternating ×3 and −2)
5. **Square/cube numbers:** Check if terms match n², n³, or triangular numbers

**Worked example:**

```
Find the next term: 2, 5, 11, 23, ___

Differences: +3, +6, +12 → the differences are doubling
Next difference: 24
Next term: 23 + 24 = 47  ✓
```

## 8. Common Misconceptions and How to Address Them

These are the mistakes that 9–11 year olds make most frequently. Each one is a potential
trap on the GL paper — and also a potential multiple-choice distractor designed to catch
children who hold the misconception.

### 8.1 "Multiplication Always Makes Bigger"

**The misconception:** Children learn multiplication with whole numbers and develop the
belief that multiplying always produces a larger answer.

**When it breaks down:** Multiplying by a number less than 1 (fractions or decimals).

```
0.5 × 8 = 4     (smaller than 8)
1/3 × 12 = 4    (smaller than 12)
0.2 × 0.3 = 0.06  (smaller than both numbers!)
```

**How to correct it:** Reframe multiplication. "Multiplication means 'groups of'. Half a
group of 8 is 4. That IS multiplication — it just doesn't make bigger."

### 8.2 "Division Always Makes Smaller"

**The misconception:** Because sharing between more people gives less each, children assume
division always reduces the number.

**When it breaks down:** Dividing by a number less than 1.

```
6 ÷ 0.5 = 12    (bigger than 6!)
8 ÷ 1/4 = 32    (much bigger than 8)
```

**How to think about it:** "6 ÷ 0.5 means: how many halves fit into 6?" There are 12
halves in 6. The answer being bigger than 6 makes complete sense.

### 8.3 "0.45 is Bigger Than 0.9 Because 45 > 9"

**The misconception:** Children apply whole-number thinking to decimals. They see 45 and 9
and use their whole-number schema: 45 is bigger.

**How to correct it:**
1. Write them with the same number of decimal places: 0.45 vs 0.90
2. Now it is clear: 90 hundredths > 45 hundredths
3. Or use money: £0.45 (45p) vs £0.90 (90p) — which would you rather have?

**Related traps:**
- 0.10 vs 0.2 → children think 0.10 is bigger (10 > 2)
- 0.125 vs 0.3 → children think 0.125 is bigger (125 > 3)

**Fix:** Always equalise the number of decimal places before comparing.

### 8.4 Confusion Between Area and Perimeter

**The misconception:** Children mix up "distance around the outside" (perimeter) with
"space inside" (area). They may use the area formula when asked for perimeter, or
vice versa.

**Why it happens:** Both concepts involve measuring shapes, both use similar-looking
formulas, and they are often taught in the same lesson.

**How to separate them:**
- **Perimeter** = fence around a garden (you walk AROUND the edge)
- **Area** = carpet to cover the floor (you cover the INSIDE)
- Perimeter is measured in cm, m (one-dimensional units)
- Area is measured in cm², m² (two-dimensional units)

**Another trap:** Children assume that if the area increases, the perimeter must also
increase. This is false.

```
Rectangle A: 4 cm × 4 cm → Area = 16 cm², Perimeter = 16 cm
Rectangle B: 2 cm × 8 cm → Area = 16 cm², Perimeter = 20 cm

Same area, different perimeters!
```

### 8.5 Adding Fractions by Adding Numerators and Denominators

**The misconception:** When adding 1/3 + 1/4, children write 2/7 (adding tops and
bottoms separately).

**Why it is wrong:** The denominator tells you the SIZE of the pieces. You cannot add
pieces of different sizes without making them the same size first. It is like adding
3 apples + 2 oranges and saying "5 appanges."

**The correct method:**

```
1/3 + 1/4

Step 1: Find a common denominator: 12 (because 3 × 4 = 12)
Step 2: Convert both fractions:
  1/3 = 4/12 (multiply top and bottom by 4)
  1/4 = 3/12 (multiply top and bottom by 3)
Step 3: Add the numerators: 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12

Answer: 7/12  ✓  (NOT 2/7)
```

**Use language to reinforce:** "3 twelfths + 4 twelfths = 7 twelfths" — when the
denominator is a word (like "twelfths"), children naturally keep it the same and only
add the numbers.

### 8.6 "Multiplying by 10 Means Add a Zero"

**The misconception:** Children are taught "just add a zero" for multiplication by 10.
This works for whole numbers but fails with decimals.

```
4.5 × 10 = 4.50  ✗  (adding a zero — WRONG)
4.5 × 10 = 45    ✓  (shifting digits — CORRECT)
```

**How to correct it:** Teach multiplication by 10 as "every digit moves one place to
the left" on a place value chart. This works for ALL numbers, including decimals.

### 8.7 Fraction Size — Bigger Denominator Does NOT Mean Bigger Fraction

**The misconception:** 1/8 is bigger than 1/3 because 8 > 3.

**How to correct it:** The denominator tells you how many pieces the whole is cut into.
More pieces means SMALLER pieces.

```
Think of a pizza:
- Cut into 3 slices: each slice is 1/3 of the pizza (big slices)
- Cut into 8 slices: each slice is 1/8 of the pizza (small slices)

1/3 > 1/8 because fewer cuts = bigger pieces
```

**Visual check:** Draw two identical bars. Divide one into 3 parts, the other into 8
parts. One piece of the 3-part bar is clearly larger.

### 8.8 The Equals Sign Means "The Answer Is..."

**The misconception:** Children think = means "write the answer here" rather than "both
sides are the same."

**Where it causes problems:**

```
3 + 4 = □ + 2

Children write 7 in the box (because 3 + 4 = 7).
But the correct answer is 5 (because 7 = 5 + 2).
```

**How to correct it:** Read the equals sign as "is the same as." Practise "balance"
problems where the answer is not at the end of the expression.

## Quick Reference: GL Assessment 11+ Exam Strategy

### Before the Exam
- Know your times tables to 12 × 12 instantly (no hesitation)
- Memorise the fraction-decimal-percentage conversion table
- Practise the building blocks percentage method daily
- Be confident with all divisibility tests

### During the Exam
1. **Read the question twice** — especially word problems
2. **Estimate first** — a rough answer protects you from big errors
3. **Use shortcuts** — do not do long multiplication for 36 × 5 when you can halve and ×10
4. **Watch the units** — cm vs m, cm vs cm², £ vs p
5. **Eliminate wrong answers** — if you can rule out 2–3 options, guess from the rest
6. **Do not get stuck** — if a question is taking too long, mark it and come back
7. **Check with inverse operations** — if you have time, verify key answers

### Time Management
- 50 questions in 50 minutes = 1 minute per question
- Easy questions: aim for 30 seconds, bank the spare time
- Hard questions: spend up to 90 seconds, then move on
- Keep 3–5 minutes at the end for checking
