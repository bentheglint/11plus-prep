## What this covers

Jason Lemkin, founder of SaaStr and one of the most widely read SaaS commentators, has written extensively on the question that every subscription business eventually faces: how and when to raise prices without destroying customer trust. His position is nuanced but clear — raise on new customers first, protect existing customers with grace, and never raise for the wrong reasons. This file documents his framework and its application to consumer edtech pricing.

## The pattern / mechanic

**The primary rule: raise on new customers, not existing ones.**

Lemkin's core position is that raising prices on existing customers is almost never worth the relationship damage. The revenue uplift from a 10–20% increase on your existing base is real but smaller than it looks, because your base is fixed by definition. New customers generate compounding revenue; alienating existing ones destroys referrals and NPS, both of which are the primary growth channels for consumer subscription products.

He frames this explicitly in his SaaStr writing: "Don't raise prices just because growth has slowed and you are out of ideas." Price increases punish your most loyal supporters at the moment when you need them most.

**If you must raise on existing customers:**

When a price increase on the existing base is unavoidable (e.g., a fundamental repricing event, a major value addition that needs to be reflected across the board), Lemkin's framework is:

1. **Give a 6-month grace period at the old price.** This is the minimum. Treat it as a thank-you for their early faith, not as a commercial concession.
2. **Never raise by more than 10% without clear, explicit value communication.** If you are raising 10%+ you need to show specifically what you have added. Vague appeals to "continued investment" do not count.
3. **Make the communication personal and transparent.** The increase should never be discovered on an invoice — it should be explained, with context, in advance.

His exact framing from SaaStr:

> "Give them an extra 6 months or more, on the old pricing."

> "Treat early customers as you'd want to be treated — reward their loyalty and faith in your product rather than extracting maximum short-term revenue from them."

**Grandfathering as a long-term asset:**

Grandfathering is not just a nice gesture — it is a referral machine. Parents who feel they have been treated fairly become the most vocal advocates. In a market driven by word-of-mouth (which the 11+ prep market demonstrably is), this compounds. A grandfathered customer who tells five other parents about the platform generates more value than the delta between old and new pricing.

Conversely, a customer who feels they have been surprised by an increase becomes a detractor. Lemkin cites a pattern he calls "taking advantage of customers" — any price increase that prioritises extraction over relationship — and warns that customers remember it: "Do it again, they'll never forget."

**The 'earn it first' principle:**

In his 2023 essay "The Year of Adding Value Again," Lemkin explicitly reframed the price-increase question. Rather than asking "how much can I charge?", the right question is "what have I shipped that justifies more?" He wrote: "If your customers love and trust you, they buy more from you — more products, more seats, bigger editions." Price increases should follow value delivery, not precede it.

**The 10% threshold:**

Below 10%, Lemkin treats increases as mostly not worth the operational overhead on a small base. At low ARR, the total uplift from a 5% increase on existing customers is negligible compared to acquiring two new customers. The exception is a multi-year grandfathered base that has never had a price touch — in that case, a 10% increase with a grace period is reasonable.

## Applicability to UK 11+ app

For an early-stage 11+ app, Lemkin's framework implies: early subscribers (beta, launch cohort) should be protected with a grandfathered rate indefinitely or for at least 12 months. When a price increase is introduced for new customers, announce it to existing customers with celebration framing — "because we've shipped X, Y, and Z, we're raising prices for new customers, but you're locked in." This turns a pricing event into a loyalty signal. In the 11+ parent community, where Mumsnet and Facebook groups amplify both positive and negative experiences, the reputational value of being seen as fair-dealing is worth far more than the margin recovered from a 10% increase on a small base.
