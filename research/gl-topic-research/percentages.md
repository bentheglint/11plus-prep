# GL Assessment Research: Percentages

## Format

- **Paper:** Maths (50 questions, 50 minutes)
- **Question type:** Multiple choice (5 options: A-E in our app)
- **Category:** Number (the dominant category in GL papers -- approximately 5x more Number questions than any other topic area)
- **Curriculum basis:** Key Stage 2 National Curriculum, primarily Year 5-6 content, with some questions stretching into early Year 6 material that may not yet have been taught in school
- **Presentation:** Mix of bare-number calculations and word problems set in real-world contexts (money, measurement, data, everyday scenarios)
- **Difficulty progression:** GL papers typically start with easier questions and increase in difficulty. Not all percentage questions cluster together -- they may appear throughout the Number section
- **No calculator:** All calculations must be done mentally or with jottings

Percentages sit within the broader "Fractions, Decimals and Percentages" (FDP) cluster in GL Assessment papers. Questions frequently require children to move between all three representations. A child who only knows percentage procedures but cannot convert fluently between fractions, decimals, and percentages will struggle.

---

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

| Skill Category | Approx. GL Weight | Description |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Finding a percentage of an amount | ~25-30% | "What is 35% of 500?" or word problems requiring this |
| FDP conversion (fraction/decimal/percentage) | ~20-25% | "Write 3/5 as a percentage" or "What is 0.35 as a percentage?" |
| Expressing one quantity as a percentage of another | ~15-20% | "What percentage of 300 is 15?" or "7 out of 28 failed -- what percentage?" |
| Percentage increase and decrease | ~10-15% | "A price of £42,400 increases by 3.4%. What is the new price?" |
| Percentage comparison and reasoning | ~10-15% | "Which is larger: 30% of 60 or 25% of 80?" or pie chart interpretation |
| Reverse percentage (working backwards) | ~5-10% | "After a 20% discount, the price is £48. What was the original price?" |
| Percentage of a percentage / multi-step | ~5% | "What is 20% of 20% of 500?" |

### Key emphasis
GL heavily favours **application over procedure**. Pure "calculate 25% of 80" questions appear at D1 but the majority of questions wrap percentage skills inside word problems that require:
1. Reading comprehension (extracting the right numbers)
2. Deciding which operation to use
3. Performing the calculation
4. Checking the answer makes sense in context

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**What it looks like:**
- Straightforward "find X% of Y" with friendly numbers
- Common benchmark percentages: 50%, 25%, 10%, 75%, 20%
- Amounts that divide cleanly (no remainders or awkward decimals)
- Single-step problems
- Direct FDP conversions with common equivalences

**Example question types:**
- "What is 25% of 80?" (Answer: 20)
- "What is 50% of 340?" (Answer: 170)
- "Write 1/4 as a percentage." (Answer: 25%)
- "10% of the 60 children chose pizza. How many chose pizza?" (Answer: 6)

**Distractor patterns at D1:**
- Decimal point errors (e.g., 2.0 instead of 20, or 200 instead of 20)
- Confusing 25% with dividing by 25 instead of by 4
- Picking the percentage itself rather than the calculated amount

**What makes it D1:** A child who knows that 10% = divide by 10, 50% = halve, 25% = divide by 4 will get these right quickly. The numbers are deliberately "clean."

---

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**What it looks like:**
- Less common percentages: 15%, 35%, 5%, 60%, 12.5%
- Word problems requiring two steps (find the percentage, then add/subtract)
- "Express as a percentage" questions where the fraction doesn't simplify to an obvious denominator of 100
- Amounts that produce decimal answers (e.g., 40% of 18 = 7.2)
- Percentage increase/decrease in context (sale prices, pay rises)
- Converting between FDP where the fraction is not one of the standard benchmarks

**Example question types:**
- "What is 35% of 500?" (Answer: 175) -- requires building from 10% and 5%
- "A shirt costs £45. It is reduced by 20% in a sale. What is the sale price?" (Answer: £36)
- "What percentage of 250 is 187.5?" (Answer: 75%)
- "Peter earns 3.2% interest on £12,500. How much interest does he earn?" (Answer: £400)
- "If 70% of 60,000,000 people have mobile phones, what percentage don't?" (Answer: 30%)

**Distractor patterns at D2:**
- Calculating the discount/increase amount but not applying it (giving £9 instead of £36 for the sale price)
- Forgetting to subtract the percentage from 100% in complement questions
- Decimal point placement errors with larger or smaller numbers
- Confusing "percentage of" with "percentage increase" (finding 20% but not subtracting it)

**What makes it D2:** Requires either a multi-step approach, a less familiar percentage, or the ability to express a proportion as a percentage. Children need to build percentages from 10% and 1% blocks rather than relying on memorised facts.

---

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**What it looks like:**
- Reverse percentage problems (given the final amount after increase/decrease, find the original)
- Percentages that produce recurring decimals (e.g., 2/12 as a percentage = 16.67%)
- Percentage of a percentage (compound problems)
- Multi-step word problems combining percentages with other operations
- Percentages greater than 100% (e.g., "What percentage of 16 is 72?" = 450%)
- Comparison problems requiring calculation on both sides before comparing
- Problems where the "whole" is not immediately obvious from the wording

**Example question types:**
- "After a 25% discount, a TV costs £270. What was the original price?" (Answer: £360 -- must recognise £270 = 75%, not calculate 25% of £270)
- "What is 20% of 20% of 500?" (Answer: 20)
- "What percentage of 748 is 68?" (Answer: ~9.09%)
- "Henry earns £42,400. After a 3.4% pay rise, what does he earn?" (Answer: £43,841.60)
- "A population increases by 10% one year then decreases by 10% the next. Is the final population the same, more, or less than the original?" (Answer: less -- this is a classic GL trap)

**Distractor patterns at D3:**
- In reverse percentage: calculating X% of the given amount and adding/subtracting (e.g., 25% of £270 = £67.50, giving £337.50 instead of the correct £360)
- In percentage of a percentage: treating it as addition (20% + 20% = 40% of 500 = 200)
- Rounding errors in recurring decimal answers
- Assuming a percentage increase then equal decrease returns to the original

**What makes it D3:** Requires deeper conceptual understanding, not just procedural fluency. The child must reason about what the percentage is "of" and work backwards or combine operations.

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Find a Percentage of an Amount
**Format:** "What is X% of Y?" or embedded in a word problem
**Method:** Find 10% (divide by 10), find 1% (divide by 100), build up to the target percentage
**GL contexts:** Money (pocket money, prices, savings), measurements (distances, weights, capacity), school scenarios (pupils, marks, votes)

### Type 2: FDP Conversion
**Format:** "Write 3/8 as a percentage" or "What is 0.6 as a percentage?" or "Express 45% as a fraction in its simplest form"
**Key equivalences children must know instantly:**
| Fraction | Decimal | Percentage |
|----------|---------|------------|
| 1/2 | 0.5 | 50% |
| 1/4 | 0.25 | 25% |
| 3/4 | 0.75 | 75% |
| 1/5 | 0.2 | 20% |
| 2/5 | 0.4 | 40% |
| 3/5 | 0.6 | 60% |
| 4/5 | 0.8 | 80% |
| 1/10 | 0.1 | 10% |
| 1/3 | 0.333... | 33.3% |
| 2/3 | 0.666... | 66.7% |
| 1/8 | 0.125 | 12.5% |
| 3/8 | 0.375 | 37.5% |
| 1/100 | 0.01 | 1% |

### Type 3: Express as a Percentage
**Format:** "What percentage of 300 is 15?" or "7 out of 28 pupils failed. What percentage failed?"
**Method:** Divide the part by the whole, multiply by 100
**GL twist:** The numbers are chosen so the answer is clean (5%, 25%, etc.) at D1-D2 but may be a recurring decimal at D3

### Type 4: Percentage Increase/Decrease
**Format:** Word problems about sales, discounts, price rises, population changes, pay increases
**Method (increase):** Find X% of the original, add to original. Or: multiply by (1 + X/100)
**Method (decrease):** Find X% of the original, subtract from original. Or: multiply by (1 - X/100)
**Common GL trap:** Children calculate the increase/decrease amount correctly but forget to add/subtract it from the original -- they give the change, not the new value

### Type 5: Reverse Percentage
**Format:** "After a 20% reduction, the price is £48. What was the original price?"
**Method:** Identify that £48 represents 80% (not 100%). Find 1% by dividing £48 by 80. Multiply by 100.
**Why it is hard:** Children instinctively want to find 20% of £48 and add it back. This gives £57.60 (wrong) instead of £60 (correct).

### Type 6: Comparison
**Format:** "Which is greater: 30% of 80 or 40% of 50?" or "Arrange in order: 2/5, 35%, 0.42"
**Method:** Convert everything to the same form (usually decimals or percentages) and compare
**GL twist:** The values are deliberately close to test precision

### Type 7: Percentage in Data Handling
**Format:** Pie chart questions using percentages, or table data where percentages must be calculated
**Crossover:** These appear in the Data Handling / Statistics section but require percentage skills
**Example:** "A pie chart shows 25% chose red, 30% chose blue, 15% chose green. What percentage chose yellow?" (Answer: 30%, because percentages must sum to 100%)

## Distractor Design

GL Assessment designs distractors (wrong answers) based on **predictable errors children actually make**, not random wrong numbers. Every distractor should be traceable to a specific misconception.

### Distractor Patterns for Percentage Questions

| Error Type | What the Child Does Wrong | Example |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------|
| **Decimal point shift** | Misplaces the decimal when dividing/multiplying by 100 | 25% of 75: gives 1.875 or 187.5 instead of 18.75 |
| **Percentage as divisor** | Divides by the percentage number instead of using it as parts per hundred | 25% of 80: does 80 / 25 = 3.2 instead of 80 / 4 = 20 |
| **Forgets second step** | Calculates the percentage amount but doesn't add/subtract from original | Sale: 20% off £45: gives £9 (the discount) instead of £36 (the sale price) |
| **Reverse percentage error** | Applies percentage to the new amount instead of recognising it as a fraction of the original | £48 after 20% off: finds 20% of £48 = £9.60, gives £57.60 instead of £60 |
| **Complement confusion** | Forgets that "what percentage don't" = 100% minus the given percentage | 70% have phones: gives 70% instead of 30% as "don't have" |
| **Additive reasoning** | Assumes 10% up then 10% down returns to original | Gives "same" instead of "less" (because 10% of the increased amount is larger than 10% of the original) |
| **FDP conversion slip** | Confuses 0.75 with 7.5% or 75 with 7.5 | 75% as a decimal: gives 7.5 instead of 0.75 |
| **Part-whole confusion** | Uses wrong number as the denominator in "express as %" | "15 out of 60" -- divides 60 by 15 instead of 15 by 60 |
| **Rounding trap** | Rounds prematurely in multi-step problems | Rounds 16.67% to 17% mid-calculation, giving a wrong final answer |

### Five-Option Design Principles
In our 5-option format (A-E), aim for:
- **1 correct answer**
- **1 "almost right" distractor** (most common error, e.g., forgot second step)
- **1 decimal point error** (shifted one or two places)
- **1 method error** (used wrong operation entirely)
- **1 plausible but further away** (catches children who guess or misread)

The correct answer should **not** always be the middle value or the "nicest" number. GL distributes correct answers across all positions roughly equally.

## Common GL Traps

1. **The "discount vs sale price" trap:** Question asks for the sale price but one distractor is the discount amount. Children who stop after one step pick this.

2. **The reverse percentage trap:** After a 20% increase, the value is £120. Children find 20% of £120 (= £24) and subtract to get £96. The correct answer is £100 (£120 = 120%, so original = £120 / 1.2 = £100).

3. **The complement trap:** "60% of children passed. What percentage failed?" A distractor of 60% catches children who misread. The correct answer is 40%.

4. **The "percentage of a percentage" trap:** 20% of 20% of 500. Children who add (20% + 20% = 40%) get 200. Children who find 20% of 500 = 100, then 20% of 100 = 20, get the correct answer.

5. **The successive percentage change trap:** "Increases by 10% then decreases by 10%." Children assume these cancel out. They don't: 100 -> 110 -> 99. The answer is always less than the starting value.

6. **The "which is the whole" trap:** "A box contains 30 red balls and 20 blue balls. What percentage are red?" Children who use 30 as the denominator (20/30) instead of 50 as the total get the wrong answer.

7. **The unit conversion trap:** "What percentage of 1 litre is 150ml?" Children who forget to convert to the same unit might calculate 150/1 = 15000% instead of 150/1000 = 15%.

8. **The decimal-percentage confusion:** Children write 0.35 when asked for a percentage (should be 35%) or write 35 when asked for a decimal (should be 0.35).

9. **The "of vs out of" language trap:** "What is 25% of 80?" versus "25 out of 80 is what percentage?" -- same words ("of", "25", "80") but completely different operations.

10. **The rounding trap:** Answer is 9.09% recurring. Distractors include 9%, 9.1%, 9.9%, and 90.9%. Tests whether the child calculated precisely and chose the closest match.

## Key Concepts Reference

### The Building Block Method (primary strategy for 11+)
This is the method most children should use under exam conditions:

1. **Find 10%** -- divide by 10
2. **Find 1%** -- divide by 100 (or divide 10% by 10)
3. **Find 5%** -- halve 10%
4. **Build the target percentage** from these blocks

Example: Find 35% of 500
- 10% of 500 = 50
- 30% = 3 x 50 = 150
- 5% = half of 50 = 25
- 35% = 150 + 25 = **175**

### The Fraction Shortcut
For benchmark percentages, convert to a fraction and divide:
- 50% = 1/2 (halve the number)
- 25% = 1/4 (divide by 4, or halve twice)
- 75% = 3/4 (divide by 4, multiply by 3)
- 20% = 1/5 (divide by 5)
- 12.5% = 1/8 (divide by 8)

### Express as a Percentage Formula
(Part / Whole) x 100 = Percentage

### Percentage Change Formula
Percentage change = (Change / Original) x 100

### Reverse Percentage
If the final value is Y after an X% increase:
Original = Y / (1 + X/100)

If the final value is Y after an X% decrease:
Original = Y / (1 - X/100)

### Percentage-means-per-hundred
"Per cent" literally means "per hundred." 45% means 45 out of every 100. This foundational understanding prevents many misconceptions.

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand these terms as they appear in GL percentage questions:

| Term | Meaning | GL usage |
|------|---------|----------|
| percentage | A proportion expressed as parts per hundred | Core concept |
| per cent / % | "Out of 100" | Always appears |
| discount | A reduction in price, expressed as a percentage | Word problems |
| sale price | The price after a discount has been applied | Word problems |
| original price | The price before any change | Reverse percentage |
| increase | Make larger by a percentage | Percentage change |
| decrease / reduce | Make smaller by a percentage | Percentage change |
| profit | Selling price minus cost price, often as a percentage | Word problems |
| loss | Cost price minus selling price, often as a percentage | Word problems |
| interest | Money earned on savings, expressed as a percentage per year | Word problems |
| VAT | Value Added Tax -- a percentage added to the price of goods | Rare at 11+ but possible |
| equivalent | Having the same value (e.g., 1/4 = 25% = 0.25) | Conversion questions |
| convert | Change from one form to another (fraction to percentage, etc.) | Conversion questions |
| proportion | A part considered in relation to the whole | Comparison questions |
| express as | "Write in the form of" -- e.g., "express 0.6 as a percentage" | Conversion questions |
| simplest form | A fraction where numerator and denominator share no common factors | FDP conversion |
| of | In percentage contexts, "of" means multiply | Critical reading cue |
| remainder / rest | What is left after a percentage is accounted for (100% minus given %) | Complement questions |
| out of | Indicates the "whole" in a proportion | Express-as-percentage questions |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 Statutory Requirements
- Recognise the per cent symbol (%) and understand that per cent relates to "number of parts per hundred"
- Write percentages as a fraction with denominator 100, and as a decimal fraction
- Solve problems which require knowing percentage and decimal equivalents of 1/2, 1/4, 1/5, 2/5, 4/5, and those fractions with a denominator of a multiple of 10 or 25

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements
- Recall and use equivalences between simple fractions, decimals, and percentages, including in different contexts
- Solve problems involving the calculation of percentages (for example, of measures, and such as 15% of 360) and the use of percentages for comparison
- Associate a fraction with division to calculate decimal fraction equivalents (e.g., 0.375) for a simple fraction (e.g., 3/8)

### Year 6 Non-Statutory Guidance
- Pupils link percentages or 360 degrees to calculating angles of pie charts
- Pupils should connect finding percentages to their knowledge of finding fractions of amounts

### GL Assessment Scope vs National Curriculum
GL papers are based on the National Curriculum up to the end of Year 5, with some content from early Year 6. However, GL intentionally includes questions that **stretch beyond** what most children have been explicitly taught, to differentiate the top 25% of candidates. This means:

- **Definitely tested:** All Year 5 percentage content (equivalences, simple "find X% of Y")
- **Very likely tested:** Year 6 content (calculating percentages of amounts, comparison, FDP fluency)
- **Possible stretch questions:** Reverse percentages, successive percentage changes, percentage of a percentage -- these are not in the National Curriculum at KS2 but appear in 11+ papers to separate the highest scorers

## Teaching Notes

### Priority Order for Preparation
1. **FDP equivalences** -- drill until instant recall (the conversion table above)
2. **Building block method** -- find 10%, find 1%, build any percentage
3. **Word problem reading** -- identify "the whole," "the part," and what the question actually asks
4. **Percentage of an amount** -- using building blocks and fraction shortcuts
5. **Express as a percentage** -- (part / whole) x 100, with unit conversion awareness
6. **Percentage increase/decrease** -- always two steps: find the amount, then add/subtract
7. **Reverse percentage** -- conceptually the hardest; save for after the above are secure
8. **Comparison and ordering** -- convert everything to the same form

### Common Misconceptions to Address Early
1. **"Percent means divide by that number"** -- children think 25% means divide by 25. Address by reinforcing that % means "out of 100" so 25% = 25/100 = 1/4.
2. **Decimal point confusion** -- 75% is NOT 7.5 as a decimal. It is 0.75. Drill "divide by 100 = move decimal point two places left."
3. **Reverse percentage as "undo"** -- children think adding 20% then subtracting 20% gets back to the start. Use concrete examples: 100 + 10% = 110, then 110 - 10% = 99, not 100.
4. **Part-whole confusion** -- in "15 out of 60," 60 is the whole (denominator), not 15. Children often get the fraction upside down.
5. **Forgetting unit conversion** -- "What percentage of 2 kg is 400g?" requires converting to the same unit first.
6. **Stopping too early** -- finding the discount amount instead of the sale price. Teach children to re-read the question after calculating.

### Exam Technique for Percentage Questions
- **Read the question twice** before starting any calculation
- **Underline what the question asks for** (the sale price? the discount? the percentage?)
- **Check whether the answer is sensible** -- if 20% of 80 gives you 160, something is wrong
- **Use estimation** -- 49% of 200 must be close to half of 200, so about 100
- **Convert units first** before any percentage work (ml to litres, g to kg, etc.)
- **For multiple choice:** eliminate obviously wrong answers first (too big, too small, decimal in wrong place), then calculate to confirm

### Time Budget
At 1 minute per question in the GL paper, percentage word problems may take longer than average. Children should:
- Spend no more than 90 seconds on any single percentage question
- If stuck, eliminate two distractors and make an educated guess
- Return to skipped questions if time permits at the end
