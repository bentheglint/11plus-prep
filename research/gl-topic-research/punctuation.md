# GL Assessment Research: Punctuation

## Format

GL tests punctuation using the same **error-spotting** format as spelling:

- A sentence or short passage line is split into **4 segments (A-D)** + option **E "No mistake"**
- Always 5 options, always multiple-choice (OMR answer sheet)
- Each line contains **at most one punctuation error** (or none if answer is E)
- Errors are always realistic mistakes children genuinely make, never absurd or random
- The child must identify which segment contains the punctuation error, or select E/N if the line is correctly punctuated

### Paper Context (Dorset Consortium)
- **English paper:** 45 minutes, ~49 questions total
- **Punctuation questions:** ~4-8 per paper (varies by sitting), roughly **16% of total English marks**
- Punctuation sits within the **Technical English / SPaG** section alongside spelling and grammar/cloze
- Technical English (spelling + punctuation + grammar) = ~50% of English marks; comprehension = ~50%
- All multiple-choice, 5 options (A-E), answered on a separate OMR bubble sheet

### Two Question Styles

GL uses two primary SPaG question styles, and punctuation appears in both:

1. **Error-spotting (primary format, ~70-80% of punctuation questions):**
   - Sentence divided into 4 labelled segments (A, B, C, D)
   - Child identifies which segment has a punctuation error, or selects E/N for "no mistake"
   - One error per line maximum

2. **Complete the sentence (~20-30% of punctuation questions):**
   - Sentence with a gap or missing punctuation
   - 5 options showing different punctuation choices
   - Child selects the option that correctly punctuates the sentence

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

| Category | Approx. GL Weight | Key Skills |
|----------|-------------------|------------|
| Apostrophes (possession & contraction) | ~25-30% | Singular/plural possession, contractions, its/it's, possessive pronouns |
| Commas (all uses) | ~20-25% | Lists, fronted adverbials, subordinate clauses, parenthetical commas, comma splices |
| Speech punctuation (inverted commas) | ~15-20% | Opening/closing speech marks, punctuation inside speech marks, reporting clauses, capitals |
| Sentence-ending punctuation | ~10-12% | Full stops, question marks, exclamation marks in correct contexts |
| Capital letters | ~8-10% | Sentence starts, proper nouns, pronoun "I", titles, days/months |
| Colons & semicolons | ~5-8% | Colons to introduce lists/explanations, semicolons between independent clauses |
| Brackets, dashes & hyphens | ~3-5% | Parenthesis using brackets/dashes, hyphens in compound words |
| Ellipsis | ~1-2% | Omission, trailing off, building suspense |

**Note:** Apostrophes and commas together account for roughly half of all punctuation questions. Speech punctuation is the third most heavily tested area. Colons, semicolons, brackets, and dashes appear less frequently but are specifically introduced in Year 6, making them higher-difficulty items.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

- **Punctuation marks from Years 2-4** that children should have fully mastered
- **Obvious, clear-cut errors** that sound wrong when read aloud
- Missing full stop at end of sentence
- Missing capital letter at start of sentence or for an obvious proper noun (London, Sarah)
- Missing or misplaced apostrophe in a simple contraction (dont → don't, cant → can't)
- Missing comma in a simple list (apples oranges and bananas → apples, oranges and bananas)
- Missing question mark after a direct question
- Other segments contain simple, unambiguous punctuation
- "No mistake" (E) is **rare** at D1
- A child who reads regularly spots these immediately

**Typical D1 error examples:**
- `The boys went to the park` (missing full stop)
- `sarah walked to school.` (missing capital for proper noun)
- `I cant believe it!` (missing apostrophe in contraction)
- `We bought apples oranges and pears.` (missing commas in list)

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

- **Year 4-5 punctuation skills** applied in more complex sentences
- Apostrophe possession with regular singular/plural nouns (the dog's lead / the dogs' leads)
- Comma after fronted adverbials (Meanwhile the children played → Meanwhile, the children played)
- Comma to separate subordinate clauses (Although it was raining they went outside)
- Basic speech punctuation errors — missing comma before opening speech marks, missing capital inside speech
- Confusion between it's (it is) and its (possessive)
- Exclamation mark used where full stop is correct (or vice versa)
- Other segments may contain correctly-used punctuation that children second-guess
- "No mistake" (E) **occasionally** appears
- Requires applying rules, not just instinct

**Typical D2 error examples:**
- `The childrens' toys were scattered everywhere.` (incorrect apostrophe placement — should be children's)
- `After the match the players celebrated.` (missing comma after fronted adverbial)
- `"Where are you going" asked Mum.` (missing question mark inside speech marks)
- `Its been a long day.` (should be It's — contraction of "it is")

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

- **Year 5-6 punctuation skills** — colons, semicolons, hyphens, dashes, parenthesis
- Semicolon vs comma vs full stop between closely related independent clauses
- Colon to introduce a list vs semicolons within a complex list
- Hyphens to avoid ambiguity (man eating chicken vs man-eating chicken)
- Parenthetical dashes vs commas for embedded clauses
- Interrupted speech punctuation (speech broken by reporting clause)
- Apostrophe traps: possessive pronouns (theirs, yours, whose — no apostrophe), irregular plurals (children's, women's, people's)
- Subtle comma placement that changes meaning ("Let's eat, Grandma" vs "Let's eat Grandma")
- **"No mistake" (E) more common** — correctly punctuated sentences with tricky-looking constructions
- Multiple segments contain advanced punctuation, creating doubt about which (if any) is wrong

**Typical D3 error examples:**
- `She packed everything she needed; her tent, sleeping bag, and torch.` (should be colon, not semicolon, before a list)
- `The well known author signed copies of her book.` (missing hyphen: well-known)
- `"I think," said Tom "that we should leave now."` (missing comma after reporting clause: said Tom,)
- `The children's, parents were invited to the assembly.` (incorrect comma — children's parents)
- No-error question where `The books on the shelf — mostly novels — belonged to Mrs Davies.` is entirely correct

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Missing Apostrophe (Contraction)
The sentence contains a contraction written without an apostrophe.
- `I didnt know` → I didn't know
- `They wouldnt listen` → They wouldn't listen
- `Weve been waiting` → We've been waiting

### Type 2: Apostrophe Misuse (Plurals vs Possession)
An apostrophe is incorrectly added to a simple plural, or missing from a possessive.
- `The apple's were delicious.` → The apples were (no apostrophe — plain plural)
- `The teachers desk was tidy.` → The teacher's desk (possession — needs apostrophe)
- `The boy's ran across the field.` → The boys ran (no apostrophe — plain plural)

### Type 3: It's / Its Confusion
The most commonly tested apostrophe trap.
- `The dog wagged it's tail.` → its tail (possessive pronoun — no apostrophe)
- `Its been raining all day.` → It's been (contraction of "it is")

### Type 4: Comma After Fronted Adverbial
A fronted adverbial (time/place/manner phrase at start of sentence) needs a comma.
- `After breakfast the family went shopping.` → After breakfast, the family...
- `Slowly and carefully she crept across the room.` → Slowly and carefully, she crept...
- `In the distance a church bell rang.` → In the distance, a church bell...

### Type 5: Comma in Lists
Missing commas between items in a list.
- `She packed shirts trousers socks and shoes.` → shirts, trousers, socks and shoes
- Note: GL follows UK convention — no Oxford comma before "and" is standard (though both are acceptable)

### Type 6: Speech Punctuation Errors
Direct speech is one of the most error-dense areas GL tests.
- Missing comma before speech: `She said "Let's go."` → She said, "Let's go."
- Missing capital inside speech: `He shouted, "come here!"` → "Come here!"
- Punctuation outside speech marks: `"I'm tired". she said.` → "I'm tired," she said.
- Missing question mark inside speech: `"Where are you going" he asked.` → "Where are you going?" he asked.
- Full stop instead of comma when reporting clause follows: `"I agree." said the teacher.` → "I agree," said the teacher.

### Type 7: Missing Capital Letter
- Start of sentence: `the children walked home.`
- Proper nouns: `We visited paris in the summer.`
- Days/months: `They arrived on tuesday.`
- Pronoun "I": `My friend and i went to the park.`

### Type 8: Comma Splice
Two independent clauses joined with only a comma (needs a conjunction, semicolon, or full stop).
- `She was tired, she went to bed.` → She was tired, so she went to bed. / She was tired; she went to bed.

### Type 9: Colon and Semicolon Usage (D3)
- Colon before a list: `You will need the following items: a pen, a ruler, and a calculator.`
- Semicolon between related clauses: `The sun was shining; it was a beautiful day.`
- Error: using comma where semicolon needed, or vice versa

### Type 10: Parenthetical Punctuation (D3)
- Brackets for extra information: `Sam went out in the storm (without a raincoat) and got soaked.`
- Dashes for parenthesis: `The old house — which had been empty for years — was finally sold.`
- Hyphens in compound adjectives: `a well-known author` (not `a well known author`)

## Distractor Design

### How GL Designs Correct Segments (to Create Doubt)

1. **Include correctly-used advanced punctuation** in non-error segments — a correctly-placed semicolon or dash makes children uncertain whether that segment is the error
2. **Tricky-looking but correct constructions** — possessive with irregular plurals (children's, women's), correctly omitted Oxford comma, apostrophe in unusual contractions (who's = who is)
3. **Homophones of punctuation-sensitive words** — their/they're/there, your/you're, its/it's placed correctly in non-error segments while the error is elsewhere
4. **Long or complex sentences** — the sheer length creates scanning difficulty; children may settle on the first thing that "looks odd" rather than the actual error
5. **Correctly punctuated direct speech** — speech with all commas, capitals, and speech marks in place (correct) alongside another segment with a subtle error

### How GL Designs Error Segments

1. **Plausible errors only** — the mistake must be one children genuinely make; a missing apostrophe in "dont" is plausible, a full stop in the middle of a word is not
2. **Common childhood errors** — apostrophe in plurals, comma splice, speech marks confusion, capital letter for common nouns
3. **Single-change errors** — one mark missing, added, or misplaced; never multiple errors in one segment
4. **Context-dependent errors** — the segment looks fine in isolation but is wrong in context (e.g., full stop where a comma should be before a reporting clause)

### "No Mistake" Questions

- Should be **15-20%** of punctuation questions (mirroring spelling)
- All segments are correctly punctuated, but at least one contains punctuation that looks potentially wrong
- Purpose: prevent children from assuming every line has an error
- More common at D2/D3 difficulty
- Often includes: correctly-used semicolons, parenthetical dashes, possessive pronouns without apostrophes (theirs, its, whose), or complex speech punctuation

## Common GL Traps

1. **Its vs It's trap (most common):** "The cat licked its paw" (correct — possessive, no apostrophe). Children who've learned "apostrophe = possession" overapply the rule. It's is always and only "it is" or "it has."

2. **Apostrophe in plurals trap:** "The dogs ran" (correct) vs "The dog's ran" (wrong — apostrophe added to plural). GL deliberately includes plural nouns near possessive constructions to trigger this confusion.

3. **Possessive pronoun trap:** theirs, yours, hers, ours, whose — **never** take apostrophes. "The book is theirs" (correct). "The book is their's" (wrong). GL tests this at D2-D3.

4. **Speech punctuation cascade:** Direct speech requires multiple punctuation marks in sequence (comma, opening speech mark, capital, closing punctuation, closing speech mark). GL typically tests one element being wrong while the rest are correct, forcing children to identify the specific fault.

5. **Comma splice trap:** "I was hungry, I made a sandwich" looks and sounds natural to children. GL offers this as an error — needs a conjunction (and/so/but), a semicolon, or a full stop.

6. **Fronted adverbial comma trap:** "Yesterday we went to the zoo" sounds fine when spoken. The missing comma after "Yesterday" is invisible to children who rely on ear rather than rule.

7. **Colon vs semicolon confusion:** Children who've just learned these marks often swap them. Colon introduces a list or explanation; semicolon joins two independent clauses. GL tests whether children know which is which.

8. **Hyphen ambiguity trap:** "The man eating chicken" (a man who is eating chicken) vs "The man-eating chicken" (a chicken that eats men). GL tests whether children know hyphens create compound modifiers.

9. **Capital letter for common nouns:** Children capitalise important-sounding common nouns (School, Teacher, Park) because they feel like proper nouns. GL includes these as errors to spot.

10. **"No mistake" doubt trap:** A correctly punctuated sentence containing a semicolon, parenthetical dash, or possessive pronoun (no apostrophe) — children doubt the correct punctuation and pick it as the "error."

## Key Punctuation Rules Reference

### Apostrophes

| Rule | Example | Common Error |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Possession (singular) | the girl's bag | the girls bag |
| Possession (plural ending in s) | the girls' bags | the girl's bags |
| Possession (irregular plural) | the children's toys | the childrens' toys |
| Contraction | don't, can't, it's (it is) | dont, cant, its (when meaning "it is") |
| Possessive pronouns (NO apostrophe) | its, theirs, yours, hers, ours, whose | it's, their's, your's |

### Commas

| Rule | Example | Common Error |
|------|---------|-------------|
| List items | apples, oranges and bananas | apples oranges and bananas |
| After fronted adverbial | Meanwhile, the rain stopped. | Meanwhile the rain stopped. |
| Before/after subordinate clause | Although it rained, they went out. | Although it rained they went out. |
| Around parenthetical phrase | My dog, a golden retriever, loves swimming. | Missing one or both commas |
| Before conjunction joining clauses | She sang, and he played guitar. | She sang and he played guitar. (acceptable but tested) |
| NOT for comma splice | WRONG: She was tired, she slept. | Need semicolon, conjunction, or full stop |

### Direct Speech

| Rule | Example | Common Error |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Comma before speech (reporting clause first) | She said, "Hello." | She said "Hello." |
| Capital letter inside speech marks | He whispered, "Come here." | He whispered, "come here." |
| Punctuation inside closing speech marks | "I'm leaving," she said. | "I'm leaving", she said. |
| Question mark inside speech marks | "Where is it?" he asked. | "Where is it" he asked? |
| Comma (not full stop) when reporting clause follows | "I agree," said Tom. | "I agree." said Tom. |
| Interrupted speech | "I think," said Tom, "we should go." | Missing comma after reporting clause |

### Colons

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Introduce a list | She packed: clothes, food, and a tent. |
| Introduce an explanation | He knew one thing: he had to leave. |
| Before an independent clause must be one | WRONG: She liked: apples and pears. (fragment before colon) |

### Semicolons

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Join two related independent clauses | The sun was out; it was a beautiful day. |
| Separate complex list items | They visited London, England; Paris, France; and Rome, Italy. |
| Both sides must be complete sentences | WRONG: Although it rained; they went out. (first part is dependent) |

### Other Marks

| Mark | Rule | Example |
|------|------|---------|
| Brackets | Extra (non-essential) information | Sam (who was nine) loved football. |
| Dashes | Parenthesis or emphasis | The castle — built in 1200 — was crumbling. |
| Hyphens | Compound adjectives / avoid ambiguity | a well-known author; a man-eating shark |
| Ellipsis | Omission or trailing off | She opened the door and saw... |
| Exclamation mark | Strong emotion or command | What a beautiful day! Stop right there! |

## Key Terminology

Children should know these terms (from National Curriculum Year 5-6):

| Term | Definition | Year Introduced |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| Apostrophe | Mark showing possession or contraction | Year 2 (contraction), Year 3 (possession) |
| Comma | Separates items, clauses, phrases | Year 2 (lists), Year 4 (fronted adverbials) |
| Inverted commas / speech marks | Enclose direct speech | Year 3-4 |
| Colon | Introduces a list or explanation | Year 6 |
| Semicolon | Joins related independent clauses; separates complex list items | Year 6 |
| Parenthesis | Extra information using brackets, dashes, or commas | Year 5 |
| Brackets | Enclose parenthetical information | Year 5 |
| Dash | Used for parenthesis or to join clauses (less formal) | Year 5 (parenthesis), Year 6 (clause boundary) |
| Hyphen | Joins compound words, avoids ambiguity | Year 6 |
| Ellipsis | Three dots showing omission or suspense | Year 6 |
| Contraction | Shortened word with apostrophe (don't, can't) | Year 2 |
| Possessive apostrophe | Shows ownership (the dog's bone) | Year 3-4 |
| Fronted adverbial | Time/place/manner phrase at sentence start, followed by comma | Year 4 |
| Subordinate clause | Dependent clause, often set off by commas | Year 4-5 |
| Independent clause | Complete sentence that can stand alone | Year 5-6 |
| Relative clause | Clause starting with who/which/that/where/when | Year 5 |
| Direct speech | Exact words someone said, in speech marks | Year 3-4 |
| Reporting clause | "he said" / "she asked" — identifies the speaker | Year 3-4 |
| Determiner | Word before noun (the, a, this, every) | Year 4 |
| Comma splice | ERROR: two independent clauses joined with only a comma | Not curriculum term but tested |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Punctuation Progression by Year

**Year 2 (should be fully secure by 11+ age):**
- Full stops, capital letters, question marks, exclamation marks
- Commas in lists
- Apostrophes for contraction (can't, don't, I'm)

**Year 3:**
- Inverted commas for direct speech (introduced)
- Apostrophes for singular possession (the girl's name)

**Year 4:**
- Apostrophes for plural possession (the girls' names)
- Comma after fronted adverbial (Later that day, ...)
- Inverted commas and other speech punctuation (commas, capitals within speech)

**Year 5:**
- Brackets, dashes, or commas for parenthesis
- Commas to clarify meaning or avoid ambiguity

**Year 6 (introduced — expected to recognise and use with guidance):**
- Semicolons, colons, dashes to mark boundaries between independent clauses
- Colon to introduce a list
- Semicolons within lists
- Hyphens to avoid ambiguity
- Ellipsis (implied through curriculum objectives)

### What This Means for GL 11+

GL tests **up to and including Year 6 content**, but with the expectation that Year 2-4 skills are fully secure and Year 5-6 skills are developing. This means:
- **D1 questions** test Year 2-4 material (should be automatic for a prepared child)
- **D2 questions** test Year 4-5 material applied in slightly complex contexts
- **D3 questions** test Year 5-6 material (colons, semicolons, hyphens) and tricky applications of Year 3-4 rules (irregular plural possession, interrupted speech, comma vs semicolon boundaries)

The statutory requirement for Year 6 is that colons, semicolons, dashes, and hyphens are **introduced** — not fully mastered. GL exploits this by including them at D3 where even strong children may be uncertain.

## Teaching Notes

### Priority Order for Preparation

Based on GL weighting and common error rates, teach/revise in this order:

1. **Apostrophes** (~25-30% of marks) — Start with contractions (secure by age 10), then singular possession, plural possession, irregular plurals, possessive pronouns (no apostrophe). The its/it's distinction deserves dedicated practice.

2. **Commas** (~20-25%) — Lists first (should be secure), then fronted adverbials, then subordinate/relative clauses, then comma splices (recognising them as errors). Comma after fronted adverbial is undertaught and frequently tested.

3. **Speech punctuation** (~15-20%) — Revise the four speech punctuation rules (comma before speech, capital inside speech marks, punctuation inside closing marks, comma not full stop before reporting clause). Interrupted speech is D3.

4. **Capital letters** (~8-10%) — Quick wins. Proper nouns, sentence starts, "I", days/months. Common error: capitalising common nouns that feel important (school, teacher, park).

5. **Colons & semicolons** (~5-8%) — Introduce with clear rules: colon = introduces a list or explanation; semicolon = joins two complete, related sentences. Don't over-teach — recognition is enough for D3.

6. **Brackets, dashes, hyphens** (~3-5%) — Teach as a group (parenthesis tools). Hyphens: compound adjectives before a noun (well-known, man-eating). Dashes: like brackets but more dramatic.

### Common Misconceptions to Address

- **"Apostrophe means possession" over-generalisation:** Children learn apostrophe = ownership, then put apostrophes in possessive pronouns (it's, their's, your's, who's). Teach the exception: possessive pronouns **never** have apostrophes.

- **"If it sounds right, it is right" reliance:** Many comma and apostrophe rules are invisible in speech. "After breakfast the family left" sounds fine but needs a comma. "The dogs bone" sounds identical to "the dog's bone." Teach that punctuation rules exist separately from how sentences sound.

- **"Commas go wherever you pause" myth:** Leads to random comma placement and comma splices. Teach specific comma rules (lists, fronted adverbials, subordinate clauses, speech) rather than the "pause" heuristic.

- **Speech punctuation fear:** Children find the multiple simultaneous rules of speech punctuation overwhelming. Break into discrete sub-rules and practise each independently before combining.

### Practice Strategies

1. **Error-spotting drills in GL format** — Sentence split into 4 segments, child identifies the error segment. Include "no mistake" options regularly (15-20% of questions).

2. **Apostrophe sorting** — Give lists of words and phrases; child sorts into contraction / possession / no apostrophe needed. Include it's/its, they're/their, you're/your.

3. **Comma insertion** — Give unpunctuated sentences; child adds commas and explains why (which rule applies). Focus on fronted adverbials and subordinate clauses.

4. **Speech punctuation correction** — Give incorrectly punctuated direct speech; child fixes all errors. Progress from single errors to multiple concurrent errors.

5. **Colon vs semicolon snap decisions** — Quick-fire: "Is this a colon or semicolon situation?" Builds automatic recognition without overthinking.

### Answer Position Distribution
- Error distributed roughly evenly across A, B, C, D — no positional bias
- GL avoids patterns where the answer clusters in one position
- "No mistake" (E/N) appears in 15-20% of questions, more often at D2/D3
