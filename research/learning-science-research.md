# Learning Science Research for 11+ Exam Preparation

## Purpose

This document compiles evidence-based research from cognitive psychology, learning science, and educational research as it applies to children aged 9-11 preparing for the GL Assessment 11+ exam. It covers 13 major domains of learning science, drawing on peer-reviewed studies, meta-analyses, and reports from bodies such as the Education Endowment Foundation (EEF). The aim is to provide a research-grade foundation for designing the study programme, app features, and parent guidance for 11+ preparation over a 12-18 month period.

This is not pop psychology. Every section cites specific researchers, studies, or systematic reviews. Where the evidence is mixed or contested, that is noted.

## 1. Retrieval Practice (The Testing Effect)

### 1.1 What the Research Shows

Retrieval practice is the act of recalling information from memory, typically through testing or quizzing. Over a century of research demonstrates that the act of retrieving information from memory strengthens that memory far more effectively than re-reading, re-highlighting, or passive review.

**The foundational study:** Roediger & Karpicke (2006) published two landmark papers. In "Test-Enhanced Learning" (Psychological Science), students studied prose passages and either took free-recall tests or restudied the material. On a test given 5 minutes later, the restudy group performed better. But on a delayed test (2 days or 1 week later), the testing group substantially outperformed the restudy group. This crossover effect is critical: testing feels less productive in the moment but produces dramatically better long-term retention.

**Roediger & Karpicke (2006), "The Power of Testing Memory" (Perspectives on Psychological Science, 1(3), 181-210):** This comprehensive review established that testing is not merely an assessment tool but a powerful learning event. They reviewed decades of laboratory research confirming that retrieval practice enhances long-term retention across multiple types of material.

**Karpicke & Roediger (2008), "The Critical Importance of Retrieval for Learning" (Science, 319, 966-968):** Students learned foreign vocabulary in repeated study-test trials. The critical finding: repeated studying after initial learning had no effect on delayed recall, but repeated testing produced a large positive effect. Even when students had already demonstrated they knew an item, continued testing (not continued studying) was what cemented long-term retention.

**Karpicke & Blunt (2011), "Retrieval Practice Produces More Learning than Elaborative Studying with Concept Mapping" (Science, 331, 772-775):** Retrieval practice outperformed concept mapping -- a well-regarded elaborative study technique -- on both verbatim recall and inference-based questions. Students predicted concept mapping would be more effective, but they were wrong. This study is important because it shows retrieval practice works even for higher-order comprehension, not just rote memorisation.

### 1.2 Why Testing Beats Re-Reading

Three mechanisms explain the testing effect:

1. **Retrieval effort strengthens memory traces.** The act of searching memory and reconstructing an answer creates stronger neural pathways than passively re-encountering information. The harder the retrieval (within limits), the stronger the benefit.

2. **Testing identifies knowledge gaps.** When a child attempts to answer a question and fails, they receive immediate diagnostic information about what they do not know. Re-reading provides no such signal -- you can re-read something ten times without ever discovering you cannot reproduce it from memory.

3. **Testing provides elaborative encoding.** During retrieval, related information is activated alongside the target memory, creating richer associative networks. Each successful retrieval adds another "route" to the information in memory.

### 1.3 Optimal Retrieval Schedules

Research consistently shows that retrieval practice is most effective when:

- **There is a delay between study and test.** Immediate testing is less beneficial than delayed testing because the retrieval is too easy. Some forgetting must occur before testing for the full benefit to emerge (this connects to desirable difficulties, Section 9).

- **Retrieval is repeated over time.** A single test helps, but multiple spaced tests produce cumulative benefits. Karpicke & Roediger (2007, Journal of Memory and Language) showed that repeated retrieval during learning is the key to long-term retention.

- **Feedback is provided.** While testing without feedback still produces benefits, testing with corrective feedback produces larger gains, particularly for items initially answered incorrectly (Butler & Roediger, 2008).

### 1.4 Implementation for 9-11 Year Olds

Research conducted in primary schools (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025) confirms that retrieval practice enhances learning in real primary classroom settings, not just laboratory conditions. The EEF notes that retrieval should not be a single, separate learning activity; it must be recalled repeatedly after a delay.

**Practical application for 11+ prep:**
- Low-stakes quizzing after every study session (the app's quiz mode already does this)
- Questions that require recall, not recognition where possible (though multiple-choice is the 11+ format, free recall during study is still valuable)
- Mixing in questions from previous topics during each session (connects to interleaving, Section 3)
- No penalty for wrong answers -- the point is the retrieval attempt, not the score
- Reviewing incorrect answers with explanations (the app's immediate feedback mechanism)

**Key insight from the EEF:** Teachers (and parents) should create questions that go beyond factual recall into higher-order thinking. Questions that are too easy boost confidence without providing genuine retrieval challenge.

## 2. Spaced Repetition

### 2.1 The Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve

Hermann Ebbinghaus conducted his pioneering research between 1880 and 1885, memorising nonsense syllables and testing himself at various intervals. His forgetting curve demonstrates that without reinforcement:

- Approximately 50-70% of newly learned information is forgotten within 24 hours
- Retention drops to approximately 20-30% within a week
- The curve eventually levels off, with some information persisting in long-term memory

The critical insight is not that forgetting is inevitable, but that strategically timed review can flatten the curve dramatically. Each review session resets the forgetting curve at a higher baseline, meaning the information takes longer to decay after each reinforcement.

### 2.2 The Spacing Effect: Core Research

The spacing effect is one of the most robust findings in all of psychology, demonstrated in over 200 studies spanning more than a century.

**Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted, & Rohrer (2006), "Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks: A Review and Quantitative Synthesis" (Psychological Bulletin, 132(3), 354-380):** This landmark meta-analysis examined 839 assessments across 317 experiments in 184 articles. The key finding: the optimal spacing interval between study sessions depends on how long you need to retain the information. The longer the retention interval (time until the exam), the longer the optimal gap between study sessions should be.

Specific findings from the research:
- For a 7-day retention interval, the optimal spacing was approximately 1-3 days between sessions
- For a 35-day retention interval, optimal spacing was approximately 7-11 days
- For a 1-year retention interval, optimal spacing was approximately 3-5 weeks

**Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan, & Willingham (2013), "Improving Students' Learning with Effective Learning Techniques" (Psychological Science in the Public Interest, 14(1), 4-58):** This comprehensive review rated 10 learning techniques. Distributed (spaced) practice received a "high utility" rating -- one of only two techniques (alongside practice testing) to receive this top rating. The benefits were demonstrated across different ages, abilities, and types of material.

### 2.3 Optimal Spacing Intervals for Children

Research with children specifically shows the spacing effect operates similarly to adults, though the literature on optimal intervals specifically for children aged 9-11 is less developed than for adults.

**Key finding for children:** Vlach & Sandhofer (2012) found spacing benefits with children as young as 3 years old, with spaced groups showing significantly greater improvement than massed groups for both simple and complex generalisation questions. This suggests the spacing effect is a fundamental property of human memory, not dependent on metacognitive sophistication.

**The expanding interval principle:** Research supports expanding schedules -- where the first review occurs soon after learning and each subsequent review is spaced increasingly further apart -- as often outperforming uniform spacing. Dr Piotr Wozniak's SuperMemo algorithm, the first computer-based spaced repetition system, formalised this as:

- 1st review: 1 day after initial learning
- 2nd review: 3 days after 1st review
- 3rd review: 7 days after 2nd review
- 4th review: 14-21 days after 3rd review
- Subsequent reviews: increasing intervals

### 2.4 The 2-3-5-7 Method and Practical Schedules

A simplified version for children, sometimes called the 2-3-5-7 method:

1. Study the material (Day 0)
2. Review after 2 days
3. Review after 3 more days (Day 5 total)
4. Review after 5 more days (Day 10 total)
5. Review after 7 more days (Day 17 total)

This is a pragmatic simplification of the research. The exact intervals matter less than the principle: **review at increasing intervals, and review before the material is completely forgotten but after some forgetting has occurred.** The slight struggle of trying to recall partially-forgotten material is what strengthens the memory (see Desirable Difficulties, Section 9).

### 2.5 Leitner System Adaptation for Children

The Leitner system, developed by Sebastian Leitner in 1972, uses physical boxes to implement spaced repetition with flashcards:

- **Box 1:** New or incorrectly answered items -- reviewed every day
- **Box 2:** Correctly answered once -- reviewed every 2-3 days
- **Box 3:** Correctly answered twice -- reviewed weekly
- **Box 4:** Correctly answered three times -- reviewed fortnightly
- **Box 5:** Well-known items -- reviewed monthly

Correctly answered cards move up one box. Incorrectly answered cards return to Box 1 regardless of their current box. This naturally concentrates practice on the material the child finds most difficult.

**Adaptation for 11+ prep (digital implementation):**
The app could implement a Leitner-style system where:
- Questions answered incorrectly are flagged for repeat within the same session
- Questions consistently answered correctly are shown less frequently
- Topics with lower accuracy scores appear more often in mixed practice
- The system tracks per-question performance, not just per-topic

**Important caveat:** The Leitner system was designed for factual recall (vocabulary, dates). For mathematical problem-solving, where the skill involves procedural fluency and conceptual understanding, pure flashcard-style repetition is less appropriate. The spacing principle still applies, but practice should involve solving varied problems within each topic, not just recognising answers.

### 2.6 Building a Spacing Schedule for 11+ Prep

For a child preparing for the 11+ exam in September 2026 with ~18 months of preparation:

**Phase 1 (Months 1-6): Foundation building**
- Introduce 1-2 new topics per week
- Review each new topic 1, 3, and 7 days after introduction
- Begin interleaving previously learned topics into practice sessions

**Phase 2 (Months 7-12): Consolidation**
- All topics introduced; focus shifts to deepening understanding
- Each topic reviewed at least fortnightly
- Weak topics identified and reviewed more frequently (Leitner principle)
- Mixed practice papers incorporating all topics

**Phase 3 (Months 13-18): Exam preparation**
- Weekly full mixed practice covering all topics
- Weak topics reviewed 2-3 times per week
- Strong topics maintained with monthly review
- Timed practice to build exam stamina

**The research-based principle:** Reviewing material within 24 hours can increase retention by up to 80% (Ebbinghaus). But the goal across 18 months is not just retention -- it is deep fluency. Spaced repetition builds the automaticity needed to perform under exam conditions.

## 3. Interleaving vs Blocking

### 3.1 The Problem with Blocked Practice

Traditional study and homework typically use blocked practice: do 20 long division problems, then 20 fractions problems, then 20 algebra problems. This feels productive because performance during practice is high -- the child knows what type of problem they are solving before they even read the question.

But this is an illusion of competence. The child is not learning to identify which strategy to use; they are merely executing a known strategy repeatedly. In an exam, no one tells you "this is a fractions question" -- the child must diagnose the problem type themselves.

### 3.2 What the Research Shows

**Rohrer & Taylor (2007), "The Shuffling of Mathematics Problems Improves Learning" (Instructional Science, 35, 481-498):** Students who practised mathematics problems in interleaved order (mixed) performed significantly better on a delayed test than those who practised in blocked order. Crucially, interleaving impaired practice performance -- students got more wrong during practice -- but dramatically improved test performance.

**Taylor & Rohrer (2010), "The Effects of Interleaved Practice" (Applied Cognitive Psychology, 24, 837-848):** Children practised four kinds of mathematics problems in either interleaved or blocked order, with spacing held constant. Interleaving impaired practice session performance but doubled scores on a test given one day later. Error analysis showed interleaving improved participants' ability to pair each problem with the appropriate procedure -- the diagnostic skill that blocked practice fails to develop.

**Rohrer, Dedrick, & Stershic (2015), "Interleaved Practice Improves Mathematics Learning" (Educational Psychology Review):** This is the landmark classroom study. 126 seventh-grade students received the same practice problems over a 3-month period, arranged in either interleaved or blocked order. On the delayed test: **interleaved group scored 72% versus 38% for the blocked group.** This near-doubling of scores is one of the largest effect sizes in educational intervention research.

**Rohrer & Dedrick (2014), "The Benefit of Interleaved Mathematics Practice Is Not Limited to Superficially Similar Kinds of Problems" (Psychonomic Bulletin & Review):** This study addressed the concern that interleaving only helps when problem types look similar. It demonstrated that interleaving benefits extend even to problem types that are superficially quite different.

### 3.3 Why Interleaving Works

Two mechanisms have been identified:

1. **Discriminative contrast.** When different problem types are juxtaposed, the learner is forced to notice what makes each type different. "This is a ratio problem because..." versus "this is a fractions problem because..." This diagnostic skill is exactly what exams require.

2. **Distributed practice.** Interleaving naturally spaces out practice of any single skill. This connects directly to the spacing effect (Section 2). However, research by Rohrer's group has shown that even when spacing is controlled for, interleaving still provides additional benefits beyond spacing alone.

### 3.4 Why It Feels Harder But Works Better

This is a crucial point for parents and children to understand. During interleaved practice:

- The child will get more answers wrong
- The child will feel more confused and frustrated
- The child may complain that "this is harder"
- Practice scores will be lower

**All of this is expected and desirable.** The research is unambiguous: the conditions that produce the best learning often feel the worst during practice. This is the core insight of desirable difficulties (Section 9). Parents need to resist the temptation to switch back to blocked practice because "it's working better" based on practice performance. What matters is test performance, and interleaving consistently wins on that measure.

### 3.5 Practical Application for 11+ Practice

**Within a single study session:**
- Mix 2-3 different topic types rather than drilling one topic
- For a 20-minute session: 3-4 questions from Topic A, 3-4 from Topic B, 3-4 from Topic C, then back to A, B, C
- Ensure the child must identify the problem type, not just execute the procedure

**Across sessions:**
- Never do an entire week on one topic
- Rotate through topics throughout the week
- Include "surprise" questions from topics not recently studied

**In the app:**
- Mixed practice mode that draws from multiple topics
- "Focused Learning" on a single topic balanced with interleaved review sessions
- Progress tracking that shows improvement on delayed tests, not just practice scores

## 4. Elaboration

### 4.1 The Research Base

Elaboration involves generating explanations for why facts or concepts are true, connecting new information to existing knowledge, and explaining material in one's own words. It is one of the six evidence-based strategies identified by The Learning Scientists (learningscientists.org).

**Dunlosky et al. (2013)** rated elaborative interrogation as "moderate utility" -- effective but with caveats about age and implementation. The technique works well for upper elementary students (ages 9-12) and older, but benefits are less clear for younger children (below age 8), likely because younger children lack the prior knowledge base needed to generate meaningful elaborations.

This is relevant for 11+ prep: at ages 9-11, children are entering the sweet spot where elaborative strategies become effective.

### 4.2 Elaborative Interrogation

Elaborative interrogation involves asking "Why?" and "How?" questions about the material being learned. Rather than simply noting that "to find 15% of a number, you find 10% and 5% and add them," the child is asked: "Why does that work? Why can we split 15% into 10% + 5%?"

**Research evidence:** Pressley, McDaniel, Turnure, Wood, & Ahmad (1987) established that elaborative interrogation significantly improves learning compared to simply reading facts. The effect has been replicated across multiple studies with school-age children.

**The mechanism:** Elaborative interrogation forces the learner to activate relevant prior knowledge and integrate new information with what they already know. This creates a richer, more interconnected memory representation that is more durable and more accessible than isolated facts.

### 4.3 The Generation Effect

The generation effect is closely related: information that is generated (produced) by the learner is better remembered than information that is merely read. This was first demonstrated by Slamecka & Graf (1978) and has been robustly replicated.

**Application for 11+ prep:**
- Rather than showing the child a worked example, have them attempt the problem first (even if they get it wrong)
- Ask the child to predict the answer before revealing it
- Have the child generate their own examples of a mathematical concept
- Use fill-in-the-blank explanations rather than complete explanations

### 4.4 Teaching Back (The Feynman Technique)

The Feynman Technique, named after physicist Richard Feynman, operationalises elaboration through teaching. The principle: if you cannot explain a concept simply and coherently, you do not understand it yet.

**The four steps:**
1. Study the concept
2. Teach it to someone else (a sibling, a parent, a stuffed toy) using simple language
3. Identify where your explanation breaks down -- these are your knowledge gaps
4. Go back, fill the gaps, and teach again more clearly

**Research support:** Chi, Bassok, Lewis, Reimann, & Glaser (1989) found that students who self-explained worked examples learned significantly more than those who did not. The act of explaining forces reorganisation and deeper processing of the material.

**For children aged 9-11:** When a child explains a maths concept aloud, they must organise ideas logically, choose simple language, and connect each step clearly. Moments where they stumble or use vague language reveal exactly where understanding is incomplete.

### 4.5 How Parents Can Use Elaborative Interrogation

After the child completes a problem or reads an explanation:

**Instead of:** "Do you understand?"
**Ask:** "Why does that work?" / "Can you explain that to me?" / "What would happen if the numbers were different?"

**Instead of:** "Let me explain it again."
**Ask:** "What part makes sense to you? Where does it get confusing?"

**Instead of:** "The answer is C because..."
**Ask:** "You got that wrong -- what do you think went wrong? Which step tripped you up?"

These questions force active processing. The child cannot nod along passively; they must construct an explanation, which requires genuine understanding.

## 5. Dual Coding

### 5.1 The Theory

Dual coding theory was first proposed by Allan Paivio in the late 1960s at the University of Western Ontario. The core claim: the mind processes information through two independent channels -- verbal (words, language) and nonverbal (images, spatial information). Information encoded through both channels simultaneously creates two separate memory traces, significantly improving recall, comprehension, and transfer to new contexts.

**Paivio's three key findings:**
1. Concrete words (which easily evoke mental images) are remembered better than abstract words
2. Pictures are remembered better than words alone
3. Combining words with pictures produces the strongest memory

**Clark & Paivio (1991), "Dual Coding Theory and Education" (Educational Psychology Review, 3, 149-210):** This comprehensive paper applied dual coding theory specifically to education, arguing that instruction combining verbal and visual information enhances student comprehension.

### 5.2 The Research Evidence

When participants were shown rapid sequences of pictures as well as words and asked to recall them, participants consistently recalled images better than words. This "picture superiority effect" is one of the most reliable findings in memory research.

**Important caveat from research:** Dual coding works when the two channels reinforce the same concept without overwhelming working memory. When the visual and verbal information conflict, or when the visual information is merely decorative (not informative), the benefit disappears or reverses. This connects directly to Cognitive Load Theory (Section 7) and Mayer's multimedia principles.

### 5.3 Application to Children's Learning

**Diagrams and visual representations:** For mathematics, visual representations are not just helpful -- they are often essential. A fraction diagram (showing 3/4 of a shape shaded) combined with the symbolic notation 3/4 creates dual coding naturally. Similarly, geometry problems benefit enormously from diagrams, and data handling questions require graphs and charts.

**The app's SVG diagrams serve this purpose directly.** Volume questions with 3D cuboid diagrams, area/perimeter questions with shape diagrams, and data handling questions with graphs all provide the visual channel that complements the verbal/symbolic mathematical content.

**Mind maps:** Research with primary school children (ages 8-12) shows mind maps can be beneficial, but the evidence is mixed. Retrieval practice with or without mind mapping tends to produce comparable results (PMC, 2013), and student-generated mind maps show stronger effects than researcher-provided ones. Mind maps may be more useful as a study planning tool than as a primary learning strategy.

**Colour coding:** Assigning colours to different question types or mathematical operations can leverage visual encoding. For example, using consistent colour coding across the app (blue for fractions, green for percentages, etc.) provides an additional visual cue that aids categorisation and retrieval.

### 5.4 Practical Implementation

- Pair every mathematical explanation with a visual representation
- Use diagrams that illustrate the concept, not just decorate the page
- Ensure visual and verbal information are spatially integrated (not separated -- see split-attention effect in Section 7)
- Encourage children to draw diagrams when solving problems
- Use colour coding consistently to aid categorisation
- Avoid purely decorative images that add visual noise without aiding comprehension

## 6. Metacognition

### 6.1 What Metacognition Is

Metacognition means "thinking about thinking." It encompasses three components:

1. **Metacognitive knowledge:** What the learner knows about how learning works (e.g., "I learn maths better in the morning," "I find fractions harder than percentages")
2. **Metacognitive regulation:** Planning, monitoring, and evaluating one's own learning (e.g., "I should check my answer by working backwards," "I'm running out of time so I'll skip this question")
3. **Metacognitive experiences:** The feelings associated with learning (e.g., "This feels too easy -- am I really learning?" "I'm confused, which means I need to re-read the question")

### 6.2 The EEF Evidence

The Education Endowment Foundation's Teaching and Learning Toolkit -- the most comprehensive synthesis of educational evidence in the UK -- ranks metacognition and self-regulation as having an average impact of **+8 months' additional progress.** This makes it one of the highest-impact strategies in the entire toolkit, alongside feedback (+6 months).

Critically, the cost of implementing metacognitive strategies is rated as "very low," making it one of the most cost-effective educational interventions available. The evidence covers ages 2-19 and shows positive effects across this entire range.

**The EEF's updated guidance (2021, revised 2024)** identifies seven key recommendations:
1. Teachers should acquire professional understanding of metacognition
2. Explicitly teach pupils metacognitive strategies
3. Model your own thinking to help pupils develop their metacognitive and cognitive skills
4. Set appropriate challenge to develop pupils' self-regulation
5. Promote and develop metacognitive talk in the classroom
6. Explicitly teach organisation and revision strategies
7. Schools should support teachers to develop knowledge and apply metacognitive strategies

### 6.3 Teaching Metacognition to a 10-Year-Old

Metacognition sounds abstract, but it can be made concrete for children through simple routines:

**Before a practice session (Planning):**
- "What topic are we working on today?"
- "What do you already know about this?"
- "What strategy will you use to solve these problems?"
- "Which types of questions do you usually find hardest?"

**During the session (Monitoring):**
- "How confident are you in that answer -- would you bet on it?"
- "Can you check your answer a different way?"
- "Are you rushing? Do you need to slow down?"
- "What step did you just do? What's the next step?"

**After the session (Evaluating):**
- "Which questions were hardest? Why?"
- "What would you do differently next time?"
- "What did you learn today that you didn't know before?"
- "Rate your understanding from 1-5."

### 6.4 The Dunning-Kruger Effect in Children

Research confirms the Dunning-Kruger effect operates in children from approximately grade 3 (age 7-8) onwards. The pattern is consistent:

- **Low-performing children overestimate their ability.** They lack the metacognitive skill to recognise what they don't know. A child who scores 30% on a fractions quiz may predict they'll score 70%.
- **High-performing children slightly underestimate their ability.** They assume that because they found it hard, others must also find it hard.

**Research with primary school children (grades 3-4):** Studies across multiple countries found low achievers consistently overestimating their item-specific confidence judgements compared to high achievers. This has been replicated with various age groups and subject areas.

**Practical implications:**
- Don't ask "Did you understand that?" -- children (especially struggling ones) will say yes even when they didn't
- Instead, ask them to demonstrate understanding by solving a problem or explaining the concept
- Use prediction activities: "How many do you think you'll get right?" followed by the actual result. Over time, this calibrates their self-assessment
- Track accuracy of self-predictions alongside actual scores -- improving calibration is itself a valuable learning outcome

### 6.5 Self-Checking Strategies for 11+ Maths

Teaching children to check their own work is pure metacognition in action:

- **Estimation first:** Before calculating, estimate the answer. After calculating, check: is your answer close to your estimate? If you estimated "about 50" and got 4.7, something went wrong.
- **Inverse operations:** Check subtraction with addition, division with multiplication.
- **Substitute back:** In algebra, put your answer back into the original equation.
- **Units check:** Does your answer have the right units? If the question asks for cm and you got 5,000, is that plausible?
- **Re-read the question:** After solving, re-read the question. Did you answer what was actually asked? (A common 11+ trap: asking for the remaining amount when the child calculates the spent amount.)

## 7. Cognitive Load Theory

### 7.1 The Theory

Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) was developed by John Sweller at the University of New South Wales in 1988. It is arguably the most important theory for instructional design, and its implications are profound for how we structure learning materials for children.

**The core premise:** Working memory has severe capacity limitations. Adults can hold approximately 7 (plus or minus 2) chunks of information in working memory (Miller, 1956), with a processing limit of only 2-4 chunks simultaneously (Cowan, 2001). Working memory duration is approximately 20 seconds without rehearsal.

**Children have even more constrained working memory.** Cowan's research on working memory development shows that capacity increases with age, with 7-year-olds having notably less capacity than 12-year-olds, and 12-year-olds less than adults. At ages 9-11, children are in a transitional period -- their working memory is substantially more developed than younger children but still significantly below adult levels.

### 7.2 Three Types of Cognitive Load

**Intrinsic load** is determined by the complexity of the material itself and the learner's prior knowledge. A multiplication problem like 3 x 4 has low intrinsic load for a child who knows their times tables, but high intrinsic load for a child just learning multiplication. Intrinsic load cannot be eliminated, but it can be managed by sequencing instruction from simple to complex and building on prior knowledge.

**Extraneous load** is caused by poor instructional design. It is the unnecessary cognitive effort imposed by how information is presented, rather than by the information itself. Examples for children:
- A cluttered question screen with too many visual elements
- An explanation that uses complex vocabulary when simple words would do
- Requiring the child to flip between a diagram and its description (split-attention effect)
- Irrelevant decorative images that don't aid comprehension
- Overly long explanations that could be simplified

**Germane load** is the cognitive effort devoted to processing, constructing, and automating schemas. This is the "good" cognitive work -- the effort of understanding, connecting ideas, and building mental models. The goal of instruction is to minimise extraneous load and redirect cognitive resources toward germane load.

### 7.3 Implications for Lesson and Question Design

**Mayer's Multimedia Learning Principles** (derived from CLT) provide specific, evidence-based design rules:

- **Coherence principle:** Remove extraneous information. Every element on screen should serve a purpose.
- **Signalling principle:** Highlight essential information. Use bold, colour, or arrows to guide attention.
- **Spatial contiguity principle:** Place corresponding words and pictures near each other, not separated.
- **Split-attention effect:** When a diagram requires a separate legend or explanation, integrate the text into the diagram rather than placing it elsewhere on the page.
- **Redundancy principle:** Don't present identical information in two forms simultaneously (e.g., reading aloud text that is also on screen). This overloads working memory rather than supporting it.
- **Segmenting principle:** Break complex procedures into discrete steps. For a multi-step maths problem, show one step at a time.

### 7.4 Working Memory Development in 9-11 Year Olds

**Gathercole & Alloway (2008), "Working Memory and Learning: A Practical Guide for Teachers":** This is the key reference for understanding working memory limitations in school-age children.

Key findings relevant to 11+ prep:
- Children aged 9-11 can typically hold 4-5 chunks in working memory (compared to 6-7 for adults)
- Working memory capacity is a strong predictor of academic achievement
- Children with lower working memory capacity are disproportionately affected by high cognitive load instruction
- Automaticity (through practice) frees working memory for higher-order thinking

**Practical implication:** A child who has automated their times tables can focus working memory on the problem-solving strategy. A child still calculating 7 x 8 uses up working memory on basic arithmetic, leaving less capacity for the actual problem. This is why fluency in basic skills matters so much for 11+ performance -- it is not rote memorisation for its own sake, it is freeing working memory for the complex reasoning the exam requires.

### 7.5 Design Principles Derived from CLT

For the 11+ prep app and study sessions:

1. **Keep question explanations simple and step-by-step.** Each step should make one point.
2. **Integrate labels into diagrams** rather than using separate legends.
3. **Remove decorative elements** that don't aid understanding.
4. **Build complexity gradually.** Start with simpler versions of each problem type before introducing complexity.
5. **Automate basic skills** (times tables, number bonds, basic fraction operations) so they don't consume working memory during complex problems.
6. **Limit the number of new concepts per session.** Rosenshine recommends small steps with practice after each.
7. **Use worked examples** for new problem types, then gradually fade support (the worked-example effect).

## 8. Growth Mindset

### 8.1 What Dweck's Research Actually Says

Carol Dweck's research at Stanford University, spanning several decades, identified two implicit beliefs about intelligence:

- **Fixed mindset:** Intelligence is a fixed trait. You are either smart or you are not.
- **Growth mindset:** Intelligence can be developed through effort, good strategies, and input from others.

**The key study (Mueller & Dweck, 1998):** Fifth-grade children were given a set of problems. Afterwards, one group was praised for intelligence ("You must be smart at this") and another for effort ("You must have worked really hard"). When later given a choice between an easy task and a challenging one:
- **Intelligence-praised children chose the easy task** -- they wanted to maintain their "smart" label
- **Effort-praised children chose the challenging task** -- they wanted to learn

When subsequently given difficult problems:
- Intelligence-praised children showed declining enjoyment, persistence, and performance
- Effort-praised children maintained or increased their engagement

**Long-term effects (Gunderson et al., 2013):** Mothers' process praise to children at ages 1-3 predicted the child's growth mindset and desire for challenge five years later, at approximately age 8.

### 8.2 The Danger of False Growth Mindset

In a 2015 article for Education Week, Dweck herself raised serious concerns about how growth mindset was being misapplied. She identified "false growth mindset" as a major problem:

**Problem 1: Equating growth mindset with effort alone.**
"A growth mindset isn't just about effort. Students need to try new strategies and seek input from others when they're stuck. They need this repertoire of approaches -- not just effort -- to learn and improve."

**Problem 2: Praising effort that isn't producing learning.**
"Too often, praise is given to students who are putting forth effort but not learning, in order to make them feel good in the moment: 'Great effort! You tried your best!' This is not growth mindset. If a child is working hard but their strategy isn't working, telling them to 'keep trying' is not helpful -- they need to try something different."

**Problem 3: Using growth mindset as consolation rather than empowerment.**
Saying "you can do it if you try harder" to a child who lacks the necessary skills or strategies is not growth mindset -- it is empty encouragement that will ultimately undermine trust and motivation.

### 8.3 What Actually Works

The research supports a specific pattern of praise and feedback:

**Praise process tied to outcomes:**
- "You found a really good strategy for solving that" (names the process)
- "When you checked your work, you caught that mistake -- that's a useful habit" (process linked to outcome)
- "You tried three different approaches before you found one that worked. That persistence paid off." (effort + strategy + outcome)

**Not:**
- "You're so clever!" (fixed mindset praise)
- "Great effort!" (empty process praise disconnected from learning)
- "Don't worry, you'll get it eventually" (vague and unhelpful)

### 8.4 How to Respond When a Child Says "I Can't Do Maths"

This is a critical moment. The wrong response reinforces the fixed belief; the right response redirects toward growth.

**Wrong responses:**
- "Of course you can!" (dismisses their feeling, provides no strategy)
- "You're really good at maths!" (contradicts their experience, feels dishonest)
- "Just try harder" (implies the only problem is effort)

**Better responses:**
- "You can't do it *yet*. Let's figure out which part is tricky." (adds "yet," redirects to diagnosis)
- "Which bit specifically are you stuck on? Let's look at that part." (narrows the problem from "all maths" to a specific skill)
- "That's a hard topic. Let's try a different approach -- have you tried drawing a diagram?" (validates difficulty, offers strategy)
- "Remember when you found fractions hard? You can do those now. This will be the same -- hard at first, then it clicks." (uses their own past experience as evidence)

### 8.5 Growth Mindset Over 18 Months of Preparation

For sustained 11+ preparation, growth mindset principles suggest:

- **Track improvement over time**, not just current scores. Show the child their progress graph.
- **Normalise difficulty.** "This topic is supposed to be hard. That's how you know you're learning something new."
- **Celebrate strategy changes**, not just correct answers. "You tried estimation first this time -- that was a smart move."
- **Discuss mistakes productively.** Every wrong answer is diagnostic information, not a failure.
- **Model growth mindset as parents.** If you say "I was never good at maths," you are modelling fixed mindset. Better: "I found maths hard at school, but I've learned a lot since then."

### 8.6 The Replication Debate

It is important to note that growth mindset research has faced replication challenges. Some large-scale studies have found smaller effects than originally reported, and some interventions show no significant impact on academic outcomes. A 2018 meta-analysis by Sisk et al. found a weak overall relationship between mindset and academic achievement.

**What this means practically:** Growth mindset is not a magic bullet. It works best as part of a broader approach that includes effective study strategies, adequate challenge, and genuine skill development. Telling a child they can improve is only useful if you also give them the tools and practice to actually improve.

## 9. Desirable Difficulties

### 9.1 The Framework

Robert Bjork of UCLA introduced the term "desirable difficulty" in 1994 to describe learning conditions that appear to slow down learning in the short term but enhance long-term retention and transfer. The core insight is that **conditions that make performance easier do not necessarily make learning better, and conditions that make performance harder often make learning better.**

Bjork draws a critical distinction:
- **Performance** is what you can do right now, during practice
- **Learning** is the relatively permanent change in knowledge or skill that supports long-term retention and transfer

These two things are frequently dissociated. High performance during practice often correlates with poor long-term learning (as with blocked practice and cramming), while low performance during practice often correlates with superior long-term learning (as with interleaving and spacing).

### 9.2 The Four Core Desirable Difficulties

Bjork identified four primary desirable difficulties, all of which have been covered in previous sections:

1. **Spacing practice** (Section 2): Distributing practice over time rather than massing it
2. **Interleaving** (Section 3): Mixing different problem types rather than blocking
3. **Retrieval practice** (Section 1): Testing yourself rather than re-reading
4. **Generation** (Section 4): Producing answers rather than recognising them; generating explanations rather than reading them

**Evidence base:** Cepeda et al.'s (2006) meta-analysis found spacing produces 10-30% better retention. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed retrieval practice improves long-term recall by approximately 50% compared to restudying. Rohrer et al. (2015) showed interleaving nearly doubled maths test scores. Studies tracking retention curves show that after several weeks, effortful learning outperforms easy learning by margins exceeding 60%.

### 9.3 Varying the Conditions of Practice

An additional desirable difficulty identified by Bjork: varying the conditions under which practice occurs. This includes:

- Practising in different physical locations
- Varying the presentation format of problems
- Changing the context in which skills are applied

Research suggests that this variability, while making practice feel more difficult, creates more flexible and transferable knowledge.

**For 11+ prep:** Solving the same type of problem presented in different ways (word problem, diagram-based, numerical) builds more robust understanding than solving identical-format problems repeatedly.

### 9.4 When Difficulties Are NOT Desirable

A difficulty is only "desirable" if the learner has the foundational knowledge and skills to benefit from the challenge. Difficulties become undesirable when:

- The child lacks the prerequisite knowledge (trying to do algebra before understanding basic arithmetic)
- The difficulty is caused by confusing instructions rather than genuine cognitive challenge
- The task is so difficult that the child cannot make any progress, leading to learned helplessness
- The child is already overwhelmed or fatigued

**The 80% success rate guideline:** Rosenshine's research suggests that during guided practice, approximately 80% of questions should be answered correctly. This indicates appropriate challenge -- hard enough to require effort but not so hard as to be demoralising. During independent practice or testing, lower success rates are acceptable because the retrieval difficulty is productive.

### 9.5 Practical Implications for Parents

Parents need to understand that:

- If practice sessions feel easy and the child is getting everything right, **learning may not be happening.** The practice is too easy.
- If the child struggles, makes errors, and feels frustrated during interleaved practice, **this is often a sign that effective learning IS happening.**
- The solution to poor practice performance is not to make practice easier -- it is to provide better feedback and strategic support while maintaining the desirable difficulty.
- Short-term practice scores are unreliable indicators of long-term learning. Track performance on delayed tests (a week later) rather than during practice.

## 10. Sleep and Learning

### 10.1 Sleep-Dependent Memory Consolidation

Sleep is not merely rest -- it is an active process of memory consolidation. During sleep, the brain replays and reorganises recently learned information, strengthening neural connections and integrating new knowledge with existing memory networks.

**Wilhelm et al. (2020), Nature Scientific Reports:** This study found more efficient sleep-dependent declarative memory consolidation in children compared with adults. Children showed superior memory consolidation during sleep, an effect potentially attributed to more abundant and deeper slow-wave sleep during childhood. This means that sleep is, if anything, *more* important for children's learning than for adults'.

**Backhaus et al. (2008), "Sleep enhances memory consolidation in children":** After sleep, children showed significant improvements of 14% on a non-word learning task and 25% on the Tower of Hanoi task, but no significant change in score following a wake retention interval. The improvements occurred specifically during sleep, not merely during the passage of time.

**Key finding from systematic reviews (Emerging Topics in Life Sciences, 2023):** Sleep seems especially valuable for declarative (factual and conceptual) memory consolidation in school-aged children -- exactly the type of knowledge required for 11+ examination.

### 10.2 Sleep Duration Requirements

**NHS guidance and the American Academy of Sleep Medicine consensus statement:**
- Children aged 6-12 need 9-12 hours of sleep per night
- The optimal range for children aged 9-11 is 10-11 hours
- Sleeping fewer than 9 hours per night is associated with worse teacher-reported academic achievement

**The consequences of insufficient sleep for children:**
- Impaired attention and concentration
- Reduced working memory capacity (already limited in children -- see Section 7)
- Poor impulse control and behavioural regulation
- Decreased ability to manage frustration (critical during challenging practice)
- Impaired mood and increased irritability
- Reduced capacity for creative problem-solving

### 10.3 Why Cramming the Night Before Is Counterproductive

The research on cramming versus spaced study is unambiguous. Over 140 years of cognitive science research consistently demonstrates that cramming is one of the least effective ways to learn.

**Specific mechanisms by which cramming fails:**

1. **Disrupted memory consolidation.** Cramming typically involves late nights and reduced sleep. Since memory consolidation occurs during sleep, less sleep means less consolidation. The child literally cannot form durable memories of what they crammed.

2. **Inflated short-term performance.** Cramming does produce temporary retrieval strength -- material is available immediately after study. But this fades rapidly. Research shows the massed study group's score drops approximately 3.6 points between immediate and delayed tests, compared to only 1.1 points for the spaced study group.

3. **No schema formation.** Cramming fills working memory without building the organised knowledge structures (schemas) that enable transfer and flexible application. The child may remember a procedure temporarily but cannot adapt it to unfamiliar problem formats -- which is exactly what the 11+ exam demands.

**Dunlosky et al. (2013):** Both distributed practice and practice testing received the only "high utility" ratings. Cramming (massed practice) was rated low utility for long-term learning.

### 10.4 Practical Recommendations

**For 11+ preparation:**
- Establish a consistent sleep schedule: same bedtime and wake time, even at weekends
- No study or screen time in the 30-60 minutes before bed (blue light disrupts sleep onset)
- Brief review of the day's learning before the wind-down period (the brain will consolidate it during sleep)
- The night before the exam: light review only, early bedtime. The exam preparation happened over 18 months, not the night before.
- If the child must miss a study session, it is better to skip the session than to extend it late into the evening at the cost of sleep

## 11. Exercise and Cognition

### 11.1 The Research Evidence

A substantial body of research demonstrates positive associations between physical activity, cognitive function, and academic achievement in children.

**Donnelly et al. (2016), "Physical Activity, Fitness, Cognitive Function, and Academic Achievement in Children: A Systematic Review" (Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise):** This systematic review found that higher-fit children demonstrate greater attention, faster information processing, and higher scores on standardised achievement tests. Importantly, the literature shows no indication that increases in physical activity negatively affect cognition or academic achievement -- the concern that "time spent exercising is time not studying" is not supported by evidence.

**Hillman et al. (2009), "Exercise and Children's Intelligence, Cognition, and Academic Achievement" (PMC):** Following aerobic exercise training, children's performance improved exclusively on tests involving executive function -- the higher-order cognitive skills (planning, working memory, inhibitory control) that are directly relevant to 11+ exam performance.

**Alvarez-Bueno et al. (2017), meta-analysis (Frontiers in Public Health):** This meta-analysis confirmed that children's physical activity is positively associated with both academic performance and cognitive functioning, with the strongest effects on executive function.

### 11.2 Acute Exercise Effects

Perhaps most relevant for 11+ preparation is the research on acute exercise -- the cognitive boost from a single bout of physical activity.

**Key finding:** A single bout of structured physical activity lasting at least 20 minutes improves concentration, reading and mathematics performance, and inhibitory control in children. Both typically developing children and children with ADHD show enhancements in inhibitory control and attentional resource allocation following 20 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise.

**Even shorter durations may be effective.** Recent research (2025) suggests that just 9 minutes of high-intensity interval exercise before testing can boost verbal comprehension scores in children aged 9-12. However, the strongest evidence base is for 20+ minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise.

**Timing matters:** The cognitive benefits of acute exercise appear approximately 10-20 minutes after exercise concludes. Vigorous exercise immediately before a cognitive task may temporarily impair performance due to physiological arousal; a brief cool-down period is optimal.

### 11.3 Practical Recommendations

**Before study sessions:**
- 15-20 minutes of moderate physical activity (brisk walk, cycling, active play) before sitting down to study
- Allow 10 minutes of cool-down time between exercise and study
- Even a brief movement break (5 minutes of jumping jacks, running up and down stairs) can provide some benefit

**Before the exam:**
- A 20-minute walk or active play on the morning of the exam
- Avoid high-intensity exercise that might cause fatigue
- Allow time to cool down and settle before the exam begins

**During sustained study periods:**
- Active breaks between study blocks (see Section 12)
- Movement-based break activities: stretching, dancing, garden play
- Avoid purely sedentary break activities (switching from study screen to entertainment screen is not a real break)

**General fitness:**
- Regular physical activity throughout the preparation period supports both cognitive development and stress management
- Team sports or active hobbies provide additional benefits through social interaction (relatedness -- see Section 13)
- Physical activity also improves sleep quality (see Section 10)

## 12. Attention and Focus

### 12.1 Attention Span in 9-11 Year Olds

The commonly cited guideline is that a child's sustained attention span is approximately 2-5 minutes per year of age. For a 10-year-old, this translates to approximately 20-50 minutes, though this varies enormously depending on:

- The child's interest in the task
- The difficulty level (too easy = boredom; too hard = disengagement)
- Environmental distractions
- Time of day
- Fatigue, hunger, and sleep quality
- Whether the task is active (requiring responses) or passive (reading/listening)

**Research-based estimates:** For school-age children aged 9-11, sustained focused attention on a moderately challenging academic task typically lasts 20-30 minutes before requiring refocusing. Highly engaging tasks may sustain attention longer; boring or frustrating tasks may lose attention much sooner.

### 12.2 Optimal Study Session Length

Research on rest breaks and learning (Tandfonline, 2023) confirms that when students learn or solve problems, attentional resources are depleted. Rest breaks restore cognitive functioning in support of learning.

**Evidence-based recommendations for 9-11 year olds:**

| Age | Focused work block | Break length | Total session |
|-----|-------------------|--------------|---------------|
| 9-10 | 15-20 minutes | 5 minutes | 2-3 blocks |
| 10-11 | 20-25 minutes | 5 minutes | 2-3 blocks |
| Before exam (exam stamina) | Build to 30+ minutes | 5 minutes | Gradual extension |

**The rule of thumb for younger learners:** Focus time in minutes = child's age + 1. For a 10-year-old, this is 11 minutes of deep focus, though this is conservative for academic tasks that include varied activities.

### 12.3 The Pomodoro Technique Adapted for Children

The Pomodoro Technique (developed by Francesco Cirillo) involves timed blocks of focused work followed by short breaks. The standard adult version uses 25-minute blocks, but this needs adaptation for children:

**Adapted Pomodoro for 9-11 year olds:**
- **Work block:** 15-20 minutes of focused study
- **Short break:** 3-5 minutes (physical movement preferred)
- **Repeat:** 3-4 blocks
- **Long break:** 15-30 minutes after 3-4 blocks

**Break activities should be:**
- Physical (stretching, walking, brief active play)
- Brief and bounded (a timer helps the child return to work)
- Screen-free (switching to a different screen is not restorative)
- Simple (not cognitively demanding -- the point is to rest the brain)

**Break activities to avoid:**
- Video games or YouTube (too engaging; difficult to stop)
- Social media or messaging (for older children)
- Starting a different complex activity

### 12.4 The Role of Variety Within Sessions

Attention is not a single resource that depletes uniformly. Switching between different types of activity can refresh attention even without a formal break:

- Switch between topic types (interleaving naturally provides variety)
- Alternate between problem-solving and explanation-reading
- Mix question formats (multiple choice, then diagram interpretation, then word problems)
- Include brief micro-lessons between quiz blocks (as the app currently does)

**This is another reason interleaving supports learning:** Beyond its memory benefits (Section 3), topic-switching provides natural attentional variety that helps maintain engagement.

### 12.5 Environmental Factors

Research on children's attention also highlights environmental factors:

- **Quiet environment:** Background noise (especially speech) significantly impairs children's working memory performance more than adults'
- **Minimal visual distractions:** A cluttered study space competes for attentional resources
- **Consistent study location:** Helps establish a "study mode" through environmental cueing
- **Good lighting and comfortable temperature:** Basic but important
- **Digital distractions removed:** Phones and tablets not being used for study should be in another room

## 13. Motivation Science

### 13.1 Self-Determination Theory

Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan, is the most comprehensive framework for understanding human motivation. It posits that motivation is not a single quantity but exists on a continuum, and that three innate psychological needs drive self-motivated behaviour:

**Autonomy:** The need to feel ownership of one's behaviour. Not the absence of structure, but the presence of choice and volition.

**Competence:** The need to feel effective and capable. The experience of mastery and growth.

**Relatedness:** The need to feel connected to others. Belonging, warmth, and mutual care.

When these three needs are satisfied, intrinsic motivation flourishes. When they are thwarted, motivation declines regardless of external rewards or pressure.

**Deci & Ryan (2000), "Self-Determination Theory and the Facilitation of Intrinsic Motivation, Social Development, and Well-Being" (American Psychologist, 55, 68-78):** This foundational paper established that autonomy-supportive environments consistently produce greater intrinsic motivation, better performance, and improved wellbeing compared to controlling environments.

### 13.2 Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation

**Intrinsic motivation:** The child studies because they find the material interesting, enjoy the challenge, or want to understand. This is the most durable and effective form of motivation.

**Extrinsic motivation:** The child studies because of external rewards (treats, screen time), punishments (losing privileges), or pressure (parental expectations). This can be effective short-term but tends to undermine intrinsic motivation and is difficult to sustain over 18 months.

**The overjustification effect (Lepper, Greene, & Nisbett, 1973):** When children who are already intrinsically motivated to do an activity are given external rewards for it, their intrinsic motivation decreases. The reward becomes the reason for the activity, and without the reward, motivation drops. This is a genuine risk with 11+ prep: if every practice session is followed by a reward, the child learns to study *for the reward*, not for the learning.

**The continuum of extrinsic motivation:** SDT identifies several types of extrinsic motivation, ranging from external regulation (studying to avoid punishment) through introjected regulation (studying to avoid guilt), identified regulation (studying because "I know it's important"), to integrated regulation (studying because "becoming good at maths is part of who I want to be"). The more internalised forms of extrinsic motivation are nearly as effective as intrinsic motivation.

### 13.3 Supporting Autonomy in 11+ Preparation

Autonomy does not mean letting the child choose whether to study. It means providing meaningful choice within the structure of preparation:

**Practical autonomy-supportive strategies:**
- Let the child choose which topic to practise (from a shortlist of 2-3 appropriate options)
- Let the child choose the order of activities within a session
- Let the child set their own target for the session ("How many questions do you want to try today?")
- Provide rationale: explain *why* each activity is useful, not just *what* to do
- Acknowledge the child's feelings: "I know this is boring, but here's why it matters"
- Minimise controlling language: "Let's try..." rather than "You need to..." or "You must..."

### 13.4 Building Competence

Competence is built through the experience of challenge met with success. This requires careful calibration:

- **Too easy:** The child is bored. No competence growth.
- **Too hard:** The child fails repeatedly. Competence is threatened.
- **Just right (Zone of Proximal Development):** The child succeeds with effort. Competence grows.

**Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD):** Learning occurs most effectively in the zone between what a child can do independently and what they cannot do even with help. Within this zone, appropriate scaffolding (hints, worked examples, guided practice) enables the child to succeed at tasks they could not manage alone, gradually building independence.

**Rosenshine's 80% success rate:** During guided practice, approximately 80% correct answers indicates the right level of challenge -- hard enough to require effort, easy enough to experience success regularly.

**Progress visibility:** Children need to see their own improvement. Progress charts, before/after comparisons, and celebrating milestones all build the sense of competence. This is not empty praise (Section 8) -- it is evidence-based recognition of genuine growth.

### 13.5 Supporting Relatedness

For 11+ prep, relatedness might include:

- Studying with a friend who is also preparing (peer learning, shared experience)
- Parents showing interest in what the child is learning (not just scores, but content)
- Connecting with other families going through the same process
- The AI tutor providing warmth and encouragement alongside instruction
- Framing preparation as a family project, not the child's burden alone

### 13.6 Maintaining Motivation Over 12-18 Months

Sustaining motivation over the full preparation period is one of the greatest challenges. Research suggests:

**Long-term goal structures:**
- Break the 18-month journey into phases with distinct goals
- Celebrate phase completions, not just the final exam
- Use mastery-based milestones ("You can now do all fraction types") rather than only performance-based ones ("You scored 90%")

**Preventing burnout:**
- Vary the type and format of practice to prevent monotony
- Include rest days -- preparation every single day for 18 months is not sustainable
- Adjust intensity based on the child's energy and mood
- If motivation drops significantly, reduce volume temporarily rather than pushing through

**Students with a long future time perspective** -- those who can set goals far into the future -- are more persistent and find more satisfaction in goal-oriented behaviours. Help the child connect current study to their future goal: not just "passing the 11+" but what comes after (the school, the opportunities, the subjects they'll enjoy).

**The autonomy principle again:** Students are more deeply engaged when they have a say in selecting topics, setting learning goals, and determining how to achieve them.

## 14. Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction

### 14.1 Overview

Barak Rosenshine's 2012 paper "Principles of Instruction: Research-Based Strategies That All Teachers Should Know" (American Educator) synthesised three bodies of research -- cognitive science, studies of master teachers, and research on cognitive supports -- into 10 principles that have become a cornerstone of evidence-based education in the UK.

### 14.2 The 10 Principles and Their Application to 11+ Prep

**1. Daily review (5-8 minutes)**
Begin each study session with a brief review of previously learned material. This activates prior knowledge, strengthens recall, and identifies gaps.
*11+ application:* Start every session with 5 quick questions from previous topics before introducing new material.

**2. Present new material in small steps**
Working memory limitations (Section 7) mean children can only process a small amount of new information at a time. Present one step, allow practice, then the next step.
*11+ application:* The micro-lesson format (5 screens, 1-2 minutes) embodies this principle.

**3. Ask a large number of questions**
Questions help students practise new information and connect it to prior learning. They also give the teacher/parent continuous feedback on understanding.
*11+ application:* The quiz-based format naturally provides this. Supplement with verbal questioning during study sessions.

**4. Provide models and worked examples**
Show the child how to solve a problem step by step before asking them to solve similar problems independently. This reduces cognitive load during initial learning.
*11+ application:* Micro-lessons teach the method; questions then apply it.

**5. Guide student practice**
After modelling, supervise initial practice. Provide hints and corrections. Don't leave the child to struggle alone with new material.
*11+ application:* Parents or the AI tutor can provide scaffolded support during initial encounters with new topic types.

**6. Check for understanding**
Frequently verify that the child actually understands, rather than assuming. Use questions, not "Do you understand?" (Section 6.4).
*11+ application:* The app's quiz mode with immediate feedback serves this function.

**7. Obtain a high success rate**
Aim for approximately 80% correct during guided practice. This indicates appropriate challenge.
*11+ application:* If a child is consistently below 60% on a topic, they need more teaching before more practice.

**8. Provide scaffolds for difficult tasks**
Hints, checklists, partially worked examples, and graphic organisers reduce cognitive load for challenging material.
*11+ application:* Explanations after wrong answers, hint systems, and step-by-step breakdowns.

**9. Independent practice**
After guided practice, the child practises independently until the skill becomes automatic. This is where spaced repetition and interleaving become essential.
*11+ application:* Regular self-directed practice sessions using the app.

**10. Weekly and monthly review**
Regular cumulative review prevents forgetting and strengthens long-term retention.
*11+ application:* Weekly mixed-topic quizzes; monthly progress assessments covering all topics.

### 14.3 Key Citation

- Rosenshine, B. (2012). Principles of instruction: Research-based strategies that all teachers should know. *American Educator*, 36(1), 12-19, 39.

## 15. Synthesis: Implications for 11+ Preparation

### 15.1 The Evidence-Based Study Session

Combining all 13 domains of research, an optimal study session for a 9-11 year old preparing for the 11+ looks like this:

**Before the session:**
- 15-20 minutes of physical activity (Section 11)
- 10 minutes to cool down and settle
- Brief planning: "What are we working on today? What do you already know about this?" (Section 6)

**Session structure (40-60 minutes total):**

*Block 1 (5 minutes): Daily review*
- 5 quick questions from previously studied topics (Rosenshine Principle 1; retrieval practice, Section 1)
- Mix topics (interleaving, Section 3)

*Block 2 (15-20 minutes): New learning or focused practice*
- If new topic: micro-lesson with worked examples, then guided practice (Rosenshine Principles 2-5)
- If revision: quiz on target topic with immediate feedback
- Include visual representations alongside text (dual coding, Section 5)
- Keep explanations simple and step-by-step (cognitive load, Section 7)

*Break (5 minutes): Physical movement*

*Block 3 (15-20 minutes): Mixed practice*
- Interleaved questions from 3-4 different topics (interleaving, Section 3)
- Include some questions from the new topic just studied
- Include questions from topics studied days/weeks ago (spaced repetition, Section 2)

*Wrap-up (5 minutes): Metacognitive reflection*
- "What did you find hardest today? What did you learn?" (Section 6)
- "How confident are you with [topic]? Rate 1-5."
- Brief preview of what's coming next session

**After the session:**
- No more study -- protect sleep (Section 10)
- The brain will consolidate during sleep

### 15.2 The Evidence-Based Weekly Schedule

| Day | Focus | Duration |
|-----|-------|----------|
| Monday | New topic + mixed review | 40-50 min |
| Tuesday | Practice on Monday's topic + mixed review | 30-40 min |
| Wednesday | Rest day or light review only | 0-15 min |
| Thursday | New topic + mixed review | 40-50 min |
| Friday | Practice on Thursday's topic + mixed review | 30-40 min |
| Saturday | Mixed practice across all topics (interleaved) | 30-40 min |
| Sunday | Rest day | 0 min |

Total: approximately 3-4 hours per week, which research suggests is more effective than 7+ hours of unfocused study. Quality and strategy matter far more than raw hours.

### 15.3 The Evidence-Based 18-Month Plan

**Months 1-3: Foundation (September-November)**
- Introduce topics systematically
- Focus on building basic fluency (times tables, number bonds, key vocabulary)
- Establish the study routine and habits
- Light, regular sessions (20-30 minutes, 3-4 times per week)
- Build positive associations with learning (autonomy, competence -- Section 13)

**Months 4-9: Expansion (December-May)**
- All topics introduced
- Increasing difficulty levels
- Regular interleaved practice
- Spaced repetition targeting weak areas
- Build to 30-40 minute sessions, 4-5 times per week
- Monthly progress checks to identify weak areas

**Months 10-15: Consolidation (June-November)**
- Deep practice on weak areas (increased spacing frequency for difficult topics)
- Regular full mixed-topic practice
- Timed practice begins (building exam stamina and time management)
- Maintain 4-5 sessions per week
- Metacognitive focus: self-checking, time awareness, question diagnosis

**Months 16-18: Exam Preparation (December-February, before September exam)**
- Weekly timed practice papers
- Focus on exam technique and time management
- Light new learning; mostly review and consolidation
- Gradually reduce intensity in final 2 weeks to prevent burnout
- Protect sleep, maintain exercise, manage anxiety
- Night before exam: light review only, early bedtime

### 15.4 Common Mistakes to Avoid (Evidence-Based)

1. **Cramming or intensive sessions close to the exam.** Evidence overwhelmingly favours distributed practice over massed practice (Section 2).

2. **Blocked practice ("Let's do a whole week on fractions").** Interleaving is consistently superior for long-term learning and transfer (Section 3).

3. **Re-reading or highlighting as primary study methods.** Dunlosky et al. (2013) rated these as "low utility." Retrieval practice and spaced repetition are far more effective (Sections 1, 2).

4. **Praising intelligence ("You're so clever!").** Praise process and strategy instead, tied to outcomes (Section 8).

5. **Interpreting struggle as failure.** Desirable difficulties are a sign of effective learning, not ineffective teaching (Section 9).

6. **Sacrificing sleep for extra study.** Sleep is when memory consolidation occurs. Less sleep means less learning, regardless of study hours (Section 10).

7. **All study, no exercise.** Physical activity enhances cognitive function. Time spent exercising is not time wasted -- it directly improves learning capacity (Section 11).

8. **Sessions that are too long.** 40-50 focused minutes beats 2 hours of unfocused study for a 10-year-old. Respect attention limitations (Section 12).

9. **Purely external motivation (rewards, threats).** Build intrinsic motivation through autonomy, competence, and relatedness. External rewards can undermine intrinsic interest (Section 13).

10. **Ignoring metacognition.** Teaching a child to monitor and regulate their own learning has one of the highest effect sizes of any educational intervention (+8 months, EEF) (Section 6).
