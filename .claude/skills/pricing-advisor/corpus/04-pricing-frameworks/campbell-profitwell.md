## Who / what

Patrick Campbell founded ProfitWell (acquired by Paddle for $200 million) after a stint at the National Security Agency doing data analysis. ProfitWell powered subscription financial metrics for roughly 20% of the global subscription market, from early-stage startups to Fortune 50 companies. That data position gave Campbell access to pricing, churn, and expansion data at a scale almost no individual researcher has matched. His writing and talks — on the Acquired podcast, at Business of Software, and via the ProfitWell blog — form one of the most cited bodies of empirical pricing knowledge for SaaS and subscription founders. He is known specifically for making pricing a rigorous, data-driven discipline rather than a gut-feel exercise.

## Core ideas (their key beliefs / frameworks)

- **The value metric is the single most important pricing decision**: a value metric is "how a customer pays you, and what they pay for." Getting it right matters more than the exact price. Companies using a genuine value metric (one that scales with the customer's benefit) grow at roughly double the rate with half the churn compared to flat-fee or per-seat models.
- **Four criteria for a strong value metric**:
  1. Closely aligned with where the customer perceives value.
  2. Easy to understand without a sales conversation.
  3. Scales naturally as the customer derives more value (expansion revenue).
  4. Passes the "gut-check": can the customer control it, and will they want it to increase even when it costs them more?
- **Per-user pricing is usually wrong**: tracking seats creates accountability problems. Shared logins undermine the metric's integrity; the metric punishes collaboration rather than rewarding value.
- **Price localisation is an underused growth lever**: if more than 15% of your customer base is outside your home region, you should localise pricing — first cosmetically (currency symbol), then substantively (purchasing-power-adjusted prices). Campbell's data shows that doing this correctly yields 25–50% higher growth rates.
- **Raise prices every year**: most companies raise prices only every three years. Campbell recommends annual increases as long as the NPS score does not exceed 20, on the basis that continuous feature additions justify continuous price adjustments.
- **Discounts are a weapon, not a default**: default position should be no discounts. When used, apply them to specific segments with strict scope and time limits, and vary the offer to prevent predictability. Discounts above 20% correlate with higher long-term churn.
- **Pricing should be reviewed every quarter**: the most successful subscription companies run at least one pricing optimisation initiative per quarter — localisation, packaging, value metric refinement, or segmentation.
- **Cross-functional pricing round table**: no single function (not marketing, not product) should own pricing alone. A pricing committee drawing on sales, product, marketing, and finance produces more durable decisions.

## Method / how to apply

**The Four-Step Pricing Process (Campbell's framework)**

1. **Acknowledge the problem and commit to a systematic approach** — most companies treat pricing as a set-and-forget decision. The first step is recognising it as an ongoing strategic discipline.

2. **Align cross-functionally** — convene a pricing committee. Each function contributes: Sales brings objections and deal feedback; Product maps features to value; Marketing articulates positioning; Finance models unit economics.

3. **Apply scientific methodology**
   - Define candidate value metrics (list 5–10 proxies for core value delivery).
   - Validate with target customers through surveys or interviews.
   - Use quantitative tools: Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter (four-question survey to find the acceptable range) and MaxDiff analysis (forces respondents to rank features by importance, revealing what they actually value versus what they say they value).
   - Test packaging hypotheses with a small cohort before rolling out.

4. **Revisit at minimum every six months** — particularly when features change, a new customer segment emerges, or competitive moves shift market expectations.

**Stage-specific guidance**
- *Early-stage*: prioritise finding the right value metric above all else. Price is secondary.
- *Growth-stage*: ensure the metric drives expansion as customers grow.
- *Series A/B*: validate that different personas have meaningfully different WTP; prune segments with poor unit economics.

**Price localisation steps**
1. Identify the percentage of revenue outside your home region.
2. If >15%, start with cosmetic localisation (correct currency, local symbol).
3. Progress to relative-price localisation using purchasing-power-parity indices.
4. Test with a geographic cohort; measure conversion lift.

## Applicability to a UK 11+ app at pre-launch / early-launch stage

Campbell would push a 11+ app founder to answer one question before anything else: what is the value metric? For a children's exam prep app, the honest candidates are "number of children on the account," "number of practice sessions completed," or a flat "family subscription." He would likely argue flat family subscription is the cleanest — parents don't want a per-child meter for an app, it penalises siblings, and a per-session model creates anxiety about usage. On price localisation, his data is directly relevant: even within the UK there are postcode-level income differences that affect WTP significantly; a London price tested against a northern market may over-price. The annual raise rule is directly applicable: launch at a confident price, then commit to a modest annual increase once you have NPS data.
