## The anchoring source of truth — verbatim GL instruction

The official GL instruction text for this type, reproduced consistently
across all six sources (web research 26 Jun 2026):

> **"In these sentences, the word in capitals has had three letters next to
> each other taken out. These three letters will make one correctly-spelt
> word without changing their order. The sentence that you make must make
> sense. Mark the correct three-letter word on the answer sheet."**

Everything below follows from this instruction. Three facts are load-bearing
and define the entire type:

1. The mutilated word appears **in FULL CAPITALS, embedded in a complete
   sentence**.
2. There are **NO gap markers** — no underscores, no brackets, no dashes.
   The position of the removed three letters is **entirely hidden**; the
   child must work out both the intended word **and** where the three
   letters slot back in.
3. The three removed letters, in their original order, must themselves
   **form a correctly-spelt three-letter word**, and the rebuilt sentence
   must **make sense**.

**Verbatim authentic GL examples (all identical format — web research
26 Jun 2026):**

| Sentence (word in capitals is mutilated) | Removed word | Rebuilt word |
|---|---|---|
| "The children GATED in the hall." | HER | gat**HER**ed (GATHERED) |
| "The cat scratched him with his CS." | LAW | c**LAW**s (CLAWS) |
| "Organic CARS are full of vitamins." | ROT | ca**ROT**s (CARROTS) |
| "The cake was BAG in the oven." | KIN | ba**KIN**g (BAKING) |
| "She said EWELL before she boarded the plane." | FAR | **FAR**ewell (FAREWELL) |

Note that in several of these the mutilated remainder is itself a real word
("GATED", "CARS", "BAG") — this is a deliberate part of the difficulty:
the child cannot find the target by hunting for a word that "looks broken."

## Format

### Question Type Classification
- **GL Type 7**: "Find the Missing Three-Letter Word"
  (`GL-11plus-English-VR-Research.md` l.160; `11plus-oracle.md` l.481).
- A **Word Manipulation** verbal-reasoning type, clustered with Types 4–8
  (Compound Words, Missing Letter, Move a Letter, Create Words Same Way)
  (`GL-11plus-English-VR-Research.md` l.154–161).
- Rated **Medium difficulty, High vocab demand** in the GL type catalogue
  (`GL-11plus-English-VR-Research.md` l.160) — it is a vocabulary +
  comprehension type wearing a letter-puzzle costume: the child must
  recover the whole word from sentence meaning, then dissect it.
- Ranks **7th by frequency** across the 21 GL VR types — a regular but not
  high-volume type (`GL-11plus-English-VR-Research.md` l.230).

### The defining mechanic
**Three consecutive letters are removed from a longer word that sits inside
a sentence and is printed in capitals. The three removed letters must
themselves form a valid three-letter word, AND the rebuilt sentence must
make sense.** The child picks the removed three-letter word
(`11plus-oracle.md` l.483–484; instruction text above). This produces the
**two non-negotiable validity checks** that define the type:

1. **The completed long word is a real word that fits the sentence.**
2. **The three removed letters form a real word on their own.**

(`11plus-oracle.md` l.502 "stolen word not a real word, or completed word
not real"; l.510–511 "Check BOTH: the complete word is real AND the three
letters form a real word"; GL instruction text "must make sense" + "make one
correctly-spelt word".)

> **Critical: this is NOT the single-missing-letter type.** A single missing
> letter that completes word fragments (`WOR(?)OOL`, `PAN(?)IGHT`) is **GL
> Type 5 — "Find the Missing Letter" / Shared Letter** (catalogued at
> `GL-11plus-English-VR-Research.md` l.158 and spec'd at `11plus-oracle.md`
> l.690–717). The library's "Missing Letters" strategy section describes the
> *single-letter* mechanic (`vr-english-strategies.md` §9, l.305–325) — it
> belongs to Type 5, which we build as our separate **Shared Letter** topic.
> `11plus-oracle.md` l.716–717 makes the distinction explicit: "Shared
> Letter = 1 letter, 2 word pairs, 4 words created. Missing Letters = 3
> consecutive letters stolen from one word." **Our old Template B ("Which
> single letter completes this word? B_AUTY → E") is therefore not GL Type 7
> and not authentic to this topic — see Design Rules below.**

## How It Appears on Paper

There is **one** authentic GL presentation (web research 26 Jun 2026): the
**sentence-embedded, capitalised, position-hidden** form. The earlier
"Mode A / Mode B" split is **withdrawn** — the isolated/position-shown
`GA _ _ _ TED` rendering is a publisher (Atom Learning) UI adaptation, not a
GL paper, and our live bank's reliance on it is the central fault the
regeneration must fix.

**The authentic on-paper item:**

> A complete sentence containing **one word in full capitals** from which
> three consecutive letters have been removed, leaving **no gap marker**.
> The child reads for sense, reconstructs the intended word, identifies the
> three removed letters, and confirms those letters form a real word.
>
> *e.g.* "The children GATED in the hall." → the intended word is GATHERED →
> the removed letters are **HER** (gat**HER**ed), and HER is a real word.

Why this is genuinely demanding (`11plus-oracle.md` l.506 "word completes
but doesn't make sense in context"; l.509 strategy; GL instruction "must
make sense"): the gap is **unmarked**, so the child cannot read off the
bordering letters and work a narrow word family. They must first recover the
whole word from **meaning**, then locate the buried three letters — exactly
the comprehension load that earns Type 7 its "High vocab demand" rating
(`GL-11plus-English-VR-Research.md` l.160).

## Authoritative MC presentation (what our app implements)

Bournemouth/Dorset GL papers are multiple-choice, electronically marked
(`hidden-words.md` l.27–34; GL instruction "Mark the correct three-letter
word on the answer sheet"). Type 7 is presented as **5 options (A–E), each a
candidate three-letter word**, 0-indexed correct answer.

**Authentic stem and options:**

> "Three letters next to each other have been taken out of the word in
> capitals. These three letters make a word. Find that word so the sentence
> makes sense: *'The children GATED in the hall.'*"
>
> Options: five three-letter words (A–E). Correct: the one word that, slotted
> back into the capitalised word in its original order, rebuilds a real word
> that makes the sentence make sense — **and** is itself a real word.

The MC mechanic (options are three-letter *words*, not letter strings) is
confirmed by the GL instruction ("Mark the correct three-letter word") and
matches the universal 5-option electronically-marked GL VR format
(`hidden-words.md` l.27–34; `11plus-oracle.md` l.484 "5 options (A-E)").

## Difficulty Calibration (30 / 40 / 30)

We follow the standard GL split used across the bank (`hidden-words.md`
l.86–90). Because there is now a **single format**, difficulty no longer
comes from "which mode." It comes from **sentence and word factors**. The
master reference's D1/D2/D3 word-level criteria (`11plus-oracle.md`
l.495–499) still apply to the buried word; we layer the sentence-transparency
factors on top, following the Hidden Words difficulty logic (`hidden-words.md`
l.58–59).

The five difficulty levers (combine them to set a band):
- **Word frequency** of the intended whole word (common → rare).
- **Sentence transparency** — how strongly context forces a single word
  (short, concrete, only-one-word-fits → longer, looser, several plausible).
- **Whether the mutilated remainder is itself a real word** (the
  "GATED"/"CARS"/"BAG" trick) — when it is, the child can't spot the target
  by hunting for a broken-looking word (`11plus-oracle.md` l.490; GL examples
  above).
- **Length and buried position** of the removed letters (short common word at
  the start/end → longer or mid-word buried deep).
- **Distractor pressure** — how many of the five options pass exactly one of
  the two checks.

**D1 (~30%).** High-frequency intended word (4–6 letters of host word);
transparent, concrete sentence where only one word realistically fits; the
mutilated remainder looks obviously incomplete (not itself a word); very
common removed word (CAR, PET, MAN, AND, HER, AGE); little distractor
pressure — only one option is even close (`11plus-oracle.md` l.495).
*e.g.* "The dog buried its BO in the garden." → ONE → b**ONE** (BONE).

**D2 (~40%).** Mid-frequency intended word (6–8 letter host); the sentence
still makes the word recoverable on the first careful read but is a touch
longer (8–12 words); **one** near-miss distractor that satisfies only one
of the two checks; the removed word is less obvious (`11plus-oracle.md`
l.497; `hidden-words.md` l.58 "medium sentence, one near-miss").
*e.g.* "She kept her savings in a wooden CHT by her bed." → ES → ch**ES**t
(CHEST).

**D3 (~30%).** Lower-frequency intended word or tricky spelling (silent
letters, doubles); longer/looser sentence (12–16 words); the **mutilated
remainder is itself a real word** so "the remainder looks broken" fails;
multiple options pass exactly one check; the buried letters sit mid-word away
from the sentence ends (`11plus-oracle.md` l.499; l.490; `hidden-words.md`
l.59 "longer sentence, buried position, multiple red herrings").
*e.g.* "The old soldier stood guard on the castle PET through the night." →
RAP → pa**RAP**et... (host PARAPET; tune the sentence so PARAPET is forced).

## Common GL Traps
1. **Passes one check, fails the other** — the removed letters form a word
   but the rebuilt long word isn't real, or the rebuilt word is real but the
   removed letters aren't a word. **This is the signature Type 7 trap and the
   spine of distractor design** (`11plus-oracle.md` l.501–502).
2. **Completes a real word but doesn't fit the sentence** — a three-letter
   word that rebuilds *a* real word, but not one that makes the sentence make
   sense (`11plus-oracle.md` l.506; GL instruction "must make sense"). This is
   intrinsic to the sentence-embedded format and is why the type carries High
   vocab/comprehension load.
3. **Similar-looking three-letter options** — APT vs ART vs ACT vs ANT vs
   APE; only one passes both checks and fits the sentence (`11plus-oracle.md`
   l.503).
4. **Mutilated remainder is itself a real word** — "GATED" is a word, "CARS"
   is a word, "BAG" is a word, so the child can't locate the target by
   looking for a broken-looking word (`11plus-oracle.md` l.490; GL examples
   26 Jun 2026).
5. **Silent / unusual letters in the completed word** — e.g. K in KNOWLEDGE,
   W in WRONG — the right answer won't "sound" right
   (`11plus-oracle.md` l.503; l.504).
6. **Hidden position** — because no gap is marked, a child who assumes the
   letters were removed from the start/end can miss a mid-word removal
   (`11plus-oracle.md` l.504; web research 26 Jun 2026 "position entirely
   hidden").

## Our App's Design Rules (regeneration)

The live bank (125 items, `src/questionData/vrData.js` from l.8813) is built
**entirely in the non-authentic isolated/position-shown form** (Template A,
e.g. id 1 "Which three-letter word completes this longer word? `CH _ _ _ ER`")
**plus** a single-letter Template B that is the wrong GL type altogether.
**Both templates are non-authentic and the whole bank must be regenerated.**

- The **isolated/underscore form** (`CH _ _ _ ER`, `_ _ _ PET`) is a
  publisher (Atom Learning) UI adaptation, **not a GL paper** (web research
  26 Jun 2026). **Drop every isolated/position-shown item.**
- **Single-letter / spelling-recall items** ("Which single letter completes
  `B _ AUTY`? → E"; "`NECE _ SARY` → S") are GL Type 5 / Shared Letter at
  best, and as written are bare spelling recall (`11plus-oracle.md`
  l.690–717, l.716–717). **Drop every single-letter item.**

Regeneration rules:

1. **Single authentic format only — sentence-embedded, capitalised,
   position-hidden.** Every item is a complete sentence containing **one word
   in FULL CAPITALS** with three consecutive letters removed and **NO gap
   marker** (no underscores, brackets or dashes) (web research 26 Jun 2026).
2. **App stem wording (authentic, GL-faithful):**
   *"Three letters next to each other have been taken out of the word in
   capitals. These three letters make a word. Find that word so the sentence
   makes sense: '<sentence with one CAPITALISED mutilated word>'"*.
3. **Two non-negotiable validity checks per item** — the rebuilt long word is
   real **and** the three removed letters form a real word
   (`11plus-oracle.md` l.510–511; GL instruction). **Machine-verify both**
   against the dictionary, as Hidden Words was verified (`hidden-words.md`
   l.86–90).
4. **The rebuilt sentence must make sense** with the intended word — and the
   sentence must give **no synonym/definition giveaway** of either the
   three-letter answer or the host word (decoy discipline carried from Hidden
   Words, `hidden-words.md` l.74–82; GL instruction "must make sense").
5. **Distractors must satisfy exactly ONE of the two checks** — the
   documented Type 7 trap (`11plus-oracle.md` l.501–502). Across each item's
   five options include at least one whose three letters form a word but
   whose insertion makes a non-word or a nonsense sentence, and at least one
   whose insertion makes a real word but whose three letters are not a word.
   Random wrong answers are not acceptable.
6. **Exactly one correct option** that passes both checks and fits the
   sentence; **5 options (A–E)**, 0-indexed `correct`; explanation ends with
   ✓.
7. **Difficulty from sentence/word factors, held at 30/40/30** — use the five
   levers in the Calibration section (word frequency, sentence transparency,
   whether the remainder is itself a word, length/buried position, distractor
   pressure). Hold the overall split at 30/40/30 (`hidden-words.md` l.86).
8. **Recommended volume ~150 items** at 30/40/30, replacing the current 125.
   **[REASONED — not directly cited]** Basis: brings this bank into line with
   Hidden Words' regenerated volume and gives enough D2/D3 spread to exercise
   the trap families without repetition; adjust after field testing.
9. **No repeated example words or sentences across the batch** — vary the
   host words, the removed three-letter answers, and the sentence frames; do
   not lean on GATHERED/CARPET/CHAPTER every item. (See generation rule:
   never reuse the same example words across a batch.)

## Strategy (for micro-lessons)

From `11plus-oracle.md` l.508–512 and the confirmed format:
- **First move:** read the whole sentence for sense and **guess the complete
  word from context** — the capitalised word's meaning, not its letters, is
  the way in (l.509; GL instruction "must make sense").
- **Then find the buried three-letter word** inside your guessed word, in its
  original letter order.
- **Always run both checks:** the rebuilt word is real and fits the sentence
  **AND** the three letters form a real word (l.510–511). A child who checks
  only one gets caught by the signature distractor.
- **Don't trust "broken-looking" words** — the remainder can itself be a real
  word ("GATED", "CARS", "BAG"), so let the sentence meaning, not the
  appearance of the capitalised word, drive the answer (l.490; GL examples).
- **Sound out combinations systematically** once you have a candidate (l.511).
- **Vocabulary is the real lever:** because vocab demand is High
  (`GL-11plus-English-VR-Research.md` l.160), wide reading plus a stock of
  common three-letter words (THE, AND, HER, APE, ANT, AGE, OWL, EAR, ARE, ATE,
  ONE, LAW, ROT, KIN, FAR) does more for this type than any trick.
