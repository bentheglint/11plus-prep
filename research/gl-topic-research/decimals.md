# GL Assessment Research: Decimals

## Format

### Paper Context
- **Paper:** GL Assessment 11+ Mathematics
- **Total:** 50 multiple-choice questions in 50 minutes (~1 minute per question)
- **Options:** 5 answer choices (A-E) per question
- **Paper type:** Non-adaptive — all children sit the same paper
- **Difficulty progression:** Questions generally increase in difficulty through the paper, though GL mixes some easier questions among harder ones to maintain confidence
- **Curriculum basis:** UK National Curriculum Key Stage 2 (Years 5-6), with some questions extending slightly beyond typical classroom coverage

### Where Decimals Sit
Decimals fall within the **Number** section of the GL specification, which is by far the largest topic area — typically **five times more number questions than any other category** (measurement, geometry, statistics, or algebra). Within the Number section, decimals overlap heavily with:
- Fractions (conversion, equivalence)
- Percentages (conversion, calculation)
- Place value (digit identification, ordering)
- The four operations (applied to decimal numbers)

### Estimated Frequency
Based on analysis of GL practice papers and published topic breakdowns:
- **3-6 questions per paper** will involve decimals directly
- **An additional 2-4 questions** will involve decimals indirectly (money calculations, measurement conversions, data interpretation with decimal values)
- This means decimals feature in roughly **10-20% of all questions** on a typical GL maths paper, making it one of the highest-yield topics

### GL Specification Wording (2022)
The GL specification states pupils should be able to:
> "Develop an understanding of place value to include up to two decimal places and use this to multiply and divide numbers by 10 and 100."

The Year 6 National Curriculum (which GL draws from) extends this to three decimal places and multiplying/dividing by 1,000.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Single-skill questions with no cross-topic integration
- Numbers use tenths only, or familiar hundredths (money values)
- Straightforward operations with no carrying across the decimal point
- Context is familiar (money, simple measurement)

**Typical question types:**
- Identify the value of a digit in a number like 3.7 ("What does the 7 represent?")
- Order 3-4 decimals that all have the same number of decimal places (e.g., 0.3, 0.7, 0.1, 0.5)
- Convert a simple fraction to a decimal (1/2 = 0.5, 1/4 = 0.25, 3/4 = 0.75)
- Add or subtract decimals with the same number of decimal places (2.4 + 1.3)
- Multiply a whole number by 10 or 100 and express as a decimal division (300 ÷ 10 = 30.0)
- Round a number like 4.7 to the nearest whole number

**Distractor profile at D1:**
- One option is the correct answer
- One option reflects a very basic place value error (e.g., misreading tenths as ones)
- Remaining options are plausible but clearly wrong if the child understands the concept
- The "trap" distractor is mild — catches only those who haven't studied decimals at all

**Example:**
> What is the value of the 6 in 4.63?
> A) 6  B) 60  C) 0.6  D) 0.06  E) 600
> Answer: C (0.6 — six tenths)

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

**Characteristics:**
- Two skills combined (e.g., convert then compare, or calculate then round)
- Numbers use hundredths or a mix of tenths and hundredths
- Decimals with different numbers of decimal places must be compared
- Simple word problem context requiring identification of the correct operation
- Carrying or borrowing across the decimal point in addition/subtraction

**Typical question types:**
- Order decimals with different numbers of decimal places (0.4, 0.38, 0.412)
- Convert between fractions, decimals, and percentages and then compare
- Multiply a decimal by a single-digit whole number (3.45 x 4)
- Divide a number to get a decimal answer (17 ÷ 4 = 4.25)
- Multi-step money problems ("Bought 3 items at £2.45 each, paid with £10")
- Multiply/divide by 10 or 100 where the answer crosses a place value boundary (0.08 x 100 = 8)
- Round to 1 decimal place when the digit to the right is 5

**Distractor profile at D2:**
- Correct answer present
- One distractor reflects the "longer is larger" misconception (thinking 0.412 > 0.5 because it has more digits)
- One distractor reflects a common calculation error (misaligned decimal points, forgetting to carry)
- One distractor is the result of applying the wrong operation
- The fifth option is a "nearly right" answer that catches rushing

**Example:**
> Which of these is the largest?
> A) 0.45  B) 0.5  C) 0.409  D) 0.51  E) 0.499
> Answer: D (0.51)
> Trap: C looks large because it has 3 digits; E looks large because 499 > 51 if you ignore place value

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

**Characteristics:**
- Three or more skills combined in a single question
- Thousandths involved, or numbers with varying decimal places
- Multi-step reasoning required (calculate, convert, compare, then select)
- Unfamiliar or abstract context
- Requires strong number sense and estimation skills
- May involve algebraic thinking ("What number, when multiplied by 0.4, gives 2.8?")

**Typical question types:**
- Order a mixed set of fractions, decimals, and percentages from smallest to largest
- Multi-step word problem with decimal division producing a remainder that must be interpreted in context
- "Which calculation gives the same answer as...?" requiring equivalence understanding
- Finding a missing number in a decimal equation (e.g., ? x 0.3 = 1.5)
- Problems requiring estimation to check which answer is reasonable
- Division producing a recurring or non-terminating decimal that must be rounded
- Metric conversion chains (km to m to cm) involving decimal arithmetic

**Distractor profile at D3:**
- All five options are plausible and close in value
- Distractors exploit multiple misconceptions simultaneously
- One distractor is the answer you get if you round incorrectly
- One is the answer from a correct method but with a decimal point in the wrong place
- One exploits the "shorter is larger" misconception (thinking 0.5 > 0.52 because fractions with fewer digits after the point must be larger, like how 1/2 > 1/52)
- Some D3 questions have distractors that are correct intermediate steps, catching children who stop calculating too early

**Example:**
> A shop sells ribbon at £1.35 per metre. Priya buys 0.8 metres. How much change does she get from £2?
> A) £0.65  B) £0.92  C) £1.08  D) £0.88  E) £1.92
> Answer: B (£1.35 x 0.8 = £1.08, then £2 - £1.08 = £0.92)
> Trap: C is the intermediate step (cost only); A assumes £1.35 was the total cost; D comes from rounding errors

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Place Value Identification
> "What is the value of the 3 in 7.034?"
- Tests understanding of tenths, hundredths, thousandths positions
- GL varies between asking for the value (0.03) and asking for the place name ("hundredths")
- D1 uses tenths; D2 uses hundredths; D3 uses thousandths or asks for the digit given the value

### Type 2: Ordering and Comparing
> "Put these in order from smallest to largest: 0.7, 0.65, 0.701, 0.8"
- The core GL decimal question — appears on nearly every paper
- GL specifically designs these to exploit the "longer is larger" and "shorter is larger" misconceptions
- D2-D3 versions mix decimals with different numbers of decimal places
- Hardest versions include a mix of fractions, decimals, and percentages to order

### Type 3: Conversion Between Forms
> "Which decimal is equivalent to 3/8?"
- Conversion triangle: fraction ↔ decimal ↔ percentage
- GL expects children to know common equivalences by heart (see Key Concepts Reference)
- D2 tests straightforward conversions; D3 tests non-standard fractions (3/8, 5/8, 1/6)
- Distractors often include the numerator or denominator as a decimal (e.g., for 3/8: offering 0.38 or 3.8)

### Type 4: Decimal Arithmetic
> "Calculate 4.56 + 2.7"
- Addition and subtraction: the trap is misaligning decimal points
- Multiplication: typically decimal × whole number at KS2 level
- Division: whole number ÷ whole number giving a decimal answer, or decimal ÷ whole number
- GL presents these as both bare calculations and word problems

### Type 5: Multiply/Divide by Powers of 10
> "What is 3.45 × 100?"
- Tests whether children understand digit shifting (not "adding zeros" or "moving the decimal point")
- GL varies direction (multiply AND divide) and power (10, 100, 1,000)
- D2 trap: 3.45 × 10 → child writes 3.450 (added a zero) instead of 34.5
- D3 version: 0.007 × 1000 = ?  or  45 ÷ 1000 = ?

### Type 6: Rounding Decimals
> "Round 3.456 to one decimal place"
- GL tests rounding to the nearest whole number, 1dp, and 2dp
- The "round up at 5" rule is critical — GL deliberately places 5 as the deciding digit
- D2 trap: rounding 3.45 to 1dp — children may round down because 4 < 5, forgetting to look at the correct digit
- D3 versions ask children to round then use the rounded value in a further calculation

### Type 7: Word Problems with Decimals
> "Tom buys 4 notebooks at £1.85 each. How much does he spend?"
- Money is the most common decimal word problem context in GL papers
- Other contexts: measurement (metres, kilograms), sports timing, temperature
- D2-D3 involve multi-step problems (calculate cost, then find change)
- GL embeds these throughout the paper, not grouped together

### Type 8: Number Line / Positioning
> "What number does the arrow point to?" [number line from 3 to 4, arrow at 3.7]
- Tests visual understanding of decimal intervals
- D1: number line marked in tenths
- D2: number line marked in fifths or with only endpoints labelled
- D3: number line with irregular intervals or requiring estimation between marks

## Distractor Design

GL distractors for decimal questions are designed around **documented misconceptions** that children actually hold. Every wrong answer should be the result of a specific, predictable error — not a random number.

### Distractor Principles

1. **Each distractor exploits one identifiable misconception** — never random values
2. **At least one distractor is a "near miss"** — correct method, small slip (e.g., decimal point one place off)
3. **At least one distractor exploits the most common misconception for that question type** (e.g., "longer is larger" for ordering)
4. **Distractors should be numerically plausible** — within a reasonable range of the correct answer
5. **For multi-step problems, intermediate answers appear as distractors** — catching children who stop early

### Misconception-Based Distractor Catalogue

| Misconception | What the Child Does | Example Distractor |
|--------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Longer is larger** | Treats decimal digits like whole numbers — 0.142 > 0.5 because 142 > 5 | For "largest": offers 0.3219 when answer is 0.5 |
| **Shorter is larger** | Applies fraction logic — fewer decimal digits = bigger | For "smallest": offers 0.9 when answer is 0.15 |
| **Ignore the decimal point** | Reads 3.7 as 37, or 0.25 as 25 | For 2.4 × 3: offers 72 instead of 7.2 |
| **Add zeros = multiply by 10** | Adds a zero to the end instead of shifting digits | For 3.5 × 10: offers 3.50 instead of 35 |
| **Misalign decimal points** | Lines up digits from the right (whole number habit) | For 3.4 + 0.56: offers 0.90 or 3.60 |
| **Denominator = decimal** | Converts 3/8 by writing 3.8 or 0.38 | For "3/8 as a decimal": offers 0.38 |
| **Carry errors** | Correct method but fumbles carrying across decimal point | For 4.67 + 2.85: offers 7.42 instead of 7.52 |
| **Round the wrong digit** | Looks at the wrong column to decide rounding | For "round 3.456 to 1dp": offers 3.5 (looked at 5 not 4) — or 3.4 (looked at 4 but rounded down when should have used 5) |
| **Stop too early** | Multi-step problem, gives the intermediate answer | For change from £10: gives the total cost, not the change |
| **Reverse the operation** | Divides when should multiply, or vice versa | For "0.6 metres in centimetres": offers 0.006 instead of 60 |

### Position Distribution
- Correct answer distributed roughly evenly across positions A-E
- GL avoids patterns where the answer clusters in one position
- No reliable "most common position" to exploit

## Common GL Traps

### Trap 1: Mixed Decimal Places in Ordering
GL deliberately presents decimals with different numbers of decimal places (0.4, 0.39, 0.401) to trigger the "longer is larger" misconception. Children must mentally pad with trailing zeros (0.400, 0.390, 0.401) to compare correctly.

### Trap 2: The Zero After the Decimal Point
Questions involving numbers like 0.08, 0.006, or 3.04 catch children who skip the zero. They may read 0.08 as "eight tenths" (0.8) rather than "eight hundredths." GL specifically includes options reflecting both readings.

### Trap 3: Multiplying by 10 — "Just Add a Zero"
The rule "add a zero to multiply by 10" works for whole numbers but fails for decimals. GL exploits this: 2.5 × 10 is NOT 2.50. Children who add a zero get 2.50 (unchanged value), while the correct answer is 25. Distractors include 2.50 and 250.

### Trap 4: Money as Disguised Decimal Arithmetic
£2.50 + £1.75 tests the same skill as 2.50 + 1.75, but the money context changes how children approach it. Some convert to pence (250 + 175 = 425), then fail to convert back correctly (writing 4.25 or 42.5). GL includes both conversion errors as distractors.

### Trap 5: Fraction-Decimal Conversion "By Inspection"
Children who convert 3/8 to a decimal by writing "3.8" or "0.38" (using the numerator and denominator as digits) rather than performing the division. GL includes these predictable wrong answers.

### Trap 6: Rounding to 1dp When the Next Digit is 5
3.45 rounded to 1dp: is it 3.4 or 3.5? The convention is to round up when the deciding digit is 5. GL tests this boundary case specifically because it catches children who are uncertain about the rule.

### Trap 7: Decimal Point Displacement in Division
When dividing and getting a decimal answer, children may place the decimal point incorrectly. For example, 7 ÷ 4 = 1.75, but distractors may offer 17.5 or 0.175. GL also tests cases like 1 ÷ 8 = 0.125, where children may offer 1.25 or 12.5.

### Trap 8: Remainders Expressed as Decimals
"Share 23 sweets equally among 4 children. How many does each get?" Answer: 5.75 — but this doesn't make sense in context (you can't have 0.75 of a sweet). GL tests whether children can interpret the decimal answer: each child gets 5 sweets with 3 left over. The distractor is 5.75 itself, for children who calculate correctly but don't interpret.

### Trap 9: Equivalent Fractions That Look Wrong
0.2 = 1/5 feels counterintuitive to children because 2 and 5 don't have an obvious relationship. GL tests these less intuitive equivalences (1/8 = 0.125, 3/5 = 0.6, 1/3 ≈ 0.333...) rather than just the familiar 1/2 = 0.5.

### Trap 10: The Recurring Decimal Trap
1/3 = 0.333... and 1/6 = 0.1666... Children may write 0.3 or 0.33 as the "exact" equivalent. GL tests whether children understand that some fractions produce recurring decimals and can identify the closest decimal approximation from the options given.

## Key Concepts Reference

### Essential Equivalences (Must Be Memorised)

| Fraction | Decimal | Percentage |
|----------|---------|------------|
| 1/2 | 0.5 | 50% |
| 1/4 | 0.25 | 25% |
| 3/4 | 0.75 | 75% |
| 1/5 | 0.2 | 20% |
| 2/5 | 0.4 | 40% |
| 3/5 | 0.6 | 60% |
| 4/5 | 0.8 | 80% |
| 1/10 | 0.1 | 10% |
| 3/10 | 0.3 | 30% |
| 7/10 | 0.7 | 70% |
| 9/10 | 0.9 | 90% |
| 1/3 | 0.333... | 33.3...% |
| 2/3 | 0.666... | 66.6...% |
| 1/8 | 0.125 | 12.5% |
| 3/8 | 0.375 | 37.5% |
| 5/8 | 0.625 | 62.5% |
| 7/8 | 0.875 | 87.5% |
| 1/100 | 0.01 | 1% |
| 1/1000 | 0.001 | 0.1% |

### Place Value Chart

| ... | Hundreds | Tens | Ones | **. (decimal point)** | Tenths | Hundredths | Thousandths |
|-----|----------|------|------|-----------------------|--------|------------|-------------|
| ... | 100 | 10 | 1 | **.** | 0.1 | 0.01 | 0.001 |
| ... | H | T | O | **.** | t | h | th |

Key relationships:
- Each place is **10 times** the place to its right
- Each place is **1/10** of the place to its left
- The places mirror around the **ones** column (not the decimal point): tens ↔ tenths, hundreds ↔ hundredths, thousands ↔ thousandths
- There is NO "oneths" place — this is a common misconception

### Conversion Methods

**Fraction → Decimal:** Divide the numerator by the denominator (3/8 = 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375)

**Decimal → Fraction:** Write the decimal as a fraction over the appropriate power of 10, then simplify (0.35 = 35/100 = 7/20)

**Decimal → Percentage:** Multiply by 100 (0.35 × 100 = 35%)

**Percentage → Decimal:** Divide by 100 (35% ÷ 100 = 0.35)

**Fraction → Percentage:** Divide numerator by denominator, then multiply by 100 (3/8 = 0.375 × 100 = 37.5%)

**Percentage → Fraction:** Write over 100 and simplify (35% = 35/100 = 7/20)

### Multiplying/Dividing by Powers of 10

**The rule:** Digits move left (for multiplication) or right (for division). The decimal point stays still.

| Operation | What Happens | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| × 10 | Digits move 1 place LEFT | 3.45 → 34.5 |
| × 100 | Digits move 2 places LEFT | 3.45 → 345 |
| × 1000 | Digits move 3 places LEFT | 3.45 → 3450 |
| ÷ 10 | Digits move 1 place RIGHT | 3.45 → 0.345 |
| ÷ 100 | Digits move 2 places RIGHT | 3.45 → 0.0345 |
| ÷ 1000 | Digits move 3 places RIGHT | 3.45 → 0.00345 |

**Critical teaching point:** NEVER say "move the decimal point." The decimal point is fixed between the ones and tenths columns. The DIGITS move. Saying "move the decimal point" creates confusion when children try to apply the rule to more complex problems.

### Rounding Rules

1. Identify the place you're rounding to
2. Look at the digit immediately to the RIGHT of that place
3. If that digit is **0, 1, 2, 3, or 4** → round down (the target digit stays the same)
4. If that digit is **5, 6, 7, 8, or 9** → round up (the target digit increases by 1)
5. All digits to the right of the target place become zero (or are dropped for decimal places)

**Examples:**
- 3.456 rounded to 1dp → look at 5 (hundredths) → round up → **3.5**
- 3.456 rounded to 2dp → look at 6 (thousandths) → round up → **3.46**
- 3.441 rounded to 1dp → look at 4 (hundredths) → round down → **3.4**
- 7.95 rounded to 1dp → look at 5 (hundredths) → round up → **8.0** (not 7.10!)

### Ordering Strategy

1. Make all numbers have the **same number of decimal places** by adding trailing zeros
2. Compare digit by digit from left to right
3. The first difference determines the order

**Example:** Order 0.5, 0.48, 0.502
→ Pad: 0.500, 0.480, 0.502
→ All start with 0, then 5, 4, 5 — so 0.48 is smallest
→ Compare 0.500 and 0.502: differ at thousandths (0 vs 2) → 0.500 < 0.502
→ Order: **0.48, 0.5, 0.502**

## Key Vocabulary

Children must understand and use the following terms confidently:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Decimal** | A number that uses a decimal point to show tenths, hundredths, etc. |
| **Decimal point** | The dot that separates whole numbers from fractional parts |
| **Decimal place (dp)** | The number of digits after the decimal point |
| **Tenths** | The first place after the decimal point (0.1) |
| **Hundredths** | The second place after the decimal point (0.01) |
| **Thousandths** | The third place after the decimal point (0.001) |
| **Place value** | The value of a digit based on its position in the number |
| **Round / Rounding** | Approximating a number to a given degree of accuracy |
| **Estimate** | A rough calculation, often using rounding first |
| **Equivalent** | Equal in value (0.5 = 1/2 = 50%) |
| **Convert** | Change from one form to another (fraction → decimal) |
| **Recurring decimal** | A decimal that repeats forever (0.333...) |
| **Terminating decimal** | A decimal that ends (0.25) |
| **Ascending** | Smallest to largest |
| **Descending** | Largest to smallest |
| **Partition** | Break a number into its place value parts (3.45 = 3 + 0.4 + 0.05) |
| **Approximate** | Close to the true value but not exact |
| **Digit** | A single number symbol (0-9) |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Year 5 Statutory Requirements
- Read and write decimal numbers as fractions (e.g., 0.71 = 71/100)
- Recognise and use thousandths and relate them to tenths, hundredths, and decimal equivalents
- Round decimals with two decimal places to the nearest whole number and to one decimal place
- Read, write, order and compare numbers with up to three decimal places
- Solve problems involving numbers up to three decimal places
- Recognise the percentage symbol (%) and understand percentage relates to "number of parts per hundred"
- Write percentages as a fraction with denominator 100, and as a decimal

### Year 6 Statutory Requirements
- Identify the value of each digit in numbers given to three decimal places
- Multiply and divide numbers by 10, 100 and 1,000 giving answers up to three decimal places
- Multiply one-digit numbers with up to two decimal places by whole numbers
- Use written division methods in cases where the answer has up to two decimal places
- Solve problems which require answers to be rounded to specified degrees of accuracy
- Associate a fraction with division and calculate decimal fraction equivalents (e.g., 0.375) for a simple fraction (e.g., 3/8)
- Recall and use equivalences between simple fractions, decimals and percentages, including in different contexts

### Non-Statutory Guidance (Year 6)
The National Curriculum notes state:
- Pupils should practise, use and understand the addition and subtraction of fractions with different denominators by identifying equivalent fractions with common denominators
- Pupils should connect conversion between fractions, decimals and percentages with equivalence
- Pupils should begin to multiply and divide numbers with up to two decimal places by one-digit and two-digit whole numbers

### GL Extensions Beyond Curriculum
GL Assessment papers may test slightly beyond standard Year 6 curriculum:
- Division producing answers to three decimal places (curriculum specifies two)
- Working with all three forms (fraction, decimal, percentage) in a single question
- Multi-step problems combining decimal arithmetic with other number topics
- Problems requiring estimation and checking using inverse operations

## Teaching Notes

### Critical Misconceptions to Address

**1. "Longer is Larger" (most common)**
Children believe 0.142 > 0.5 because 142 > 5. This is the single most documented misconception in decimal education research, persisting even into secondary school. It stems from applying whole number logic to decimal digits.

**Fix:** Regularly practise ordering decimals with different numbers of decimal places. Use the "padding with zeros" strategy (0.5 = 0.500) to make comparison visual. Use money as a bridge: "Would you rather have £0.50 or £0.142?"

**2. "Shorter is Larger" (less common but persistent)**
The inverse misconception: children believe 0.3 > 0.45 because with fractions, "thirds are bigger than forty-fifths" or because fewer digits means a simpler, bigger number. This affects about 10-15% of children.

**Fix:** Use number lines extensively. Show where 0.3 and 0.45 sit relative to each other. The padding strategy also works here.

**3. Misunderstanding Place Value Symmetry**
Children believe the places reflect around the decimal point: ones ↔ "oneths", tens ↔ tenths. There is no "oneths" — the reflection is around the **ones** column.

**Fix:** Build and label a place value chart explicitly. Show the symmetry: hundreds ↔ hundredths, tens ↔ tenths, with ones in the centre.

**4. "Move the Decimal Point"**
Teaching children that the decimal point moves when multiplying/dividing by 10 creates confusion. The decimal point is FIXED. The digits move.

**Fix:** Use a place value grid where children physically slide digit cards left or right. The decimal point column stays in place; the digits shift.

**5. Doubling/Halving Errors**
When doubling 0.52, children get 0.104 instead of 1.04 (they double each part but don't carry across the decimal). When halving 5.3, they get 2.15 instead of 2.65 (they halve 5 to get 2, halve 3 to get 1.5, then combine wrong).

**Fix:** Encourage estimation first: "0.52 is about 0.5, and double 0.5 is 1. So the answer should be about 1." If they get 0.104, that's about 0.1 — clearly wrong.

### GL-Specific Preparation Strategies

1. **Memorise the equivalence table** — the 19 fraction/decimal/percentage conversions listed above should be automatic recall. GL doesn't give time to calculate 3/8 as a decimal from scratch.

2. **Practise mixed-form ordering** — ordering sets that include fractions, decimals AND percentages (e.g., "Order: 0.35, 1/4, 30%, 2/5"). This forces conversion before comparison.

3. **Use estimation to eliminate distractors** — before calculating, estimate the answer's size. If the question asks for 3.45 × 8 and the answer should be "about 28", immediately eliminate options like 2.76 or 276.

4. **Practise decimal arithmetic under timed conditions** — GL gives roughly 1 minute per question. Children must be fluent enough to add, subtract, multiply and divide decimals without column method every time.

5. **Read word problems twice** — GL decimal word problems often have two steps. Children who give the intermediate answer (cost instead of change, total instead of individual share) lose the mark.

6. **Check by reversing** — if the answer to 4.5 ÷ 3 is 1.5, check: does 1.5 × 3 = 4.5? This catches decimal point displacement errors.

### Common Errors by Question Type (Quick Reference)

| Question Type | Most Common Error | What Went Wrong |
|--------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Ordering decimals | Picks longest decimal as largest | Whole number thinking applied to decimals |
| Place value identification | Gives 0.3 instead of 0.03 | Miscounts decimal places |
| × 10/100/1000 | Adds zeros instead of shifting | Learned faulty rule for whole numbers |
| Fraction → decimal | Writes 3/8 as 0.38 | Uses digits rather than dividing |
| Rounding to 1dp | Rounds 3.456 to 3.5 | Looks at wrong digit (5 not 4) |
| Addition | Misaligns columns | Lines up from right (whole number habit) |
| Word problem | Gives intermediate answer | Stops after first step |
| Division | Decimal point in wrong place | Doesn't estimate to check reasonableness |
