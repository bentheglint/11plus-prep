## Overview

Times Tables Rock Stars (TTRS) is a UK-built multiplication and division fluency app developed by teacher Bruno Reddy and now published by TT Rock Stars Ltd. It targets children aged 5–14 with a focus on automatic recall of times tables (and related division facts) through timed challenges, competitive leader boards, and a rock-band theming that lets children "level up" from Wannabe to Rock Hero. TTRS is one of the most widely deployed school maths apps in the UK — adopted in thousands of primary schools — and has a companion family subscription for home use. Its sister product, NumBots, covers addition and subtraction for younger learners. Subject scope is deliberately narrow: multiplication and division only.

## Free vs paid

- Schools can **start a free trial** before purchasing (trial length not specified on pricing page).
- Family subscriptions do not appear to have a published free trial; the low price point (£9/year) likely reduces the perceived need for one.
- No permanent free tier exists for either families or schools.
- A **30-day money-back guarantee** has been cited in secondary sources for the family plan, though this was not confirmed on the current pricing page at verification date.

## Family / sibling pricing

The family plan is flat-rate: £9/year covers up to 3 child accounts and 2 adult accounts. At this price, the per-child cost is £3/child/year for a 3-child family — the lowest per-child annual cost of any product in this comparison set by a significant margin. The price point is so low that it is unlikely to be the primary decision factor; the main barrier to adoption is awareness, not cost.

## Schools licensing

TTRS has a robust school licensing model that has driven widespread adoption:

- Flat per-school annual fee (£116.80) regardless of pupil count — a fixed-cost model that is very attractive for large schools.
- Optional add-ons allow schools to activate enhanced analytics and structured session tools.
- The fixed annual fee model (vs per-pupil pricing) creates strong value-for-money perception at scale and reduces administrative overhead for school business managers.
- Paper worksheets are included in school plans, supporting offline practice and classroom use.
- The combination of school penetration and family plans creates a school-to-home funnel: children who use TTRS at school are the warm leads for family subscriptions.

## Unusual mechanics

- **Rock band progression system:** Children progress through ranked tiers (Wannabe → Newbie → Apprentice → ... → Rock God) based on speed and accuracy. The themed identity progression is a retention mechanic.
- **No chat, no ads, no in-app purchases:** TTRS is explicitly positioned as child-safe: "No chat functions. No in-game purchases. No ads." This is a meaningful trust signal for parents and schools alike.
- **Adaptive difficulty:** Question difficulty automatically adjusts based on individual performance, so the challenge level is always calibrated to the individual learner.
- **Annual-only family pricing:** Unlike most UK edtech products, there is no monthly family option. The low annual price makes this reasonable.
- **School-to-home flywheel:** The product's dominance in primary schools means most UK primary children already know TTRS, reducing the marketing cost of family subscription acquisition.
- **NumBots companion:** The sister product (addition/subtraction) is tightly integrated and can be added to school plans, creating a wider numeracy offer for schools at a marginal cost.

## Applicability to UK 11+ app pricing

TTRS demonstrates that a UK edtech product with strong school penetration can charge a minimal family subscription (£9/year) because the customer acquisition cost is near zero — schools do the marketing. A UK 11+ app without this school footprint must charge considerably more to be viable, but can legitimately reference TTRS's safety positioning ("no ads, no in-app purchases") as a shared trust signal for the same parent demographic.
