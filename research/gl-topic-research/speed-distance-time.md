# GL Assessment Research: Speed, Distance, and Time

## Format

- **Paper:** GL Assessment 11+ Maths — 50 multiple-choice questions in 50 minutes
- **Options:** Always 5 (A–E), machine-marked answer sheet
- **No negative marking** — raw marks standardised by age
- **SDT weighting:** Speed, Distance, and Time is a minor topic within the Measurement strand. Expect 1–3 questions per paper, sometimes zero. GL papers are dominated by Number (~50% of questions), with Measurement, Geometry, Data, and Algebra sharing the remainder.
- **Position in paper:** SDT questions tend to appear in the middle-to-later portion of the paper, as they require multi-step reasoning and unit manipulation. Straightforward "plug into formula" questions may appear earlier.
- **Time pressure:** ~1 minute per question. SDT questions often require 2–3 steps, so children who hesitate on unit conversions lose disproportionate time here.
- **Context:** Questions are always word problems set in real-life UK scenarios — car journeys, cycling, running, trains, ferries, school trips. GL never presents a bare formula to solve.

## Skills Tested & Category Weightings

| Skill Category | Approx. GL Weight | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Calculate speed** (S = D ÷ T) | ~25% | Given distance and time, find speed |
| **Calculate distance** (D = S × T) | ~25% | Given speed and time, find distance |
| **Calculate time** (T = D ÷ S) | ~20% | Given speed and distance, find time |
| **Unit conversion** (minutes ↔ hours, m ↔ km, m/s ↔ km/h) | ~15% | Convert before or after applying the formula |
| **Multi-part journeys / average speed** | ~10% | Two-leg journeys requiring total distance ÷ total time |
| **Timetable & schedule reading** | ~5% | Reading bus/train timetables to calculate journey duration or departure/arrival times |

**Note:** Pure distance–time graph interpretation is rare at 11+. The National Curriculum introduces this formally at KS3 (Year 7–8). However, GL may occasionally present a simple table of distances and times that functions similarly.

## Difficulty Criteria

### D1 (Easy ~30%)

- **Single-step formula application** — one clean division or multiplication with whole numbers
- Time given in whole hours (no conversion needed)
- Distance and speed are "friendly" numbers that divide evenly (e.g. 150 km in 3 hours)
- Speed given in one unit, answer expected in the same unit
- No mixed units (everything in km/h or everything in m/s)
- Context is simple and familiar: "Emma drives 150 km in 3 hours"
- Only one formula rearrangement needed (usually S = D ÷ T)

**Example:** "A delivery van travels 300 km at 75 km/h. How long does the journey take?"
Answer: 300 ÷ 75 = 4 hours.

**Distractor profile:** Options are evenly spaced whole numbers or simple decimals (3, 3.5, 3.75, 4, 4.5). The correct answer sits among plausible neighbours.

### D2 (Medium ~40%)

- **Two-step problems** — typically requiring a unit conversion before or after applying the formula
- Time given in hours and minutes (e.g. 1 hour 30 minutes = 1.5 hours) or just minutes (e.g. 20 minutes = 1/3 hour)
- May require converting the answer to a different unit (hours → minutes, or m → km)
- Speeds produce non-integer intermediate values but clean final answers
- km/h ↔ m/s conversion (multiply by 1000, divide by 3600, or equivalently ÷ 3.6)
- "How many minutes does the journey take?" — forces conversion from decimal hours
- Distance given in metres when speed is in km/h (or vice versa)

**Example:** "Jake cycles 12 km in 20 minutes. What is his speed in km/h?"
Step 1: 20 minutes = 1/3 hour.
Step 2: Speed = 12 ÷ (1/3) = 36 km/h.

**Distractor profile:** One distractor is the answer you get if you forget to convert units (e.g. 12 ÷ 20 = 0.6, which doesn't appear but a rounded/scaled version does). Another distractor uses a nearby wrong conversion (e.g. treating 20 minutes as 0.2 hours instead of 1/3).

### D3 (Hard ~30%)

- **Multi-step problems combining several skills:**
  - Two-part journeys requiring total distance ÷ total time for average speed
  - Double unit conversion (metres to km AND minutes to hours) before applying formula
  - Awkward fractions of an hour (40 minutes = 2/3 hour, 25 minutes = 5/12 hour)
  - "Remaining journey" problems: given total distance, distance covered, time used, find speed needed for rest
  - Converting between m/s and km/h with non-standard numbers
- Numbers that don't divide cleanly, requiring confident fraction/decimal work
- Distractors deliberately target the "average the speeds" trap (see Distractor Design)
- May combine SDT with other topics (e.g. ratio, or reading data from a table)

**Example:** "A cyclist covers 24 km in 40 minutes. What is the speed in km/h?"
Step 1: 40 min = 40/60 = 2/3 hour.
Step 2: Speed = 24 ÷ (2/3) = 24 × (3/2) = 36 km/h.

**Example:** "Ben drives 80 km in 2 hours, then 60 km in 1.5 hours. What is his average speed for the whole journey?"
Total distance = 140 km. Total time = 3.5 hours. Average speed = 140 ÷ 3.5 = 40 km/h.

## Common Question Types

### Type 1: Calculate Speed
"A car travels [distance] in [time]. What is the average speed?"
- Straightforward S = D ÷ T
- D1 if whole numbers; D2/D3 if conversion needed

### Type 2: Calculate Distance
"A [vehicle/person] travels at [speed] for [time]. How far do they go?"
- D = S × T
- D1 with whole hours; D2 with "2 hours 15 minutes" or similar

### Type 3: Calculate Time
"A journey of [distance] at [speed]. How long does it take?"
- T = D ÷ S
- D2 if answer must be converted to minutes; D3 if answer is a fraction of an hour that must be expressed as hours and minutes

### Type 4: Unit Conversion Required
"[Person] travels [distance in metres] in [time in minutes]. What is their speed in km/h?"
- Requires converting both distance (m → km) and time (min → hours) before applying formula
- Almost always D2 or D3

### Type 5: km/h to m/s (or vice versa)
"A car travels at 72 km/h. What is this in metres per second?"
- Multiply by 1000, divide by 3600 (or divide by 3.6)
- Usually D2 — the method is mechanical once learned

### Type 6: Multi-Part Journey / Average Speed
"[Person] drives [d1] in [t1], then [d2] in [t2]. What is the average speed?"
- Total distance ÷ total time (NOT the average of the two speeds)
- Always D3 because of the common trap

### Type 7: Remaining Journey
"A driver must cover 300 km total. She has driven 180 km in 3 hours. What speed must she average to finish in 2 more hours?"
- Remaining distance = total − covered
- Speed = remaining distance ÷ remaining time
- D2 or D3 depending on numbers

### Type 8: Timetable / Schedule Reading
"A bus leaves at 09:15 and arrives at 10:42. How long is the journey?"
- Requires counting across hours (or bridging across noon/midnight)
- "Which bus should Sarah catch to arrive by 14:00?"
- May combine with SDT calculation: "The bus stop is 2 km away and Sarah walks at 4 km/h..."
- D1 if straightforward subtraction; D2 if bridging midnight or combining with distance calculation

### Type 9: Time Arithmetic
"A 26-minute programme starts at 23:59. When does it end?"
- Tests understanding of the 24-hour clock and bridging past midnight
- D1/D2

## Distractor Design

GL distractors are never random — they are **engineered from common mistakes**. For SDT questions, the typical distractor strategies are:

### 1. The "Forgot to Convert" Distractor
If time is given in minutes but the answer needs km/h, one distractor is the answer you get by dividing distance by time-in-minutes rather than time-in-hours.
- **Example:** 12 km in 20 minutes → wrong answer: 12 ÷ 20 = 0.6 (scaled up, a distractor might be 6 km/h)
- Correct: 12 ÷ (1/3) = 36 km/h

### 2. The "Averaged the Speeds" Trap
For two-part journeys, one distractor is always (speed1 + speed2) ÷ 2.
- **Example:** Drive at 60 km/h then 40 km/h → trap answer: 50 km/h
- Correct: total distance ÷ total time (which is usually NOT 50)

### 3. The "Wrong Operation" Distractor
One distractor uses the wrong formula rearrangement — multiplying where you should divide, or vice versa.
- **Example:** Find time when D = 200 km, S = 80 km/h → trap: 200 × 80 = 16,000 (obviously too large, so GL will use a subtler version)

### 4. The "Decimal-Time Confusion" Distractor
One distractor assumes base-10 time: treating 30 minutes as 0.3 hours (instead of 0.5), or 1 hour 15 minutes as 1.15 hours (instead of 1.25).
- **Example:** 1 hour 15 min treated as 1.15 hours → Distance = 60 × 1.15 = 69 (distractor) vs correct 60 × 1.25 = 75

### 5. The "Close Neighbour" Spread
Distractors are evenly spaced around the correct answer (e.g. 55, 58, 60, 62, 65) to prevent elimination by estimation. The correct answer is rarely the smallest or largest option.

### 6. The "Partial Calculation" Distractor
For multi-step problems, one distractor is the intermediate answer (e.g. the total distance when the question asks for average speed, or the time in hours when the question asks for minutes).

## Common GL Traps

### Trap 1: Minutes ≠ Decimal Hours
The single most common error. Children treat time as base-10:
- 30 minutes → 0.3 hours (should be 0.5)
- 15 minutes → 0.15 hours (should be 0.25)
- 45 minutes → 0.45 hours (should be 0.75)
- 1 hour 20 minutes → 1.2 hours (should be 1.333...)

**GL exploits this** by making both the correct and incorrect answers appear in the options.

### Trap 2: Average Speed ≠ Average of Speeds
"What is the average speed for the whole journey?" almost always tests whether children add the two speeds and divide by 2.

Correct method: **Total distance ÷ Total time**. Always.

The harmonic mean formula (2ab/(a+b)) is relevant when equal distances are covered at different speeds, but at 11+ level, children should simply calculate total distance and total time separately.

### Trap 3: Forgetting to Convert Answer Units
The question asks "How many minutes?" but the child calculates hours and picks the hour-equivalent option. Or the question asks for km/h but the child calculates m/s.

### Trap 4: Multiplying Instead of Dividing (or Vice Versa)
Children who haven't internalised the SDT triangle sometimes multiply when they should divide. "150 km at 50 km/h → how long?" and they calculate 150 × 50 = 7,500.

### Trap 5: Mixed Units Across the Problem
Distance in metres, speed required in km/h. Or time in minutes, distance in km. The question deliberately presents information in mismatched units to test whether children convert before calculating.

### Trap 6: Tricky Fractions of an Hour
- 20 minutes = 1/3 hour (children struggle with this)
- 40 minutes = 2/3 hour
- 25 minutes = 5/12 hour
- 10 minutes = 1/6 hour

Questions using these values are automatically D2/D3 because the fraction arithmetic is demanding.

### Trap 7: Time Arithmetic Across Boundaries
Calculating duration when a journey starts at, say, 10:45 and ends at 13:20. Children must bridge across the hour (10:45 → 11:00 = 15 min, 11:00 → 13:00 = 2 hours, 13:00 → 13:20 = 20 min, total = 2 hours 35 minutes). Crossing noon or midnight adds further difficulty.

## Key Formulae Reference

### The SDT Triangle
```
       D
      ───
     S × T
```
Cover what you want to find:
- **Speed** = Distance ÷ Time
- **Distance** = Speed × Time
- **Time** = Distance ÷ Speed

### Unit Conversions — Time
| From | To | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| Minutes → Hours | ÷ 60 | 30 min = 0.5 h |
| Hours → Minutes | × 60 | 2.5 h = 150 min |
| Seconds → Minutes | ÷ 60 | 120 s = 2 min |
| Minutes → Seconds | × 60 | 3 min = 180 s |

### Unit Conversions — Distance
| From | To | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| Metres → Kilometres | ÷ 1000 | 2500 m = 2.5 km |
| Kilometres → Metres | × 1000 | 3.5 km = 3500 m |
| Miles → Kilometres | × 1.6 (approx) | 5 miles ≈ 8 km |
| Kilometres → Miles | × 0.625 (approx) | 8 km ≈ 5 miles |

### Unit Conversions — Speed
| From | To | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| km/h → m/s | ÷ 3.6 | 72 km/h = 20 m/s |
| m/s → km/h | × 3.6 | 25 m/s = 90 km/h |

**Shortcut:** km/h → m/s: multiply by 1000, divide by 3600. This simplifies to dividing by 3.6.

### Common Time Conversions Children Must Know
| Minutes | As a fraction of an hour | As a decimal |
|---|---|---|
| 10 min | 1/6 | 0.1667 |
| 12 min | 1/5 | 0.2 |
| 15 min | 1/4 | 0.25 |
| 20 min | 1/3 | 0.3333 |
| 25 min | 5/12 | 0.4167 |
| 30 min | 1/2 | 0.5 |
| 40 min | 2/3 | 0.6667 |
| 45 min | 3/4 | 0.75 |

## Key Vocabulary

Children must recognise and understand these terms in word problems:

| Term | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | How fast something is moving (distance per unit time) | Compound measure — always has two units (km/h, m/s, mph) |
| **Average speed** | Total distance ÷ Total time | NOT the mean of two speeds |
| **Distance** | How far something travels | May be given in m, km, or miles |
| **Time** | How long a journey takes | May be in seconds, minutes, hours, or mixed |
| **km/h** (kilometres per hour) | Standard metric speed unit | Most common unit in GL questions |
| **m/s** (metres per second) | Metric speed for shorter distances | Used for running, sprinting, animals |
| **mph** (miles per hour) | Imperial speed unit | Less common at 11+ but appears occasionally |
| **Journey** | A trip from one place to another | May have multiple legs/stages |
| **Depart / Departure** | Leave / The time of leaving | Timetable vocabulary |
| **Arrive / Arrival** | Reach destination / The time of reaching | Timetable vocabulary |
| **Duration** | How long something lasts | Used in timetable questions |
| **Constant speed** | Speed stays the same throughout | Simplifying assumption in most 11+ problems |
| **Convert** | Change from one unit to another | Critical skill — the source of most errors |
| **Per** | "For each" or "divided by" | "km per hour" = km ÷ hours |

## National Curriculum Alignment

### Where SDT Fits in the KS2 Programme of Study

Speed, Distance, and Time is **not explicitly named** as a standalone topic in the Year 5–6 National Curriculum. However, it draws on several statutory requirements:

**Year 5 — Measurement:**
- "Solve problems involving converting between units of time"
- "Use all four operations to solve problems involving measure, using decimal notation, including scaling"
- Convert between metric units (km ↔ m, etc.)

**Year 6 — Measurement:**
- "Solve problems involving the calculation and conversion of units of measure, using decimal notation up to 3 decimal places"
- "Use, read, write and convert between standard units, converting measurements of length, mass, volume and time from a smaller unit to a larger unit, and vice versa"
- "Convert between miles and kilometres"

**Year 6 — Ratio and Proportion:**
- "Solve problems involving the relative sizes of 2 quantities where missing values can be found by using integer multiplication and division facts"

**Year 6 — Algebra:**
- "Use simple formulae" — the SDT formula is a natural application
- "Express missing number problems algebraically"

**Year 6 — Non-statutory guidance:**
- "Compound units for speed (e.g. miles per hour) **may** be introduced"
- "Pupils connect conversion work (e.g. km to miles) to graphical representation"

### Key Insight for 11+ Preparation
The National Curriculum says compound units for speed "may" be introduced in Year 6 — it is optional, not required. This means many children sitting the 11+ in September of Year 6 will have had **minimal or no formal teaching on SDT**. GL Assessment tests it anyway because:

1. It naturally combines multiplication, division, fractions, decimals, and unit conversion — all KS2 statutory content
2. It tests whether children can apply formulae in context (a Year 6 algebra objective)
3. It differentiates strongly between candidates — children who can handle compound measures are likely strong mathematically

This makes SDT a **high-value preparation topic** — relatively few questions on the paper, but the children who can do them tend to score well overall.

## Teaching Notes

### The SDT Triangle Is Essential
Every tutor resource emphasises the triangle method as the primary teaching tool. Children should be able to draw it from memory and use it to derive all three formula rearrangements. Practice covering each variable (D, S, T) repeatedly until it becomes automatic.

### Unit Conversion Is the Battleground
The formula itself is straightforward — most children grasp S = D ÷ T quickly. The difficulty is almost entirely in **unit conversion**. Teaching priorities:

1. **Minutes to hours** — the base-60 issue. Drill the common fractions (15 min = 1/4, 20 min = 1/3, etc.) until automatic
2. **Hours-and-minutes to decimal hours** — "2 hours 30 minutes = 2.5 hours" must be instant
3. **Metres to kilometres** — straightforward (÷ 1000) but children forget under pressure
4. **km/h to m/s** — teach the ÷ 3.6 shortcut alongside the full method (× 1000 ÷ 3600)

### Common Misconceptions to Address Early

1. **"30 minutes = 0.3 hours"** — the decimal-time confusion. Address this head-on with counterexamples. Ask: "If 30 minutes = 0.3 hours, what would 100 minutes be? 1.0 hours? But we know 60 minutes = 1 hour..." This logical contradiction helps children self-correct.

2. **"Average speed = add speeds and divide by 2"** — explicitly teach why this is wrong using a concrete example: "If you drive 60 km at 60 km/h (takes 1 hour) then 60 km at 30 km/h (takes 2 hours), the simple average would be 45 km/h. But total distance is 120 km, total time is 3 hours, so average speed is 40 km/h."

3. **Multiplying when you should divide** — the triangle provides a visual check. If it's below the line, you multiply. If it's the top value, you divide.

### Build Confidence with D1 Before Pushing to D2/D3
Children who are insecure with the basic formula will panic when unit conversion is added. Ensure they can reliably get D1 questions right (whole numbers, matching units, single step) before introducing conversion challenges.

### Real-Life Context Helps
GL always uses real-life scenarios. Practice with journeys the child knows — "How far is it to Grandma's house? How long does it take? So what's our average speed?" This makes the abstract formula concrete and memorable.

### Timetable Reading Is a Separate Sub-Skill
Some children can do SDT calculations but struggle with timetables because they involve:
- Reading across rows and down columns simultaneously
- Identifying which row/column corresponds to which station/stop
- Calculating time differences that bridge across hours
- Selecting the correct service from multiple options

Practice timetable reading separately before combining it with SDT calculations.

### Watch for These Signs of Confusion
- Child writes "30 min = 0.3 h" — base-60 misconception
- Child averages two speeds instead of using total D ÷ total T
- Child cannot identify which value is speed, distance, or time in a word problem
- Child uses the right formula but forgets to convert the answer to the requested unit
- Child freezes on "unusual" fractions like 20 min = 1/3 hour

### Exam Technique
- **Read the question twice** — check what unit the answer should be in
- **Convert units first** — before touching the formula
- **Check reasonableness** — "Is 500 km/h a sensible speed for a cyclist? No — I've made an error"
- **Use the triangle** — cover what you want, read what's left
- **Don't average speeds** — always use total distance ÷ total time for average speed
