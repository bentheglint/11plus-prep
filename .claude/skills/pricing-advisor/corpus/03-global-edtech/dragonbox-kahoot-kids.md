## Overview

DragonBox was a Norwegian edtech company (founded 2011, Oslo) specialising in maths learning games for children — most notably DragonBox Algebra, widely cited as among the most effective maths games ever created. In 2019, Kahoot! acquired DragonBox as part of its shift from corporate quiz games into family and children's learning. The combined entity now markets children's educational games under the Kahoot! Kids+ subscription, which bundles DragonBox apps alongside other acquired children's content (Poio reading, Marble It Up, and others). This entry covers the Kahoot! Kids+ subscription as the current commercial vehicle for DragonBox content, alongside the legacy individual app purchase model.

## Pricing tiers

**Individual App Purchases (Legacy DragonBox model)**
- Price: $4.99–$9.99 per app (approx. £4–£8); one-time purchase
- Billing: One-time
- Included: Permanent access to a single app (e.g. DragonBox Algebra 5+, DragonBox Numbers, DragonBox Big Numbers). No ongoing content updates after initial purchase in most cases. Apps are sold through Apple App Store and Google Play.

**Kahoot! Kids+ (Subscription)**
- Price: From $3.00/month (approx. £2.40/month) billed annually (~$36/year, approx. £29/year)
- Monthly billing: ~$5.99/month (approx. £4.80/month)
- Billing: Monthly or annual
- Included: Access to the full Kahoot! Kids+ app bundle — DragonBox maths games, Poio reading, Marble It Up physics game, and periodic new additions. Multi-child under one parent account (typically up to 4 child profiles).

**7-Day Free Trial**
- Available on Kahoot! Kids+. Trial auto-converts to paid subscription at end of period. Credit card required.

**Kahoot! (Business/Education — separate product)**
- Price: Custom / per-seat for schools and corporates; not directly relevant to consumer family pricing.
- Note: Kahoot!'s main business is B2B quiz/training software. Kids+ is a separate consumer product.

## Free vs paid (the core question for global edtech)

The original DragonBox apps used a pure paid-upfront model with no free tier and no ongoing subscription — each app was a premium one-time purchase. This model worked for parents who discovered DragonBox through word-of-mouth or teacher recommendation but struggled to generate sustained growth in an App Store environment where free apps dominate discovery.

Kahoot!'s acquisition and subsequent Kids+ subscription represents a strategic pivot: bundle the premium content into a low-cost subscription, reduce per-app friction, and build a recurring revenue stream. The $3/month annual price point is deliberately low — designed to compete with "the cost of one coffee" positioning and minimise parental hesitation. The trade-off is that revenue per user is lower than the old per-app model unless users stay subscribed for more than 2–3 months.

## Family / multi-user pricing

Kahoot! Kids+ is inherently a family product: the subscription covers multiple child profiles (typically up to 4) under a single parent account. There is no per-child charging and no separate family tier — the base subscription is already family-priced. This is a strong design choice for a children's edtech product: the purchasing unit is the household, and the price reflects that. At $36/year for up to 4 children, the per-child annual cost is effectively $9, which is competitive against any individual-child subscription in the market.

## Unusual mechanics

- **Premium-to-subscription transition**: The DragonBox-to-Kahoot! transition is a case study in migrating a premium one-time purchase product to a subscription model. The individual apps remain available as one-time purchases (legacy distribution), while the subscription bundles them at a lower per-app cost. This dual-track approach avoids alienating existing customers while capturing new users on a recurring model.
- **Bundle-and-upsell strategy**: Kahoot! has used acquisitions (DragonBox, Poio, Drops, Actimo) to bulk up the Kids+ bundle, increasing the perceived value of the subscription without increasing price. Each acquisition makes the subscription harder to cancel ("I'd lose all those apps").
- **7-day trial**: Shorter than the 30-day trials offered by Brilliant and others. The 7-day window is sufficient for parents to see engagement but is deliberately brief to encourage conversion before the novelty effect wears off.
- **App Store dependency**: A significant portion of Kids+ subscriptions are managed through Apple and Google's billing systems, which take a 15–30% platform commission. This compresses margins and creates dependency on platform policies.
- **No gamification beyond the game itself**: DragonBox's core mechanic is the game — there are no external streaks, hearts, or leaderboards layered on top. The engagement mechanic is intrinsic to the learning experience.

## Positioning copy

Kahoot! Kids+ is positioned around breadth and value: "Award-winning educational games for kids in one subscription." The "award-winning" framing references DragonBox's numerous educational awards, lending credibility to what is now a bundle product. Parent-facing copy emphasises child engagement and screen time justification: "Screen time your kids will love — and you will too." Pricing copy uses the annual breakdown: "Less than a cup of coffee a month." The 7-day trial CTA is prominent: "Try free for 7 days."

## Lessons for a UK 11+ app at smaller scale

The DragonBox trajectory — from premium one-time purchase to subscription bundle — illustrates the long-term pull of recurring revenue even for products with strong standalone value. For a 11+ app, the lesson is to build for subscription from the start rather than retrofitting it later. The $36/year for 4 children ($9/child/year) is an interesting floor: it demonstrates that parents will pay even at very low price points if the content is demonstrably educational and the product is trusted. Kahoot!'s bundling strategy — each acquisition making cancellation harder — is a playbook for a small product: add free content drops and features over time to increase the perceived cost of leaving.
