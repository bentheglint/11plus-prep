## Who / what

Madhavan Ramanujam is a partner at Simon-Kucher & Partners, the global pricing and commercial strategy consultancy that has conducted more than 10,000 pricing projects. His 2016 book *Monetizing Innovation: How Smart Companies Design the Product Around the Price* (co-authored with Georg Tacke) synthesises what separates the roughly 20% of product launches that succeed financially from the 80% that don't. The book's core provocation is that most companies treat pricing as the last step of product development; Ramanujam argues it must be the first.

## Core ideas (their key beliefs / frameworks)

- **Price-first development**: willingness-to-pay (WTP) conversations with customers must happen before engineering resources are committed. If you cannot answer "what will someone pay for this?" you have not yet earned the right to build it.
- **The Four Monetisation Failures** — these are the four ways products fail financially:
  1. **Feature shock** — cramming too many features into one product, producing something too complex and expensive for any single segment.
  2. **Minivation** — a genuinely good product priced too low; the right product, the wrong price, leaving substantial revenue on the table.
  3. **Hidden gem** — a blockbuster product that never reaches market because it sits outside the core business and senior leaders won't champion it.
  4. **Undead** — an innovation customers simply do not want, kept alive by internal politics despite market indifference.
- **How you charge > how much you charge**: the choice of pricing model (subscription, usage-based, per-seat, freemium, pay-as-you-go) has a larger effect on revenue than the exact price point chosen.
- **Segment by willingness to pay, not demographics**: customer segments should be built around differences in WTP, not firmographics or user personas. The question is "who values this differently, and why?"
- **Leaders, fillers, and killers** (product configuration): features driving purchase decisions are "leaders"; nice-to-haves are "fillers"; features that fewer than 20% of customers want but that block deals are "killers" — and killers must be removed from the core bundle.
- **Fewer tiers, cleaner bundles**: maintain fewer than four product tiers with fewer than nine benefit statements per tier. Over-tiering creates decision paralysis.
- **Maintain pricing integrity**: discounting should be controlled and deliberate. Desperate rate cuts signal low confidence and train buyers to wait for deals.
- **C-suite ownership**: pricing is too strategically important to delegate to a pricing analyst. Senior leaders must own it.

## Method / how to apply

**Step 1 — Have the WTP conversation before development**
Conduct one-on-one customer interviews, focus groups, or quantitative surveys early in the product discovery phase. Ask:
- "What seems like an acceptable price?"
- "What would feel expensive but still possible?"
- "What would be prohibitively expensive?"
- "Would you pay £X?" (then always follow with: "Why?")

**Step 2 — Segment by WTP differences**
Plot responses and look for natural clusters. Customers willing to pay 2–3× more than others are a different segment with different needs — find the feature pattern that explains the gap.

**Step 3 — Configure bundles using the leaders/fillers/killers model**
Map every feature onto the three categories for each segment. Remove killers. Build tiers from leaders + fillers. Do not bundle features customers in one segment actively dislike just to fill out a tier.

**Step 4 — Choose the pricing model deliberately**
Evaluate five models: subscription, dynamic pricing, auction/market-based, pay-as-you-go, freemium. The model should align with how value accrues to the customer. Usage-based works when value is variable; subscription works when value is continuous.

**Step 5 — Apply behavioural tactics**
Six behavioural techniques from the book:
1. *Compromise effect* — place the target tier in the middle, between a stripped-down and a premium option.
2. *Anchoring* — lead with the highest price or the most expensive tier to set context.
3. *Quality signalling* — use price itself to imply premium positioning.
4. *Razor and razor blades* — low entry cost, recurring variable fees.
5. *Pennies-a-day* — reframe annual cost as a daily amount.
6. *Psychological thresholds* — avoid cliff prices (e.g., do not price at exactly £100 if customers psychologically resist crossing that round number).

**Step 6 — Monitor with sales KPIs**
Track win/loss ratios, price deviation from list, deal cycle time, and customer metrics. Run regular deal deconstructions with cross-functional teams.

## Applicability to a UK 11+ app at pre-launch / early-launch stage

Ramanujam would say the single most important thing a pre-launch founder can do is talk to parents about WTP before finalising the subscription price — not after. For a UK 11+ app, where parental WTP varies considerably between tutoring-heavy families (who already spend £40–80/week on tutors and have high willingness to pay) and families seeking a lower-cost alternative, segmenting by WTP rather than child age or school type is the sharper lens. His framework also warns directly against the "minivation" trap: pricing a genuinely strong product at £4.99/month because it feels safe, when the same parents would pay £14.99 without hesitation if the value was communicated clearly.
